# The Witch Bababoefoe

*Origin story · slug: `witch-bababoefoe` · seasonal*

The purple Bababoefoe found a **witch hat** — tall, pointy, a little crumpled at the
tip — snagged on a bramble at the edge of the wood. It set the hat on its round head,
and at once it knew, the way Bababoefoes always know, that it was a witch now.

But not the kind of witch you might worry about. A Witch Bababoefoe's magic is the
smallest, kindest magic there is: it can make a worried thing feel a little bit
better. That is all. It cannot turn anyone into a toad. It would never want to; toads
are lovely as they are.

In its **hidden pocket**, the Witch Bababoefoe carried **a single dried flower** — a
tiny purple aster, saved from autumn. This was its whole spellbook. Whenever it
needed magic, it would take out the flower, breathe in its faint sweet smell, and
remember that even after the cold comes, flowers come back. That was the spell. It
worked every time.

One grey afternoon, the wind blew and blew, and a little critter at the burrow door
grew frightened of the noise. The trees creaked. The leaves rushed. Everything felt
too big and too loud, the way the world sometimes does.

So the Witch Bababoefoe went to work. It took the dried flower from its pocket and
held it up to the frightened little one. "Smell this," it said. "It's a small brave
smell." And the frightened critter breathed in, and the smell was warm, and the
world got just a little smaller and softer.

But the best magic was not the flower. The best magic came next. The Witch
Bababoefoe reached out its paw — magnetic, like every Bababoefoe paw — and clasped
the frightened one's hand, and then reached for another, and another, until a whole
**boefoe-chain** stood together in the doorway, facing the wind side by side. And a
funny thing happened: when you are one small critter, the wind is enormous. But when
you are a chain of friends all holding on, the wind is just weather, and rather
exciting, and nothing to fear at all.

"That's the real spell," said the Witch Bababoefoe, tucking the flower back into its
pocket. "Holding on. Everything else is just the flower."

And the wind blew itself out by evening, the way winds do, and nobody had been
turned into a toad, which everyone agreed was for the best.

If you ever meet a Witch Bababoefoe, ask to smell the flower in its pocket. Then
take its hand. That is the whole of the magic, and it is enough.
