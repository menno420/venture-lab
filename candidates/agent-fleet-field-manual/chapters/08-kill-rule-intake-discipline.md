# Chapter 8 — Kill-Rule Intake Discipline: Every Candidate Declares How It Dies

The failure mode of a productive agent fleet is not idleness — it is *sunk cost*. A fleet that can build things cheaply will happily keep building a thing that will never sell, because building feels like progress and there is no moment that forces the question "should this stop?" The discipline that supplies that moment is the **kill-rule intake**: before any candidate is built, it gets an intake document that states, in binding terms, the conditions under which it is abandoned. This book's own intake is the worked example.

## The three binding fields

An intake in this lane must declare three kill-rule fields. They are not aspirations; they are the terms by which the candidate is later judged.

**Validation signal.** A specific, observable, time-boxed condition that must be met, or the candidate is ledgered as a negative result. This book's intake (`candidates/agent-fleet-field-manual/INTAKE.md`) states it precisely: *within 14 days of ≥2 free chapters going live, at least one of — ≥100 organic reads on a chapter, ≥5 signups on a free-chapter CTA, or a first paid sale. Else ledgered negative.* Notice the shape: a deadline, a trigger event (chapters live), and a menu of concrete thresholds any one of which suffices. There is no wiggle room for "it feels promising."

**First-ten-customers path.** The named channels by which the first ten customers would actually arrive, with owner-gated steps marked. This book's path is honest to the point of self-indictment: free chapters as SEO articles, then X/LinkedIn threads, then a Gumroad listing, then r/ClaudeAI and Indie Hackers posts — every step owner-gated. And the intake attaches a warning to its own path: *it rides the SAME saturated community funnel as the membership kit and template packs — it does not diversify the lane's channel risk.* An intake that cannot name how customers arrive has not earned a build; an intake that names a funnel already known to be saturated says so.

**Max agent-effort budget.** A token ceiling to a shippable v0.1. This book's cap is **90k tokens**. Over budget *without* the validation signal is, by rule, a ledgered negative. The budget is what converts "we can always do a bit more" into a bright line.

## Why the intake is binding, not decorative

The three fields work as a unit. The budget bounds the *cost*; the validation signal bounds the *time and defines success*; the first-ten path forces an honest look at *distribution* before a line of the product exists. Together they make abandonment a *defined outcome* rather than a failure of nerve. When a candidate hits its budget without its signal, no one has to argue for killing it — the intake already decided, in advance, when everyone was calm and nothing was sunk.

This book's intake also carries the scoring that got it here — a weighted rubric across distribution, agent-buildability, owner-click cost, speed-to-first-dollar, and willingness-to-pay, totaling 3.55 out of 5. The two lowest scores are the honest ones: distribution (3, "soft organic pull and heavy free-substitute competition") and willingness-to-pay (2, "guides and eBooks have low, soft WTP; free threads and blog posts substitute heavily"). The intake even has a section titled "Why this might fail," which names the softest-category problem and the channel-concentration problem in plain words. A candidate that documents its own weakest axes is a candidate that can be killed on evidence rather than defended on ego.

## Honesty about this candidate specifically

It would be dishonest to teach kill-rule discipline in a book whose own intake I did not hold to the same standard. So: this book is candidate #4, scored 3.55, in the *softest* revenue category on the board, riding a funnel already known to be saturated by two sibling products. Its conservative first-90-day revenue is $0–$156 (0–4 sales at $39), and $0 without distribution. Its intake originally **deferred** the build pending test-kit validation; it was un-deferred by a direct owner steer, not by the intake's own signal being met. All of that is in the intake, in the open, before this chapter was written. That is the discipline demonstrating itself.

## The rule you take away

Make every candidate declare, before it is built, exactly how it dies: a time-boxed validation signal with concrete thresholds, a named first-ten-customers path with its weaknesses admitted, and a maximum agent-effort budget. Over budget without the signal is a ledgered negative, decided in advance so no one has to find the courage later. An intake that cannot state its own kill conditions — or that flinches from naming its weakest axes — has not earned the build.
