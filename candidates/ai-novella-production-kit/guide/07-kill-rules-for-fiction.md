# 7. Kill rules for fiction

## The trap this chapter closes

Fiction production has a uniquely seductive failure mode: **writing
book N+1 is always more rewarding than checking whether anyone bought
book N.** A productive AI-assisted pipeline makes this worse — when a
finished novella costs days instead of months, you can build a
20-title catalog with zero sales and call it momentum. The production
lane behind this kit ran (and runs) explicit kill rules against
itself; this chapter is those rules, applied to books.

## The rules

1. **Intake before build.** Before a new title/series gets drafting
   time, it gets a scored intake: distribution (weighted heaviest —
   can you *reach* this book's readers?), buildability, launch
   effort, speed to first sale, willingness-to-pay. Score below the
   line = do not write it, however fun. The production lane killed
   concepts at this gate in writing, with the arithmetic shown —
   including concepts that scored *above* 3.0 but had a fatal single
   axis (distribution 2 under a 35% weight kills a 3.175).
2. **A validation signal, bound before launch.** Each title carries a
   concrete, dated signal: *≥1 sale OR ≥N reads on its funnel article
   within 30 days of listing* — decided while sober, before the
   launch, so "it just needs more time" has to argue with a number
   you set yourself.
3. **Kill clocks, armed at publish.** T+7: any signal at all on the
   funnel (reads, traffic)? T+30 (or T+14 for faster categories): the
   validation signal, or the ledger entry. Put the dates in the
   packet where you cannot lose them.
4. **The negative ledger.** A miss is *recorded*, permanently: what
   was expected, what happened, what the lesson costs going forward.
   The production convention treats kills as assets — the record
   stops the same concept being re-pitched from memory in six months
   (it happened; the ledger caught it).
5. **Series economics obey the same gate.** Book 2 of a series whose
   book 1 has zero signal is a *new intake*, not a default. The
   honest exception — series where later books lift earlier ones —
   must be argued in the intake as a distribution claim, not assumed.
6. **Editions don't dodge the clock.** A novella cut or translation
   of an unsold base manuscript inherits the base's signal state;
   deriving editions (chapter 3) is cheap supply, and the kill rules
   exist because supply was never the constraint.

## Budget honesty

The production intakes bind a build budget (agent-effort/tokens or
your hours) per title and record overruns instead of hiding them.
Fiction word counts make this natural: you know the band, you know
the drafts — write down what the book actually cost. A catalog whose
per-title cost is honest is a catalog that can notice when the
economics stop working.

## Honesty block

These rules were battle-run on the *product* side of the source
catalog (real kills at intake, real T+7/T+14 clocks armed on a live
listing, a real negative-ledger practice); on the *fiction* side the
catalog is fully gated but pre-launch — no book kill clock has fired
yet, because no book has been published yet. The rules transfer; the
fiction-side evidence is of gates honestly applied, not of kills
survived. And kill rules do not make books sell — they make not
selling cost you the truth instead of another manuscript.
