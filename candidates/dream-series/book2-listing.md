## One-line hook (logline)
When his lifelong nightmare turns out to be a *place* — a drowned, mirror-thin city where the seam between real and fake never closes — twelve-year-old Sam Vermeer goes to Venice to learn to hold history steady, and discovers the people sworn to keep the record true once broke it themselves.

## Short blurb (≈50 words)
Sam's nightmare has a date now — and Venice, the mirror city, is where he'll learn what it means. Training with the Vigil among covered mirrors and rising water, Sam learns to Anchor, meets a warm voice from his past turned half-ally, and uncovers a four-hundred-year-old lie that could drown a city — and a boy.

## Back-cover copy
*Every mirror in Venice runs a half-second late. That's how you know you're still awake.*

Sam Vermeer used to think his 4 a.m. nightmare was just a bad dream. Now he knows it's a real moment, waiting for him on a day that hasn't come — and his lost father is in it. To survive what's coming, the Vigil send him to the one city built on the line between real and fake: **Venice**, where the seam never fully closes, and an old mirror-maker will teach him to hold the impossible steady with his bare hands.

But Venice is sinking, one centimetre a year, and it's sinking for a reason. Four hundred years ago the keepers of the true record did the one thing they swear they never do — they *forged* a moment, to save the city — and the wound has never healed. Now the Palimpsest has come to tear it open and prove there was never any difference between the two sides at all.

Caught between a teacher who knew his father, a warm voice who won't say whose side she's on, and a best friend who refuses to be left behind, Sam is about to learn the hardest rule of all: that everyone can do the terrible arithmetic — *one life against thousands* — right up until it's someone they love.

And then there's no clean edit. Not even to save the person you can't live without.

*The second adventure in **Lull** — for readers who check whether their own reflection blinks a half-second late.*

## Content notes (honest, for the owner)
- Themes: the cost of power; whether the ends justify the means; grief and a missing parent; the ethics of "fixing" the past. These are load-bearing, not incidental — the book argues, in kid-legible terms, that there is no free edit.
- One character betrays the protagonist for a defensible reason (saving a city). It is framed as tragic, not villainous — deliberate, for the series' moral spine.
