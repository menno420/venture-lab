## The one image
A drowned, doubled city. **Venice at the blue hour, seen across still black water that mirrors it — but the reflection is subtly *wrong*** (the tell of the whole book: the mirror-city where reflections run late). In the real half: the leaning towers and domes of Venice. In the reflected half, bleeding up through the water like the old text under a scraped page: the **bruised purple-yellow sky and ash of the dead city (Pompeii)** — so the single image carries both the setting and the secret. A small warm point of light — **the Lantern** — burns at the vanishing point where the two halves meet, the only warm colour on the cover, with a small dark wading figure behind it.

Foreground, small, back to us (series-consistent — keep the narrator's face unseen): **a boy on a low black boat / on green water-steps, holding the lit brass lantern up over the dark water.**

## Mood & palette
- **Mood:** eerie wonder, thin-ice unease, warmth-in-the-cold. Beautiful first, ominous underneath — exactly the "is this real?" feeling (world.md §0/§5).
- **Palette:** deep lagoon black-greens and blue-hour indigo; wet-stone greys; the one warm accent (lantern gold) doing all the emotional work; a bruise of purple-yellow smuggled into the reflection.
- **Light:** single warm source (the lantern) against a sourceless cold glow — nods to the dream-tell "light has no single source" (§6.1).

## Type & series system
- Big, kid-legible title; **LULL** as the series mark (small, consistent across all three spines); *THE MIRROR CITY* as the book title, largest.
- Establish a **three-book spine system** now: Book 1 water/Amsterdam-cold, Book 2 mirror/Venice (this), Book 3 ash/Pompeii-heat — same layout, shifting element (water → mirror → fire) and accent temperature (cold → doubled → warm).

## Must-haves / must-nots
- **Must:** the lantern (the series prop-echo); doubled/mirrored composition; water; a hidden second image in the reflection; the narrator's face hidden.
- **Must-not:** no gore, no literal skeletons/plague-corpses (age-appropriate — peril not horror); no photo-real Venice postcard; no character's face; no volcano drawn *literally* on the front (it lives only as the bruised reflection — a reveal, not a spoiler).

## Age & positioning line (for the cover's back-flap feel)
Middle-grade, 9–13, crossover to YA. "For readers who check whether their own reflection blinks a half-second late."
