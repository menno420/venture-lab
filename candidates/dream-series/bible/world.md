## 0. The one-sentence premise

Every dream is a real place — a true moment of the past, the future, or a
parallel world — and a handful of people can wake up *inside* the dream, act
there, and drag the results back across the line; two secret factions have been
fighting over that power for as long as there have been stories about magic.

The whole series lives on one feeling: **the line between real and fake is thin,
and sometimes neither you nor the reader can tell which side of it you're on.**

---

## 1. Metaphysics — how dreams work

**Dreams are travel, not invention.** When you sleep, your attention leaves and
lands somewhere that genuinely exists or existed or will: a moment of history, a
moment yet to come, or a moment in a parallel version of the world. You don't
*make* the dream. You *visit* it. This is true for everyone — the sleeping mind
is a tourist that never keeps its ticket stubs.

**Three destinations.** Past (a moment that happened), future (a moment that
will, or one of the moments that could), parallel (a world that split from ours).
Most dreams are shallow past-visits — that's why dreams feel like scrambled
memory. Vivid, "too-real" dreams are usually future or parallel.

**Almost nobody can act.** A Sleeper (the ordinary dreamer) can't choose where
they go, can't change anything, and forgets on waking — the forgetting is not an
accident, it's the same mechanism that hides the magic in the waking world (see
§5, **the Lull**). A tiny minority can do three things Sleepers can't:
1. **Wake inside the dream** — become aware, on purpose, that this is real.
2. **Act with intent** — move, hide, take, leave a mark.
3. **Carry a thread back** — bring something across the line, or change a moment
   so that the change bleeds into waking reality.

Those people are the story. Everyone else is scenery who will swear nothing
happened.

**Recurring dreams are snags.** A dream you have again and again is a real moment
your attention keeps catching on — usually because it *matters*: something
happened there, or will, or should have gone another way. A recurring nightmare
is a snag loud enough that other dreamers can feel it, like a struck bell.
> **Why the story starts on a sixth birthday:** a snag that loud is a beacon.
> The narrator's recurring 4 a.m. nightmare, begun on his sixth birthday, is how
> both factions find him. (It also lands on the exact age the Lull is meant to
> seal over a child — see §5 — so his was the year the seal didn't quite take.)

## 2. The line, the seam, and the bleed

**The line** is the border between dreaming and waking. It is not a wall; it is a
**seam** — stitched, not solid. Skilled dreamers can *thin* the seam. Where the
seam is thin, dream-stuff leaks into waking and waking-stuff can be pulled into a
dream. Where the seam is naturally thick (bright, busy, brand-new places) it
resists, and thinning it is hard, exhausting work.

When the seam is thin, **you cannot always tell which side you are on.** This is
the engine of every ambiguous scene in the series (§6).

**The bleed** is the cost of change. Anything altered across the line leaves a
trace — a seam-mark where reality was overwritten. Bleeds show up as small
*wrongnesses*: a clock with thirteen hours, a street sign spelled almost right, a
stranger you've now passed three times, a reflection that blinks a half-second
late. The more you change, the more it bleeds. **Bleeds are how the two factions
hunt each other** — and they are the glitches the reader is invited to catch
(§6.2). The villains' own name is built on this flaw (§4).

## 3. The Rules of dream-control (costs and hard limits)

Crisp rules are what make the "is this real?" ambiguity *land* — the reader can
audit a scene against them. Dream-control has six.

**Rule 1 — Conservation of attention (the Ledger).** You can only pull across
what you can hold whole in your mind at once. A coin: easy. A bicycle: you must
hold every spoke, or it arrives wrong — fused, missing a wheel, warm where it
should be cold. Overreach and the working *collapses*, and the collapse charges
you a **backwash**: you lose a real memory to pay for the fake you forced across.
Power, at bottom, is *how much you can hold steady while it's thinned.*

**Rule 2 — The bleed (see §2).** Every change leaves a trace. There is no clean
edit. The best dreamers change as little as possible and *darn* the bleed behind
them (a Vigil craft, §4).

**Rule 3 — You can't unlive a life.** You travel to real moments, but you are a
guest, not the author. You can *nudge* a moment — move an object, whisper an
idea, block a doorway, hide — but you cannot rewrite a person's will directly.
To change history you must change the *conditions* and let the person choose.
This is why the power-faction plays a patient, deniable long game instead of
snapping its fingers, and why "keeping history true" is a defensible fight and
not a lost one.

**Rule 4 — The waking tax.** Every crossing costs sleep-debt. Small pulls: you
wake tired. Big workings: you wake wrecked, or you don't wake for a while —
**going under**, days lost to a sleep you can't be shaken from. Push far enough
and you don't come back. This bounds the power and raises the stakes: the
protagonist's own father *went under* years ago and never surfaced (Book-1 hook).

**Rule 5 — Shadow and pull (the street gift).** Even an ordinary skilled dreamer
can reliably do two small things: **pull** a small object across (a key, a coin,
a knife), and **shadow-step** — thin the seam around themselves so they slip
half out of the waking world: hard to see, hard to touch, but they can't breathe
well there and can't stay long. These are the everyday, chase-scene powers.

**Rule 6 — The spectrum (see §7).** Power is the amount of reality you can hold
steady while it's thinned, *and* the thickness of seam you can thin at all. Most
can manage a coin and a few seconds of shadow in a quiet place. The rare elite
(the **Vivid**, §7) can hold themselves fully real in a bright, crowded, waking
square — and hold *large* mass across the line (**Anchoring**, §7). The rarest
can hold a moment of *history* steady enough to nudge it and make it stick.

## 4. The two factions

### The Palimpsest — the ones who rewrite (owner's blank #1; recommended name)

*A palimpsest is a manuscript scraped clean and written over, where the old text
bleeds back through.* That is exactly their method and their flaw.

- **Goal:** total power over the whole world — not by armies but by *revision*.
  Change the conditions of the past and future, patiently, until the world they
  rule is simply the world that "always was." Every legend of magic and every
  unexplained thing traces back to one of their revisions.
- **Belief:** history is a rough draft and they are its editors. They call
  themselves gardeners pruning the timeline toward "a better world" — theirs.
- **Method:** small, deniable nudges (Rule 3), stacked over years. Their signature
  is the **bleed** they can never fully hide (Rule 2) — the old text always shows.
- **Structure (cells, patient, patrician):** apprentices → **Scriveners**
  (working dreamers who make the small edits) → **Vivids** (the elite muscle) →
  **the First Draft** (the inner circle who decide which moments get rewritten).
- **Recruitment:** they hunt strong young dreamers by reading recurring-dream
  beacons (§1) and by running quiet **dream-fairs** in seam-thin places. They
  offer belonging, safety, and power, and they are *good* at it — the recruiter
  in Book 1 is the most charming person the narrator has ever met.

### The Vigil — the ones who keep the record true (keeper-faction; author-named)

The owner's fragment named only the villains; the counter-faction is named here.

- **Goal:** keep history *un-forged*. Not frozen — un-stolen. They believe a life
  that didn't happen the way it happened is a life someone took.
- **Belief:** "Keep the record true." A darned bleed is a wound closed; a forged
  moment is a person erased.
- **Method:** they **darn** bleeds (mend the seam-marks the Palimpsest leaves),
  keep archives of *the true record* (photographs, ledgers, testimony — the one
  thing that can outlast the Lull, §5), and undo revisions before they set.
- **Structure (poor, scattered, reactive):** a resistance of night-shift workers,
  librarians, insomniacs, ferry hands, museum guards — people whose jobs put them
  in seam-thin places at seam-thin hours. Smaller and poorer than the Palimpsest,
  and — crucially for Book 2 — **not clean**: they have buried a bleed of their
  own.
- **Recruitment:** they find the same beacons and arrive offering *truth and a
  choice* rather than power. Slower, less shiny, and the narrator almost doesn't
  pick them.

Both factions want the narrator. That tug-of-war is the engine of Book 1.

## 5. The Lull — why the world doesn't notice (owner addition, 2026-07-11; CANON)

The owner's second core mechanic: impossible things happen in plain sight, but
regular people "see what they want to believe." The device that does this is
named here **the Lull** (options in `names.md`; explicitly *not* "the Mist").

**The unifying idea (owner-confirmed canon):** the Lull is the *waking cousin of
dream-forgetting.* The same soft pull that wipes a Sleeper's dream on waking also
wipes a bystander's memory of the impossible — and it always overwrites the truth
with the most *sleepable* story: **"I must have dreamed it. I misremembered.
Trick of the light."** So the one mechanism that hides the magic is *also* the
reason nobody — sometimes not even the narrator — can be sure what was real.
Concealment and the thin-line ambiguity are the same device. The filter isn't
just a cloak; it is the ambiguity engine.

**What it is (and isn't).** Not fog, not a substance, not a place. It is a
*property of ordinary attention.* An impossible event registers for a second or
two — then the mind, reaching for the story it can rest on, overwrites the memory
and files the true version under "dreamed it." You don't fail to see the magic.
You *unsee* it, a heartbeat later, and feel calmer for it.

**Who sees through it (and why):**
- **Wakers and above** — knowing dreamers are immune, because they've learned the
  tells (§6.1) and *hold the true memory* on purpose. Seeing through the Lull is
  the first thing every dreamer learns to do.
- **Young children (under ~6–7)** — not yet fully "asleep to the world." They see
  true, but can't explain it and aren't believed. The Lull is *meant* to close
  over a child around six. (This is why the narrator's story starts on his sixth
  birthday: the year his seal should have set and didn't.)
- **The feverish, the dying, the badly sleep-deprived** — their boundary is
  already thin, so the Lull's grip slips. Useful and dangerous.
- **Animals** — often. A dog that barks at nothing is barking at something.

**How mundane minds rationalize (the cover story is always the boring one):**
the Lull picks the most true-*adjacent*, least alarming explanation and makes the
witness believe they thought of it themselves.
- A man vanishing into shadow → "he must've ducked round the corner."
- A bicycle appearing from nowhere → "it was there all along, I just didn't clock
  it."
- A flooded midnight square → "must've been a burst water main. It rained."
The cover is never a lie the witness catches; it's a memory they now own.

**Edge cases:**
- **Photos and recordings.** The Lull works on *minds*, not sensors — a camera
  captures the truth. But a Lulled mind *viewing* that footage rationalizes it
  ("compression glitch, bad edit, obviously faked"), so a recording never
  convinces a Lulled viewer. Worse, media of a moment that was changed across the
  line slowly *bleeds* too (Rule 2), the image degrading over days. A photograph
  is therefore the closest thing to proof that exists — which is exactly why the
  Vigil hoards archives, and exactly why proof always slips their grip. It's a
  tool with a hole in it.
- **Crowds Lull faster.** A crowd reaches for one shared boring story and
  reinforces it — consensus is the Lull's strongest form. This is *why*
  manifesting in a crowded square is an elite feat (§7): the Vivid isn't
  invisible; the square simply, collectively, refuses to have seen them, and the
  Vivid has to hold their own reality steady against a thousand minds pushing to
  erase it.
- **The narrator against himself.** Because the Lull's cover story is literally
  "you must have dreamed it," the narrator can't always sort his real memories
  from Lulled-over ones — and neither can the reader. The confession-frame voice
  flags this directly ("I've told this part different ways; the Lull got into my
  own head too"). This is the ambiguity engine at full power.

**How strong dreamers touch it:**
- **Push the Lull** — feed a crowd a *specific* cover story (nudge which boring
  explanation they'll pick). The Palimpsest's favorite tool for keeping revisions
  quiet.
- **Thin the Lull / "wake" someone** — a Vigil darner can lift the Lull off one
  person briefly. Dangerous: a woken bystander who can't handle the truth breaks.
- **Cut the Lull (a "clearing")** — the rarest can clear the Lull from a whole
  space so the magic stands plainly visible. Ruinously expensive; a last resort.

## 6. The ambiguity toolkit (concrete narrative devices)

These are the craft techniques that make "is this a dream?" a *game the reader
can play*, not a cheat.

### 6.1 The tell-list (unreliable sensory tells)
In this world, dreams have tells. The narrator learns them; so does the reader:
- **You can't read text in a dream** — letters swim and rearrange.
- **Clocks won't hold a number** — look away, look back, it's different.
- **You can't feel your own heartbeat.**
- **Light has no single source** — everything is lit, nothing casts a clean
  shadow.
- **You never remember how you got there.**
Then the book *weaponizes* the list: a scene where the narrator checks the clock
and it *holds* — so he relaxes, and so does the reader — but he never checked the
light. The tells are a promise the story is allowed to break exactly once, when
it counts.

### 6.2 Catchable continuity glitches (the bleed, §2)
Plant small wrongnesses in "waking" scenes — a tram number, a misspelled sign, a
stranger who recurs — that read as *texture* on the first pass and pay off later
as *proof* the scene was thinned. Reward the re-reader.

### 6.3 Re-readable scenes
Write at least one early scene so it works completely as ordinary life *and* as a
dream. When the rules are revealed, the reader is sent back to it and finds it
was both all along. (Book 1: the "normal morning" of Ch. 2 shares its props with
the Ch. 1 nightmare.)

### 6.4 The unreliable frame
The confession narrator occasionally admits the record is soft — "this is the
truest version I've got" — because memory of thinned events decays (the waking
tax on recall; the Lull on himself). Keeps the reader productively unsure without
cheating them.

### 6.5 Prop-echo
An object crosses the seam — dream → waking → dream — and the reader tracks it to
stay oriented (Rule 1's "held thing"). The Book-1 nightmare carries one specific
prop (the lantern) that recurs across the line and finally proves the nightmare
was real.

### 6.6 The false wake (the double surface)
A character "wakes," feels relief, ordinary morning — then a single tell breaks
it. Used sparingly (once or twice a book) so it always lands.

## 7. The power spectrum (named tiers)

| Tier | Who | Can do |
|---|---|---|
| **Sleepers** | everyone | Passive dream-tourists. No memory, no agency. Fully under the Lull. |
| **Wakers** (the Woken) | skilled dreamers who *know* | Wake inside dreams; **pull** small objects; **shadow-step**; immune to the Lull. The Palimpsest's Scriveners and the Vigil's rank-and-file. |
| **Vivid** *(owner blank #2; recommended)* | rare — roughly one to a city | Hold themselves fully real in a bright, crowded, waking place (against the crowd's Lull); hold *large* mass across the line. |
| **Anchoring** *(owner blank #3; recommended — the ability, not a tier)* | the strongest Vivids | **Anchor**: pull *and pin* a large object (or themselves) into waking so solidly it stays after they let go. The top of the visible spectrum. |
| **(arc-level, rarest)** | maybe one a generation | Hold a moment of *history* steady enough to nudge it and make the nudge stick. Both factions would burn the world to own one. |

**Where the narrator sits:** found as a Waker with unusual reach — and, at the
Book-1 cliff, shown doing something a Waker shouldn't be able to (make a small
change to a *moment* stick). The hint is planted, never confirmed: he may be
arc-level. And his recurring nightmare may not be a past he visits but a *future*
being forged — which is why both factions need him alive and on their side.

## 8. Where the magic hides in real geography

**The rule:** magic hides where the seam is naturally thin — thresholds,
in-between places, places heavy with sleep or layered with many pasts. That lets
the mythology sit on **real, visitable places**, which is the point: the
"could-this-be-real?" feeling is strongest when the setting is somewhere the
reader could actually go. The map is Netherlands-first (honoring the Dutch
source), widening across Europe as the arc widens (§ arc).

**Book 1 — Amsterdam** (justified anchors):
- **The canals** — water is a mirror, a mirror is a natural seam. The whole old
  city is faintly thin; the new districts are thick.
- **The Begijnhof** — a real medieval courtyard hidden behind an ordinary door
  off the Spui, silent and centuries-old. Reimagined as a **Vigil safehouse**:
  a *still room* where the seam is darned so tight nothing bleeds.
- **Amsterdam Centraal at 4 a.m.** — the dead hour. The Palimpsest runs a
  **dream-fair** on a platform that isn't on any timetable (an in-between track).
- **The Rijksmuseum night galleries** — paintings are *held moments*. The Night
  Watch is a captured crowd. A Vivid can step into a painting's moment. (The
  narrator's surname, **Vermeer**, quietly nods to a painter of captured light —
  a set-piece pays it off in Book 1.)
- **Liminal transit** — the IJ ferry ("the pont"), the night train, Schiphol at
  3 a.m., a multi-storey car park, an endless hospital corridor: all thin because
  they are neither-here-nor-there.

**Books 2–3 — widening Europe** (same rule, bigger stage): **Venice** (a whole
city that is a mirror), the **Paris catacombs** (layered dead), **Pompeii** (a
moment of history frozen mid-collapse — the Palimpsest's holy site, and where the
narrator's nightmare finally has its date). Mirror-cities and frozen-time ruins
are naturally seam-thin, so the magic keeps landing on real ground even as the
map opens up.

## 9. Vocabulary (quick reference for drafting)

- **the line / the seam** — the border between dreaming and waking.
- **thinning** — making the seam permeable. **the bleed** — the trace a change
  leaves.
- **pull** — bring a small object across. **shadow-step** — slip half out of
  waking. **Anchor** — pull-and-pin large mass.
- **going under** — sleep you can't be woken from (the waking tax, Rule 4).
- **the Lull** — the perception filter; **to push / thin / cut** the Lull.
- **darning** — mending a bleed (Vigil craft). **the true record** — the Vigil's
  archives.
- **Sleeper / Waker / Vivid** — the tiers. **the Palimpsest / the Vigil** — the
  factions. **Scrivener / the First Draft** — Palimpsest ranks. **darner** — a
  Vigil member.
- **a snag** — a recurring dream (a real moment attention keeps catching on).
