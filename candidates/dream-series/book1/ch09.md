# Chapter Nine — Night Watch

*Lull, Book One · draft*

Wessel got us into the Rijksmuseum the way the Vigil gets in anywhere, which is that one of them already works there.

Her name was Truus, and she was about a hundred, and she'd been a night guard in the great galleries for longer than my mum had been alive, and she was a darner, and when Wessel and I came up the service stair a little after midnight she looked at me over her glasses and said, "So this is the bell," in a voice like a door in an old house, and that was the whole of my welcome. She had a ring of keys that opened doors that weren't on any plan and she moved through the dark museum like water finds a channel, and she did not once turn on a light.

You have to understand what a museum is at night, once you know what I now knew. In the day it's a building full of pictures and a river of people looking at them. At night, with the crowds gone and the lights down and the guards' torches off, it is the single thinnest place in the whole city, and Truus had spent fifty years darning it so it wouldn't bleed, the way a nurse spends fifty years on one ward.

"Paintings," Wessel said, low, as we went up through the dark toward the Gallery of Honour, "are held moments. That's all a great one is. A dreamer stood in front of a real moment — a kitchen, a face, a crowd stepping off into the night — and held it so hard, so whole, every fold and every glint, that it *stuck,* out here, on this side, in paint. That's what your surname is, kid, if you ever wondered. Vermeer. There was a painter of that name, three streets and four hundred years from here, who could hold light so whole it never left the room he put it in. His moments are up here on these walls, still lit, still true, four centuries on. That's the highest form of the thing you and I do the cheap version of. Anchoring a moment so hard it holds without you."

"And the Palimpsest wants to get *into* one," I said.

"The Palimpsest," said Truus, from the dark ahead, "wants to get into the biggest one there is."

And we came out into the Gallery of Honour, and at the far end of it, alone on its own great wall, lit by nothing and glowing anyway, was the Night Watch.

---

I'd seen it on school trips. Everyone here has. It's enormous — a wall of a painting, dark gold and shadow, a whole militia company of seventeenth-century men caught in the act of marching out, their captain in black stepping forward with his hand thrown out, a lieutenant in gold beside him, a little girl lit up bright as a coin in the middle of all the men and the muskets and the dark, a drum, a dog, a forest of raised pikes. In the daytime it's a painting you shuffle past in a crowd while someone's dad reads the label aloud.

At night, with what I now had behind my eyes, it was a held moment the size of a house, and it was *breathing.*

I don't mean it moved. I mean I could feel it the way I'd felt the flooded square feel me back — a whole crowd of real people, caught mid-step four hundred years ago, held so hard by a dead genius that they were still *there,* still stepping out, still about to march off the edge of the frame into a night that hadn't ended because he'd never let it. Forty-odd men and a girl and a dog, all of them true, all of them held. A captured crowd. And a crowd, Wessel had taught me, is the strongest Lull there is — a thousand minds all agreeing. Turn that around and a held crowd is the strongest *anchor* there is. Forty real people all holding one moment steady forever.

"Now you see why," Wessel murmured. "If you could get inside a moment held this hard — inside the very night this shows — and *change* something in it, and let the painting's own anchor-strength hold your change steady forever the way it holds the moment... you wouldn't be nudging history. You'd be carving it. And the whole crowd of held men would keep your lie true for four hundred years and counting, because that's their job, that's what they've been doing all along."

"What's in it?" I said. "What moment is it? What do they want to change?"

Truus answered, very quietly, from beside me in the dark, an old darner looking up at fifty years of her own careful work.

"It's a company of Amsterdam men," she said, "stepping out into the night on a real evening, to do a real thing, that we do not talk about, because the men in this picture are on their way to a decision, and the decision they made that night — a small one, a civic one, a vote taken in a back room an hour after this — is one of the hinges the whole modern world swung on. Rembrandt didn't paint the vote. He painted them walking to it. But the moment's *in* here, held, four hundred years, and if you got inside and turned that company round before they ever reached the room —" She stopped. "You'd change what they voted. And forty held men would hold the new vote true forever, and no one alive would ever know the world had turned on a different hinge, because there'd be no bleed to catch. The painting itself would be the darning. It's the perfect crime, Sam. It's the one revision that leaves no trace. That's why it's the holy thing to them and the nightmare to us."

---

And then I did the thing that scared Wessel worse than the Night Watch did.

I hadn't planned to. I want that on the record. I was standing looking up at the little girl lit bright in the middle of all that dark, and I felt the moment breathing, felt it *near,* felt the seam in front of it go soft as it always went soft for me, softer than it should, softer than it ever went for anyone — and some part of me, the reaching part, the part that woke in the dream on its own and lulled Fenna on its own, reached out toward the held moment the way you reach for a warm bath, without deciding to, just to feel it, just to be a little closer to it —

and my hand went into the painting.

Not through it. *Into* it. Up to the wrist, into the dark gold, into the very air of a four-hundred-year-old night, and it was cool in there, and I could smell it — woodsmoke, and canal water, and a company of men who'd been drinking — and I heard, from inside, faint and far, a drum.

Behind me Wessel said a word I won't write down and Truus said "*Mother of God,*" and I pulled my hand back out into the dark museum, shaking, gold-smelling, and turned round to two faces I'll never forget, because they were looking at me the exact way I'd looked at the coin the first time it came into my hand — like I was a thing that shouldn't be possible and had just happened anyway.

"Nobody," Wessel said, and his voice was strange, "nobody just *reaches into* the Night Watch, kid. It took Truus twenty years to learn to darn its edges without falling in. A strong Vivid works for an hour to thin the seam on a held moment that size, and it half kills them, and you did it by *leaning toward it because it looked nice.*" He came and took me by both shoulders and looked into my face and for the first time since the ferry I saw that the ferryman was afraid *of me,* a little, the way you're afraid of weather. "Vermeer," he said, almost to himself. "A painter of held light. And a boy who steps into a held moment like it's a puddle." He let go. "Sam. Listen to me. Whatever you are, they already know it, because you just rang it out across every thin place in this city — and if the Palimpsest means to get into this painting and turn that company round, they don't need a hard slow way in anymore."

His eyes went past me, to the great dark breathing wall, to the little door I'd just proved was a puddle.

"They've got you," he said. "You're the way in. That's what she's wanted all along. Not to recruit you. To *use* you as the door. And we've walked you right up to it."

Down the length of the dark gallery, behind us, unhurried, a brass bell rang once — the bell of a chocolate shop that had never existed, three streets away, ringing here — and a warm voice I'd have known anywhere said, delighted, out of the dark between the marble pillars:

"Oh, don't stop on my account. He was doing so *beautifully.*"
