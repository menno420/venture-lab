# Chapter Three — The Man on the Pont
*DREAMLINE, Book One · draft*

I didn't sleep much, and in the morning I did what any sensible almost-twelve-year-old does when a silent man in a long coat has been standing outside his house getting closer without walking.

I told Fenna.

Not all of it. You can't tell someone *there's a lantern from my nightmare on my shelf and it smells of the sea,* because that's the kind of sentence that makes your best friend do a face and text your mum. But you can say *I think some weird guy's been hanging round my street,* which is true and normal and gets you the good kind of Fenna, the one who takes it seriously and immediately makes a plan.

"We'll go the long way," she decided, at lunch, through a mouthful of sandwich. "After school. Past yours, and if Coat Man's there we get a proper look at him in daylight, and if he's actually being weird we tell someone, and if he's just some man, which he is, you buy me an ice cream for wasting my afternoon."

"Why do I buy you the ice cream?"

"Emotional labour," said Fenna, who had recently learned this phrase and was getting a lot of mileage out of it.

So that's how the two of us ended up, at the drowsy grey end of that afternoon, cycling not home but the other way, north, toward the water — the big water, the IJ, the wide harbour behind Centraal station where the city stops being canals and starts being a real working river, cold and dark and busy with things too big for the little grachten. Because Coat Man wasn't at mine. And Fenna, who cannot stand a plan that doesn't pay out, had decided that if we were already out we might as well take the pont across to the north bank and get patat and make an evening of it.

The pont is the ferry. It's free — really free, no ticket, no gate, you just wheel your bike on — and it runs back and forth across the IJ all day and half the night, and it is, I can tell you now with some authority, one of the thinnest places in the whole city.

I didn't know that then. I just knew I liked it.

We rattled our bikes down the ramp behind the station and onto the flat steel deck along with the usual crowd — a dozen commuters with their earbuds in, a woman with a dog, two tourists photographing a seagull like they'd never seen a bird, an old man with a shopping trolley, a delivery guy with a stack of insulated boxes. The horn went, a big flat honk that you feel in your teeth, and the ramp lifted, and the pont pushed off into the harbour.

And this is the thing about the pont, the thing I only have words for now: for the few minutes you're on it, you are nowhere.

You've left one shore and you haven't reached the other. Under you is the IJ, black and wide and deep and moving, and it doesn't hold a reflection like the little canals do — it's too dark, too restless, it just swallows the last of the light and gives nothing back. The station falls away behind you, all its noise thinning to nothing. The north bank hangs ahead, low and far. The gulls wheel and cry. The wind comes straight off the open water with a cold in it that has nothing to do with the mild evening on either shore. You're standing on a slab of steel between two worlds, and for those few minutes you belong to neither of them.

Fenna was leaning on the rail doing an impression of the seagull tourists. I was half-listening and half looking back at the city, at the lights coming on along the waterfront, and that's when I saw the coat.

He was at the back of the deck, near the bikes. Long dark coat, too heavy for the season. Standing perfectly still while everything around him swayed with the boat. Not looking at the water. Looking at me.

"Fen," I said.

"— and the second one goes, 'get its good side' —"

"*Fen.* He's here."

She followed my eyes, and I felt her go still beside me, which is not a thing Fenna does. "The coat guy? From your street?"

"That's him."

"On the boat." She said it flatly, testing it, because it was a lot — because there is a difference between a man who lingers on your street and a man who is on the same free ferry as you across a river you decided to cross forty minutes ago on a whim, and even Fenna, who explains everything, couldn't quite explain that. "Okay. Okay, that's — okay. We just don't get off where he gets off. We stay by people. There's tons of people."

There were tons of people. That turned out not to help at all, but I understand now why she said it. She was right the way normal people are right, which is right about the world as it usually works. She just didn't know yet that we'd stepped off the edge of the world as it usually works and onto a steel deck in the middle of the thin dark water.

The man started toward us.

He didn't hurry. He came up the deck between the parked bikes with that same stillness he carried even while walking, the crowd sort of parting and closing around him without seeming to see him, the way water had parted round my ankle in the dream, and I grabbed the rail behind me because my legs had gone stupid, and Fenna moved a half-step in front of me without seeming to decide to, and the gulls screamed, and the man came on.

He got close enough that I could see under the collar at last.

I'm not going to tell you his face was a monster's. That's the thing nobody warns you about. It was just a face — a grey, ordinary, tired man's face, middle-aged, unremarkable, the face of someone you'd stand next to in a queue for a year and never remember. That was the worst of it. That the thing that had been standing outside my house, that crossed a river without walking, wore a face so plain you'd apologise to it for bumping its trolley.

He looked at me with pale flat eyes and he opened his mouth and he said — and his voice was wrong, too even, no breath under it — he said, "You've been ringing a long time, little snag. Come here."

And he reached for me.

Two things happened at once, so fast I've had to slow them down in my head a thousand times since to get them in order.

The first is that a hand closed on the coat man's shoulder from behind. A big hand, scarred, with oil in the knuckle-creases, attached to a big man in a ferry-crew fleece who had crossed the whole deck in about two seconds without appearing to rush — a gruff, heavy, grey-stubbled man I'd half-noticed loading bikes at the ramp, the kind of man who is so much part of the furniture of a ferry that you never really look at him. He got the coat man by the shoulder and said, low and dry and almost bored, "Not on my boat, you don't."

And the second thing is that the coat man came apart.

I don't have a better word for it. I've tried. He didn't run and he didn't fall and he didn't vanish in a puff of anything. He *thinned.* Between one blink and the next the solid grey man went flat and dark and stretched, like a shadow when a cloud moves off the sun, like ink dropped in water, like a reflection when the water shivers — he smeared sideways into the long shadow the wheelhouse threw across the deck, and got thin, and got dark, and was part of the shadow, and then the shadow was just a shadow, and the deck where he'd stood was empty, and the ferry-hand's big scarred hand was closing on nothing at all.

Gone. In front of everyone. A dozen people. A man had unfolded himself into a shadow and blown out like a candle in a wind, in the grey ordinary evening light, on a public ferry, in front of at least a dozen people.

And here is the part I have to tell you carefully, because this — not the man, not the shadow — this is the thing that broke something in me that has never quite grown back.

Nobody screamed.

I stood there with my heart trying to climb out of my mouth, waiting for the woman with the dog to scream, for the tourists to start filming, for somebody, anybody, to say *did you see that, did you SEE that,* and instead I watched it happen — I watched them decide it hadn't.

I watched it roll across the deck like weather.

The woman with the dog frowned. Just for a second — a real frown, a *something-is-wrong* frown, her eyes going to the empty patch of deck where a whole man had been — and then her face cleared, smoothed right out, and she looked away and gave a little laugh at herself and said, to nobody, "must've got off already, silly thing," and the awful part, the part I felt in my teeth, was that she believed it, she'd already believed it, her mind had already reached out and grabbed the nearest boring true-shaped thing and swallowed the real thing whole. *He got off.* We were in the middle of the river. There was nowhere to get off to. And she'd never in her life think of that, because she wasn't looking anymore, she was scratching her dog's ears and the moment was gone out of her like water out of a cracked cup.

One of the tourists said something about a gull startling him — a gull, there'd been no gull, there'd been a *man* — and nodded like that settled it. The old man with the trolley squinted at the low grey sky and muttered about the light going, the light plays tricks, and looked satisfied, and turned away. All round the deck I could see it working, person after person, a little frown, a little clearing, a little boring story sliding neatly into the hole where the truth had been, and everyone getting quietly, comfortably calmer for it — I *knew* that calm, that was the shelf, that was the lantern, that was the exact warm settling calm that had come over me in my bedroom and told me it was just an old lamp — and I understood, standing there, that the thing that had happened to me two nights ago in my room was happening to a whole boatful of people at once, from the outside, where I could see the shape of it.

They weren't lying. That's what I couldn't get over. Not one of them was pretending. They had *unseen* it, honestly, all the way down, and were happier.

And then Fenna.

Fenna, next to me, my Fenna, who punched Ruben for my hat, who explains everything, who had been standing a half-step in front of me with her fists half-up ready to fight a grown man for me — Fenna frowned.

For exactly one second she frowned at the empty deck, and I saw it, I saw the real thing move behind her eyes, I saw her almost have it —

and then it went.

Her face cleared, like all the others, smoothed and brightened, and she turned to me with her ordinary Fenna grin like nothing at all had happened in the world, and she said, "So do you want mayo on your patat or are you finally going to admit peanut sauce is better?"

I couldn't answer her.

I stood on the deck of the pont with the cold wind off the black water going through me and I looked at my best friend's happy ordinary face, at the clean smooth nothing where a minute ago she'd been ready to fight for me, and it was *this* — not the shadow, not the vanishing, not the grey man reaching for me with his breathless voice — it was this that made me want to sit straight down on the wet steel and never get up.

Because it hadn't touched me.

Whatever had reached into all of them and taken the man away and tucked them into their calm — it had rolled right over me and not caught. I still had it. The man, the shoulder, the thinning, the shadow, the empty deck — all of it, sharp and whole and true and *mine,* and nobody else's, not even hers. I was the only one on a crowded ferry still holding a thing that had actually happened, and everyone I could see had agreed, without a word, without even trying, that it hadn't.

That's the loneliest I have ever been. I want you to know that, before this gets big and strange and full of factions and history and everything I have to tell you. The loneliest single moment of the whole thing wasn't a monster. It was standing next to Fenna while she asked me about peanut sauce.

"Peanut sauce is better," said the ferry-hand.

I turned round. The big grey-stubbled man had come to stand at the rail beside me, not looking at me, looking out at the north bank sliding closer, wiping his oily hand on a rag like he'd just finished with a jammed hinge and not with a man who'd turned into a shadow.

"It is," he said, to the water. "But she said it to needle you, so tell her mayo. Keep her happy. She won't remember why she asked." He glanced at me then, one flat grey look, up and down, unimpressed, the way you'd check a rope for fraying. "You kept it," he said. It wasn't a question. "Course you did. You've been ringing like a struck bell for six years, of course the Lull slides off you. Six years." He shook his head slowly, and there was something in it that wasn't quite pity and wasn't quite respect. "You have absolutely no idea what you are, do you."

"What —" My voice came out wrong. I tried again. "What did you — that man, he — where did he —"

"Not here." He said it quietly and completely, a door closing, exactly the way my mum says *he did.* "Not out loud, not on the open water, not with your friend standing right there half-woke and ready to catch it. You want to have this conversation, you have it careful, and you don't have it now." He folded the rag and put it in his pocket. "But I'll give you one true thing, because you're owed at least one true thing after six years of nobody telling you anything, and because if I don't, the next one that comes for you won't bother being polite about it like your grey friend just was."

He leaned on the rail. The north bank was close now. Lights, low buildings, another ramp waiting.

"The dream you've been having," he said, not looking at me, "since you were a little kid. The water. The lantern." 

Everything in me went cold and still and quiet as the flooded square.

"I never told you about a dream," I whispered.

"No," he agreed. "You didn't have to. It's the loudest thing about you. It's the reason he was on this boat and the reason you're going to have a very interesting week." He said *interesting* like it was the worst word he knew. "That dream is not a dream. And you're going to wish for the rest of your life that somebody had told you that when you were six, and here I am telling you now, so remember I did."

The pont bumped the far dock. That soft heavy shudder, steel on rubber, the deck settling, the horn giving its flat honk.

And across the water — I looked back, I don't know why, the way I'd looked up at the clock, some animal reflex to check the far edge of things — across the black harbour, on the south quay we'd left, in the last of the grey light, there was a young woman.

Standing alone at the very edge of the water. Watching the ferry. Watching, I was completely certain, across all that dark distance, *me.* Too far to make out a face, and yet I somehow knew she was beautiful, knew it the way you know the sun's out with your eyes shut, a kind of warmth coming off her that reached me over the cold width of the whole river — and she was smiling. Right at me. A wide, delighted, dazzling smile, like she'd found exactly the thing she'd come to find, like I was a present with her name on it, and she lifted one hand, slow, and gave me a little wave. Just fingers. Just for me.

I didn't wave back. Something told me not to. It's about the only thing I got right that whole evening.

The ramp came down with a clang and the commuters started wheeling their bikes off toward the ordinary north-bank night and their ordinary dinners, Fenna among them, calling over her shoulder for me to hurry up, already forgetting she'd waited, and the ferry-hand put one big scarred hand flat on the rail beside mine, close, and dropped his voice so low it was almost lost under the water and the gulls and the honk of the horn.

"Wessel," he said. "That's my name. You'll want it. And here's the rest of your one true thing, kid, so listen."

"You're a snag. Six years you've been ringing like a bell. They can all hear you now."

"And they're all coming."
