# Chapter Twelve — The Choice

*Lull, Book One · draft*

The night held.

I felt it go — felt forty men and a lit girl and a drum and a dog snap hard and true and immovable under my open arms, the true evening pinned so deep into what-really-happened that Lena's clever warm fingers found no edge to lift, no slack to scrape, nothing loose anywhere in the whole four-hundred-year dark. She reached for the captain's ear and there was no room. She reached again and the night simply *was,* the way the clock in my dream simply *was,* hard and certain and closed as a shut door.

And Lena Marchetti, the most seen-making person I had ever stood near, the Vivid who could pull a marble table out of a painting and pin it to the floor of the world, knelt down in the woodsmoke in front of a twelve-year-old holding a moment shut, and I watched the delight go out of her face and something I'd never seen there come in.

It was fear. And under the fear, worse, it was *grief* — real grief, the cheapest and truest thing she had — because she'd found the rarest thing alive, the thing she'd been so patient for, and in the same instant she'd found it, it had chosen against her.

"Oh, Sam," she said, and her voice broke on it, and I'll never know how much of that was true and it doesn't matter, because the not-knowing is the wound she leaves in everyone. "You could have been *kind.*"

"I could have been used," I said. My arms were shaking. The backwash had its hand in me already, feeling for its coin. "You said it yourself. You'd have counted me against forty men and spent me. He'd have let my dad stay under to keep me safe. And you'd both have called it love." I held the night. I held it and held it. "She told me to pick a side by what it's willing to lose. You're not willing to lose *anything.* You'd overwrite forty men and call it counting. He'll lose *me* to keep the record true and he hates that he has to, and he'll do it anyway." My voice was going. "So I pick the tired poor ones with the folded arms. I pick the record. I'm sorry. I pick true."

---

She ran, in the end. That's the thing about the Palimpsest, the thing that makes them so hard to be rid of — they're patient, and patience knows when to leave. Lena stood, and the grief folded back down out of her face into something smooth and warm and already planning, and she looked at me one last time in the held dark and said, "You've no idea what you've just made yourself. Both sides know now. You held the Night Watch shut at twelve — you think this is the *end* of people wanting you?" She was thinning already, stepping back toward the propped door, the one I'd opened, the one only I could open, which meant she'd be back the moment she needed me to open another. "I'll see you again, Sam Vermeer. When it costs you everything, I'll be the warm voice in your ear. And you'll remember I was kind to you first." And she smeared sideways into the four-hundred-year dark and was gone, and somewhere far off a chocolate-shop bell that had never existed rang once and stopped.

And then the backwash took its coin.

I want to tell you what it took, because I paid it and I've a right to name it, and because you should know what *keep the record true* actually costs, so you don't think it's free when you cheer for it.

It reached into me, gentle as a grown-up taking a knife off a toddler, and it took my father's face.

Not the fact of him. Not that I have a father, or his name, or that he went under, or the photo over my heart. It took the *him* of him — the last clear memory I owned, the one from my sixth birthday, the weight of him sitting down on the edge of my bed so it dipped and rolled me against him, the coffee smell he wasn't supposed to have at night, the big flat warm hand between my shoulder blades, *I've got you.* It took that. It reached in and lifted it out clean, to pay for the true night I'd forced to hold, and where it had been there is now a smooth place my mind keeps going to the way your tongue goes to a gap, and I know there was something warm there, I know it was the best thing I owned, and I cannot get it back, and I gave it up to keep forty dead men true, and I would — this is the part I can't explain to anyone who wasn't there — I would do it again.

I have a photograph of my father now. It's a good photograph. It's the only face of his I've got left, and a machine printed it, and it's three weeks old and it shows him asleep, and I gave up the warm hand and the coffee smell to buy the truth, and I kept the photo, and that's the trade, that's the whole trade, that's what this costs. I told you at the very start I made a lot of people angry and that some of it still isn't the thing I can tell without my throat closing. This is near it. Not quite it. But near.

---

I came out of the painting into the dark museum on my knees, gold-smelling and hollow, and Wessel caught me before I hit the marble, and Truus was there, and the two Scriveners were gone, run off after their mistress, and the great slab of Anchored marble sat cooling on the gallery floor, a piece of a painting made forever real, which the Vigil would spend a week working out how to put back.

"It held," I said. "The night held. I kept it true."

"I know," Wessel said. His voice was rough. He was looking at me the way you look at someone who's come back from a war you sent them to. "I felt it hold from out here. Kid, there's not a dreamer alive who — you held the Night Watch *shut.* At twelve." He didn't say it like a triumph. He said it like a weight he was going to have to carry now, which is what it was. "What did it cost you? Backwash that size. What did it take?"

I told him. He shut his eyes.

"I'm sorry," he said. "I'd have paid it for you if I could. That's the job. That's the whole rotten beautiful job." And then, because he was Wessel, and because a true thing plainly said is the only comfort he's got: "You chose us. Under fire, with her warm voice in your ear and your dad's photo over your heart, you chose the record. Your father would —" he stopped, and started again, gentler. "*I'm* proud of you. For whatever that's worth from a tired man on a boat."

It was worth a lot. It was worth more than the warm platform and the whole dream-fair. That's how you can tell the difference, in the end. One of them makes you feel seen. The other one is just true.

---

I walked home in the grey before dawn, and I want to close this part where it started, on my own windowsill, in the last of the dark before four o'clock, because the Lantern was waiting for me there.

Not on the shelf, where I'd left it between the trophy and the sea-glass. On the sill. In the exact place the sun would find it. And when I picked it up it was heavy, dense, real, a made thing full of metal — and it was *warm.* It had never been warm. It had been cold and wet and salt every time, cold from somewhere cold. Now it was warm, warm as a hand, and when I lifted the little smoked-glass door of it a flame was burning inside that I had not lit and that no match in my house could light, a small steady flame that leaned and guttered and did not go out, throwing a wobble of warm light across my ordinary bedroom, the only warm thing in the whole world, come all the way across the black water and the six years and the seam, into the room where I keep my socks, and stayed.

The lantern from my nightmare. Crossed the line. Lit. Real. In my hands.

Which meant the nightmare was real too. All of it. The flooded square and the silence and the Lantern Man wading toward me one slow boot at a time, patient as tide, and the girl saying *not yet,* and the clock that held at four, and the fogged little window and the face behind it that I knew and could not name.

Real. Every bit of it. Not a memory. Waiting.

I set the warm lantern on my windowsill, where it burns to this day, and I lay down on my bed as the green numbers ticked toward four, and I was so tired, tired all the way down, the way you're tired after you've held a moment of the world shut and paid for it with the best thing you owned. And I could feel the dream coming up under me the way the tide comes up under the pont, and I knew — the girl had told me — that this time the letters would hold. This time the clock would show me more than four o'clock. This time I would read the date.

I want to tell you I was brave about it.

I closed my eyes, and I went down into the black water one more time, toward the swinging light and the slow bright boots and the face I've known since I was six, to find out at last what night is coming for me.

And that's where I have to leave it, for now — right at the edge of the water, right before the letters hold — because the rest of it is the part that made everyone so angry, and I promised to tell it careful, and not out loud, and not yet.

Not yet.

But soon.
