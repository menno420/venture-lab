# Chapter Ten — Into the Frame

*Lull, Book One · draft*

Lena came down the dark gallery between the pillars, and she was not alone, and she was not entirely the person who'd bought me chips-worth of kindness at the school railings.

Behind her walked two grey coats, the breathless flat-faced Scriveners, and ahead of her, unhurried, she carried the whole warm weight of herself into that cold marble room like she'd built it, and I saw for the first time what a Vivid actually is. In the still museum, in front of forty held men, she was *more real than the room.* The shadows leaned toward her. The dark got out of her way. Truus, fifty years a darner, took an unconscious step back, and Wessel put himself in front of me, and Lena smiled at both of them like they were furniture she was fond of.

"Hello, Wessel," she said. "You look tired. You always look tired. Have you told him yet what he is, or are you still hoping if you don't say it out loud it won't be true?" She turned the smile on me and it was the same smile, exactly the same, warm and delighted and *there you are,* and that was the worst thing, that she could be doing this and still mean it. "Sam. You reached into the Night Watch by leaning at it. Do you understand that there are maybe three people alive who could do what you just did, and you did it by accident, at twelve, on a school-trip painting? Do you understand now why I was so *patient?*"

"You want to use me to get in," I said. "As the door."

"I want to open a door that has been shut to my people for three hundred years," she said, "yes. And I want *you* to want to, once you understand what's on the other side of it." She came closer, past Wessel, who couldn't stop her, whose whole poor tired faction couldn't stop her, and she stood in front of me and dropped her voice to the warm private thing she'd used on the platform. "The men in this painting are walking to a vote, Sam. A cruel one. That night, in a back room, forty men decided a thing that put four hundred years of suffering into the world that did not have to be there — and Rembrandt, bless him, caught them one hour before, still innocent, still able to choose differently. I'm not going to hurt anyone. I'm going to walk in there and I'm going to *ask them, better.* Change the conditions, that's all — a word in the right ear, a door held shut, and let them choose the kinder thing they were always capable of. And the painting will hold the kinder world true forever, and no child four centuries from now will grow up in the cruelty those men are about to vote into being." She held out her warm hand. "Come and do one good thing with me. One. And then decide."

And God forgive me, I wanted to. You have to know that I wanted to. That's what the girl in the dream had tried to warn me about — *kindness is the cheapest thing either of them has got* — and here it was, the cheapest and the strongest, my father over my heart and a kinder world on offer and the most seen-making person I'd ever met holding out her hand.

Wessel said, quietly, behind me: "Ask her what she's not saying, kid. She never says the whole thing. Ask her what happens to the *forty men.*"

Lena didn't look at him. She kept looking at me. But something crossed her face — quick, gone — and I'd learned enough in a week to know that Wessel had found the seam in her, the thing she wasn't saying.

"What happens to them?" I asked her. "The forty. If you turn them round."

"They choose better," she said.

"That's not what he asked," said Truus, from the dark.

Lena's smile stayed on. Her eyes changed. "A moment held this hard," she said, lightly, like it was a technicality, "can only hold *one* version. If I write a kinder night over it, the true night — the men as they really were, the choice they really made, the whole real evening Rembrandt caught — that gets scraped off to make room. The way a palimpsest is scraped. They don't die, Sam, they were dead four hundred years ago. But the true them, the held them, the them that's been standing in this room keeping that night alive since 1642 —" she shrugged, one shoulder, elegant "— they'd be *overwritten.* Forty real men, unmade, so that four hundred years of children get a kinder world. Forty, against millions. I'd do it in a heartbeat. I think, if you're honest, so would you. That's not villainy, Sam. That's just being able to *count.*"

And that was the moment the war stopped being two readings of a chocolate shop and became forty held men in the dark behind me, breathing, about to be scraped off to make room for a kindness.

"No," I said.

---

She stopped being kind then. Not cruel — Lena was never cruel, that was the horror of her — just *done,* the way you're done being patient with a child at the edge of a road. "All right," she said, almost sadly. "The hard way, then. It was always going to be the hard way with a boy who collects promises." She lifted one hand, and the two Scriveners moved, and Wessel and Truus moved to meet them, and the quiet gallery came apart into a fight in the dark — but I want to tell you about Lena's other hand, the one that mattered, because that's where I first saw Anchoring done by someone who could really do it.

She turned to the wall beside the Night Watch, to a smaller painting hung there — a marble table, a silver ewer, a woman in blue reading a letter by a window, all of it held in a light so whole and so still it hummed — and she *reached into it,* the way I'd reached, but with intent, with a whole trained Vivid's strength, and she took hold of the heavy marble table inside the painting, the real four-hundred-year-old table in the held light, and she *pulled it out.*

It came. A slab of marble the size of a door, dense, cold, impossible, sliding out of a painting into a museum, and — this is the thing, this is Anchoring — she didn't just pull it across, she *pinned* it, drove its reality into the waking world so hard that when she let go it *stayed,* crashing down onto the marble floor between us with a boom that shook the pillars, solid, permanent, a piece of a painting made forever real, blocking Wessel off from me.

"That's Anchoring, Sam," she called, over the noise, almost teaching me, because she couldn't help it, because she really did think I was the best thing to happen to either side in a hundred years. "Pull and *pin.* Any strong Vivid can do it with an object. You —" and here she smiled her real smile one last time, terrible and warm "— you could do it with the *night.* Now be a good boy and hold still while I use your lovely open door."

And she stepped up to the Night Watch, and put both hands flat against the held moment, against forty breathing men and a little lit girl and a drum and a dog, and I felt the seam in front of it start to give — give the way it only gave for me, because I was standing right there, because I was the door, because just by being near it I was holding it open for her —

and I understood that Wessel was right. That she hadn't needed to recruit me. That my just *being here,* wanting to see, leaning toward the beautiful thing, had done her whole job for her.

So I did the only thing left. I stopped being the door by going through it first.

I reached into the Night Watch ahead of her — easy, so easy, a puddle, a warm bath — and I pulled the whole of myself across the seam into the held moment, into the four-hundred-year-old night, and the museum and Wessel and the crash of marble and Lena's warm terrible voice all fell away behind me, and I was standing in the cold dark, on a real street, four centuries ago, in the smell of woodsmoke and canal water, with a drum beating somewhere close and a company of armed men forming up around me in the shadow, and every single one of them turning, slowly, to look at the boy in the wet anorak who had just appeared out of nowhere in the middle of their held and breathing night.
