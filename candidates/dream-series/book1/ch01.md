# Chapter One — The Fourth Hour
*Lull, Book One · draft*

Before you read on, you should know this was supposed to stay a secret.

Not a fun secret, like where your mum hides the good hagelslag. A real one. The kind people have kept for longer than there have been countries, the kind you're not meant to find out about unless somebody chooses to tell you, carefully, in a safe room, with the door shut.

Nobody chose to tell me. I found out by accident, the way you find out a step is missing — with your whole face. And in the finding out I made a great many people very, very angry. Some of them are still angry. Some of them, I think, will be angry at me forever, which is a long time when you consider who they are and what they can do to time.

So here is the thing, up front, before you decide whether to trust me:

Dreams are not made up.

I know. That's not how it goes. You're supposed to think a dream is a little film your brain shows itself at night, stitched out of leftover worry and cheese and whatever you saw on the tram. That's what I thought too, for the first eleven years and three hundred and sixty-something days of my life.

It isn't true. Every dream is a real place. A true moment — one that happened, in the past, or one that hasn't happened yet, in the future, or one happening right now in a world that isn't quite this one but is close enough to touch. When you dream, you don't invent anything. You *go* somewhere. You're a tourist who never keeps the ticket stub, which is why you can't remember the trip in the morning.

Almost everyone is just a tourist. But a few people — a very few — can wake up *inside* the dream, and know it's real, and do something about it. They can reach out and take a thing and carry it back across into the morning. And those people are not friends with each other.

They're split into two sides, and they have been at war since before anybody wrote anything down. One side wants to reach back into what already happened, and forward into what hasn't, and change it — patiently, quietly, a nudge here and a nudge there — until the whole world bends to their hand. That's their actual goal. Not gold. Not a throne. The world, the entire thing, arranged the way they like it. Here's a free fact you can check later, once you know how to look: every single story you have ever heard about magic — every witch, every wish granted, every man who bargained at a crossroads — traces back to one of them, and to something they changed.

The other side is smaller, and poorer, and tired, and they only want one thing. They want to keep the record true. They want what happened to *stay* happened. That's the whole fight. One side rewrites; the other side stands over the true version with its arms folded and says *no.*

I've told this part different ways, to different people, in different rooms. The truth got a little worn in the middle from handling. You'll understand why later — there's a word for the thing that does that to a memory, wears it soft and swaps in something easier, and I'll get to it. For now just know that when I say *this is what happened,* what I really mean is *this is the truest version I've still got.* Which is not the same thing. I'm sorry. I didn't make the rules. I mostly broke them.

It started on my sixth birthday.

I woke up at four in the morning from a nightmare. I know it was four, and I know it was my sixth birthday, because I have had the exact same nightmare ever since — every week, then every month, for six whole years — and it always begins the same way, at the same dead hour, and it is the truest memory I own.

Here it is.

---

I'm standing in the middle of Dam square, in the middle of the night, and there is water.

Not a puddle. The whole square is flooded, shin-deep, edge to edge, right up to the doors of the shops and the steps of the palace. Black water, dead flat, so still it isn't like water at all — it's like the square has been paved in a single sheet of dark glass and somebody poured the sky underneath it.

It's cold. I feel that first, the cold climbing up my legs. I'm in my pyjamas, the ones with the little rockets, and my feet have gone numb, and when I take a step the water doesn't splash. It just parts around my ankle and closes again without a sound.

That's the second thing. The silence.

You have to understand — Dam square is never quiet. It's the loudest place in Amsterdam. There are always trams grinding round the corner and pigeons doing pigeon crimes and a man playing an accordion badly and about four hundred tourists taking photographs of each other. In the dream there is none of that. No trams. No birds. Not one other person. The buildings stand around me tall and dark with their windows out, like a room full of grown-ups who have all decided not to speak until I've stopped doing whatever I've done.

And it's my birthday. That's the part that never makes sense and never goes away. There's a paper crown floating past my knee — a gold one, the kind you get out of a party bag, gone soft and heavy with water. And out on the flat black surface, a little way off, one candle is burning. Just one, standing up somehow on the water, its little flame leaning and guttering and not going out, throwing a wobble of light that doesn't reach anything.

Six-year-old me stands there in the cold and looks around for the edge of it. For the way out. For Mum.

I look up at the shops to read the signs — I could just read, then, I was proud of it, I'd read the whole cereal box that morning — and the letters won't hold still. They're there, big brass letters over a doorway, and I know they're words, but every time my eyes land on one it slides sideways into the next like wet paint, rearranges itself, becomes a shape that means nothing, and my chest starts to go tight because reading was the thing I was *good* at and now the letters are behaving like something with a mind of its own that would rather I didn't.

I look up higher, at the tower clock across the square — there's always a clock — to find out what time it is, because if I know what time it is then Mum is coming to get me in a certain number of hours and I can count them. And the clock won't hold a number either. The hands drift. The little numbers around the face swim like fish in a bowl. I look away, blink hard, look back, and it's a different time, and then another, sliding, never settling, and I press my hand flat against my own chest to feel my heart going — because my heart is going, it must be, I'm this scared —

and I can't feel it.

Nothing. My chest is quiet as the square. No thud, no flutter, no anything, just my cold hand on cold ribs and a stillness inside me exactly as deep as the stillness on the water, and *that* is the moment, every single time, that six-year-old me understands with his whole small body that something here is wrong in a way that grown-ups don't have a rule for.

That's when the light moves.

Out at the far side of the square, across all that black glass, something is coming.

At first it's just the light — a warm swinging point of it, low down, near the water, throwing a long smear of gold across the flooded stones that slides and stretches as it swings. Left. Right. Left. Coming closer. And behind the light, taking shape out of the dark, there's a man.

Except he isn't shaped like a man should be. He's too round in the wrong places, too smooth, too slow. As he comes into the candle-glow I see why: he's wearing a diving suit. An old one. The really old kind, from a hundred years ago, heavy canvas and brass, and on his head is a great round helmet, a brass ball with a little round window in the front of it, and I can't see his face through the window, only the dark. He is wading toward me through the flood, one enormous slow boot at a time, and each step pushes a low fat ripple ahead of it that spreads across the whole dead-flat square and lifts the paper crown and rocks the candle and finally, at last, reaches me, and laps cold around my ankles like something introducing itself.

In one hand he carries the lantern. A little brass ship's lantern, swinging on its handle, and it's the only warm thing in the whole world, and it is coming for me.

I can't move. You never can. Your feet have grown down into the black glass and your legs won't answer and all you can do is watch him come, one slow boot, then the next, patient as the tide, like he has done this before and will do it again and there is no hurry at all because where exactly did I think I was going to go.

And that's when the girl says it.

"Not yet."

A girl's voice, close to my ear, coming from nowhere and no one. She isn't scared. That's the strangest part — everything in me is screaming and her voice is calm, almost gentle, almost *kind,* the way you'd speak to a little kid who was about to touch a hot pan. *Not yet.* Like this was a thing with a proper time, and this wasn't it, and she was sorry, and it was going to be all right, none of which I believed, but which loosened something in my chest all the same.

I looked up at the clock again. I don't know why. To count the hours till Mum, maybe. To find something in the whole square that would sit still and be true.

And this time the clock held.

The hands stopped drifting. The numbers stopped swimming. The face settled, clear and certain and steady as a photograph, and it said four o'clock. Exactly four. Straight up, both hands, no argument.

And I want to tell you that felt like safety. That's what a held clock is supposed to feel like — *finally, something real, something that stays.* But it didn't feel like safety. It felt like the worst thing that had happened yet, worse than the letters, worse than my quiet chest, because some animal part of me knew that everything in this place had been sliding and wrong precisely *because* it wasn't real — and the one thing that had just gone hard and true and would not slide was the one thing I most needed to be a lie.

Four o'clock. Steady. Waiting.

The Lantern Man reached the middle of the square.

He stopped. He lifted the lantern — slowly, the way he did everything — and the light swung down and across and fell full on the water between us, and for one breath, one single breath, the black glass at his feet didn't show the square.

It showed a street. A different street, somewhere I'd never been, narrow buildings leaning close, and above them a sky that wasn't night and wasn't day but a deep bruised purple-yellow, the exact colour of the place under your eye three days after you walk into a door. A whole other somewhere, hanging upside-down in the water for the length of one heartbeat I couldn't feel — and then the ripple from his next step crossed it and smeared it away and it was just the flooded square again.

"Not yet," the girl said, and now she wasn't calm. Now it was fast and low and *urgent,* right against my ear, the way you say a thing when the time you were sure about has suddenly got very short. "Not yet — not yet —"

The Lantern Man raised the lantern to my face.

He held it up level with the little round window in his helmet, so the light was between us, and I could see the glass of the window at last, and behind the glass — from something breathing, in there, in the dark — the window fogged. A soft grey bloom of breath, spreading and shrinking on the inside of the little round pane, in and out, patient, the breathing of somebody who was in no hurry whatsoever.

And through the fog, just for a second, before it thickened, I saw a face.

I couldn't tell you whose. I have tried, awake, for six years, and I can't. But six-year-old me, standing frozen in the black water with the cold in his bones and the calm voice gone frantic in his ear — six-year-old me knew that face. Knew it the way you know your own name. Knew it and could not say it, and opened his mouth to scream —

---

— and woke up.

Four o'clock. My own room. The little green numbers on my clock-radio said 4:00, and then, mercifully, 4:01, and kept going, kept *moving,* the way numbers are supposed to. My rocket pyjamas. My own ceiling with the crack shaped like a duck. My birthday — my sixth birthday — with a stack of presents downstairs I'd been counting for a week.

I was screaming, apparently. I don't remember starting.

The door opened and the landing light came in and it was my dad.

He didn't say *it's just a dream, go back to sleep,* which is what people say, and which never once helped anybody. He came in and sat down on the edge of my bed so it dipped and rolled me against him, and he was warm and enormous and smelled of the coffee he wasn't supposed to drink at night, and he put one big hand flat on my back, right between the shoulder blades, and just left it there, heavy and still, until my breathing started matching his instead of the other way round.

"I've got you," he said. Not *it isn't real.* Just: *I've got you.* Which was better, because at least it was true.

I told him about the water and the man and the light, in the gulpy way you tell things at six, and he listened to all of it like it was important, like it was the news, nodding, his hand never moving off my back. And when I ran out he said the man couldn't come here, because here was ours, and he'd checked, and there wasn't any water. And to prove it he reached down and pulled my blanket up over my cold feet and tucked it under, and said, "See? Warm and dry. Go on."

My feet weren't dry, though.

I felt it when he tucked the blanket round them. They were cold, properly cold, cold in a way that a bedroom in summer doesn't make your feet — and damp, faintly damp, the sheets gone cool and clinging at the very bottom of the bed. And I opened my mouth to tell him, and then I thought about how you can kick your covers off when you thrash around, and how the window was maybe open, and how it was probably that, it was definitely that, and I decided it was that, and I didn't say anything, and I let him tuck me in and kiss the top of my head and turn out the light.

I've thought about that a lot, since. That I decided.

He stayed on the landing till I was asleep. I could see his shadow under the door.

That's one of the last times I remember my dad clearly — the whole of him, the weight of him on the bed, the coffee smell, the flat warm hand. He went away when I was eight. That's the word we use in our house. *Away.* I'll tell you about it, but not yet — see, now I'm doing it too — and it isn't what you think, and it's the one part of this whole story that still, after everything, I can't tell without my throat closing.

I had the dream again the next week. And the week after. And then it settled into once a month, regular as anything, always at four, always the water, always the slow bright swing of the lantern coming across the black, always the calm voice going frantic at the end, *not yet, not yet,* and the face behind the fogged little window that I knew and couldn't name.

For six years I thought it was just a nightmare. A bad one, a stubborn one, but just a nightmare — the little film your brain shows itself at night, out of leftover worry and cheese and whatever you saw that day.

I was almost twelve when it stopped being just a nightmare.

When it started, very quietly, coming true.
