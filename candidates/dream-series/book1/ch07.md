# Chapter Seven — The Tells

*Lull, Book One · draft*

I showed Wessel the photograph the next morning, on the pont, at the grey cold start of the day, and I want to tell you I did it calmly, like someone weighing two sides. I didn't. I shoved it at him with my hand shaking and my voice gone high and I said, "Three weeks ago. That's three weeks ago. You said he was as good as dead and she has a *photograph* from three weeks ago and she says you could go and get him and you never said — you never once —"

Wessel took it. He looked at it a long time, the ferry deck swaying under us, the gulls going over. His face didn't do anything, which I've since learned is the thing his face does when it's doing the most.

"It's real," he said at last. "That's the cruelty of it. It's a real photo of your real dad, sleeping under, somewhere I'd have to work to find. She didn't fake it. She didn't need to." He handed it back. "You want to know why I didn't tell you we could go and get him? Because we can't. Because going after someone who's gone as far under as your father has is a working that would take a stronger dreamer than me down with him, and probably you too, and she knows that, and she'll take you there anyway one day, right to the edge of him, and she'll let you reach — and then it'll cost you exactly what it cost him, and she'll have what she wants, which is you, arc-deep and desperate and hers. That's not a rescue, kid. That's the bait with your dad's face on it. The kindest-looking hook there is."

I hated him for being right in that flat sure way. I hated that it fit. Some part of me — the part the girl had warned me about, *pick a side because of what it's willing to lose* — filed it away: the ferryman would let my father stay under to keep me safe, and Lena would spend my father, and me, to get what she wanted. Neither of those was *go and get him.* Neither of them was my dad's warm hand flat on my back. I put the photo in my inside pocket, over my heart, where it stayed for the rest of everything.

"Right," Wessel said, and it was almost gentle. "You've been in the dream on your own, haven't you. Don't lie, I can see it on you, you're ringing different. And you've been to her fair, and she's shown you the warm room and the photo, which means we are *out of time* and I have to teach you in days what your dad had years to learn. So. No more free coins. Today it costs. Today you learn the tells, and the Lull, and how not to die, and you're going to hate all three."

---

The tells first, because the tells are how you keep your own mind.

"In a dream," Wessel said, "the world can't do certain things, and if you check for them you can always, always tell where you are, which is the only defence you've got against her sort, because her whole trade is making you unsure which side of the seam you're standing on." He counted them off on his scarred fingers. "One. You can't read. Letters swim, rearrange, won't hold. Two. Clocks won't keep a number — look away, look back, it's changed. Three. You can't feel your own heartbeat; put your hand on your chest and there's nothing there. Four. There's no one place the light comes from — everything's lit and nothing throws a clean shadow. Five. You never remember how you got there. You check those, kid, all five, every time something feels thin, and you'll know. A liar can fake you a whole street. Faking all five at once is beyond nearly anyone alive."

I thought about the clock in the nightmare that *held.* That said four o'clock, straight up, steady and true, when everything else was swimming. "But mine held," I said. "The clock. In the dream. It went hard and true and read four."

Wessel looked at me for a long moment.

"Yeah," he said quietly. "I know. That's the thing that scares me about your dream, if you want to know, more than the diver, more than the water. A tell that *breaks* is a tell someone's spending enormous power to break, on purpose, to tell you one true thing in a place where everything else is meant to lie. Somebody wants you to be able to read that clock. Somebody's holding it steady for you across six years. And I don't know who, and I don't know why, and in my experience a gift you didn't ask for in a place like that is the most expensive thing in the world." He shook it off. "Not today's problem. Today: pull. For real. For cost."

---

He had me pull the coin again, ungreased this time, and it came, tired, but it came. Then a heavier thing — a shackle-key off the ferry's chain locker, iron, notched, cold. I held it whole, the weight and the notches and the cold, and I pulled, and it came, and I grinned, and Wessel didn't.

"Now," he said, "the lesson. I'm going to hand you something too big to hold whole, and you're going to reach for it anyway, because you're twelve and you've had three wins, and I'm going to let you, because you need to feel the tax *once,* small, with me standing here, instead of the first time being alone with her voice in your ear telling you you can lift your father."

He set a boat-hook on the deck. A long one, two metres, iron head, wooden shaft, a coil of wet rope lashed round the middle. "Hold all of it," he said. "Every splinter. The rope's grain. The rust. Go on. Pull it into the shadow with you and out again."

I couldn't hold it whole. I tried. I got the head and lost the shaft; I got the shaft and the rope slid out of my mind like water; I grabbed for all of it at once and *reached,* overreached, felt the seam give too far, felt myself pouring attention into a thing that would not stay whole in the cup of my head —

and it collapsed.

I can't describe the collapse well because part of what it did was take the describing. There was a lurch, a white nowhere, a sense of something being *charged* against me, some account I didn't know I had going suddenly overdrawn — and then I was on my knees on the wet steel deck with the boat-hook lying there ordinary and un-pulled, and Wessel's hand on my shoulder, and a hole in me.

"There it is," he said grimly. "Backwash. You forced a working past what you could hold and it collapsed and it charged you. It always charges you the same coin, kid. A real memory. It reaches in and it takes a true one to pay for the fake you tried to force across, and it takes it clean, so you don't even feel the theft, you just —"

"What did I lose?" I said. My voice was shaking. "What did it take?"

Wessel's face was very kind and very tired. "That's the cruelty of it. You can't know. It's gone smooth, like it was never there." He helped me up. "But I'll tell you what I saw go out of your eyes, because I was watching for it, so you'd have at least the shape of your own loss and not a clean nothing. You were talking, half a minute back, while you set your grip. About your dad. About a layer of hagelslag so thick you couldn't see the bread, and how he said the rules about sweetness were made up by people with less nerve than him." He watched me. "You don't have that anymore, do you."

I reached for it. For my dad and the hagelslag and the too-much. I knew — the way you know a tooth is gone by the gap your tongue keeps going to — that there had been something there, something warm, something about my father and breakfast and nerve, and that it was smooth now, gone, paid out to buy a boat-hook I didn't even get.

I sat down on the wet deck and I cried, and Wessel let me, and didn't say *it's just a memory,* which is what people say, and which never once helped anybody.

---

We didn't get to shadow-stepping gently. It came the way most real lessons come, which is too fast, and from the wrong direction.

I was cycling home at dusk, wrung out, the photo over my heart, and I clocked him at the Prinsengracht bridge — the grey coat, the too-heavy coat for the mild evening, the stillness. Not the same man from the ferry; a different plain forgettable face over the same nothing. A Scrivener. Come for the loud bell that I was. And this one wasn't going to be polite, because Wessel had told me the next one wouldn't, and here he was, and behind him, at the next bridge, a second grey coat, and my street was between them, and my legs went stupid the way they do.

I did the only thing my hands knew how to do. I'd felt the seam give all day, greased and raw from the backwash; and when the first coat came off the bridge toward me too fast, faster than a walking man, I grabbed the give and I pulled it *around myself* — thinned the seam over my own skin, the way Wessel had described it once in passing, *slip half out of waking, hard to see, hard to touch* — and I stepped sideways into the shadow of the bridge and the world went thin and grey and cold and *close,* like being inside a held breath, and the coat man's hand went through where I'd been.

I couldn't breathe in there. Wessel had said that too — *you can't breathe well there, can't stay long* — and it was true, my chest heaving on nothing, the grey pressing in, the two Scriveners turning, quartering, hunting the wrongness of me. I held it as long as my lungs would let me, slid three doorways down in the thin cold grey, and fell back into the waking world gasping in the dark of someone's stoop, and ran, wheeling my bike, heart hammering — I could feel it now, I could feel my heart, so I knew I was awake — and I got inside my own front door and bolted it and stood in the hall shaking.

Fenna was on the stairs.

She'd let herself in with the key we keep under the third pot, to return a book, to wait for me, ordinary Fenna in the ordinary hall — and she was looking at me the way you look at a stranger who's come in wearing your friend's face. Because I'd come through the door soaked in cold nothing, grey to the lips, out of breath from air I hadn't been breathing, with a stolen hole in me where my father used to be.

"Sam," she said. She didn't do a joke. That's how I knew how bad I looked; Fenna always does a joke. "Sam, what is going *on* with you. You've been — for a week you've been — you go grey. You disappear. You looked *through* me on the ferry, do you remember, you never even answered about the peanut sauce —"

And here is the thing I did, that I have to tell you, because I promised to tell it straight even the bad parts. I looked at my best friend, who punched Ruben for my hat, who was standing in my hall actually frightened for me for the first time in her life, and I opened my mouth to tell her the truth — the whole of it, the water and the lantern and Wessel and Lena and my dad asleep three weeks ago —

and instead I reached, without deciding to, tired and raw and full of the seam, and I felt for the soft place in her that the Lull uses, the place that wants a boring story it can rest on.

And I gave her one.

"I've been really tired," I heard myself say, warm and easy, in a voice that wasn't quite mine. "Loads of school stuff. It's nearly my birthday. You know what I'm like."

I watched her face clear. I watched the fear smooth right out of it, the way it had smoothed on the ferry, the little frown and the little brightening, her mind reaching gratefully for the rest I'd handed it. "Yeah," she said, easy now, the worry gone out of her like water out of a cracked cup. "God, you *are* dramatic. Mayo on your patat, obviously. I'll get chips, you look terrible." And she went to get chips, my Fenna, lulled by her own best friend, happy.

I stood in the hall with the door bolted and the cold still on my skin and I understood two things at once, and they've never left me.

The first was that I could do to her what the whole ferry had done to itself. The Palimpsest's own trick. I'd done it without deciding to, the way I'd woken in the dream without deciding to. It was already in my hands.

The second was that it had been *easy.* Easier than the truth. Warm, and quick, and it made her happier, and it was the single worst thing I had done in my whole life, and it had felt exactly, precisely like tucking someone in.

That's when I stopped being able to tell myself I was one of the good ones just because Wessel was.
