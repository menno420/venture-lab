# Chapter Six — A Better World

*Lull, Book One · draft*

She was waiting for me outside school the next afternoon, and I want to be honest with you about how much I liked it, because the liking is the whole trap and if I pretend I saw through her you won't understand a thing about how the Palimpsest actually gets people. They don't get you with fear. Fear you can run from. They get you with being *seen.*

She was leaning on the railing by the bike racks in the low gold light, and she was the young woman from across the water, the one who'd waved, and she was — I don't have the grown-up word, I only had the almost-twelve one, which was that she was the most impossible-to-stop-looking-at person I had ever stood near. Dark hair, a long coat the colour of wet slate, a way of holding still that made everything busy and awkward around her, all the shrieking kids and clattering bikes, look like weather she was letting happen. And when she saw me her whole face opened into that same delighted smile from the ferry — *there you are, the thing I came to find* — and she said my name like it was good news.

"Sam Vermeer," she said. "At *last.* I've been so patient. Do you know how rare patience is, in my line? Lena." She didn't offer a hand. She tipped her head at the street, at the whole gold evening. "Walk with me. I'm not going to touch you, I'm not going to make you do anything, and if at any point you want to go home you just go home, and I'll be sad, but I'll wave you off. That's more than the ferryman offered you, I'd bet. Did he even let you sit down before he told you your father was as good as dead?"

I stopped walking.

"Ah," she said, softly, and the smile went, and something that looked exactly like real sorrow came up in its place, and to this day I can't tell you whether it was real or the best-made thing I've ever seen. "He did. Of course he did. *Went under, never surfaced, worst of all three, so sorry, kid.*" She said it in Wessel's own flat cadence, dead on, and it landed like a slap. "That's the Vigil for you. They love a hard truth. They think handing you a grief is the same as respecting you. Walk with me anyway. I'm going to show you something, and then you can decide who respects you."

I should have gone home. I'd promised Wessel. I'd promised the girl. I was collecting promises like Fenna collects reasons I owe her.

I walked with her.

---

We walked north toward Centraal in the falling light, and she talked, and it was the best conversation I'd had in years, which is a terrible thing to admit and completely true. She didn't lecture me about factions. She asked me things. She asked what my dad had been like, and listened — actually listened, the way my dad used to listen, like it was the news — and when I told her about the hagelslag, about how he put on a whole layer so you couldn't see the bread, she laughed, delighted, and said, "*That's* a man who understood that the rules about how much sweetness a thing is allowed are made up by people with less nerve than him," and I felt something in my chest come loose that had been clenched for four years, and I hated her a little for it even as I loved her a little for it, and that's the Palimpsest, that's exactly the whole thing, right there.

"Here's what they won't have told you," she said, as the great dark bulk of Centraal station came up ahead, all lit windows and gulls. "Because they're frightened of it. The world is a *draft,* Sam. That's all it is. It's a first attempt, scribbled down in a hurry, full of cruelty that didn't have to be there and accidents that ruined good people and moments that went one stupid way when they could just as easily have gone another. The ferryman stands over that draft with his arms folded going *no, no, it must stay exactly as terrible as it came out.* And he calls that *keeping the record true,* and he calls us thieves for wanting to fix a typo." She looked at me. "Do you think the world's as good as it could be? An honest boy. Do you?"

"No," I said. It was the truest thing I'd said out loud in days.

"No," she agreed, gently. "Neither do I. So I do something about it. We do. Come and see."

---

She took me into Centraal, but not the Centraal you'd take a train from.

It was near four in the morning by then — I don't know how, I want you to understand that I *don't know how,* one moment the sky was gold and the next we were walking under the great departure board at the dead hour and the whole station was empty and blue and echoing, and I never felt the hours pass, and that not-feeling should have frightened me and instead it felt like being carried. That's a bleed. That's a whole evening she pulled across the seam and folded up small so it fit in a walk to the station. I know that now. Then I just felt looked-after.

She led me down the platforms to the very end, past track 15, past where the tracks are supposed to stop, to a platform that is not there. I've been back since, in daylight, and counted, and there is no platform past 15, there is a wall and a maintenance door and the smell of brakes. But at four in the morning, with Lena's hand not-quite-touching my shoulder, there was a platform 4¾ — she said the number like a private joke, *the in-between track,* and I laughed because I couldn't help it — and on it there was a fair.

A dream-fair. That's the Vigil's word for it; the Palimpsest just call it a gathering, which is worse, somehow, for being so mild. Imagine a night market that's also a party that's also, underneath, a shop where the thing being sold is *possibility.* Lantern-strings that weren't plugged into anything. Stalls where a woman poured you a cup of a summer that hadn't happened to you yet, and you drank it, and for a second you'd *been* there. A man selling small bottled regrets, uncorking them, letting people sniff the version where they'd said the brave thing instead of the safe one. Music that came from a band that wasn't there. And people — beautiful, easy, unhurried people, none of them tired, none of them poor, none of them looking over their shoulder, laughing in a warm crowd on a platform that didn't exist, and every single one of them turned, as I came down the steps, and looked at me with Lena's exact expression: *there you are.*

"This is us," Lena said, quietly, at my ear. "This is what we're building. Not a war. Not folded arms. A *better version.* And there's a place in it for you that there is nowhere else on earth, because of what you can do, which the ferryman won't even name for you because it frightens him — but I'll name it, when you're ready, because I'm not frightened of you, Sam, I think you're the best thing that's happened to either side in a hundred years." She turned me, gently, by the shoulder, to face her. "Join us. Not tonight. I'd never ask tonight. But know the door's open, and know it's warm in here, and know one more thing."

And here it came. I felt it coming the way you feel the last stair.

"We look for people, in the thin places," she said. "The ones who went under. The Vigil write them off — *worst of all three, never surfaced, so sorry.* We don't. We go looking. We're the only ones who go looking." She reached into the slate-grey coat. "It took me a long time to be sure. I didn't want to show you until I was sure, because a thing like this, shown wrong, is a cruelty, and I'm not the Vigil." She held it out. "I'm fairly sure now."

It was a photograph.

Not a Lull-degraded one, not a swimming smear — a real photograph, sharp, ordinary, the kind that comes out of a machine at a chemist's. It showed a man asleep. On a narrow bed, in a plain room, in the grey flat light of somewhere I didn't know, one big hand open on the blanket. Older than I remembered. Grey coming into the stubble. Deeply, terribly asleep, in the way of someone who has been asleep a very long time.

My dad.

And in the corner of the photograph, printed small and orange the way the machine prints them, the way it printed every holiday snap in the shoebox under my mum's bed, was a date.

It was from three weeks ago.

"He's under," Lena said, very softly, watching my face come apart the way she'd known it would, "not gone. And we know where. And the ferryman does too, Sam, and he stood you in a quiet garden this morning and told you your father was as good as buried, and never once said *we could go and get him.*"

I couldn't speak. I couldn't breathe. I was staring at my dad's sleeping face and the little orange date three weeks old.

"You don't have to decide anything," said Lena, kind as morning. "Take the photo. Keep it. Show the ferryman, even — ask him why he didn't tell you." She folded my hand around it. Her hand was warm. Everything about her was warm. "And when you're done being angry at him for the right reasons, come and find the warm platform. It'll be here. So will your father."
