# Chapter Four — Keep the Record True

*Lull, Book One · draft*

I didn't tell my mum.

I want that on the record early, because a lot of what went wrong later goes back to that one decision, made on a Wednesday, at the kitchen table, over cold chips I couldn't eat. She came in near one in the morning smelling of the hospital, and she put her cold hand on the back of my neck the way she does, and she asked was everything all right, and I looked up at her tired real face and I said yes.

I've thought about why. The easy answer is that *there's a lantern from my nightmare on my shelf and a man turned into a shadow on the ferry* is not a sentence you say to your mum at one in the morning when she's worked a double. But that's not the whole of it, and I promised you I'd tell this straight, so here's the rest. Some quiet part of me had already decided that this was mine. That whatever was happening was happening to *me*, and had chosen me, and that if I handed it to my mum I'd be handing away the first thing in years that was only mine and not shaped like the hole my dad left. I was almost twelve. That's the age you start keeping things. Nobody tells you that the first big secret you keep is practice for the ones that cost you.

So I said yes, everything's fine, and I ate a cold chip to prove it, and she kissed my hair and went to bed, and the next morning I went to find Wessel.

---

He'd told me where, on the pont, in that low voice under the horn. Not an address. Wessel didn't deal in addresses. "There's a door on the Spui," he'd said, "next to the bookshop that's always shut. Ordinary door. You'll walk past it four times. Fifth time, go in, and mind you shut it behind you, and don't say a word until I do."

The Spui is a square in the old middle of the city, all bookshops and bicycles and a statue of a small boy that everyone photographs, and I did walk past the door four times, exactly like he said, which frightened me more than anything had yet — not because it was hidden but because it wasn't. It was a plain brown door with a brass handle gone green, the kind of door you've walked past your whole life and never once wondered about, and every time I went to look straight at it my eyes slid off it onto the bookshop window, onto a bicycle, onto anything, the way the letters slid in the dream, the way the lantern slid out of the middle of my head. I had to make myself stop. I had to plant my feet on the cobbles and turn my whole body at it, the way Wessel had turned the coat man into a shadow, and *look*, and only then did the door agree to be a door.

Fifth time, I went in, and I shut it behind me.

And I was somewhere that could not fit behind that door.

---

If you've been to Amsterdam you might know the Begijnhof, though most people don't, which is the point of it. It's a courtyard — a real one, seven hundred years old, a ring of narrow houses around a green with a chapel in it, tucked behind an ordinary archway off the Spui where a hundred thousand tourists a day walk past without ever turning their heads. It is the quietest place in the whole loud city. You step through and the traffic just stops existing, like someone shut a lid on it.

I know all that now. That morning I only knew that I'd gone through a plain door in a wall and come out into a silent green courtyard under a white sky, with the noise of the city switched clean off, and Wessel standing in the middle of the grass in his ferry fleece with his hands in his pockets, watching me not-look at everything.

"There he is," he said. "Shut it properly?"

I nodded. My voice had gone away again.

"Good. This is the stillest room in the city, and I'd like it to stay still." He didn't say *room*. There was no room. There was a courtyard and the sky. But he said it like the whole green space was one room with the lid on, and I understood, the way you understand things here before anyone explains them, that it was. "You feel that?" he said. "That quiet?"

I did. It wasn't like a library quiet, a keeping-your-voice-down quiet. It was the quiet of the flooded square. It was the quiet of my chest when I couldn't feel my heart. I felt it and my skin went cold.

"That's the seam darned tight," Wessel said. "This is a still-room. Oldest one in the country, maybe. Seven hundred years of praying women wore the seam here so thin you could nearly fall through it, so we came and we stitched it shut, tight as a drum, so that nothing bleeds in and nothing bleeds out and a person can hear himself think. It's the one place in Amsterdam I can tell you the truth out loud." He nodded at a wooden bench under a bare tree. "Sit down, kid. You look like you're about to."

---

I sat down. And Wessel told me the truth, and I'm going to give it to you the way he gave it to me, plain, because he was right that it lands better plain.

"Dreams are real," he said. "You worked that much out, or your body did. Every dream you've ever had, you weren't making it up — you went somewhere. Somewhere real. The past, mostly. Sometimes a maybe-future. Sometimes sideways, a world that split off ours. You went, you had a look round, and you forgot on the way home, because forgetting a dream is the same trick the world uses to make grown people forget the impossible while they're wide awake. That trick's got a name. You met it Tuesday, on your shelf, when you decided a wet salt lantern was a flea-market lamp."

"The Lull," I said. My first word inside the still-room. It came out small.

Something moved in his face — not a smile, Wessel didn't do smiles, but a kind of grudging weight, like I'd lifted something he'd expected to lift for me. "So you did keep it. Good. Most don't. Most, you say the word out loud and they reach for the calm and it takes them right there in front of you." He leaned forward, forearms on his knees, the scarred oily hands hanging. "Almost everyone's a Sleeper. Goes, looks, forgets, wakes up believing the world's exactly the flat ordinary thing it looks like. And they're happy, and I'll thank you not to feel above them, because it's a mercy, mostly. Then there's us. The ones who can wake up *inside* it and know it's real and do something. We're rare. And we're at war, and we have been since before there was a word for war, over one single question."

He looked at me.

"Does what happened get to stay happened."

---

He laid it out, then, all of it, and I'll spare you the four hours and give you the shape. There are two sides. One side — he wouldn't say their name in the still-room, said names carry, said I'd learn it soon enough and wish I hadn't — one side wants to reach back into what happened and forward into what hasn't and *change* it, a nudge here, a nudge there, patient as tide, until the whole world is bent into the shape they like and has, as far as anyone remembers, always been that shape. "Every story you ever heard about magic," Wessel said, "every wish, every curse, every man who met the devil at a crossroads and came back rich — that's them. That's a change that bled." Every edit leaves a trace, he said; that's the one law even they can't beat. Change something across the line and it leaves a mark, a wrongness, a little seam that shows — a clock with the wrong number of hours, a sign spelled almost right, a stranger you've now passed three times.

"A fourteen tram," I said slowly, "that doesn't run there."

Wessel went very still. "You saw a fourteen."

"Tuesday. On the Prinsengracht. I thought — my head said it was fine."

"Your head," said Wessel, "is going to try to kill you, and it'll do it kindly." He straightened up. "That's a bleed. That's their signature. You catch a bleed, you know one of them's been at the moment. And catching them at it, undoing it before it sets, darning the seam shut behind them so the true version holds — that's us. That's the whole job. We don't win. We're not trying to win. We're standing over the true version of what happened with our arms folded, saying *no.* That's the entire fight, kid. Keep the record true."

The other side had a name. His side. He said it like it cost him nothing and everything.

"The Vigil," he said. "The watch. That's what I am. That's what half the night-shift in this city is, if you knew how to look — the ferry hands and the librarians and the ones who can't sleep and the museum guards, the people whose work puts them in the thin places at the thin hours. We're poor and we're tired and there's not enough of us and we lose more than we hold. And we keep the record true anyway, because the other thing they do — " his voice dropped, went rough " — a life that didn't happen the way it happened is a life somebody *took.* You understand me? They don't just change a fact. They steal a person's real one out from under them and hand them a forged one and the poor soul never even knows they've been robbed. We stand against that. That's worth being tired for."

---

Then he taught me to pull.

He took a two-euro coin out of his fleece and set it on the bench between us and told me to shut my eyes and hold it in my head — not the idea of a coin, *this* coin, the exact weight and cold and the milled edge and the gold ring round the silver, hold the whole of it steady and true — and then, without opening my eyes, without moving my hand, want it into my palm.

"You've got reach," he said, over my shoulder, quiet. "Six years you've been ringing loud enough to bring a Scrivener onto my boat. The reach was never your problem. Your problem's going to be the opposite. Now. Hold it whole. And pull."

I held it whole. The cold, the weight, the milled edge, the gold ring, the little map of Europe on the back gone smooth with handling. I held it whole and I felt the world go thin around my closed eyes — thin the way the ferry had been thin, the way the door had been thin, a give in things, a slack — and I pulled.

The coin was in my hand.

I opened my eyes and the bench was bare and the coin was in my palm, cold, real, mine, and I made a sound I'm not proud of, and Wessel let out a breath through his nose that was as close as he came to being pleased.

"There," he said. "That's the smallest true thing there is. Everything else is just that, bigger, and every bit of it costs. Remember it cost you nothing this time because I greased the seam for you. It won't be free again."

I was still staring at the coin. And because I was almost twelve and I'd just done a magic trick, I asked the question I'd actually come to ask, the one under all the others, the one I'd been not-asking my whole life.

"My dad," I said. "He didn't go to Groningen. Did he."

The stillness in the still-room got a little stiller.

"No," Wessel said. "He didn't."

"He was one of you."

"He was one of us." Wessel looked at me for a long moment, weighing something, deciding how much a nearly-twelve-year-old could carry across the seam of a single morning. "Your dad didn't leave you, Sam. I want you to have that one true thing and I want you to have it before anyone with a prettier voice than mine gets to you. Your father went *under.* You go too deep, too long, hold too much — the crossing charges you sleep you can't pay back, and you go down into it and you don't come up. Some come up after a day. Some after a week." He stopped. "Some don't. Your dad went under, years ago, on a working, and he never surfaced. That's the *away.* That's the word your mum can't finish. He's not dead and he's not gone and he's not coming back, and that's the worst of all three and the truest, and I'm sorry, and I'm the first person to tell you and I hate that I am."

I couldn't say anything. The coin was so cold in my hand.

"He was a good darner," Wessel said, gentler than I'd have thought he had in him. "One of the best I ever worked a night with. And he talked about you. On the thin water at four in the morning, when a man tells the truth whether he means to or not, your dad talked about his boy who could already read the whole cereal box." He looked away, out at the green. "So. Now you know why the dream matters, and why they want you, and why I'm not going to let them have you cheap. That's enough truth for one—"

He stopped.

He was looking past me, at the far wall of the courtyard, at the old brick and the little chapel and the bench where nothing was, and his whole body had gone wrong, gone tight, gone still in the bad way, the coat-man way.

I turned round.

On the white-painted wall of the chapel, in the stillest room in the country, where nothing bleeds in and nothing bleeds out, where seven hundred years of quiet had been stitched shut tight as a drum — someone had left a mark.

A little smear of dark, low on the white wall, no bigger than a hand. It hadn't been there. I knew it hadn't been there. And as I watched it it moved, it *swam,* the way the letters swam, resolving out of nothing into a shape, into curly old-fashioned letters that shouldn't be able to exist in this of all places, spelling two words in a hand like wet ink, already beginning to run.

*Hello, Joris.*

And behind me, very quietly, in a voice with no ferry-bravado left in it at all, Wessel said, "That's not possible. Nothing gets in here."
