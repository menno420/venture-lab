# Chapter Two — Almost Twelve
*Lull, Book One · draft*

Here is what a normal morning looked like, back when I still got to have them.

I woke up at ten past seven to the sound of my mum trying very hard to be quiet, which is louder than just being normal. She'd been on a night shift — she's on more night shifts than not — and she was moving round the kitchen in her socks putting things away too carefully, and the too-careful clink of a bowl going into the cupboard is a sound I could pick out of a thousand. It means: *she's home, she's tired, be nice.*

I am, mostly, nice.

I came down and she was at the counter with both hands round a mug of coffee, still in her work fleece, her hair doing the thing it does after twelve hours where it decides gravity is optional. There were dark smudges under her eyes like she'd rubbed them with a pencil. She looked at me and did the smile she keeps for me, the real one, tired all the way through but real.

"Gefeliciteerd, bijna-jarige," she said. *Congratulations, nearly-birthday-boy.* In Holland, when it's your birthday, the whole family sits in a circle and congratulates *each other* — your aunts congratulate your other aunts on you, which makes no sense and which I have given up explaining to Fenna, who is from here and also finds it weird.

"Three days," I said, and got the hagelslag down from the shelf.

If you don't know what hagelslag is, I'm sorry for you and I'll be quick: it's chocolate sprinkles, the little ones, and here it is a completely respectable thing to put on buttered bread and eat for breakfast at any age, on a Tuesday, as a whole meal. There is a wall of it in every supermarket. My mum thinks this is one of civilisation's great achievements and honestly, most mornings, so do I. I made two slices, milk chocolate, pressed down hard so the sprinkles stick, which is the correct method, and I will not be discussing it further.

"You slept?" she asked. She always asks. She asks the way other mums ask *did you do your homework* — like the answer matters more than she wants to let on.

"Yeah," I said.

I hadn't had the dream. Not that night, not for about a week — and a week, lately, counted as a long gap. It used to come about once a month, regular as a bill, you could nearly mark it on a calendar. But this last while it had been speeding up, creeping in closer and closer, and the closer it crept the more of it started coming true in the daylight, which is a thing I'll get to and which I didn't understand yet. Once a month, then every couple of weeks, then — so a whole week clear felt like a lot, felt almost like it had let go of me, and I remember thinking, chewing my bread, *maybe I'm finally growing out of it.* Twelve felt like the kind of age you might grow out of things. I was wrong about that the way you're wrong about the weather right before it does something dramatic to you, but I didn't know that yet, so I ate my hagelslag and I was happy.

"Dad used to put too much on," I said, before I'd decided to. "Hagelslag. Like a whole layer. You couldn't see the bread."

The kitchen went a little bit still.

My mum looked into her coffee. "He did," she said. Just that. She has a way of saying two words that closes a door so gently you can't even be cross about it. *He did.* And then she got up and rinsed her mug and said I'd be late, which I would be, and that was the end of Dad for the morning, the way it usually is.

We don't talk about him much. When I was eight he went *away* — that's the family word, everyone uses it, my aunts, my mum, the way you'd say someone went to Groningen — and he didn't come back, and nobody will tell me the rest. Not that he died, because they'd say died. Not that he left us, because when I was little and shouted that at her once my mum went so white and so quiet that I never did it again. Just: away. There's a look my mum gets if I push, a look like something in her is holding a very heavy door shut with her whole body, and I've learned to stop before she has to do that. You learn the shape of the thing you're not allowed to touch. You learn it by touching it once.

So. Hagelslag. Bag. Bike. Out.

---

The morning was doing its clean early trick where the light comes low along the water and everything looks like it's been rinsed. Our house is on a gracht — a canal, one of the little ones, three bridges from anything anyone's heard of — and I wheeled my bike out and swung my leg over and pushed off along the water the way I've done about four thousand times.

The canal was flat that morning. Really flat. No wind, no boats yet, and the water lay there mirror-still, holding the whole street upside-down in it: the leaning brick houses, the crooked line of parked bikes, the white morning sky. A pigeon walked along the edge of its own reflection. I remember the flatness because it snagged at me for half a second, some small hook of a feeling, *I know this,* and then a delivery van rattled over the bridge and the feeling let go and I forgot it, the way you forget a word that's right there.

(I'll tell you now, since you can't catch me at it later: nearly everything in that ride was trying to tell me something, and I sailed straight past all of it. The flat water that held the street upside-down. The clock. The man. You'll see. I didn't. That's kind of the whole point of what I'm about to explain to you, further along — how a thing can be standing right in front of you, waving, and your own head will quietly turn you away from it and tell you you saw a bus.)

I cycled along the water toward the Prinsengracht end, past the café on the corner where the man with the enormous dog has his coffee, past the church, and I looked up at the church clock the way you do to check if you're late, and it said twenty past seven, and I thought *fine,* and looked back at the road. That's all. A clock said a normal time and I believed it and pedalled on. Hold onto that. It's going to matter that a clock, that morning, held still and said an ordinary number and I didn't think twice.

Fenna was waiting at our corner with her bike, one foot on the pedal, one on the ground, doing the impatient thing where she's already talking before you're in range.

"— because Meester Boonstra said, and I quote, 'we'll see how funny it is at the assessment,' which, one, threatening, and two, it *was* funny, everyone laughed, that's data —"

"Morning," I said.

"You're late. Gefeliciteerd. Did you bring it?"

"Bring what?"

"The thing. The — Sam. The maths." She looked at me with the specific despair she saves for me. Fenna is small and quick and has opinions about everything and is right about most of them, which is exhausting and also the reason she's my best friend. We've been in the same class since we were five. She once punched a boy called Ruben for taking my hat, in the second week, and got in trouble, and has never once let me forget that she owes me nothing and I owe her everything. She's probably the most normal person I know. I mean that as the highest thing I can say about anyone, now, knowing what I know. She is gloriously, restfully normal. She was my anchor to the ordinary world and she didn't even know she was doing it.

"I forgot the maths," I said.

"You *forgot* the maths."

"I had a lot on. It's nearly my birthday."

"It's been nearly your birthday since October," Fenna said, and pushed off, and I went after her, and we rode into school with her telling me exactly how the maths was going to go, which turned out to be wrong, but that's a different story.

---

School was school. I'll spare you. Meester Boonstra did in fact bring up the assessment; Fenna did in fact say something funny and get away with it because teachers secretly like her; a boy called Daan got a nosebleed during gym and we all had to look at the ceiling. Ordinary. The last completely ordinary day I got, and I spent it drawing a shark in the margin of my notebook and thinking about cake. If I'd known, I'd have paid more attention. But that's the thing about the last ordinary day. It never tells you.

It was after, at home, that it started.

---

Mum had gone back out — a double, she'd be late, there was money on the counter for chips and a note in her tired handwriting that said *eat a vegetable* with a drawing of a carrot, which was the vegetable, technically. I did my not-maths at the kitchen table and then went up to my room to get my headphones, and that's when I saw it, on the windowsill.

The lantern.

A small brass ship's lantern, sitting on my windowsill in the last of the afternoon light, exactly where the sun would catch it, as if someone had set it there to be found.

I want to be really honest with you about the order things happened in my head, because the order is the whole point, and later on people are going to make me tell it over and over until I get it right.

First: I knew it. Instantly, in my body, before any thought — I *knew* that lantern. The little dented brass. The handle worn shiny on top from swinging. The dark smoked glass of it. It was the lantern from the dream. Not one like it. It. The exact one the man carried across the black water, the one warm swinging point of light coming for me for six years, sitting on my windowsill in the sun as though it had every right.

Second: I picked it up, and it was real.

I need you to understand how real. It was heavy — properly heavy, dense, the weight pulling my wrist down, a made thing full of metal. It was cold, colder than the warm room, cold like it had come out of somewhere cold. And it was *wet.* Beaded with it, a film of water on the brass that came off on my fingers, and when I lifted it to my face — I don't know why I did that, you do strange things — it smelled of salt. Not canal water. The sea. Cold and green and far away, salt, on a lantern, on my windowsill, three bridges and a whole country from the actual sea.

For about four seconds I stood there holding an impossible object and I *knew* it was impossible. My heart was going like it did in the dream, except this time I could feel it, hammering, and my skin had gone tight and cold all over, and every hair on my arms was up, because the wall between the thing that comes for me at night and the room where I keep my socks had just gone thin as paper and something had reached through.

And then.

Then my head did the thing.

I felt it happen. I didn't know that then — I couldn't have described it, I had no word for it — but I felt something in my own mind take hold of the terror, gently, like a grown-up taking a knife off a toddler, and start to set it down.

*It's just an old lamp,* my head said, reasonably.

And wasn't that true? It was an old lamp. Brass. A bit battered. You saw them all over — in the junk shops on the Elandsgracht, on the market at Waterlooplein, hanging outside every third brown café pretending to be nautical. There was nothing strange about an old brass lamp. I'd probably seen this one a hundred times.

*Mum must have got it at the flea market,* my head went on, warming to it. And yes — yes, that was exactly the kind of thing Mum did, she couldn't pass a stall of old junk without buying some heavy useless beautiful thing, the flat was full of them, and she must have picked this up cheap and stuck it on my sill, meaning to say, forgotten to say, she was so tired lately, of course she forgot.

*And it's wet because the window,* my head said, *the window's open, look, and it rained earlier, it must have.* Had it rained? I didn't think it had rained. It had definitely rained. And the salt smell was — brass smells like that, sort of, old metal, tarnish, that was all that was.

*And you're tired,* my head finished, kind now, almost tender, tucking me in. *It's a big week. You've got a big imagination, everybody says so, Meester Boonstra literally wrote it on your report. You had that dream for years and it's still rattling round in there, and you saw an old lamp and your brain went — you know what your brain's like. You dreamed the rest onto it. That's all. That's all this is.*

And I put it down.

I set the lantern on my shelf, between a football trophy I didn't earn and a jar of sea-glass, where it looked completely at home, like it had lived there for years. And I got my headphones. And I stopped thinking about it. Not like I forced myself — that would have been something, that would have been me knowing there was a thing to force. It just slid out of the middle of my head to the edge and then off the edge, easy, and I felt fine. Better than fine. I felt calm. A warm, settled, sleepy sort of calm, all through me, the calm of a problem solved, and I went downstairs to order chips and I did not think about the wet salt lantern from my six-year nightmare sitting on my shelf again for two whole days.

That calm — remember the calm. That was the Lull, and I didn't have a name for it yet.

That's the only time I'm going to step out and tell you plainly like that, so mark it. Everything else, you and me both have to catch as it goes past.

---

There were other things that afternoon and evening. I only know about them now because I've gone back over that day so many times, turning it in my hands, looking for the seams. Little things. Wrong things. They didn't stick to me at all at the time. They slid right off, same as the lantern, and it's only lining them up afterward that you see they were all pointing the same direction, like iron filings do when there's a magnet under the table you can't see.

There was a tram. I was cycling to the chip shop and a tram went past me over the Prinsengracht bridge, and I clocked the number on the front the way you do without meaning to — a 14 — and thought nothing, and it was three streets later that some quiet back part of me went, *the 14 doesn't run there,* and the front part of me said *sure it does* and kept pedalling. It doesn't, by the way. There is no 14 down that line. There isn't a 14 at all anymore. I could have told you that if you'd stopped me and asked. I just didn't ask myself.

There was a sign. The chip shop's window said, in painted gold letters I'd read a hundred times, VLAAMSE FRITES, Flemish fries, except that evening for just a second it said VLAMSCHE FRITEZ, spelled wrong, spelled *old,* the letters a little too curly, and I blinked and it said the normal thing again and I ordered my chips with mayonnaise and did not mention it to the man behind the counter, who I'd known my whole life, and who that evening I could not have picked out of a crowd, though I didn't notice I couldn't.

And there was a man in a long coat.

I saw him twice. Once outside the chip shop, standing on the far side of the little bridge, not doing anything — just standing, in a long dark coat too heavy for the mild evening, looking at the water. And once on my way home, further along, the same coat, the same not-doing-anything, so that a small cold part of me went *that's twice,* and the warm calm part said *lots of men have coats,* and I believed the warm part, because believing the warm part was so easy, it was the easiest thing in the world, it was like lying down.

I ate my chips. I watched something. Mum texted that she'd be past midnight and to lock up, and I texted back a thumbs up and a carrot, our joke, and I locked up, and I went to bed with the window open because the night was warm.

And at dusk — this was the last thing, the thing that finally, barely, got a hook in me — I was pulling my curtains and I looked down at the gracht, at the black water and the three bridges strung with their little lights, and there was the man in the long coat.

On the other side of the canal. Directly across from our house. Looking up.

Not at the water this time. Up. At the front of our house. At my window. At me, I think, though it was too dark to see a face under the collar of that coat, just the pale suggestion of one, tilted up, still as the church clock, still as flat water, still as a man who has all the time there is.

I told myself he was waiting for someone. I told myself a lot of things. I looked away — deliberately, to prove I could, to prove it was nothing, I looked away for maybe two seconds down the length of the dark canal — and then I looked back to catch him being nothing.

He'd moved.

He hadn't moved the way people move. I hadn't seen him walk. But he was closer now — one bridge closer, standing at the foot of the next bridge along, in that same coat, in that same total stillness, with his face still tilted up at my window.

And this time the calm didn't come.
