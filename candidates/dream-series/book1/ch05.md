# Chapter Five — The Girl Who Says Not Yet

*Lull, Book One · draft*

Wessel got me out of the still-room fast after that.

He wiped the mark off the chapel wall himself, with his thumb, roughly, muttering, and it came away like it had never been paint at all, just a wrongness that had decided to look like paint — but his hand wasn't quite steady doing it, and I'd already learned enough that morning to know what it means when the man teaching you the rules is frightened by something the rules say can't happen. He walked me back to the plain brown door and stopped me with a hand on my shoulder before he opened it.

"Two things," he said. "One. You tell nobody. Not your mum, not your friend, nobody. Not to keep our secret — to keep *you.* The more people know a snag's been found, the faster the wrong ones come. Two." He looked at me hard. "You're going to want to go into the dream on purpose now. You can feel it, can't you, now you know it's a door and not a film. You want to push on it."

I did. I hadn't known I did until he said it. But it was true — since the coin came into my hand there'd been a pull in me toward sleep, toward the four o'clock water, a curiosity that felt almost like homesickness.

"Don't," said Wessel. "Not yet. Not alone. Not until I've taught you the tells and the way home, because a snag as loud as you, going in green and unguarded — that's ringing the bell and leaving the door open. You wait for me. Promise me."

"I promise," I said.

I want you to notice I said it. I want you to notice I meant it. I'm not going to pretend I didn't. I meant that promise with my whole almost-twelve-year-old heart, standing in the archway with the city's noise coming back up around us like water closing over a stone.

And then I went home, and I ate dinner with my mum, who asked if I was all right and got a *yes,* and I went up to my room where the lantern sat on the shelf between the trophy I didn't earn and the jar of sea-glass, and I lay down on my bed just to think, just for a minute, just to hold it all in my head and look at it —

and I fell asleep before I'd decided to, the way you do, the way everyone does, the way the whole trap is built on everyone doing.

And I went in.

---

I want to tell you I fought it. I didn't get the chance. One moment I was on my bed with my shoes still on and the next I was standing in the flooded square in my rocket pyjamas, shin-deep in the black glass water, and it was four o'clock in the morning of the whole world.

Except.

It was the dream and it wasn't. Six years I'd had it, and it always ran the same, on rails, a film I couldn't stop — the cold, the silence, the paper crown, the one candle, the letters that wouldn't hold, the clock that wouldn't hold, my chest that wouldn't beat, and then the light swinging across the water and the Lantern Man wading toward me, patient as tide. It always ran the same because I was a Sleeper in it, a tourist, going where the rails went.

This time I woke up inside it.

I *knew.* Standing there in the cold water, I knew where I was and that it was real, the way Wessel had said, and the knowing changed everything, because a thing you know is real is a thing you can look at properly. And the first thing I did, the very first, God help me, was exactly what Wessel had warned me not to do the moment before I'd broken my promise — I looked around, awake, curious, *reaching* — and I felt the square feel me back.

I felt it the way you feel a room go quiet when you walk in. Everything in that flooded square — the water, the dark buildings, the guttering candle — turned some attention it shouldn't have had toward the small boy who'd just woken up in the middle of it. I'd rung the bell. I'd left the door open. I'd done it in my sleep, without deciding to, which is the thing nobody warns you about your own self: that the promise you keep awake is not always the promise your sleeping hands will keep for you.

Across the square, the Lantern Man stopped wading. And lifted his helmet.

Not to me. He *listened.* The great brass ball of his head tilted, the little round window catching the candle-glow, the way a dog lifts its head at a sound too high for you — and I understood that he'd heard me wake up, heard the bell of me ring out clear across the black water, and that the patient slow thing that had been walking toward six-year-old me for six years now knew that the boy in front of it was, for the first time, awake and looking back.

He started toward me faster.

"*There* you are," said the girl. "You absolute — you woke up. In *here.* On your *own.* Do you have any idea —" and then, because there wasn't time, she stopped telling me off and did the only useful thing. She grabbed my hand.

---

I'd heard her voice for six years and never once seen her, and I'll tell you now that she was not what I'd built in the dark. I'd made her older, in my head, a grown-up, a mother-shaped calm. She was a kid. My age, near enough — thin, sharp-faced, dark hair cut like she'd done it herself in a hurry and been cross about it, and eyes that were doing four things at once, watching the Lantern Man and watching the water and watching me and watching the way out all at the same time. She had a smudge of something on her cheek. She looked like she hadn't slept in a year, which, I'd learn, was funny, in a way that wasn't.

"Don't look at his light," she said, fast, low, pulling me back through the water that didn't splash. "When he lifts it he shows you a place and if you look at the place you go there and you do *not* want to go where he keeps his places. Look at me. Only at me. Can you walk? Course you can't, your legs have gone, everyone's legs go the first time, come *on* —"

"You're the girl," I said, stupidly, wading, gripping her hand. "You say *not yet.* You've been saying it since I was six."

"Yes."

"Why?"

She glanced at me, and for half a step she wasn't a frightened kid in a hurry, she was something older and sadder wearing a kid, and she said, "Because it isn't yet. Because if it were yet, you'd be gone, and I've spent a long time making sure it stays not-yet." Then the older thing folded back down out of her face and she was just cross again. "Which you nearly ruined tonight, waking up in here like a — down. Get *down.*"

She pulled me down behind the stone lip of the flooded palace steps, into the cold black water up to our chests, and the Lantern Man's light swung over our heads, and where it passed the water lit up and showed, for a breath, that street again — the narrow leaning buildings, the bruised purple-yellow sky the colour of the place under your eye three days after you walk into a door — and I felt it pull at me, felt my head start to turn toward it, felt how *easy* it would be, and she took my chin in her cold hand and turned my face back to hers and said, "*Me.* Only me," and held it there until the light swung past.

---

"I don't have long," she said, when it was dark again, "and neither do you, because you rang the bell and now the whole record knows you're awake, both sides, so listen and don't argue, because you argue, I can tell, you've got an arguing face." She had her back to the stone, breathing hard, watching the swing of the light move off across the far side. "You've met the ferryman. The Vigil. He'll have told you *keep the record true* and it'll have sounded good, because it is good, it's the best of a bad choice. And soon a beautiful person is going to find you and be kinder to you than anyone's ever been and offer you the world and your father back, and that'll sound good too, and it'll be a lie with a hook in it. And they'll both be right about the other one and wrong about themselves and they will *both* want to use you, because of what you are."

"What am I?" I said. "Everyone keeps —"

"I know they do." Her voice cracked, just slightly. "I can't tell you. Not because it's a secret. Because if I say it out loud in here, in front of *him,*" — a flick of her eyes toward the light — "it's true faster, and I need it to stay not-yet a while longer. Just — don't pick a side because they're kind to you. Kindness is the cheapest thing either of them has got. Pick a side, if you have to pick one, because of what it's willing to *lose.* Promise me that much."

"I keep promising people things tonight," I said, and I don't know where it came from, some scrap of my mum in me, some scrap of Fenna.

And the girl laughed. A real one, surprised out of her, quick and bright as the candle, gone as fast. "Yeah," she said. "You do. It's going to get you killed and it's the only thing I like about you." She stood up, and pulled me up, and the water ran off us silent. The Lantern Man was small and far now, still listening, still coming. "Last thing. The most important thing. Your nightmare." She looked at me, dead serious, the older thing all the way up in her face now. "It's not a memory, Sam. It's not something that happened. You've been visiting it back to front your whole life and thinking it was the past because that's what dreams usually are. It isn't the past."

"What is it?"

"It's a *date,*" she said. "It's one particular night that hasn't come yet. It's got a day on it, a real one, and when the letters in the dream finally hold still long enough for you to read them — and they will, they're already trying, that's what all this is — you'll see it. And you're going to wish you couldn't." She was going now, thinning, the way the coat man had thinned, the way everything here goes when its time is up. "Wake up. Wake up properly, and don't come back in here without the ferryman, and — Sam."

"What?"

"When you read the date," she said, from very far away now, from the edge of everything, "and you see who's standing in the water with you — don't do the thing you're going to want to do. Not yet."

---

I woke up.

My own room. My shoes still on. The little green numbers said 4:00, then, mercifully, 4:01. My mum's low snore through the wall. The lantern on the shelf. Everything ordinary and safe and mine.

Except my pyjama legs — no. I was dressed, I'd fallen asleep in my clothes, I told you that. My jeans, then. The bottoms of my jeans, cuffed at the ankle.

They were wet. Cold, and soaked through, and stiffening as they dried, and when I sat up and put my hand down to the very foot of my bed the sheet was wet too, wet in a long stripe the exact width of a boy who'd waded chest-deep through black water, and the smell coming up off it was salt, cold and green and far away, the sea, three bridges and a whole country from the actual sea.

And this time, sitting there in the dark at four in the morning with the cold wet proof of it drying on my legs, I did not tell myself a single comfortable thing.

That was the night I stopped being able to.
