# Chapter Eleven — Not Yet

*Lull, Book One · draft*

The men in the held night did not run me through, which is the first small mercy, and I've thought a lot about why.

It's because they weren't awake to me, not the way the ferry crowd hadn't been awake to the shadow. They were a held moment, doing the one thing they were held doing — forming up, hoisting pikes, the drummer finding his beat, the captain in black about to throw out his hand and step off toward a back room and a vote — and a boy appearing in the middle of them registered for exactly the second and a half a thing registers before the Lull files it, and then forty seventeenth-century faces did the little frown and the little clearing, and a big bearded man simply stepped around me the way you step around a bollard, and the night went on, and the drum went on, and I stood in the middle of a moment four hundred years old and understood that I was a ghost in it. The intruder. The thing that shouldn't be there.

"You came in ahead of me," said Lena, behind me. "Oh, that's *good.* That's better than I hoped."

She'd come through after me, of course — a Vivid could follow where the door was already open, and I'd opened it, I was always going to open it, that was the whole design. She stood in the cobbled dark in her slate coat, breathing the woodsmoke, more real than the real men around her, and she was looking at me with a delight so total it had gone all the way through into something like reverence.

"Do you feel it?" she said softly. "You're *inside a held moment,* Sam. Not visiting a dream — standing in a thing a genius pinned to the wall of the world four hundred years ago. And it didn't cost you anything. You're not even out of breath. Do you know what I had to spend to follow you through the door you propped open by *existing?*" She shook her head slowly. "The Vigil have a name for what you are and they won't say it. I'll say it. You're arc-level. One a generation. The rarest thing there is — a dreamer who can hold a moment of *history* steady enough to change it and make the change stick. That's not a Waker. That's not even a Vivid. That's the thing both my faction and his would burn the world to own, and you've been eating chocolate sprinkles on it your whole life."

---

And that's when I understood the shape of the whole week, all at once, the way you understand a magic trick the instant it's over and feel like a fool.

"You didn't need this painting," I said slowly. "Did you. The vote, the forty men, the kinder world. You didn't build the chocolate shop to lay a road to the Night Watch." The cold of it came up through my feet. "You built it so *I'd* read it. So I'd feel the lean and follow it here and reach in. The whole thing was a — a test. To see if I'd do the impossible thing. To see if I'm what you think I am."

Lena's smile didn't waver, and that was the answer.

"Both of us," she said, almost gently, almost proud of me for getting there. "You think the ferryman brought you to this painting by accident? He walked you up to the one door in the city only an arc-level dreamer could open, and he watched you open it, same as me. He needed to know what you are as badly as I did. He just tells himself a nicer story about why." She spread her hands in the woodsmoke. "We *both* set this. We've been reading you against each other all week, Sam, the chocolate shop, the ferry, the fair — running you like an experiment, because whoever finds out first what you are, and gets you, wins the next four hundred years. Congratulations. You're the most important person alive. Now." Her voice dropped, and the whole held night seemed to lean in with it. "Turn the company for me. You don't even have to want to. Just hold this moment steady the way only you can, one heartbeat, and let me put a word in the captain's ear — and we'll have done one enormous kindness together, and you'll never be lonely again, because I'll be the person who was there when you found out you were a god."

I looked at the captain in black, mid-stride, his hand thrown out, one hour from a room and a vote. I looked at the little girl lit bright as a coin among all the muskets, held there, breathing, four hundred years. I looked at forty real men who'd be scraped off the world to make room for a better one.

And a voice I'd known for six years said, very close to my ear, calm and almost kind, the way you speak to a kid about to touch a hot pan:

"Not yet."

---

She wasn't there and she was. The dream girl — the smudge on her cheek, the hair she'd cut herself in a hurry, the eyes doing four things at once — standing in the held night beside me where no one should have been able to reach, thin as breath, spending something enormous to be there at all.

"You can't hold the moment for *her,*" she said, fast, low, urgent, the frantic edge already in it. "But you can hold it for it*self,* Sam. That's the thing she doesn't want you to know she's afraid of. She can only scrape this night off if it's *loose.* So don't fight her. Don't turn the company. Do the opposite." Her cold hand found mine, the way it had in the water. "*Anchor the true night.* Hold it — all forty of them, exactly as they are, exactly as they chose, the real cruel true evening Rembrandt caught — hold it whole and pin it down so hard she can't get a fingernail under it. Keep the record true. You're the only one who can, because you're the door, and a door can be *shut.*"

"I can't hold forty men," I said. "I couldn't even hold a boat-hook. It'll backwash, it'll take —"

"It'll take something," she said, and her voice cracked, and the older, sadder thing came all the way up in her face. "Yes. It always takes something. That's what *keep the record true* costs, that's why his side is so tired, that's why it's the hard right thing and not the easy one. She's offering you a kindness that costs forty men. He's asking you a truth that costs *you.* That's the whole choice, Sam, that's the only choice there's ever been, and I have kept it *not yet* for six years so that when it finally came you'd be old enough to make it yourself." She was going, thinning, spent. "Make it. And — Sam."

"What?"

"After this, you'll be able to read the clock," she said, from the edge of everything. "The date will hold. I won't be able to keep it not-yet anymore, once you've done a thing this big — you'll ring too loud, the whole record will turn to look. So when you get home, and you sleep, and you finally read the night that's coming for you." Her voice was almost gone. "Remember it was worth it. Remember you chose true once, when it cost you, so you'll believe you can do it again when it costs you everything."

And she was gone, and Lena's warm hand was on my shoulder saying *now, Sam, now,* and the drum was beating, and the captain was one stride from stepping off toward the vote, and forty held men breathed in the four-hundred-year-old dark waiting to find out which way the world would go.

So I did the thing a Waker can't do.

I didn't turn the company. I got down on my knees on the cold cobbles in the middle of them, and I opened my arms, and I held the whole night — all forty of them, the captain and the lieutenant in gold and the drummer and the dog and the little lit girl and the woodsmoke and the canal-smell and the exact true terrible choice they were an hour from making — I held it the way Wessel taught me to hold a coin, whole, every fold and every glint, except it wasn't a coin, it was a moment of the world, and it was too big, it was so much too big, and I held it anyway. And I *pinned* it. I drove the true night down into what-really-happened so hard that when Lena reached for the captain's ear there was no slack left, no loose edge, nothing to scrape — the night had gone hard and true and would not slide, the way the clock in my dream had gone hard and true, and would not, and would not, and *would not.*

I felt the working start to collapse. I felt the account come due. I felt the backwash reach into me to take its coin, the way it had taken the hagelslag, taking something true out of me to pay for the true night I'd forced to hold —

and I let it take it, whatever it was, and I held the record true.
