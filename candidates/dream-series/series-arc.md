## Working title & alternates
**DREAMLINE** (the line between real and fake; and the timeline). Alternates for the owner: *The Fourth Hour* (the 4 a.m. wake); *Bleed*; *The Real of Dreams*; *Un-Dreamt*; *Nightside*.

## Principal cast
- **Sam Vermeer** — the narrator; turns twelve in Book 1; Amsterdam. Surname nods to a painter of captured light (pays off at the Rijksmuseum). Found because his recurring nightmare is a beacon; tests as a Waker with unusual reach; may be far more.
- **Anna Vermeer** — Sam's mother; a Sleeper; raising Sam alone since his father went under.
- **Joris Vermeer** — Sam's father; went under years ago (world.md Rule 4) and never surfaced. The family's private grief and the series' slow-burn mystery.
- **Fenna** — Sam's best friend; a Sleeper; his anchor to ordinary life and the reader's "wait, what?" stand-in.
- **Wessel** — a gruff night-hand on the IJ ferry; a Vigil darner; Sam's reluctant first teacher. "Keep the record true."
- **Lena Marchetti** — a young, dazzling Palimpsest recruiter and Vivid; Italian (seeds Book 2's Venice); the most charming person Sam has ever met, which is the problem.
- **The Lantern Man** — the figure from Sam's recurring nightmare: a diver in an old brass suit, wading across flooded water toward him with a lantern. His identity is the series' engine.

## The series spine (one paragraph)
A kid whose recurring nightmare turns out to be a real moment both factions need discovers that dreams are real, and is pulled between the Palimpsest (who want to use him) and the Vigil (who want to protect the true record). He slowly learns his nightmare is not a past he visits but a future being forged — and that he may be the rarest kind of dreamer, one who can hold a moment of history steady. Across three books he goes from recruited pawn → trained agent → the one person who must decide whether a single terrible moment stays true or gets rewritten — learning that "keep the record true" and "make the world better" are not the same thing, and that the line between saving history and stealing it is as thin as the line between real and fake.

## Book 1 — DREAMLINE: The Fourth Hour
**Logline:** When the 4 a.m. nightmare he has had since his sixth birthday starts leaking into daylight, twelve-year-old Sam Vermeer discovers that dreams are real places — and that two secret factions of dream-walkers will do anything to recruit him, because his nightmare is a warning about something that hasn't happened yet.
**Complete adventure:** Sam learns the rules the hard way (pull, shadow-step, the tells, the Lull), is courted by both Wessel (Vigil) and Lena (Palimpsest), and uncovers a small Palimpsest revision underway in Amsterdam. Self-contained win: he foils the revision in a set-piece inside a Rijksmuseum painting.
**Series turn:** he chooses the Vigil over the Palimpsest — barely — and at the climax does something a mere Waker shouldn't be able to (makes a small change to a *moment* stick), which both factions notice. The final beat reveals his nightmare carries a *date* that hasn't arrived: the nightmare is a future, and his father is in it.

## Book 2 — DREAMLINE: The Mirror City ✅ DRAFTED (2026-07-19)
*Title confirmed: **The Mirror City** (D-003). Full draft in `book2/` (12 chapters, ~23.3k words); chapter map in `book2/outline.md`. All the beats below are executed on-page.*
**Logline:** Training with the Vigil in Venice — a whole city built on mirrors, where the seam never fully closes — Sam hunts the location in his nightmare, learns to Anchor, and discovers the Vigil has buried a bleed of its own.
**Complete adventure:** a heist-through-history across Venice's thin places (a dive into the frozen plague-night of 1630); Lena returns, now half-ally; Sam learns Anchoring; a betrayal (Serafina's merciful sum — the city over the boy).
**Darkening:** the Vigil isn't clean — four hundred years ago they forged the 1630 plague-night "for the greater good" (the Salute, the rising water), the crack the Palimpsest exploits by moving to *un-forge* it.
**Series turn (on-page):** to save Fenna, Sam makes a bigger change than he should — forges a night and it *sticks*, confirming he is arc-level; the backwash takes his **mother's** face (escalating Book 1's father's-face cost). Both factions stop recruiting him and start fighting *over* him (a loaded gun on a table). He identifies the nightmare's place: **Pompeii** — and learns his father Joris went under diving into it, and the Lantern Man is a rescuer wading toward him (Joris, or Sam's own future self — held ambiguous, per Book 3 below).
**Book-3 setup confirmed:** date (from Book 1) + place (Pompeii) = the battlefield; the Lantern Man's identity and the "girl who says *not yet*" carried forward as the running mysteries; the Vigil's forgery now exposed; Lena genuinely undecided which side she'll take "at the water."

## Book 3 — DREAMLINE: The Fourth Hour Comes ✅ DRAFTED (2026-07-19) — FINALE
*Title confirmed: **The Fourth Hour Comes** (D-004). Full draft in `book3/` (12 chapters, ~22.8k words); chapter map in `book3/outline.md`. The finale — every beat below is executed on-page and every open series thread is RESOLVED.*
**Logline:** At the frozen moment of Pompeii, on the date his nightmare finally names — his own thirteenth birthday, the fourth hour — Sam has to choose between letting a true and terrible thing stay true and rewriting it, and discovers a third thing neither faction ever found.
**Complete adventure + series climax:** the confrontation at the nightmare's real date and place; both factions converge in the water (the Palimpsest to crack Pompeii open, the Vigil to anchor it shut with Sam inside forever); Lena chooses; Sam refuses both edits and keeps the record true by **witnessing and testimony**, not force.
**The date, paid to the reader:** it is Sam's own birthday — the anniversary the 4 a.m. nightmare has carried since his sixth (given at cost in ch01, per the frame's rule).
**The Lantern Man, resolved:** Joris *and* Sam's own older self — one figure, because a father and a son each diving for the other look identical through fogged glass; the ambiguity *was* the answer (love in the same brass suit).
**The girl who says *not yet*, resolved:** **Vibia**, a Pompeii child, herself arc-level, who woke inside her own death-moment and held it true from the inside for two thousand years (the real pin under the ten thousand); "not yet" meant *not this way, and not until the one who can do the third thing* — she is freed, not replaced.
**The Vigil's forgery, resolved:** proven *unnecessary* — testimony (not force) keeps the record true, so Serafina finally tells the true story of 1630, the forced darning ends, and Venice begins to heal.
**Lena's allegiance, resolved:** she walks out of the Palimpsest for good and stands with the boy — the wild card lands human.
**Sam's lost parent-memories, resolved:** restored (the true record heals what forgeries stole) — but at the cost of his **reach**: he gives up dreaming itself forever to seal Pompeii's door behind him, trading the magic for his family and un-loading himself as a weapon.
**Resolution of the confession frame:** *the thing that made everyone angry* is that Sam proved BOTH factions unnecessary — the record was always kept by telling, not force. The frame closes: the narrator is **Sam, grown and no longer a dreamer, confessing to the next child who has begun waking at four** — being the person no one was for him (closes hard against Book 1 ch01). It stays secret because a story told too loud becomes an anchor that can be stolen; kept soft, told hand to hand, it is un-scrapeable. The frame's unreliability is explained: he held too much true at once and gave up the reach, so he can no longer check the record in the dream — only Fenna's notebooks are truer. Ends on the series refrain, resolved: *Not yet* — *now.* **THE END.**

## The confession frame (spans all three books)
The older narrator tells the story from after Book 3, addressing the reader directly (adapted from the owner's fragment). The frame is deliberately unreliable (world.md §6.4): the Lull worked on him too, so "this is the truest version I've got." Each book opens and closes on a short frame beat; the frame's final payoff is the Book-3 ending.

## Tone & audience
Middle-grade/YA crossover (like Percy Jackson): fast chapters, cliff-hangers, humor carried by the narrator's voice, real stakes but age-appropriate (peril, loss, no gore). Real, visitable geography throughout so the "is this real?" feeling stays grounded.
