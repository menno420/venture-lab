## One-line hook (logline)
On the fourth hour of his thirteenth birthday — the date his nightmare has counted toward since he was six — Sam Vermeer dives into the frozen moment of Pompeii to pull his lost father out of the worst catastrophe in history, and finds both secret factions waiting in the water for him to become the thing he's fought.

## Short blurb (≈50 words)
The date has come. At the frozen instant of Pompeii, the Palimpsest want Sam to crack the past wide open, and the Vigil want to seal it shut forever with him inside. His father is drowning in the middle of it. And a girl who's whispered *not yet* for two thousand years is finally out of time.

## Back-cover copy
*You can buy a ticket to the worst moment in the history of the world. Sam Vermeer has to dive into it.*

For seven years, Sam's 4 a.m. nightmare has counted down to a single date — and now he knows it. It's his own birthday. It's tomorrow. And it leads to **Pompeii**: the hardest-frozen moment there is, ten thousand people holding their own end steady since before anyone wrote anything down — where his father went under, forty years ago, and never surfaced.

Both sides are already at the water. The Palimpsest means to crack the moment open and rewrite the whole world inside it. The Vigil means to shut it forever — by anchoring an arc-level dreamer into it, to hold it true with his whole self, and stay. One offer will give Sam his father back and make him a god. The other will make him a hero and a prisoner. Both are the same terrible arithmetic wearing different faces.

But there's a girl down there who has been holding a door for two thousand years, and a best friend up top with nothing but a notebook and the truth, and between them they know the one thing neither faction ever learned: that you don't keep the record true by holding it. You keep it true by *telling* it.

To save everyone he loves, Sam will have to find the third thing — and pay for it with the only currency big enough, which is the wonder itself.

*The finale of **Lull.** The fourth hour comes for everyone who's still awake at ten to. Sam Vermeer has been awake his whole life.*

## Age range & reading level
- **Age:** 9–13 (middle grade), crossover appeal to 14+ / adult MG readers.
- **Content:** peril, loss, grief, and a costly, hard-won ending handled age-appropriately; no gore, no graphic violence. A frightening-but-not-horror register (a frozen-catastrophe atmosphere, the eruption kept as sky, ash, and rising water rather than shown as bodies). The hero comes home — and pays a real, permanent price to do it.

## Content notes (honest, for the owner)
- Themes (load-bearing, the trilogy's spine landing): the cost of power; whether the ends justify the means; grief and a returned parent; that "keep the record true" and "make the world better" are not the same; and — the finale's argument — that truth is kept by witnessing and telling together, not by force. There is no free edit, not even a good one.
- The climax turns on a real moral cost: Sam refuses to un-write a true historical catastrophe even to save lives, and gives up his own gift permanently. This is deliberate and earned across three books, framed as costly-but-right rather than triumphant.
- A returned-parent thread is handled with honesty: the father comes home changed and disoriented, not magically restored — the reunion is joyful and hard at once.
