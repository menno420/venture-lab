# Chapter Ten — Nobody Recruits a God

*Lull, Book Two · draft*

They came for me that same night, both of them, and I want you to notice that neither of them tried to be kind about it, and that this — the end of the kindness — was how I knew everything had changed.

Lena came up out of the black cracked mirror on the workshop floor, the one that would never open on 1630 again, stepping out of the ruin of it into the room like it was a puddle — a Vivid can follow where an arc-level door has been, and I'd blown that door so wide the whole seam of Venice was standing open. She was breathing hard and her slate coat was soaked to the waist with four-hundred-year-old water and for once she did not look delighted. She looked *afraid,* which I had seen on her exactly once before, kneeling in the woodsmoke of the Night Watch when I chose against her.

Behind her, filling the doorway, not entering, the weight — the First Draft, the many-throated hole in the world, standing in the acqua alta on the water-steps as if it could not quite be bothered to come in for me anymore, as if I were a thing it had already decided it owned.

"Well," said Lena, quietly. "You've done it now, little bell. You held a painting shut and both sides said *maybe.* You've just forged a night to save a Sleeper and made it *stick,* in front of the First Draft itself." She wiped water off her face. "There's no maybe left. There's no *recruiting* left. Do you understand what you've done? Nobody recruits a god, Sam. You don't send a charming girl with a photograph to *recruit* the one dreamer in a generation who can hold history steady and change it and make it hold. You just —" her voice did something I'd never heard it do; it wobbled " — you just take him. However you have to. Before the other side does."

"Then take me," I said. I was still on the floor, hollow, holding Fenna's hand. "Go on. You said it. Nobody recruits me anymore. So do the other thing."

And the strangest silence fell in that room of mirrors. Because neither of them moved.

---

I understand it now, though I didn't then. I'll explain it to you the way Serafina explained it to me later, dry and exhausted, because it's the shape of the whole war from here on and you'll want it for what comes next.

The reason neither of them took me off that floor is the same reason nobody had grabbed me in Amsterdam once I started ringing: I was too loud, and now I was *arc-level* loud. If the Palimpsest seized me, every darner in Europe would feel it and the Vigil would come down on them with everything they had left, because an arc-level dreamer in the First Draft's hands is the end of the world and they know it. If the Vigil locked me away, the Palimpsest would tear the continent apart to get me back, because an arc-level dreamer is the only thing that can un-drown their four hundred years of patient work. I had become, in one night, in one flooded moment, too valuable for either side to *hold* — because the instant either one held me, the other would go to open war to take me, and neither was sure it would win.

"You are a loaded gun on a table between two frightened people," Serafina said. "And nobody will pick you up, because whoever picks you up gets shot at by the other. So they will do the next thing. They will *guard* you, and watch you, and wait — each of them making sure the other doesn't get you — and they will circle each other around you for years if they must, until the one day comes when they *have* to use you, the one moment worth going to war over." She looked at me, and her fierce old eyes were wet. "And you already know when that day is, boy. You've had the date since your sixth birthday. That is the day they will stop circling. That is the day the whole war lands on one child in one place at one hour, and both sides throw everything they have into the water to own you, because whoever holds the arc-level door on *that* day writes the world." She said it very quietly. "The date is not your nightmare, Sam. The date is the battlefield. And you've been dreaming the battlefield your whole life."

I want you to sit with what that meant for a second, because I didn't, not for weeks, and it did me damage. I had spent a whole month terrified of being *taken.* Of a grey coat closing on my shoulder, of Lena's warm voice, of being used. And in one night I'd graduated past all of it into something lonelier: I was too dangerous to take, and that meant I was never going to be *rescued* either. Nobody was going to swoop in and carry me somewhere safe, because there was nowhere safe to carry a boy who lit up every mirror between Venice and the sea, and because the moment anyone tried, the war would land on whoever tried it. I wasn't a prize anymore. I was a weather system. People were going to arrange themselves around me — carefully, at a distance, watching each other over the top of me — and wait for the one day the storm made landfall. And I was twelve, and I wanted, more than I have ever wanted anything, for one single grown-up to be *in charge* of this, and there was no one, there was only me, standing in the water at the bottom of everyone's arithmetic.

Wessel, when he heard — Nico sent word north that same night, the Vigil have their ways — Wessel didn't come. He sent back one line, by the same silent channel, and Nico read it out, and it's the truest thing anyone said to me in that whole ruined week: *"Tell the boy I'm not staying away because I don't care. I'm staying away because if I stand next to him now, I'm just one more person the other side has to count. The kindest thing left is to be somewhere else. He'll understand that one day and hate it, and he'll be right."* I did understand it. I do hate it. He was right.

Lena did not deny it. That was the terrible confirmation. She stood in the ruined mirror-light, soaked and afraid, and she looked at me on the floor and she said the truest thing she ever said to me, and I've turned it over a thousand times.

"I came back to Venice half meaning to save you," she said. "Not for the First Draft. For *me.* Because when you held that painting shut and paid your father's face for it, you did the one thing I have never once managed in my whole clever life — you chose true when it cost you, and I have wanted, ever since, to be near the kind of person who can do that, the way you want to sit near a fire when you've been cold a long time." Her jaw set. "And instead I watched you forge a night to save your friend and I watched it stick, and now you're not a fire, Sam. Now you're the thing my masters and his mistress will burn the world to own, and I can't save you from that, because there's nowhere on earth to hide a boy who lights up every mirror between here and the sea." She turned to go, and stopped, and did not look back, and said: "I'll see you at the water, on the day. We'll both be there. Everyone will. And I don't know, this time, which side I'll be standing on — and that, God help me, is the nicest thing I've ever been able to say to anyone." And she stepped into the cracked black mirror and the First Draft folded up off the water-steps like a shadow at noon, and they were gone, and the acqua alta lapped the workshop floor, and it was just us again, the four of us, wet and wrecked in a room full of mirrors all showing the same dead city under the same bruised sky.

---

Fenna sat up, finally, and coughed the last of 1630 out of her, and looked around the room at the dozens of mirrors all showing the ash and the stone and the leaning pale streets, and then she looked at me, and she did not do a joke.

"That's your dream," she said. "Isn't it. That's the place under the water. It's in all of them now. Since you — since the thing you did for me." She swallowed. "You lit it up. Whatever you turned into, saving me, it lit up the place you've been trying to find all month, in every mirror in the city, all at once." She wiped her mouth with the back of her hand, and her voice was very small and very steady, my furious brave best friend who narrated her own drowning. "Sam. You said the backwash took something. Last time it took your dad's face. What did it take this time. You've gone a colour I don't like. What did it *take.*"

And I looked at Fenna, whom I had refused to leave, whom I had bought back out of a dead city with the only currency the world would take — and I found I could not tell her. Not because of the Lull. Because of shame. Because the answer was *my mother,* because I had spent my own mum's face to keep my best friend's, and Fenna would carry that for the rest of her life the way I was going to, and I could not — I could not do that to her, not after everything, not on top of everything.

So for the tenth time, and the last time, and the only time I have ever been glad of it, I did not tell Fenna the truth. But I didn't lull her either. I just looked at my best friend in the mirror-light and I said, "Nothing you have to carry," and I let her see that it was a wall and not a smoothing, a door I was holding shut on purpose and not one I was tucking her behind — and Fenna, who knew the difference now, who had a notebook full of the difference, looked at me for a long moment and then nodded, once, and let me keep it. Which is its own kind of love, and a harder one, and the one we had left.

Then Serafina came and stood over the two of us, over Joris's boy and the girl he'd broken every rule to save, in her ruined workshop under her uncovered mirrors, and she looked at the dead city burning in all that glass, and all the fight had gone out of her, and all the deflection, and all the forty years of holding a door shut, and she said, in a voice like the drowned church:

"All right. All right, Sam. I've one more truth to give you, and it's the worst one I own, and it's about your father, and it's about that place in the glass, and I have kept it four hundred — no." She stopped. Her old mouth twisted. "That's the lie I tell. I told you the mirror was four hundred years of my grief and it was a *deflection,* boy, the way you deflect, we're all of us the same — it isn't four hundred years old, my grief, it's *forty.* That mirror is your father's. And I know exactly what city that is, and what happened there, and why he could never come home, because I'm the one who forbade him to go, and he went anyway, and he did not come back the same, and if you're going to walk into it too — and you are, I can see the date on you like your father wore it — then you'll walk in knowing. Sit up. Both of you. And let me tell you about Pompeii."
