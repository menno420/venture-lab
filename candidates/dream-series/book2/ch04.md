# Chapter Four — Anchoring

*Lull, Book Two · draft*

Serafina didn't send me away, though she stood in that doorway a long time deciding to.

What changed her mind was Wessel, who said nothing clever at all — he just took my father's brass shackle-key out of my hand, without asking, and held it up so the old woman could see it in the dead-flat light off the black water. She looked at the key, and something moved in her face that was older than the anger, and she said a word in Venetian I didn't know, soft, the way you say a name at a graveside, and she stood back from the door.

"He kept it," she said. Not to me. To the key. "Forty years and he kept the key." Then she looked at me, and the fierceness came back down over the grief like a shutter. "Come in, then, boy. Bring the loud girl and the tired ferryman. And wipe your feet — I have spent sixty years keeping the water out of this room and I'll not have a Dutchman track the lagoon across my floor."

---

Her workshop was the strangest room I have ever stood in, and I have stood in a painting.

It was a *specchieria* — a mirror-maker's workshop, the last real one in Venice, she told us, the craft nearly dead now, kept alive by exactly one furious old woman and the boy who rowed her groceries. And it was full of mirrors. Hundreds of them, of every age and size, hung and stacked and leaned along every wall, some silvered bright and some gone black and cloudy with the centuries, some no bigger than a coin and one so tall it went from the floor to the dark beams of the ceiling. Every one of them was covered. Sheets, cloths, sheets of brown paper, old velvet gone bald — every mirror in the room had something draped over it, so that walking in was like walking into a room full of standing ghosts, all facing you, all hooded.

"You feel it," Serafina said, watching me. It wasn't a question; she said it the way Wessel did. "This is a still-work, boy, but not the kind your ferryman keeps. In the north you darn the seam shut — you stitch it tight as a drum and nothing bleeds, and you call that safe." Her mouth went thin. "You cannot do that in Venice. The seam here is too old and too thin and too tired; stitch it shut in one place and it tears open in three others, like mending a rotten sail. So we do not close it. We *manage* it. A mirror is a piece of tamed seam — a little doorway that we keep, and cover, and open only on purpose, so that all the thinness of the city can be gathered up and held in the palm of your hand instead of running loose in the streets." She touched the nearest hooded frame, gently, the way you'd touch a sleeping animal. "Every mirror in this room is a place the seam would otherwise be. I keep them covered because an uncovered mirror in Venice does not always show you the room you are standing in. Sometimes it shows you *when* you are standing in it. Sometimes it shows you somewhere else entirely." She turned. "And that, boy, is the thing your father could do that no one else could, and it is the thing that is going to get you killed, and it is why I am going to teach you Anchoring before I teach you anything else. Because a dreamer who can open doors and cannot hold anything shut is a door propped open in a house full of thieves."

---

Anchoring, it turns out, is the opposite of pulling, and harder, and I want to try to explain it the way Serafina explained it to me over the next three days, because you'll need to understand it for what comes after.

When you *pull,* Wessel had taught me, you hold a small thing whole in your mind and want it across the seam into your hand. It arrives, and it's real, and it stays because it's small enough that the world just accepts it — a coin, a key, a knife.

Anchoring is for the big things. And the trouble with a big thing isn't holding it whole — Serafina said I had more reach for that than anyone she'd trained, my father included, which she did not say as a compliment — the trouble is that a big thing pulled across the line *doesn't want to stay.* Reality notices. Reality pushes back. Pull a marble table out of a painting, the way I'd watched Lena do it, and the world will spend the next hour quietly trying to un-happen it, to smooth it back out of existence, because it doesn't belong. So Anchoring isn't the pull. Anchoring is what you do *after* the pull. It's *pinning.* It's holding the pulled thing so hard and so whole, and driving its reality down into the waking world so deep, that when you finally let go, the world has given up arguing and simply accepts that the thing was always there.

"Pull is a question," Serafina said. "*May I have this.* Anchor is a sentence. *This is here now.* And the difference between them is everything, boy, because a sentence cannot be taken back."

She made me start small. A brass candlestick from across the room, pulled into my hand — easy, I'd done heavier off the ferry. Then: *keep it.* Don't just hold it — pin it. Push it down into now so hard that I could open my hand and it would stay warm and solid and real on my palm without my mind holding it there at all. The first six times it went cold and thin the instant I let go, and faded, and was gone, back to wherever candlesticks go. The seventh time I felt it — felt the moment the world stopped pushing back, felt the thing go from *borrowed* to *belonging,* a soft heavy click deep under everything, like a key turning in a lock four rooms away.

I opened my hand. The candlestick sat there, real, mine, permanent.

And every covered mirror in the room shivered.

Not much. A ripple, a flinch, all of them at once under their cloths, like a room full of hooded people all flinching at the same small sound. Serafina's head came up sharp. Fenna, who had been writing it all down in her notebook, stopped writing.

"That," Serafina said quietly, "is the thing I was afraid of." She came and took the candlestick out of my hand and turned it over, frowning at it. "You anchored it. Good. Clean, even — cleaner than your father at your age, God rot him. But you anchored it *too well.* You didn't pin one candlestick into now, boy. You pinned it into now so hard the whole seam felt it — every mirror in the room, every thin place in the *sestiere,* probably every darner between here and the mainland. When you drive a nail, you drive it into one board. You just drove a nail into the whole house at once, and the whole house rang." She set the candlestick down, very carefully, as if it might go off. "You have your father's reach and none of his fear, which is exactly the wrong way round. He was frightened of what he could do. You are not frightened of anything yet. We are going to fix that, and I am sorry, because fixing it is going to hurt."

---

She was right that it hurt, and she was right that it took days, and I'll give you the shape of them because the days matter — because a thing you learn slow, one candlestick at a time, is a thing you own, and I was about to be very glad I owned it.

She'd set a thing on the far bench each morning and make me pin it into now and hold it and let it go, over and over, all morning, and every time I anchored too hard she'd rap my knuckles with a mirror-maker's little steel rule — not for the hurt, she said, but so my hands would learn to be afraid before my head was, "because your head is a Vermeer and it will never be afraid in time." I anchored a spoon. I anchored a candle, lit, and held the flame steady across the seam, which is harder than it sounds because a flame doesn't want to belong anywhere. I anchored a cup of water without spilling a drop, which took two whole days and taught me more than anything, because water, Serafina said, is the true test — "it remembers it was somewhere else, water, it is the most homesick thing there is, it will always try to run back across the line, and if you can pin a cup of water into now and make it stay, boy, then one day, God help you, you might pin a *flood.*"

I didn't understand why she looked so grim when she said *flood.* I did, later. I understood it up to my chest in black water four hundred years deep. But that's ahead.

In the afternoons, when my head was wrung out and Serafina had no more patience, Nico took us out. And I want to put Nico properly in the record here, because he saved my life twice before this part is over and he never once made it about that. He was a Vigil kid — a young Waker, born to it, his whole family ferry-folk and glass-folk going back to the doges — and he treated the entire impossible secret war as if it were a slightly annoying family business he'd been born into, like a bakery. "My nonna darns the Rialto, my uncle darns the fish market, I row the groceries and the loud foreign bells," he said, cheerfully, sculling us under a bridge so low we lay flat in the boat. "One day I darn something important. For now — you see that? Third window, blue shutter? Thin. Somebody dreamed too hard in that room a hundred years ago and it never healed. We do not knock on that door. Now, gelato."

Fenna took to Venice the way she took to everything, which is to say she catalogued it. She filled a notebook and started a second. She learned the vaporetto map before I did and corrected Nico on his own canals twice, which he loved. And on the third evening, sitting on the workshop steps with the acqua alta lapping our feet and gelato going soft in the heat, she said, not looking at me, "I feel it now. The thin. Since the ferry, since you kept — since you stopped smoothing me. It's like a draught under a door. I can feel where the cold comes in." She licked her spoon. "Is that what it's like for you all the time?"

"All the time," I said. "It doesn't switch off."

"Huh." She thought about it, the way Fenna thinks about everything, all the way to the bottom. "That's really lonely."

"Yeah," I said.

"Well," said Fenna, "it isn't now. I'm here. Eat your gelato, it's melting, you're the worst." And that was the whole of it, and it was enough, and I have never told her how much.

---

That night — my first quiet night in the mirror city, in a narrow high room over the black water with Fenna asleep across the hall and Nico snoring somewhere below and Wessel gone back north on the night train "before the city gets a taste for me too" — I couldn't sleep, because for the first time in a month I *wasn't* afraid to. The bell of me was quiet here. The flooded square felt far away. And I lay awake in the strange thin dark listening to the water lap the steps below my window and I thought, with a kind of guilty lightness, *maybe I could just be here. Maybe I could learn to darn and stay quiet and let the date come and go somewhere else. Maybe.*

And then the biggest mirror in the room next door — the one that went floor to ceiling, the one Serafina had covered with three separate cloths and turned to the wall besides — the big mirror uncovered itself.

I heard the cloths slide off, one, two, three, soft as breath in the dark. I got up. I shouldn't have. I went to the door of my room and looked across the hall into the workshop, and the great mirror had turned itself around to face me, and it was not showing me the dark workshop or my own pale face in the doorway.

It was showing me a street. A different street, somewhere I'd never been, narrow buildings of pale stone leaning close together, and the paving of the street was thick with something grey and soft that fell out of the sky like snow that wasn't snow — and above the leaning buildings hung a sky that wasn't night and wasn't day, a deep bruised purple-yellow, the exact colour of the place under your eye three days after you walk into a door.

I knew it. I had seen it for one heartbeat six years ago, upside-down in black water, when the Lantern Man lowered his light. It was the place under the water. It was the somewhere the diver kept his places. It was the *inside* of my nightmare, and it was standing in Serafina's mirror in the middle of the night, waiting, and on the far side of the falling grey, small and dark, a warm point of light began, very slowly, to swing.

Behind me, from nowhere, close to my ear, calm and almost kind, a girl's voice said: "*Not yet.*" And Serafina came out of the dark in her nightdress with a face like a slammed door and threw all three cloths back over the glass at once and said, "*You do not look in that mirror,* boy — not that one, not ever, not until I say," and I said, "I know that place, I've seen that place, what *is* it," and the old woman went so white and so still that I had my answer before she gave me the lie:

"It's nothing," Serafina said. "It's a dead city. It's four hundred years of my grief and it is *none of yours.* Go back to bed."
