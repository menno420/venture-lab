# Chapter One — The Date

*Lull, Book Two · draft*

I told you, at the end of the last part, that I went down into the water one more time to read the date, and that I'd tell you the rest when I could tell it careful, and not out loud, and not yet.

Well. It's later now. And I've decided you've earned the date, because you stayed this long, and because you're going to need it to understand everything that happens in the drowning city, and because — I'll be honest with you, the way I promised — I am tired of carrying it by myself.

So here it is. I went down into the flooded square, and the Lantern Man came wading toward me one slow boot at a time, patient as tide, and the girl said *not yet* in my ear, going frantic at the end the way she always does — and this time, because I'd held the Night Watch shut and paid for it and rung myself out across every thin place in Europe, this time the letters held.

I looked up at the tower clock across the black water, the one that had never once kept a number for me in six years, and it went hard and true and certain the way it had gone the night I turned six. Four o'clock. Straight up. And then, under it, where a clock has no business having anything, more letters swam up out of the brass and stopped swimming and *stayed,* and spelled a day and a month and a year.

I'm not going to write it here. Not yet. I know, I *know,* I keep doing that, the girl's right about me. But there's a reason, and the reason is that a date written down is a date made a little bit more true, and I have spent a long time keeping this one as un-true as I can, and I'm not going to hand it to you casually on the first page like the specials board outside a café. You'll have it. When it costs me to give it to you, the way it cost me to read it, you'll have it, and not before.

Here's what I *will* tell you. It hasn't happened. It's a day that is still, as I write this, out ahead of us, coming, the way four o'clock comes for everyone who's still awake at ten to. My nightmare is not a memory. It never was. It's a *future,* and it has a date on it, and the girl who's spent six years whispering *not yet* was never soothing a scared kid. She was buying time. She was holding a door.

And in the water, in the future, in the flooded square with the paper crown and the one candle that won't go out — my father is there. I didn't see him. I want to be clear about that, because later on people made me say I saw him and I didn't. But I *knew* it, the way I know my own name, the way six-year-old me knew the face behind the fogged little window. The date isn't just a date. It's the day I get my father back, or the day I lose him for good, and the dream has never once let me tell which, and that not-telling is the cruelest thing anyone has ever done to me, and I still don't know who did it.

I woke up on my bedroom floor with the lantern burning warm on the sill and the green numbers ticking past four, and I was twelve years and one day old, and I knew the date, and I threw up.

---

That was the beginning of the end of Amsterdam for me. I want to walk you through it quickly, because the real story of this part happens somewhere else, in a city that is more mirror than stone, but you have to see why they took me out of my own city first, and what it cost to go.

It started coming true faster after that. All of it. Not just the lantern — the whole dream began leaking into the daylight like the seam over my life had worn through to a hole. I'd be cycling along the Prinsengracht in the ordinary morning and the water in the canal would go flat and black and *dead,* dead the way it's dead in the square, and for three whole seconds there'd be no trams, no birds, no accordion, no four hundred tourists — just the silence of the flooded square laid over my own street like a wet cloth, and then a delivery van would rattle over the bridge and it would all come back and I'd be shaking on my pedals.

Bleeds followed me now. Wessel said it was because I'd rung too loud at the Rijksmuseum — that holding the Night Watch shut was like standing up in the middle of a dark theatre and shouting your own name, and now everyone in the theatre knew exactly where I was sitting. The clocks around me stopped keeping numbers even when I was awake. A stranger in a long coat became two strangers, then three, standing at the ends of bridges, not-walking, patient. The 14 tram that doesn't run anymore ran past me four times in one week, and once it slowed, and the doors sighed open, and inside it was four o'clock in the morning and there was black water sloshing along the floor of the carriage, ankle-deep, and a paper crown floating in it, and I did not get on, and I have never in my life been so proud of not getting on a tram.

The worst of it happened at school, which is the part that finished Amsterdam for me. I was at my desk in the ordinary afternoon, Meester Boonstra droning about rivers, Fenna two rows over — and I looked up at the classroom clock to see how long till the bell, the way you do, and the clock wouldn't hold a number. The hands drifted. The little numbers swam like fish in a bowl. And then the daylight went out of the windows, all at once, not like a cloud, like a lid coming down, and the classroom filled up ankle-deep with cold black water that no one else could see, lapping silent round the legs of the desks, and a paper crown drifted past my chair, and across the room, where the door should have been, a warm point of light began, very slowly, to swing.

I made a sound. I must have. Because the next thing I knew Fenna had me by the arm out in the corridor — she'd got me out somehow, she was strong when she needed to be — and the water was gone and the light was gone and the daylight was back, and she was looking at me with her face doing the frown, the real one, and I could feel my own hand already reaching for the soft place in her to smooth it away, because that had become as automatic as breathing, and I hated it, and I did it anyway, and I watched my best friend's fear clear into nothing while black water dripped, actual water, off the cuffs of my jeans onto the school lino.

That's when I knew it too, before Wessel ever said it. That the city had become the dream. That there was no line left, for me, between the flooded square and the corridor outside my classroom, and that if I stayed I was going to walk into the water in front of everyone I loved and let the light come, because some tired part of me had started to *want* it to.

"They're not all coming to *take* you now," Wessel said, on the pont, at the grey cold start of a day. He'd stopped pretending the ferry was anything but our meeting place; he barely bothered loading bikes anymore, just stood at the rail with me in the thin dark water and talked low. "They know they can't just grab a snag that rang the way you rang and hold him. You're too loud, you'd bring the other side down on whoever had you. So now they *watch.* Both sides. They're waiting to see which way you'll break, so they can be standing there kind and ready when you do." He looked at me sideways, grim. "And you're breaking, kid. Look at you. You're grey to the lips at nine in the morning. This city's too thin for you now. Every canal's a mirror, every bridge is a threshold, and you've turned into a bell that won't stop ringing in a room made of glass."

"So what do I do," I said. My voice had gone small and flat. I'd been sleeping maybe two hours a night, in twenty-minute pieces, terrified of the water each time.

"You leave," Wessel said. "You go somewhere the Vigil can teach an arc-level dreamer without the whole neighbourhood catching fire. Somewhere the seam is *honest* about being thin, so you can learn to stand in it instead of drowning in it every time you close your eyes." He said the next part like it tasted of salt. "There's a place we send the ones who ring too loud for their own cities. A school, near enough. The oldest still-work we've got, older than the Begijnhof, in the one city in the world that was *built* on the line and never tried to pretend otherwise."

A gull screamed. The north bank slid closer.

"Venice," Wessel said. "We're taking you to Venice, kid. The mirror city. Where the seam never closes, and everybody's a little bit drowned, and nobody notices one more bell."

---

I want to tell you I felt something grand when he said it. A boy from a small canal being told he's going to Venice — you'd think there'd be wonder in it. There wasn't, that morning. There was just the cold, and the tiredness, and one clear thought that came up out of me before I could decide to say it, the way the true things always did.

"My dad," I said. "Did he ever go? To Venice. To the — school."

Wessel went still at the rail, the particular still that his face does when it's working hardest.

"Yeah," he said, at last, quietly. "Your dad trained in Venice. Long time ago. Before you, before your mum, back when he was ringing loud himself and they had to move him for the same reason we're moving you." He didn't look at me. "There's a woman there. Old even then. She taught your father to darn, and she taught his teacher, and she'll teach you, if she'll have you, which is not a thing I'd bet the boat on, because she and your dad didn't part friends, and I don't know the whole of why, and I've learned not to ask." He pushed off the rail. "So that's the other reason it's Venice. It's where the answers are, if there are any. It's where he *was.* You'll be walking the same wet stones he walked. Some people would find that a comfort."

"Do you?"

"No," said Wessel. "I find it a warning. But I'm a tired man on a boat, and you're the loudest snag in fifty years with your father's own reach in your hands, and it's not my comfort that matters." The pont bumped the far dock, that soft heavy shudder, the horn giving its flat honk. "Go home. Tell your mum tonight. All of it, this time." He looked at me, and there was something almost gentle under the grey. "She's earned the truth more than you think, that one. More than you know. Tell her, and see what she does. And then pack a bag."

I went home to tell my mum I was going to Venice, and to lie to her one more time about why — and my mum, who had asked me *did you sleep* every single morning of my life like the answer mattered more than she'd let on, was sitting at the kitchen table in the dark with the light off and a shoebox open in front of her, the one from under her bed, and she looked up at me with her tired real face, and she said, before I could get a single word out:

"You've read the date. Haven't you." And then, quieter, so quiet it was almost the flooded square: "Your father read it too. The week before he went under. He had that exact look you've got now — like he'd swallowed a clock." She pushed the shoebox an inch toward me across the table. "Sit down, Sam. It's my turn to tell *you* something for once. And then I'll help you pack."
