# Chapter Seven — Into the Worst Night

*Lull, Book Two · draft*

Here is how you break into four hundred years ago, in case you ever have to.

You need a door, which was me. You need someone who can read the moment so you go into the right night and not into the black between nights, which was Serafina. You need light you can trust, which is a harder thing than it sounds when every lamp in a frozen moment lies — and that turned out to be the lantern, my lantern, the warm lit brass lantern from my nightmare that had crossed the seam and burned on my sill, which Serafina took one look at and said, "*That* you brought and did not tell me — of course you did, you are your father's son to the bone," and made me carry, because a light that has crossed the line and stayed lit will burn true in any dream there is.

And you need someone to keep the record. Which was Fenna, and I fought it, and I lost, and I have never been so glad to lose a fight or so sorry.

"You are not coming," I said, in the workshop, while Serafina drew the great covered mirror out from the wall — not the forbidden one; a different one, an old cloudy oval the height of a man, the one she said opened onto the plague night. "Fen. It's the worst night this city ever had. There's *plague* in there. You're a Sleeper —"

"I'm a Sleeper who *knows,*" said Fenna, already lacing her boots, "which the ferry man said three times is the rarest thing, and which means the Lull can't get to me and I can't get lost in a boring story, which is exactly what happens to you the second something goes sideways — you reach for the easy version, Sam, it's your whole *problem,* you've been doing it since the lantern on the shelf. You need somebody in there whose head stays hard. That's me. That's the job. I wrote it down." She stood up. "Also you're not the boss of me and never were. Ruben's hat, remember. You owe me everything. I'm collecting."

Serafina looked at the two of us, and at Nico, who had simply picked up a boat-hook and taken up a position by the mirror as if the argument were already over, and she said something under her breath that I think was a prayer, and she pulled the last cloth off the cloudy oval, and it stopped being a mirror and became a doorway, and on the far side of it was the worst night Venice ever had, waiting for us, four hundred years deep.

"Hold hands," she said. "All of you, hold on, and do not let go inside, whatever you see, because what you see will be *true,* and the true things in there died a long time ago and some of them will want you to stay. Sam. Open the door."

I took the warm lantern in one hand and Fenna's hand in the other, and I leaned toward the frozen moment the way I'd leaned toward the Night Watch, and the seam went soft, softer than it ever went for anyone, a puddle, a warm bath — and I pulled the whole of us across into 1630.

---

I have to tell you about the smell first, because it's the thing that made it real, and I've never been able to make anyone understand it who wasn't there.

It was cold, and it was dark, and it was Venice — I knew the shape of the canals even four hundred years gone — but it was a Venice out of a bad dream: fires burning on the quays, not for warmth but for the smoke, because they thought the smoke kept the sickness off; doors marked with white crosses, house after house after house; and boats, low black boats, moving slow on the dark water with lanterns at their prows and a shape under a cloth in the middle of each one, and more of them than there were house-lights, a whole silent fleet of them going out toward the lagoon in the dark. And over all of it, the bells — not ringing a tune, just tolling, one at a time, all across the city, on and on, a bell for a death and then another bell for a death and then another, so many and so steady that after a minute your own heart started keeping their time.

"Don't look at the boats," Serafina said, low and hard, gripping my hand. "Look at the church. Look at where the church will be."

Out on the point, where the great white Salute would stand for four hundred years, there was nothing yet but a muddy spit of land and a few pilings driven into the black water — because we were *before,* we were in the night the forgery was made, the night her seven-grandmothers-back gathered the darners and decided to steal a mercy. And over that empty point, in the dark, the air was *wrong* the way the Salute had been wrong from across the canal — flickering, scraping, the seam being worked — because the Palimpsest were already here. They'd come into the same night from the other side, and I could see them now, down at the water's edge, grey coats in the firelight, and Lena among them, and at their centre the weight, the hole in the world, the strong one the First Draft had sent, standing with both hands raised toward the empty point where the church wasn't yet, patiently pulling the mercy apart before it could ever be made.

"They're going to stop the forgery from happening at all," Nico breathed. "Not un-write it after. *Prevent* it. If the darners never make the lie tonight, the church never stands, the plague never breaks —"

"— and ten thousand people who lived for four hundred years never lived," Serafina finished. "It all comes up at once. The whole true night, four centuries of it, drowning back in. Sam." She turned to me, and there was no fierceness left, only the terrible calm. "You cannot stop them scraping. You are not strong enough to fight that thing at the water's edge and neither am I. But you don't have to fight it. You have to do the other thing. The thing you did in the painting. Get to the point — get to where the darners are making the forgery — and *Anchor it.* Hold the mercy true. Pin it so deep they cannot get it loose, no matter how they scrape. Keep the record —" she stopped, and gave a short, awful laugh, four hundred years of irony in it " — keep the *forged* record true. That is what I have brought Joris's son across four centuries to do. Hold my lie shut. Go."

---

We ran.

Or we did the thing that passes for running in a frozen moment, which is a kind of terrible wading, because the night didn't want extra people in it and pushed back on us like deep water, and the dead-boats slid past silent on the canals, and the bells tolled, and twice a door with a white cross on it opened as we passed and something looked out that I will not describe to you because you are, I hope, younger than I was when I saw it, and Fenna's hand was crushing mine and her lips were moving, and I leaned in to hear what she was praying and she wasn't praying, she was *narrating,* fast and flat and steady under her breath — "*door with a cross, boat with a lantern, Sam's got the light, Nico's got the hook, Serafina's leading, we're going to the point, we are going to the point, we do not look at the boats*" — keeping the record, holding her own head hard with the only tool she had, which was the truth said out loud, and I understood in that moment exactly what Wessel had seen in her on the train, and exactly why the Lull had never really caught her: my best friend was a darner. She'd been one her whole life. She just hadn't had a seam to work on until now.

Halfway there the night tested us, the way Serafina had warned it would.

A boat drew alongside on the black canal — a dead-boat, low, a lantern at its prow, a shape under a cloth amidships — and the man poling it turned his head and looked at us, at the four living warm loud people wading through his frozen grief, and he *saw* us, which the dead in a held moment are not supposed to do. His face was grey and wet and hopeless and it fixed on Fenna, and he said something, low, in the old Venetian, and reached out one hand off his pole toward her — not cruel, that was the worst of it, just *asking,* the way a drowning man asks — and Fenna stopped narrating and stopped dead in the water and I felt her start to reach back, because that is who Fenna is, she reaches back, and Serafina hissed "*do not take his hand, girl, he has been dead four hundred years and he only wants company*" and Nico got his boat-hook across Fenna's chest and hauled her bodily back and the boat slid on into the dark with the grey man still reaching, still asking, until the fog took him.

Fenna was shaking. "He looked *right at me.*"

"You're a Sleeper who knows," Serafina said, hauling us on. "You're the warmest, most awake thing in this whole dead night — of course they see you. That's why you should not have come, and it's too late now, so keep your head *hard* and keep the record and do not, do not, give a dead man your hand, no matter how he asks. Move."

We came out onto the muddy point in the firelight, and there they were — the Vigil of 1630, seven or eight of them in the plain dark clothes of four hundred years ago, kneeling in the mud in a ring with their hands pressed flat to the ground, and at the center of the ring an old woman with white hair scraped back and a face I knew, a face I was holding by the hand, a face Serafina would wear in four hundred years — her own seven-grandmothers-back, mid-forgery, mid-theft, mid-mercy, holding the drowning city up out of the true night by main strength and about to lose it.

And between us and them, turning slowly, unhurried, rising up out of the grey-coated ranks at the water's edge, the weight, the hole in the world, the First Draft's strong one, lowered its raised hands from the empty point and turned them, instead, toward *me* — toward the boy who had just waded a whole living door into a frozen night — and a voice came out of it that was not one voice but many, dry and patient and pleased, the sound of a page being scraped clean:

"*There* he is," said the First Draft. "The little door. We didn't come for the church, boy. We never come for the thing you're looking at. We came for *you* — and your teacher has known it since the bells started, and brought you anyway, because a Venetian will always, always spend one child to save ten thousand. Ask her. Ask her what she promised us for the city. Go on." The many-voiced weight tilted, almost gently, toward Serafina, whose hand had gone slack and cold in mine. "Ask her why the door was left open behind you."
