# Chapter Twelve — The Fourth Hour Coming

*Lull, Book Two · draft*

We took the train home, the long way, over the ground, the way the Vigil do.

Venice let me sleep the whole journey north, the same strange mercy as before — a bell rings quiet in a city that's all bells, and rings quieter still going away from it — and I slept without the flooded square for the last easy nights I would get for a long while, with Fenna across the aisle keeping her notebook, writing down everything that had happened while it was still true, filling page after page in her small furious hand so that at least one true record of the mirror city would exist that no Lull could ever soften. She's still got them, those notebooks. When my own memory goes wrong — and it does, more and more, that's the tax, that's what all this costs — hers are the truest version there is. Truer than mine. I told you at the very start that when I say *this is what happened* I mean *this is the truest version I've still got.* Fenna's is truer. If you ever meet her, ask her. She'll tell you straight. She always did.

Nico came as far as the mainland to see us off, cheerful and wet-eyed at once, and made me promise to come back to Venice one day when it wasn't ending, and I promised, because I collect them, and because I meant it, and because I have learned that a promise you mean is worth making even when you can't keep it. Serafina didn't come to the station. She'd said her goodbye in the workshop, with her hand between my shoulder blades, and some goodbyes you don't improve by repeating. But she gave me two things to carry north, and I'll tell you both, because they're the last of Book Two and the first of what comes next.

The first was my father's mirror. The tall one. The forbidden one. Crated up and shipped by slow freight to a still-room in Amsterdam, because — she said — a thing like that shouldn't be in a city that's already drowning, and because it was mine now, my father's dive folded up in glass, and a boy should keep his own map even when it's a map he's afraid of.

The second was a sentence, murmured on the water-steps with the acqua alta round our ankles, that I didn't understand until much later and that I'll give you now to carry too: "The girl in your dream. The one who says *not yet.*" Serafina had looked at me strangely. "Your father heard her too, boy. On his dive. Same voice, same words, holding him back from the edge of Pompeii for years before he went in. We never knew who she was. But I'll tell you what your father came to believe, at the end, and it frightened him worse than the diver." She'd paused. "He thought she wasn't warning him *off* the moment. He thought she was warning him *toward* it. He thought *not yet* didn't mean *never.* It meant *not yet — but when it's time, you'll have to.* He thought she was on his side. And he could never work out how a girl could be waiting inside a moment two thousand years old, saying the same two words to a father and a son across forty years, unless —" and there Serafina had stopped, and shaken her head, and said only, "unless time is even thinner than the seam. Ask her yourself, one day. You'll get the chance. You're going to the one place she lives."

---

Here is where the war stands, now that you've come this far, so you'll understand the size of what's still ahead.

Both sides know what I am. Nobody recruits me anymore. The Palimpsest and the Vigil circle each other around me like two dogs around one bone, each making sure the other doesn't close its jaws, and they will keep circling — for months, Wessel thinks, maybe a year — right up until the day the circling has to stop. Because there is a date. It has been coming toward me since my sixth birthday, and now I know the place it's coming at, and the two of them together make a battlefield: a bright morning by the sea, two thousand years ago and also not yet, under a mountain that is about to come apart, in the worst and hardest-frozen moment in the whole record of the world. Pompeii. On the day the fourth hour finally arrives.

On that day I am going to go down into the ash and the rising water to pull my father out — because he's down there, the diver, wading toward me across the drowning town with a lantern for six years, and I cannot leave him, I have already proved to the whole watching world that I cannot leave the people I love in the water, whatever the sum says. And on that day both sides will be standing in the water with me. The Vigil, to stop me changing the hardest-frozen moment there is, because they know exactly what the bleed of that would cost — they have a white church and a drowning city to prove it. The Palimpsest, to *make* me change it, to use my father's face as the kindest-looking hook there is and get me to crack open the holiest moment on earth so they can write the whole world over inside it. And Lena, who does not yet know which side she'll be standing on, and neither do I, and that not-knowing is the only wild card I've got.

And me. Twelve years old, going on the age you have to be to do a thing like this, which is an age nobody ever is. With a lantern that crossed the seam and stayed lit. And no living memory of either of my parents' faces, only two photographs and a warm hand I can't picture. And the knowledge, which I would give anything to un-have, that to save my father I will have to become, completely and forever, the thing I chose against in a painting when I was younger and better: someone who reaches into a true and terrible moment and rewrites it, and makes it stick, and calls it love.

That's the thing I did, in the end, that made so many people so angry. Or it's the thing I *didn't* do. I've told you all along that I'd get to it, careful, and not out loud, and I still can't, not quite, not yet — but you can see it now, can't you, the shape of it, coming up out of the water like the old text under a scraped page. You know the choice. You've known it since the flooded square on my sixth birthday. Let the true and terrible thing stay true. Or rewrite it, and save him, and be exactly what I fought.

---

The night we got home to Amsterdam, my mum opened the door, and I didn't know her face until she said my name — *Sam* — and then I knew her all at once, the whole of her, everything but the picture of her, and I put my arms around my mother whom I'd spent to save my best friend, and I held on, and she held on, and neither of us said what we both knew, which is the family way, and she made hagelslag even though it was midnight, and I ate two slices pressed down hard so the sprinkles stick, which is the correct method, and I will not be discussing it further.

And then I went up to my room, where the lantern burns on the windowsill to this day, warm and lit and real, come all the way across the black water and the six years and the seam. And I lay down on my bed as the green numbers ticked toward four. And I could feel the dream coming up under me the way the tide comes up under the pont — but it's a different dream now. Now I know the place. Now I know it's my father wading toward me through the fire and the flood, patient as tide, carrying the only warm light in the world, coming to reach me before I come in after him. Now, when the diver lifts the lantern to the little fogged window in his helmet, and the breath blooms grey on the glass, and the face swims up behind it that I've known since I was six and never once could name —

I look. I make myself look. And I still can't tell whose face it is.

It's my father's. I'm nearly sure. It's the face in the two photographs, older, sadder, breathing on the glass.

Except sometimes, in the half-second before the fog thickens and the ripple smears it away, it isn't my father's face at all.

Sometimes it's mine. Older. Sadder. The face of a man who came back from the water changed, and has been trying to warn a boy across all the thin years since, in the only two words the seam will carry.

*Not yet.*

But soon.
