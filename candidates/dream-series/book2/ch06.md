# Chapter Six — The White Church

*Lull, Book Two · draft*

I went to Serafina that night and I asked her straight, because I'd learned exactly nothing from Book One about how the truth lands, and I asked her the way you throw a stone at a window to see if anyone's home.

"Is it true the Vigil forged a moment to stop the plague. Four hundred years ago. Is that your grief in the mirror. Is that why the water keeps coming up."

She was cleaning a mirror when I said it — one of the small ones, a coin-sized oval gone black — and her hands went still on the cloth, and she did not turn round, and for a long time the only sound was the acqua alta lapping the bottom step of the workshop door, an inch higher than it had been that morning.

"Who told you," she said. Very quiet.

"Does it matter?"

"It matters *enormously,* boy, but no, I don't suppose you'll say." She set the mirror down. When she turned round her face had gone old in a way I hadn't seen, older than fierce, old like the drowned church. "Sit. You've been listening to something with a lovely voice, and half of what it told you will be true, which is the most dangerous kind of lie there is, and I'll not have you carrying the half-true version. If you must have it, have it whole. It's mine to give and I've earned the giving of it, God knows."

---

She told me, and I'll give it to you the way she gave it to me, because it's the wound this whole part turns on, and because I've come to think the only honest thing you can do with a wound is show it.

Four hundred years ago the plague came to Venice, and it did not stop. A third of the city in eighteen months. The bells rang for the dead until the ringers themselves were dead. And the Vigil of Venice — mirror-makers, night-workers, keepers of the true record, arms folded over what happened saying *no* — the Vigil of Venice watched their city die and they broke.

"There was a night," Serafina said, "the worst night, when the doge's own physicians gave the city up for lost, and the Vigil master of that time — my own blood, seven grandmothers back, I have her name in a book I can't read without weeping — she gathered the darners into this room, and she said: *we keep the record true, and the true record is that Venice ends. I will not keep it. I will forge it. And I will carry the sin of it to God myself and let Him judge me, but the children will live.*" The old woman's voice was perfectly steady, which was the terrible part. "And they went into the frozen moment of that night, all of them together, the way you go into a painting, and they changed the conditions. Not one great lie — a hundred small ones. A ship turned back at the mouth of the lagoon. A well left uncontaminated. A quarantine begun a week that it was not begun. A hundred nudges, stacked, until the true night where Venice fell was scraped away and a night where Venice lived was written over it. And it *held.* The plague broke. The city rose from its own deathbed. And they built the great white church out on the point — the Salute, *salute,* it means *health,* it means *salvation* — to thank God for a mercy they had stolen through a mirror, and they never told a living soul, and the water has been coming up one centimetre a year for four hundred years ever since, because —" she spread her hands " — because there is no clean edit. Even for saints. Even to save a city. The bleed of that one beautiful forgery never healed, and it never will, and it is drowning us slow, and every mirror in this room is me and mine trying to darn a wound the size of Venice with a needle, one night at a time, forever, and *losing.*"

The workshop was very quiet. A mirror flinched somewhere, softly, in the dark.

"I'll tell you the part that I've never told anyone," Serafina said, and her voice dropped, "because you should know exactly what a forgery for the greater good actually looks like, up close, so you're never fool enough to think it's clean. When they scraped the true night away — the night the city fell — they didn't scrape a number. They scraped *people.* There were ten thousand who died in the true version and lived in the forged one, and that's the mercy, that's the miracle, that's the white church. But a forgery is a scraping, boy, and the old text always shows through — and every mirror-maker in my line, for four hundred years, has been able to *see them.* The scraped ones. The people who died in the true Venice and don't know they were ever dead. On the worst nights, in the oldest glass, I can still catch them — a face at a window that the forged city has no room for, a child in a doorway who is a little too grey, a little too still, a little too much like the old text under the new. My own grandmothers stole those people back from death, and I love them for it, and I have spent my whole life haunted by the ones they stepped on to do it, and I cannot tell you, to this hour, whether it was the most beautiful thing the Vigil ever did or the most terrible, and *that,* Sam — that not-knowing, forever — is the real price of the merciful sum. Not the water. The not-knowing." She wiped her eyes with the heel of her hand, roughly. "Do the sum if you must. But never, ever tell yourself it came out even."

The workshop was quiet again for a long moment. Even the mirrors held still.

"So you see," Serafina said, "why I would not let your father reach into a frozen catastrophe and *change* it, however good the reason. Because I know exactly where that road ends. It ends in a white church and a rising sea and an old woman with a needle. I have spent my whole life being the proof that *keep the record true* is not a rule the Vigil follows because we're better than the Palimpsest. It's a rule we follow because we *tried the other thing,* once, for the best reason there has ever been, and it is drowning us still. That is the difference between us and the pretty voice on the step. Not that we would never forge a moment. That we *did,* and we know exactly what it costs, and we have chosen to pay it forever rather than do it again." She looked at me. "Your father never understood that. He thought if the Vigil forged one moment to save a city, the rule was a fraud and he could forge his own. He couldn't see that the drowning *is* the rule. Do you see it, boy? Because if you don't, you'll do what he did, and I'll bury you the way I as good as buried him."

I opened my mouth to say I saw it. I want to tell you I saw it.

That's when the bells started.

---

Not the church bells. The other kind — the kind only I could hear, the kind Serafina could hear, the kind that made every covered mirror in the workshop shiver at once and then *ring,* a high thin dreadful note like a wet finger on glass, all of them together. Serafina was on her feet before I understood I was standing. Nico came pounding up the stairs three at a time with Fenna behind him, and Nico's cheerful face had gone the colour of the lagoon.

"The Salute," he gasped. "Serafina — the *church* — there is a bleed on the Salute —"

We went out onto the workshop's water-steps into the acqua alta and the dark, and across the wide mouth of the Grand Canal, out on its point where it has stood for four hundred years, the great white church was *wrong.* I could see it even from there, even across all that black water — the way you see a bleed, the way you can't un-see it once you know — the white dome swimming, the stone going soft at the edges, the whole enormous building flickering between the church that is there and something else, some other night, the old dark drowning night it had been built to write over, bleeding up through the paint of the world like the old text under a scraped page.

And standing on the church steps, tiny and far and perfectly still in a coat the colour of wet slate, was Lena. And she was not alone. Behind her, along the whole white front of the Salute, stood a row of grey coats — Scriveners, more than I'd ever seen, a dozen, two — and at the centre of them, where I could not see a face but could feel the *weight,* the way you feel a hole in the world, stood something that made even Lena look small, something the First Draft had sent, something strong enough to stand on a four-hundred-year forgery and start, very patiently, to *scrape.*

"They're not attacking the city," Serafina breathed, and I heard in her voice the exact moment a thing she'd feared her whole life stopped being a fear and started being a Tuesday. "They're going to *un-forge* it. They're going to tear open the one wound and let the true night come back up — let Venice fall the way it was always meant to fall, four hundred years late — and drown ten thousand people to prove one thing to the whole watching world." She turned to me, and her fierce old face was full of a horror that was almost calm. "That we are exactly what they are. That *keep the record true* was always a lie. They'll rip the mercy out of my city and let it drown, and they'll be *right,* and the only way to stop them —" she stopped.

"— is to send in a dreamer who can hold a frozen moment shut," I said. Because I'd learned that much. "One who can Anchor a whole night. One who can stand on your forgery and pin it down so hard they can't get a fingernail under it." The cold water was up around my ankles now, rising, flat and black and silent. "You need the door. You need me."

"God forgive me," said Serafina, who had spent her whole life making sure no one ever asked this of a child again. "Yes. I need Joris's boy to do the thing I could not forgive Joris for wanting. I need you to go into the worst night this city ever had, and hold its beautiful lie *true.*"

And out across the black water, on the steps of the white church, Lena lifted one hand and gave me a small, sad, unmistakable wave — just fingers, just for me — the way she had across the harbour on the very first night, when I still thought a wave was a friendly thing.
