# Chapter Two — Everyone Knew But Me

*Lull, Book Two · draft*

Here is the thing my mother told me at the kitchen table in the dark, and I'm going to give it to you the way she gave it to me, plain, because she'd earned plain and I'd wasted a lot of her patience being clever.

"I know what you are," she said. "I've known since you were six and woke up screaming with wet feet on a summer night, and I tucked you back in and told you the window was open, and it wasn't. I know what the lantern is. I know why the fourteen tram frightens you. I know a man turned into a shadow on the pont and I know you've been keeping it off me like it was a kindness." She turned the shoebox so the lid came all the way off. "You've been lying to me for a month, love, and it broke my heart a little every night, and I let you, because I did the exact same thing to your father, right up until the week he didn't wake up. So we're even. And now we stop."

I couldn't say anything. All the excuses I'd built up over four weeks — *she's tired, she wouldn't understand, it's mine* — went flat and grey and blew off the edge of me the way the Lull used to blow the truth off strangers.

"I'm not a dreamer," Anna said. "Not the way you mean. I'm a Sleeper, same as anyone. I go, I look, I forget. I married one, though. I married the loudest, kindest, most stubborn darner the Vigil had, and I knew *exactly* what I was marrying, and I'd do it again in a heartbeat even knowing the end of it." Her voice didn't shake. That was the frightening thing. She'd cried this cry years ago and there wasn't any left, just the fact of it, hard and true. "Your father didn't hide the world from me, Sam. That was the deal we made. He hid it from *you.* Both of us did. Because a snag that doesn't know it's a snag rings quieter, and we thought — we honestly thought — if we could just keep you ordinary until you were grown, keep the seal shut over you the way it shuts over every other child at six, then the loud ones would pass you by and you'd get to have a life with hagelslag in it and nothing worse than maths." She almost smiled. "The seal didn't take. We knew that the night you turned six. We knew it and we decided to pretend anyway, for as long as we could get away with it. That was the lie. The window was always the lie. I'm sorry."

And then she reached into the shoebox — the one I'd thought was full of holiday snaps, the one with the little orange dates printed in the corner — and she started taking my father out of it and setting him on the table between us.

---

A brass shackle-key, notched and cold, exactly like the one Wessel had made me pull off the ferry's chain locker. "He carried this his whole life. Said the first thing he ever pulled across the line was a key, and the last thing he'd pull would be one too, because a key's the smallest true thing that *opens* something." She set it down. "It's yours now."

A photograph, old, square, the colours gone warm and orange the way they go — a much younger Joris, laughing, squinting into hard bright Italian sun, standing on a stone quay with green water behind him and a city of leaning towers and domes rising up out of it like something dreamed. He had his arm around a woman. She was old even then, tiny and fierce, with white hair scraped back and a pair of small round spectacles catching the sun, and she was not laughing; she was looking at the camera the way you look at a rope you're not sure will hold.

"Venice," my mother said. "The year before I met him. He talked about it his whole life — the one place, he said, where the world stops lying about what it is. He loved it and it frightened him and he never once told me the whole of why he left, only that he had, and that he could never go back, and that the woman —" she touched the old fierce face in the photo " — Serafina. He said her name maybe three times in fifteen years and every time it was like watching him touch a bruise. She taught him everything. And something happened, at the end, that he wouldn't put words to. So." Anna looked up at me. "If they're taking you to Venice, they're taking you to *her.* Which means you're going to learn the thing your father learned, in the room your father learned it, from the person your father couldn't forgive. And I can't stop that, and I'm not sure I'd want to, because the answers about him are down there and you've a right to them." She pushed the photograph across to me next to the key. "But I want you to carry this, and I want you to remember, whatever she tells you about him, that I *knew* the man, all the way to the end, and he was good. Tired, and secret, and gone too soon. But good. Don't let anyone in a mirror tell you different."

I put the photo in my inside pocket, next to the other one, the three-week-old one of him asleep. Two pictures of my father now, and not one living memory of his face — the backwash had taken that at the Rijksmuseum — and I sat in my mother's dark kitchen navigating by photographs toward a city made of mirrors, and if that isn't the whole of what this book is about then I've told it wrong.

"Look at me a second," my mum said, and I did, and she took my chin in her hand the way you do with a little kid, tired and gentle, and she looked at my face for a long time — really looked, the way you look at something you're trying to memorise — and I didn't understand why then, and I understand it too well now, and I can't write about it without stopping for a bit. "You've got so much of him in your face," she said. "It used to hurt to look at you, when he first went. Now it's the only place I get to see him." She let go. "Whatever you find down there — whoever that old woman is to him — you come home to me, Sam Vermeer. That's the deal. Your father went south to a mirror city and came back not-quite, and then he went under, and I have spent four years being the person who stayed. I'm good at it. I'm the best at it there is. But I would very much like, for once in this family, for somebody to *come back all the way.* Do you hear me? You go, you learn, you come home whole. You come home *knowing my face.*" She said it as a joke — she meant it as a joke — and I laughed, and neither of us knew it was a prophecy, and I have never forgiven the world for letting her say it.

"You'll let me go," I said. It wasn't quite a question.

"I'll let you go," said my mum, "because the other choice is you stay here and drown in your own street, and I have already lost one of you to that water. I am not doing it twice." She stood up and put the kettle on, ordinary as anything, which is how you know a Dutch conversation is truly over. "Now. There's the matter of Fenna."

---

The matter of Fenna was that Fenna had stopped believing me.

I'd been lulling her. I told you about the first time, in the hall, when I reached into the soft place in my best friend and handed her a boring story because it was easier than the truth — and I told you it was the single worst thing I'd done, and it was, and I did it again. And again. Every time she got the frown, the real *something's-wrong-with-Sam* frown, I smoothed it off her, gentle as tucking her in, and each time it was a little easier and I hated myself a little less, which is exactly the wrong direction and exactly how it gets you.

But here's what nobody had told me, because nobody knew it, because it had maybe never happened before to anyone. You cannot lull the same person over and over and have it keep working. The Lull is the waking cousin of dream-forgetting, and forgetting is a muscle, and if you make somebody's mind reach for the same boring story a dozen times it stops being restful and starts being a *rut,* a worn groove the mind can feel itself sliding into — and Fenna, who is the most stubborn person I have ever known, who punched Ruben in the second week and has never let me forget I owe her everything, felt the groove. And planted her feet. And climbed out.

She was waiting on my stairs when I came up from the kitchen, ordinary Fenna in the ordinary hall, except her arms were folded and her jaw was set and she was looking at me the way you look at a lock you've finally found the key to.

"Don't," she said.

"Don't what?"

"Don't do the *thing.*" She stood up. "The thing where I get scared for you and then I'm suddenly not and I can't remember why and I say something about peanut sauce. I've been keeping a *notebook,* Sam. Every time it happens I write it down the second I feel it going, before it goes, and then I read the notebook back later when you're not around to smooth it, and do you know how many times it's happened?" Her voice cracked, right down the middle, and it was worse than shouting. "*Nine.* Nine times my best friend has reached into my head and turned me off like a tap because it was easier than trusting me. I felt it every time by the end. I feel it right now, you're doing it right now, you've got the — stop it, Sam, I can see your *face* doing it —"

And I was. I'd reached for the soft place in her without deciding to, tired and cornered and terrified of the one conversation I'd been avoiding for a month, my hand already halfway to tucking her in.

I stopped.

It was the hardest small thing I have ever done. I want that on the record, because holding the Night Watch shut cost me my father's face and this cost me nothing anyone could measure and it was *harder,* because there was no monster, no fire, no Lena's warm voice — there was just my best friend standing on the stairs asking me to do the difficult true thing instead of the easy kind one, and everything in my hands wanting to be kind.

I took my hand back. I sat down on the stairs. And I told Fenna the truth. All of it. The water, the lantern, Wessel, Lena, my dad asleep three weeks old, the Night Watch, the date I wouldn't say, the nine times I'd lulled her and how each one had felt like tucking her in. I didn't leave out the ugly parts. Especially not the ugly parts.

She listened to all of it with her arms folded, and she didn't do a single joke, and when I ran out she was quiet for a long time. Then she said, in a voice I'd never heard her use:

"You're going to Venice."

"Tomorrow night. The train."

"With the ferry man. To learn how to do *more* of that." She nodded slowly, working it, the way Fenna works everything. "And you're going to leave me here. In a city that's turning into your nightmare. Knowing what I know now — which I can't un-know, thanks, because you finally told me the truth like I *begged* you to — knowing there are people who turn into shadows and want to use you, and one of them already knows my face because she watched me on a ferry, and you're going to get on a train and leave me on this exact street for them to find." She wiped her eyes, furious about it. "Do you have the *first idea,* Sam Vermeer, how stupid a plan that is."

I opened my mouth.

"I've already packed," said Fenna. "Obviously. I packed while you were in the kitchen. My mum thinks it's a school science trip to Italy and I've forged the letter and it's *very* convincing, I used the good printer." She hoisted a rucksack I hadn't even noticed onto one shoulder, and underneath the fury, just for a second, there was the old Fenna grin, the one that meant a plan was about to pay out. "I'm not your anchor to the ordinary world anymore. You broke that. So I'm coming to the *not*-ordinary one, where at least I'll know when you're lying, because I'll be watching your stupid face do it. Now come on. Your mum's making stroopwafels for the train and I'm not missing those for anybody."
