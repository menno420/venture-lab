# Chapter One — The Date

I told you, at the end of the last part, that I went down into the water one more time to read the date, and that I'd tell you the rest when I could tell it careful, and not out loud, and not yet.

Well. It's later now. And I've decided you've earned the date, because you stayed this long, and because you're going to need it to understand everything that happens in the drowning city, and because — I'll be honest with you, the way I promised — I am tired of carrying it by myself.

So here it is. I went down into the flooded square, and the Lantern Man came wading toward me one slow boot at a time, patient as tide, and the girl said *not yet* in my ear, going frantic at the end the way she always does — and this time, because I'd held the Night Watch shut and paid for it and rung myself out across every thin place in Europe, this time the letters held.

I looked up at the tower clock across the black water, the one that had never once kept a number for me in six years, and it went hard and true and certain the way it had gone the night I turned six. Four o'clock. Straight up. And then, under it, where a clock has no business having anything, more letters swam up out of the brass and stopped swimming and *stayed,* and spelled a day and a month and a year.

I'm not going to write it here. Not yet. I know, I *know,* I keep doing that, the girl's right about me. But there's a reason, and the reason is that a date written down is a date made a little bit more true, and I have spent a long time keeping this one as un-true as I can, and I'm not going to hand it to you casually on the first page like the specials board outside a café. You'll have it. When it costs me to give it to you, the way it cost me to read it, you'll have it, and not before.

Here's what I *will* tell you. It hasn't happened. It's a day that is still, as I write this, out ahead of us, coming, the way four o'clock comes for everyone who's still awake at ten to. My nightmare is not a memory. It never was. It's a *future,* and it has a date on it, and the girl who's spent six years whispering *not yet* was never soothing a scared kid. She was buying time. She was holding a door.

And in the water, in the future, in the flooded square with the paper crown and the one candle that won't go out — my father is there. I didn't see him. I want to be clear about that, because later on people made me say I saw him and I didn't. But I *knew* it, the way I know my own name, the way six-year-old me knew the face behind the fogged little window. The date isn't just a date. It's the day I get my father back, or the day I lose him for good, and the dream has never once let me tell which, and that not-telling is the cruelest thing anyone has ever done to me, and I still don't know who did it.

I woke up on my bedroom floor with the lantern burning warm on the sill and the green numbers ticking past four, and I was twelve years and one day old, and I knew the date, and I threw up.

---

That was the beginning of the end of Amsterdam for me. I want to walk you through it quickly, because the real story of this part happens somewhere else, in a city that is more mirror than stone, but you have to see why they took me out of my own city first, and what it cost to go.

It started coming true faster after that. All of it. Not just the lantern — the whole dream began leaking into the daylight like the seam over my life had worn through to a hole. I'd be cycling along the Prinsengracht in the ordinary morning and the water in the canal would go flat and black and *dead,* dead the way it's dead in the square, and for three whole seconds there'd be no trams, no birds, no accordion, no four hundred tourists — just the silence of the flooded square laid over my own street like a wet cloth, and then a delivery van would rattle over the bridge and it would all come back and I'd be shaking on my pedals.

Bleeds followed me now. Wessel said it was because I'd rung too loud at the Rijksmuseum — that holding the Night Watch shut was like standing up in the middle of a dark theatre and shouting your own name, and now everyone in the theatre knew exactly where I was sitting. The clocks around me stopped keeping numbers even when I was awake. A stranger in a long coat became two strangers, then three, standing at the ends of bridges, not-walking, patient. The 14 tram that doesn't run anymore ran past me four times in one week, and once it slowed, and the doors sighed open, and inside it was four o'clock in the morning and there was black water sloshing along the floor of the carriage, ankle-deep, and a paper crown floating in it, and I did not get on, and I have never in my life been so proud of not getting on a tram.

The worst of it happened at school, which is the part that finished Amsterdam for me. I was at my desk in the ordinary afternoon, Meester Boonstra droning about rivers, Fenna two rows over — and I looked up at the classroom clock to see how long till the bell, the way you do, and the clock wouldn't hold a number. The hands drifted. The little numbers swam like fish in a bowl. And then the daylight went out of the windows, all at once, not like a cloud, like a lid coming down, and the classroom filled up ankle-deep with cold black water that no one else could see, lapping silent round the legs of the desks, and a paper crown drifted past my chair, and across the room, where the door should have been, a warm point of light began, very slowly, to swing.

I made a sound. I must have. Because the next thing I knew Fenna had me by the arm out in the corridor — she'd got me out somehow, she was strong when she needed to be — and the water was gone and the light was gone and the daylight was back, and she was looking at me with her face doing the frown, the real one, and I could feel my own hand already reaching for the soft place in her to smooth it away, because that had become as automatic as breathing, and I hated it, and I did it anyway, and I watched my best friend's fear clear into nothing while black water dripped, actual water, off the cuffs of my jeans onto the school lino.

That's when I knew it too, before Wessel ever said it. That the city had become the dream. That there was no line left, for me, between the flooded square and the corridor outside my classroom, and that if I stayed I was going to walk into the water in front of everyone I loved and let the light come, because some tired part of me had started to *want* it to.

"They're not all coming to *take* you now," Wessel said, on the pont, at the grey cold start of a day. He'd stopped pretending the ferry was anything but our meeting place; he barely bothered loading bikes anymore, just stood at the rail with me in the thin dark water and talked low. "They know they can't just grab a snag that rang the way you rang and hold him. You're too loud, you'd bring the other side down on whoever had you. So now they *watch.* Both sides. They're waiting to see which way you'll break, so they can be standing there kind and ready when you do." He looked at me sideways, grim. "And you're breaking, kid. Look at you. You're grey to the lips at nine in the morning. This city's too thin for you now. Every canal's a mirror, every bridge is a threshold, and you've turned into a bell that won't stop ringing in a room made of glass."

"So what do I do," I said. My voice had gone small and flat. I'd been sleeping maybe two hours a night, in twenty-minute pieces, terrified of the water each time.

"You leave," Wessel said. "You go somewhere the Vigil can teach an arc-level dreamer without the whole neighbourhood catching fire. Somewhere the seam is *honest* about being thin, so you can learn to stand in it instead of drowning in it every time you close your eyes." He said the next part like it tasted of salt. "There's a place we send the ones who ring too loud for their own cities. A school, near enough. The oldest still-work we've got, older than the Begijnhof, in the one city in the world that was *built* on the line and never tried to pretend otherwise."

A gull screamed. The north bank slid closer.

"Venice," Wessel said. "We're taking you to Venice, kid. The mirror city. Where the seam never closes, and everybody's a little bit drowned, and nobody notices one more bell."

---

I want to tell you I felt something grand when he said it. A boy from a small canal being told he's going to Venice — you'd think there'd be wonder in it. There wasn't, that morning. There was just the cold, and the tiredness, and one clear thought that came up out of me before I could decide to say it, the way the true things always did.

"My dad," I said. "Did he ever go? To Venice. To the — school."

Wessel went still at the rail, the particular still that his face does when it's working hardest.

"Yeah," he said, at last, quietly. "Your dad trained in Venice. Long time ago. Before you, before your mum, back when he was ringing loud himself and they had to move him for the same reason we're moving you." He didn't look at me. "There's a woman there. Old even then. She taught your father to darn, and she taught his teacher, and she'll teach you, if she'll have you, which is not a thing I'd bet the boat on, because she and your dad didn't part friends, and I don't know the whole of why, and I've learned not to ask." He pushed off the rail. "So that's the other reason it's Venice. It's where the answers are, if there are any. It's where he *was.* You'll be walking the same wet stones he walked. Some people would find that a comfort."

"Do you?"

"No," said Wessel. "I find it a warning. But I'm a tired man on a boat, and you're the loudest snag in fifty years with your father's own reach in your hands, and it's not my comfort that matters." The pont bumped the far dock, that soft heavy shudder, the horn giving its flat honk. "Go home. Tell your mum tonight. All of it, this time." He looked at me, and there was something almost gentle under the grey. "She's earned the truth more than you think, that one. More than you know. Tell her, and see what she does. And then pack a bag."

I went home to tell my mum I was going to Venice, and to lie to her one more time about why — and my mum, who had asked me *did you sleep* every single morning of my life like the answer mattered more than she'd let on, was sitting at the kitchen table in the dark with the light off and a shoebox open in front of her, the one from under her bed, and she looked up at me with her tired real face, and she said, before I could get a single word out:

"You've read the date. Haven't you." And then, quieter, so quiet it was almost the flooded square: "Your father read it too. The week before he went under. He had that exact look you've got now — like he'd swallowed a clock." She pushed the shoebox an inch toward me across the table. "Sit down, Sam. It's my turn to tell *you* something for once. And then I'll help you pack."

# Chapter Two — Everyone Knew But Me

Here is the thing my mother told me at the kitchen table in the dark, and I'm going to give it to you the way she gave it to me, plain, because she'd earned plain and I'd wasted a lot of her patience being clever.

"I know what you are," she said. "I've known since you were six and woke up screaming with wet feet on a summer night, and I tucked you back in and told you the window was open, and it wasn't. I know what the lantern is. I know why the fourteen tram frightens you. I know a man turned into a shadow on the pont and I know you've been keeping it off me like it was a kindness." She turned the shoebox so the lid came all the way off. "You've been lying to me for a month, love, and it broke my heart a little every night, and I let you, because I did the exact same thing to your father, right up until the week he didn't wake up. So we're even. And now we stop."

I couldn't say anything. All the excuses I'd built up over four weeks — *she's tired, she wouldn't understand, it's mine* — went flat and grey and blew off the edge of me the way the Lull used to blow the truth off strangers.

"I'm not a dreamer," Anna said. "Not the way you mean. I'm a Sleeper, same as anyone. I go, I look, I forget. I married one, though. I married the loudest, kindest, most stubborn darner the Vigil had, and I knew *exactly* what I was marrying, and I'd do it again in a heartbeat even knowing the end of it." Her voice didn't shake. That was the frightening thing. She'd cried this cry years ago and there wasn't any left, just the fact of it, hard and true. "Your father didn't hide the world from me, Sam. That was the deal we made. He hid it from *you.* Both of us did. Because a snag that doesn't know it's a snag rings quieter, and we thought — we honestly thought — if we could just keep you ordinary until you were grown, keep the seal shut over you the way it shuts over every other child at six, then the loud ones would pass you by and you'd get to have a life with hagelslag in it and nothing worse than maths." She almost smiled. "The seal didn't take. We knew that the night you turned six. We knew it and we decided to pretend anyway, for as long as we could get away with it. That was the lie. The window was always the lie. I'm sorry."

And then she reached into the shoebox — the one I'd thought was full of holiday snaps, the one with the little orange dates printed in the corner — and she started taking my father out of it and setting him on the table between us.

---

A brass shackle-key, notched and cold, exactly like the one Wessel had made me pull off the ferry's chain locker. "He carried this his whole life. Said the first thing he ever pulled across the line was a key, and the last thing he'd pull would be one too, because a key's the smallest true thing that *opens* something." She set it down. "It's yours now."

A photograph, old, square, the colours gone warm and orange the way they go — a much younger Joris, laughing, squinting into hard bright Italian sun, standing on a stone quay with green water behind him and a city of leaning towers and domes rising up out of it like something dreamed. He had his arm around a woman. She was old even then, tiny and fierce, with white hair scraped back and a pair of small round spectacles catching the sun, and she was not laughing; she was looking at the camera the way you look at a rope you're not sure will hold.

"Venice," my mother said. "The year before I met him. He talked about it his whole life — the one place, he said, where the world stops lying about what it is. He loved it and it frightened him and he never once told me the whole of why he left, only that he had, and that he could never go back, and that the woman —" she touched the old fierce face in the photo " — Serafina. He said her name maybe three times in fifteen years and every time it was like watching him touch a bruise. She taught him everything. And something happened, at the end, that he wouldn't put words to. So." Anna looked up at me. "If they're taking you to Venice, they're taking you to *her.* Which means you're going to learn the thing your father learned, in the room your father learned it, from the person your father couldn't forgive. And I can't stop that, and I'm not sure I'd want to, because the answers about him are down there and you've a right to them." She pushed the photograph across to me next to the key. "But I want you to carry this, and I want you to remember, whatever she tells you about him, that I *knew* the man, all the way to the end, and he was good. Tired, and secret, and gone too soon. But good. Don't let anyone in a mirror tell you different."

I put the photo in my inside pocket, next to the other one, the three-week-old one of him asleep. Two pictures of my father now, and not one living memory of his face — the backwash had taken that at the Rijksmuseum — and I sat in my mother's dark kitchen navigating by photographs toward a city made of mirrors, and if that isn't the whole of what this book is about then I've told it wrong.

"Look at me a second," my mum said, and I did, and she took my chin in her hand the way you do with a little kid, tired and gentle, and she looked at my face for a long time — really looked, the way you look at something you're trying to memorise — and I didn't understand why then, and I understand it too well now, and I can't write about it without stopping for a bit. "You've got so much of him in your face," she said. "It used to hurt to look at you, when he first went. Now it's the only place I get to see him." She let go. "Whatever you find down there — whoever that old woman is to him — you come home to me, Sam Vermeer. That's the deal. Your father went south to a mirror city and came back not-quite, and then he went under, and I have spent four years being the person who stayed. I'm good at it. I'm the best at it there is. But I would very much like, for once in this family, for somebody to *come back all the way.* Do you hear me? You go, you learn, you come home whole. You come home *knowing my face.*" She said it as a joke — she meant it as a joke — and I laughed, and neither of us knew it was a prophecy, and I have never forgiven the world for letting her say it.

"You'll let me go," I said. It wasn't quite a question.

"I'll let you go," said my mum, "because the other choice is you stay here and drown in your own street, and I have already lost one of you to that water. I am not doing it twice." She stood up and put the kettle on, ordinary as anything, which is how you know a Dutch conversation is truly over. "Now. There's the matter of Fenna."

---

The matter of Fenna was that Fenna had stopped believing me.

I'd been lulling her. I told you about the first time, in the hall, when I reached into the soft place in my best friend and handed her a boring story because it was easier than the truth — and I told you it was the single worst thing I'd done, and it was, and I did it again. And again. Every time she got the frown, the real *something's-wrong-with-Sam* frown, I smoothed it off her, gentle as tucking her in, and each time it was a little easier and I hated myself a little less, which is exactly the wrong direction and exactly how it gets you.

But here's what nobody had told me, because nobody knew it, because it had maybe never happened before to anyone. You cannot lull the same person over and over and have it keep working. The Lull is the waking cousin of dream-forgetting, and forgetting is a muscle, and if you make somebody's mind reach for the same boring story a dozen times it stops being restful and starts being a *rut,* a worn groove the mind can feel itself sliding into — and Fenna, who is the most stubborn person I have ever known, who punched Ruben in the second week and has never let me forget I owe her everything, felt the groove. And planted her feet. And climbed out.

She was waiting on my stairs when I came up from the kitchen, ordinary Fenna in the ordinary hall, except her arms were folded and her jaw was set and she was looking at me the way you look at a lock you've finally found the key to.

"Don't," she said.

"Don't what?"

"Don't do the *thing.*" She stood up. "The thing where I get scared for you and then I'm suddenly not and I can't remember why and I say something about peanut sauce. I've been keeping a *notebook,* Sam. Every time it happens I write it down the second I feel it going, before it goes, and then I read the notebook back later when you're not around to smooth it, and do you know how many times it's happened?" Her voice cracked, right down the middle, and it was worse than shouting. "*Nine.* Nine times my best friend has reached into my head and turned me off like a tap because it was easier than trusting me. I felt it every time by the end. I feel it right now, you're doing it right now, you've got the — stop it, Sam, I can see your *face* doing it —"

And I was. I'd reached for the soft place in her without deciding to, tired and cornered and terrified of the one conversation I'd been avoiding for a month, my hand already halfway to tucking her in.

I stopped.

It was the hardest small thing I have ever done. I want that on the record, because holding the Night Watch shut cost me my father's face and this cost me nothing anyone could measure and it was *harder,* because there was no monster, no fire, no Lena's warm voice — there was just my best friend standing on the stairs asking me to do the difficult true thing instead of the easy kind one, and everything in my hands wanting to be kind.

I took my hand back. I sat down on the stairs. And I told Fenna the truth. All of it. The water, the lantern, Wessel, Lena, my dad asleep three weeks old, the Night Watch, the date I wouldn't say, the nine times I'd lulled her and how each one had felt like tucking her in. I didn't leave out the ugly parts. Especially not the ugly parts.

She listened to all of it with her arms folded, and she didn't do a single joke, and when I ran out she was quiet for a long time. Then she said, in a voice I'd never heard her use:

"You're going to Venice."

"Tomorrow night. The train."

"With the ferry man. To learn how to do *more* of that." She nodded slowly, working it, the way Fenna works everything. "And you're going to leave me here. In a city that's turning into your nightmare. Knowing what I know now — which I can't un-know, thanks, because you finally told me the truth like I *begged* you to — knowing there are people who turn into shadows and want to use you, and one of them already knows my face because she watched me on a ferry, and you're going to get on a train and leave me on this exact street for them to find." She wiped her eyes, furious about it. "Do you have the *first idea,* Sam Vermeer, how stupid a plan that is."

I opened my mouth.

"I've already packed," said Fenna. "Obviously. I packed while you were in the kitchen. My mum thinks it's a school science trip to Italy and I've forged the letter and it's *very* convincing, I used the good printer." She hoisted a rucksack I hadn't even noticed onto one shoulder, and underneath the fury, just for a second, there was the old Fenna grin, the one that meant a plan was about to pay out. "I'm not your anchor to the ordinary world anymore. You broke that. So I'm coming to the *not*-ordinary one, where at least I'll know when you're lying, because I'll be watching your stupid face do it. Now come on. Your mum's making stroopwafels for the train and I'm not missing those for anybody."

# Chapter Three — The Mirror City

The Vigil don't fly. Wessel told me why on the platform at Amsterdam Centraal, at the dead hour, waiting for a train that took the long way south through the dark: "You go up in a plane, you cross six seams in an hour at a height where the air's already half a dream, and you land somewhere your body doesn't believe it is. Sleepers do it fine, the Lull tidies it up for them. A snag like you'd arrive scrambled, half in and half out, ringing like a dropped bell. We take the train. The train stays on the ground and keeps the record of where you've been." He'd handed me and Fenna each a paper bag of my mum's stroopwafels, still warm, and said, "Sleep if you can. But mind — you'll be sleeping on the move now, over a lot of thin ground, so keep your tells to hand and don't go anywhere you can't walk back from."

I slept, and I dreamed, and it wasn't the flooded square. That was the first strange mercy of the journey. It was as if, the moment the train started rolling south, something eased its grip — the closer we got to the mirror city, the quieter the bell of me rang, until for the first time in a month I slept a whole clean hour without waking, and dreamed only ordinary rubbish, cheese and worry and a maths test with a shark in it. Wessel watched me sleep with a face I couldn't read and, when I woke somewhere in the Alps with the dawn coming pink off the snow, he said only: "See. Told you. A bell rings loud in a quiet room. Not so loud in a city that's *all* bells."

Fenna spent the whole journey with her nose to the glass and her notebook out, writing everything down, refusing to be smoothed by the tiredness or the wonder or anything else. That was her job now, she'd decided, somewhere over Switzerland: she was the one who wrote it down while it was true, before the Lull could get to it. "Photographs slip and minds slip," she said, when I asked, "but I'm a Sleeper who *knows,* which the ferry man says is rare, and a notebook doesn't reach for a boring story. So I'm the record. You do the magic, I'll keep it true." She said it lightly, but I saw Wessel's head come up when she said *keep it true,* saw him look at this small furious Dutch girl who'd invented the entire Vigil creed on a train because it was obviously the sensible thing to do, and I saw him decide something about her that I wouldn't understand the size of until much later.

---

You come into Venice over the water.

That's the thing nobody prepares you for. The train runs out across a long low bridge over a wide flat lagoon, and the mainland falls away behind you, and for a few minutes you are crossing open water on a thread of stone with nothing on either side but grey-green shallows and the sky doubled underneath them — and then the city rises up out of the sea ahead of you exactly the way it rose up out of the sea in my father's orange photograph, towers and domes and leaning brick, a whole impossible city standing in water that should have swallowed it a thousand years ago.

And I felt the seam.

I'd felt thin places before — the pont, the still-room, the dark museum. This wasn't a place. This was a whole *city* of it. The moment the train came off the mainland and out over the lagoon, the world went soft around me, soft the way it goes right before a working, soft all the way to the edges of everything — and it did not go hard again. It just stayed. The give never came out of it. Wessel had said the seam in Venice never closes, and I'd taken it for a figure of speech, and it wasn't. Venice is built on the line itself. It floats on the exact border between real and fake, and it has for fifteen hundred years, and it has stopped pretending otherwise the way a very old person stops pretending they're not tired.

"Watch the water," Wessel murmured, as we came into the station and out onto the steps above the Grand Canal, into the hard bright southern light and the noise and the boats. "It's the tell that never turns off here. Watch the reflections."

I watched the water.

Every canal in Amsterdam holds the street upside-down, clean and true — I'd grown up on that, the pigeon walking along its own reflection. Here the reflections were *late.* Only by a half-second, only just, but once you saw it you couldn't stop: a gull would bank over the canal and its reflection would bank a heartbeat behind it; a woman would raise her hand to shade her eyes and the woman in the water would raise hers a moment after; the whole doubled city lagged its real self by the length of a held breath, as if the mirror underneath Venice were tired too, and couldn't quite keep up, and didn't care who noticed.

"The seam's so thin here the reflection can't decide which side it's on," Wessel said. "That's your one true tell in this city, and it's a strange one, so learn it now: everywhere else, a clock that *holds* means you're awake. Here, a reflection that's *late* means you're awake. When the water starts keeping perfect time with the world —" he looked at me hard " — when the gull and its reflection move together, clean, no lag — that's when you check the rest of your tells fast, because something's smoothing the seam, and in Venice a smooth seam is a trap being set. A true mirror is the most dangerous thing in this whole city. Remember that. It's going to matter more than anything else I teach you."

I did not know yet how much. I filed it, the way you file the important things you don't understand, and I looked at the late reflections and the leaning towers and the green water full of boats, and a boy about my age in a stripy shirt sculled up to the canal steps in a little battered black boat and shouted up at us, cheerful as anything, in an accent I could have cut with a knife:

"You are the Dutch bell! *Madonna,* you are loud even asleep, I hear you all the way from the mainland, you woke my nonna's dog. Get in, get in, before you ring the whole *sestiere* — I am Nico, I take you to Serafina, mind the — no, the other foot, you are going to — *ecco,* there, in you fall, welcome to Venice, everybody falls in the first day, it means the city likes you."

---

Fenna, for the record, did not fall in. Fenna stepped into the little boat like she'd been getting into boats her whole life, sat down in the stern, took out her notebook, and wrote — I read it later — *Venice. Reflections are half a second late. Sam has gone the colour he goes. Boy called Nico, talks like a tap left on. The whole city is thin. Note: do not trust any mirror that agrees with the world.* She'd been in the city four minutes. She was already keeping the record better than I was.

Nico took us the back way, which in Venice means the water way, down canals so narrow the boat's shoulders nearly touched the brick on both sides, under bridges where you had to duck, past doorways that opened straight onto the water with their bottom steps green and furred and vanishing down into the dark. He talked the entire time — Nico, I would learn, is physically incapable of a silent minute — and half of what he said was tour guide and half of it was Vigil, mixed together so you couldn't always tell which.

"That church, drowned. Floor's a metre under the paving now, they said Mass there for eight hundred years and now the eels have it. — That palace, thin, don't sleep on that side of the boat. — That corner, a *specchiaio* died there, a mirror-maker, hundred years back, and his mirrors still show you Tuesday when it's Thursday, we keep them covered. — You see how the water is up on the steps today? *Acqua alta.* High water. It comes higher every year, everybody says the sea, the sea is rising, and they are right, but they are also, how do you say — they have the little frown, and then they have the little smile, and they decide it is only the sea." He looked back at me over his shoulder, and for just a second the cheerful tour-guide dropped clean off him and something much older and much more tired looked out, the same look I'd seen on Serafina's face in the photograph. "It is not only the sea, Dutch bell. Venice is sinking because Venice is *bleeding,* and it has been bleeding from the same old wound for four hundred years, and the person who is going to teach you to darn is the same person who has spent her whole life darning that one wound and losing, one centimetre a year, forever." He turned the boat into a shadow so deep and so still the reflections stopped lagging and simply stopped, black glass, dead flat, silent as the flooded square, and my whole body went cold. "Here. This is her water. Get out quiet. And whatever she says to you when she sees your face —" he steadied the boat against a green stone step below a plain shut door " — remember it is not you she is angry at. It is who you look like."

The door opened before I touched it. And a tiny fierce old woman with white hair scraped back and small round spectacles stood in it, exactly as she'd stood in my father's photograph forty years ago, and she looked at me — at my face, at the face I have that I'm told is my father's face — and every bit of colour went out of her, and she put one hand flat against the doorframe as if the city had tilted, and she said, in a whisper that carried across the dead-flat water like a stone dropped in it:

"*Joris.* No. No, you're the boy. You're his *boy.*" And then, with a grief and a fury so tangled I couldn't tell where one ended: "He sent me his *child.* After everything. He sent me his child to finish it."

# Chapter Four — Anchoring

Serafina didn't send me away, though she stood in that doorway a long time deciding to.

What changed her mind was Wessel, who said nothing clever at all — he just took my father's brass shackle-key out of my hand, without asking, and held it up so the old woman could see it in the dead-flat light off the black water. She looked at the key, and something moved in her face that was older than the anger, and she said a word in Venetian I didn't know, soft, the way you say a name at a graveside, and she stood back from the door.

"He kept it," she said. Not to me. To the key. "Forty years and he kept the key." Then she looked at me, and the fierceness came back down over the grief like a shutter. "Come in, then, boy. Bring the loud girl and the tired ferryman. And wipe your feet — I have spent sixty years keeping the water out of this room and I'll not have a Dutchman track the lagoon across my floor."

---

Her workshop was the strangest room I have ever stood in, and I have stood in a painting.

It was a *specchieria* — a mirror-maker's workshop, the last real one in Venice, she told us, the craft nearly dead now, kept alive by exactly one furious old woman and the boy who rowed her groceries. And it was full of mirrors. Hundreds of them, of every age and size, hung and stacked and leaned along every wall, some silvered bright and some gone black and cloudy with the centuries, some no bigger than a coin and one so tall it went from the floor to the dark beams of the ceiling. Every one of them was covered. Sheets, cloths, sheets of brown paper, old velvet gone bald — every mirror in the room had something draped over it, so that walking in was like walking into a room full of standing ghosts, all facing you, all hooded.

"You feel it," Serafina said, watching me. It wasn't a question; she said it the way Wessel did. "This is a still-work, boy, but not the kind your ferryman keeps. In the north you darn the seam shut — you stitch it tight as a drum and nothing bleeds, and you call that safe." Her mouth went thin. "You cannot do that in Venice. The seam here is too old and too thin and too tired; stitch it shut in one place and it tears open in three others, like mending a rotten sail. So we do not close it. We *manage* it. A mirror is a piece of tamed seam — a little doorway that we keep, and cover, and open only on purpose, so that all the thinness of the city can be gathered up and held in the palm of your hand instead of running loose in the streets." She touched the nearest hooded frame, gently, the way you'd touch a sleeping animal. "Every mirror in this room is a place the seam would otherwise be. I keep them covered because an uncovered mirror in Venice does not always show you the room you are standing in. Sometimes it shows you *when* you are standing in it. Sometimes it shows you somewhere else entirely." She turned. "And that, boy, is the thing your father could do that no one else could, and it is the thing that is going to get you killed, and it is why I am going to teach you Anchoring before I teach you anything else. Because a dreamer who can open doors and cannot hold anything shut is a door propped open in a house full of thieves."

---

Anchoring, it turns out, is the opposite of pulling, and harder, and I want to try to explain it the way Serafina explained it to me over the next three days, because you'll need to understand it for what comes after.

When you *pull,* Wessel had taught me, you hold a small thing whole in your mind and want it across the seam into your hand. It arrives, and it's real, and it stays because it's small enough that the world just accepts it — a coin, a key, a knife.

Anchoring is for the big things. And the trouble with a big thing isn't holding it whole — Serafina said I had more reach for that than anyone she'd trained, my father included, which she did not say as a compliment — the trouble is that a big thing pulled across the line *doesn't want to stay.* Reality notices. Reality pushes back. Pull a marble table out of a painting, the way I'd watched Lena do it, and the world will spend the next hour quietly trying to un-happen it, to smooth it back out of existence, because it doesn't belong. So Anchoring isn't the pull. Anchoring is what you do *after* the pull. It's *pinning.* It's holding the pulled thing so hard and so whole, and driving its reality down into the waking world so deep, that when you finally let go, the world has given up arguing and simply accepts that the thing was always there.

"Pull is a question," Serafina said. "*May I have this.* Anchor is a sentence. *This is here now.* And the difference between them is everything, boy, because a sentence cannot be taken back."

She made me start small. A brass candlestick from across the room, pulled into my hand — easy, I'd done heavier off the ferry. Then: *keep it.* Don't just hold it — pin it. Push it down into now so hard that I could open my hand and it would stay warm and solid and real on my palm without my mind holding it there at all. The first six times it went cold and thin the instant I let go, and faded, and was gone, back to wherever candlesticks go. The seventh time I felt it — felt the moment the world stopped pushing back, felt the thing go from *borrowed* to *belonging,* a soft heavy click deep under everything, like a key turning in a lock four rooms away.

I opened my hand. The candlestick sat there, real, mine, permanent.

And every covered mirror in the room shivered.

Not much. A ripple, a flinch, all of them at once under their cloths, like a room full of hooded people all flinching at the same small sound. Serafina's head came up sharp. Fenna, who had been writing it all down in her notebook, stopped writing.

"That," Serafina said quietly, "is the thing I was afraid of." She came and took the candlestick out of my hand and turned it over, frowning at it. "You anchored it. Good. Clean, even — cleaner than your father at your age, God rot him. But you anchored it *too well.* You didn't pin one candlestick into now, boy. You pinned it into now so hard the whole seam felt it — every mirror in the room, every thin place in the *sestiere,* probably every darner between here and the mainland. When you drive a nail, you drive it into one board. You just drove a nail into the whole house at once, and the whole house rang." She set the candlestick down, very carefully, as if it might go off. "You have your father's reach and none of his fear, which is exactly the wrong way round. He was frightened of what he could do. You are not frightened of anything yet. We are going to fix that, and I am sorry, because fixing it is going to hurt."

---

She was right that it hurt, and she was right that it took days, and I'll give you the shape of them because the days matter — because a thing you learn slow, one candlestick at a time, is a thing you own, and I was about to be very glad I owned it.

She'd set a thing on the far bench each morning and make me pin it into now and hold it and let it go, over and over, all morning, and every time I anchored too hard she'd rap my knuckles with a mirror-maker's little steel rule — not for the hurt, she said, but so my hands would learn to be afraid before my head was, "because your head is a Vermeer and it will never be afraid in time." I anchored a spoon. I anchored a candle, lit, and held the flame steady across the seam, which is harder than it sounds because a flame doesn't want to belong anywhere. I anchored a cup of water without spilling a drop, which took two whole days and taught me more than anything, because water, Serafina said, is the true test — "it remembers it was somewhere else, water, it is the most homesick thing there is, it will always try to run back across the line, and if you can pin a cup of water into now and make it stay, boy, then one day, God help you, you might pin a *flood.*"

I didn't understand why she looked so grim when she said *flood.* I did, later. I understood it up to my chest in black water four hundred years deep. But that's ahead.

In the afternoons, when my head was wrung out and Serafina had no more patience, Nico took us out. And I want to put Nico properly in the record here, because he saved my life twice before this part is over and he never once made it about that. He was a Vigil kid — a young Waker, born to it, his whole family ferry-folk and glass-folk going back to the doges — and he treated the entire impossible secret war as if it were a slightly annoying family business he'd been born into, like a bakery. "My nonna darns the Rialto, my uncle darns the fish market, I row the groceries and the loud foreign bells," he said, cheerfully, sculling us under a bridge so low we lay flat in the boat. "One day I darn something important. For now — you see that? Third window, blue shutter? Thin. Somebody dreamed too hard in that room a hundred years ago and it never healed. We do not knock on that door. Now, gelato."

Fenna took to Venice the way she took to everything, which is to say she catalogued it. She filled a notebook and started a second. She learned the vaporetto map before I did and corrected Nico on his own canals twice, which he loved. And on the third evening, sitting on the workshop steps with the acqua alta lapping our feet and gelato going soft in the heat, she said, not looking at me, "I feel it now. The thin. Since the ferry, since you kept — since you stopped smoothing me. It's like a draught under a door. I can feel where the cold comes in." She licked her spoon. "Is that what it's like for you all the time?"

"All the time," I said. "It doesn't switch off."

"Huh." She thought about it, the way Fenna thinks about everything, all the way to the bottom. "That's really lonely."

"Yeah," I said.

"Well," said Fenna, "it isn't now. I'm here. Eat your gelato, it's melting, you're the worst." And that was the whole of it, and it was enough, and I have never told her how much.

---

That night — my first quiet night in the mirror city, in a narrow high room over the black water with Fenna asleep across the hall and Nico snoring somewhere below and Wessel gone back north on the night train "before the city gets a taste for me too" — I couldn't sleep, because for the first time in a month I *wasn't* afraid to. The bell of me was quiet here. The flooded square felt far away. And I lay awake in the strange thin dark listening to the water lap the steps below my window and I thought, with a kind of guilty lightness, *maybe I could just be here. Maybe I could learn to darn and stay quiet and let the date come and go somewhere else. Maybe.*

And then the biggest mirror in the room next door — the one that went floor to ceiling, the one Serafina had covered with three separate cloths and turned to the wall besides — the big mirror uncovered itself.

I heard the cloths slide off, one, two, three, soft as breath in the dark. I got up. I shouldn't have. I went to the door of my room and looked across the hall into the workshop, and the great mirror had turned itself around to face me, and it was not showing me the dark workshop or my own pale face in the doorway.

It was showing me a street. A different street, somewhere I'd never been, narrow buildings of pale stone leaning close together, and the paving of the street was thick with something grey and soft that fell out of the sky like snow that wasn't snow — and above the leaning buildings hung a sky that wasn't night and wasn't day, a deep bruised purple-yellow, the exact colour of the place under your eye three days after you walk into a door.

I knew it. I had seen it for one heartbeat six years ago, upside-down in black water, when the Lantern Man lowered his light. It was the place under the water. It was the somewhere the diver kept his places. It was the *inside* of my nightmare, and it was standing in Serafina's mirror in the middle of the night, waiting, and on the far side of the falling grey, small and dark, a warm point of light began, very slowly, to swing.

Behind me, from nowhere, close to my ear, calm and almost kind, a girl's voice said: "*Not yet.*" And Serafina came out of the dark in her nightdress with a face like a slammed door and threw all three cloths back over the glass at once and said, "*You do not look in that mirror,* boy — not that one, not ever, not until I say," and I said, "I know that place, I've seen that place, what *is* it," and the old woman went so white and so still that I had my answer before she gave me the lie:

"It's nothing," Serafina said. "It's a dead city. It's four hundred years of my grief and it is *none of yours.* Go back to bed."

# Chapter Five — The Warm Voice Returns

I want to tell you what I did with what I'd seen, because it matters, and because it's the sort of small quiet choice that this whole part turns on.

I didn't believe her.

Not the whole of it. I believed Serafina was grieving something four hundred years old — you don't go that white over nothing. But I'd stood in front of that mirror and looked at that street, and I'd grown up on Amsterdam brick and I'd just spent three days learning Venice, and I knew stone when I saw it, and *that street was neither.* Those were not northern canal-houses and they were not leaning Venetian palazzi. Pale stone, low, close, foreign, under a sky the colour of a bruise, with grey falling out of it like the ash off a struck match — that was nowhere I had a name for, and it was certainly not a drowned city in a lagoon. Whatever Serafina thought her forbidden mirror had shown me, it had shown me something else. Something that was *mine.* The inside of my nightmare. The place under the water where the diver keeps his places.

So I did the thing you do when a grown-up you half-trust tells you a lie to protect you: I said *yes, all right,* and I went back to bed, and I kept it, the way I keep everything, filed and cold and mine, and I decided I would find out what that place was without her, because I was twelve and I had learned exactly the wrong lesson from Book One, which is that the truth is worth any price, including the price of not being told it kindly.

I want you to notice that. It's going to cost somebody. It always does, when I decide a thing is mine to find out.

---

The days settled into a shape. Mornings, Serafina taught me Anchoring in the workshop, and I got better, and quieter, and learned to drive a nail into one board instead of the whole house — to pin a thing into now so gently that only the mirror nearest it flinched. She was a hard teacher and a fair one and never once warm, and slowly, in the spaces between the lessons, I got pieces of my father out of her the way you get a splinter out, a little at a time, sideways.

Joris had been her best student in fifty years. Joris could open the mirrors — could look into the seam and see not just *when* but *where,* could stand in front of the great covered glass and read the frozen moments the way I'd read the lean of a chocolate shop. Joris had wanted to use it. That was the fight, I gathered, though never the whole of it: her student had wanted to reach into a frozen catastrophe and *change* it, for the best of reasons, and she had forbidden it, for reasons she wouldn't name, and he had done it anyway, once, and something had gone wrong that she carried like a stone in her chest, and he had left Venice and never come back and gone north and met my mother and had me and gone under, and now here I was, his face, in her workshop, learning his craft, and she could barely look at me some mornings.

Afternoons, Nico took me and Fenna out into the city — "so you learn where the thin is before you fall in it," he said, which was the Vigil reason, though mostly I think Nico just liked showing off his city. He took us to the drowned church where the eels lived. He took us to the Bridge of Sighs and told us the true version, which is sadder than the tourists' one. He took us up a bell-tower and out to the glass furnaces on Murano where the whole island roars with fire day and night, "the one place in Venice the seam is *thick,*" he said, "because you cannot dream near a thing that hot — that is why the mirror-makers came here, five hundred years ago, to make their glass where the seam couldn't spoil it, and then they carried the mirrors *back* into the thin, which is a joke, if you think about it, only nobody is laughing anymore."

And Fenna wrote all of it down, and caught me twice a day trying to steer Nico toward pale stone and bruised skies, and said nothing, and wrote *that* down too.

She cornered me about it on the fifth night, because of course she did. "You keep asking Nico about *dead cities,*" she said, in the dark of the room we shared a wall through, her voice coming low under the door. "You did it at the fish market. You did it up the bell-tower. You're hunting the mirror. The one she covered." A pause. "Sam. She's frightened of that mirror, and she is not a woman who frightens easy, and you are about to do the exact thing you always do, which is decide a secret is yours to dig up because nobody handed it to you nicely." Another pause, longer. "I'm not going to stop you. I gave up stopping you on the stairs in Amsterdam. But I'm writing down that I *told* you, tonight, with the date and everything, so that when it goes wrong — and it's going to go wrong, I can feel it, it's got that feeling — you can't tell yourself nobody warned you. That's my job now. I keep the record. Even the record of you being an idiot. *Especially* that one. Goodnight."

She was right, of course. She was right the way she is always right, which is right about the world as it usually works — and about me, which is worse. I lay awake and I did not stop hunting the mirror. I want that on the record too, next to her warning, because I've promised you the ugly parts and this is one: I was warned, plainly, by the person who loved me best, and I went looking anyway, and what I found is what nearly drowned her. Remember that when we get to the water.

---

She found me on the fourth afternoon, which I should have expected, because the whole city is a mirror and I'd been ringing my quiet bell in it for four days.

I was alone for once — Fenna gone with Nico to buy the bread, me sitting on a green stone step at the edge of a small silent campo where the acqua alta had come up and lay flat and black and still across the paving, exactly, *exactly* like the flooded square, so that I'd sat down without deciding to and gone somewhere in my head — and a voice I'd have known anywhere, warm and delighted and full of terrible affection, said from behind me:

"There you are. God, you've grown up in a month. That's the water does it. This city ages you a year a week — why do you think I love it?"

Lena Marchetti sat down next to me on the green step, in a coat the colour of wet slate, in the hard bright light, and she was exactly as impossible-to-stop-looking-at as she'd been at the school railings, and my whole body went tight, and she saw it, and she laughed, sad and soft.

"Peace," she said, and held her empty hands up. "I'm not here to take you. I couldn't if I wanted to — you'd bring the whole lagoon down on me, you ring like a cathedral now, it's *magnificent.* And besides." Something crossed her face, quick, gone. "It's complicated, what I want, these days. It got complicated the night you held the Night Watch shut and chose the tired poor ones with the folded arms. You made me look at some things I'd rather not have." She turned to me, and the delight went out of her, and what was left was almost — I still don't fully believe it, and I was *there* — almost honest. "I came to warn you, Sam. Because nobody else will, and because I owe you one true thing, the way your ferryman owed you one on the boat, and I pay my debts, which is more than your side does."

"You don't have a side anymore?"

"I have *my* side," said Lena. "I always did. That's the difference between me and the First Draft, and it's why they don't quite trust me, and it's why I'm sitting on a wet step warning a boy I'm supposed to be delivering." She looked out over the flat black water. "Here's your one true thing. The Vigil are lying to you. Your fierce old teacher, your tired ferryman, the whole watch — they've built their entire faith on *keep the record true,* and they let you hold a painting shut and paid your father's face for it, and the whole time they have been sitting on a forgery so big it is *drowning this city,* and they have never once told you, because if they told you, you might ask the only question that matters, which is: *if it was right for them, why was it wrong for me?*"

I didn't say anything. Something cold was climbing up my legs that had nothing to do with the water.

"Four hundred years ago," Lena said, quietly, "this city was dying. Plague. A third of them, in eighteen months — the bells never stopped, the boats went out full of the dead every dawn. And your Vigil — the darners, the keepers of the true record, the arms-folded *no* — your Vigil looked at their drowning saints and their dying children and they did the thing they hang us for. They reached back into a frozen moment and they *forged* it. They changed the conditions. They let the city choose to live when the true record says it should have fallen. And it worked. The plague broke. Venice was saved, and they built a great white church on the water to say thank you to God for a mercy *they* smuggled in through the seam — and they have been darning the bleed of that one beautiful lie ever since, four hundred years, one centimetre of rising water a year, losing, forever, because there is no clean edit, Sam, not even for saints. *Especially* not for saints."

She stood, and brushed the water off her slate coat, and looked down at me with an expression I couldn't read at all.

"They forged a moment for the greater good," Lena said, "and called it a miracle. I forged a chocolate shop for a dead family and called it mercy. And you held a painting shut and called it true. Three edits, Sam. Ask your teacher which one of us she thinks is going to Hell." She started away across the campo, then stopped, and said, without turning round, in a voice that had gone very quiet and not warm at all: "They're going to make you choose again, down here. Both of us are. Only this time it won't be forty dead men in a painting. This time it'll be somebody you brought with you. So learn fast, little bell. And when your teacher finally tells you what's in the mirror she won't let you look at — because she will, at the end, they always do — remember who warned you first."

And she stepped sideways into the shadow of the church and thinned and was gone, and the flat black water of the flooded campo, which had lagged its reflections all week the way all Venice does, went for one moment perfectly, horribly *smooth* — the church, the sky, the light, all doubled clean and true with no lag at all — and every tell I had went off at once, and I did not know, I still to this day do not know, whether the whole conversation had happened on the waking side of the seam or the other.

# Chapter Six — The White Church

I went to Serafina that night and I asked her straight, because I'd learned exactly nothing from Book One about how the truth lands, and I asked her the way you throw a stone at a window to see if anyone's home.

"Is it true the Vigil forged a moment to stop the plague. Four hundred years ago. Is that your grief in the mirror. Is that why the water keeps coming up."

She was cleaning a mirror when I said it — one of the small ones, a coin-sized oval gone black — and her hands went still on the cloth, and she did not turn round, and for a long time the only sound was the acqua alta lapping the bottom step of the workshop door, an inch higher than it had been that morning.

"Who told you," she said. Very quiet.

"Does it matter?"

"It matters *enormously,* boy, but no, I don't suppose you'll say." She set the mirror down. When she turned round her face had gone old in a way I hadn't seen, older than fierce, old like the drowned church. "Sit. You've been listening to something with a lovely voice, and half of what it told you will be true, which is the most dangerous kind of lie there is, and I'll not have you carrying the half-true version. If you must have it, have it whole. It's mine to give and I've earned the giving of it, God knows."

---

She told me, and I'll give it to you the way she gave it to me, because it's the wound this whole part turns on, and because I've come to think the only honest thing you can do with a wound is show it.

Four hundred years ago the plague came to Venice, and it did not stop. A third of the city in eighteen months. The bells rang for the dead until the ringers themselves were dead. And the Vigil of Venice — mirror-makers, night-workers, keepers of the true record, arms folded over what happened saying *no* — the Vigil of Venice watched their city die and they broke.

"There was a night," Serafina said, "the worst night, when the doge's own physicians gave the city up for lost, and the Vigil master of that time — my own blood, seven grandmothers back, I have her name in a book I can't read without weeping — she gathered the darners into this room, and she said: *we keep the record true, and the true record is that Venice ends. I will not keep it. I will forge it. And I will carry the sin of it to God myself and let Him judge me, but the children will live.*" The old woman's voice was perfectly steady, which was the terrible part. "And they went into the frozen moment of that night, all of them together, the way you go into a painting, and they changed the conditions. Not one great lie — a hundred small ones. A ship turned back at the mouth of the lagoon. A well left uncontaminated. A quarantine begun a week that it was not begun. A hundred nudges, stacked, until the true night where Venice fell was scraped away and a night where Venice lived was written over it. And it *held.* The plague broke. The city rose from its own deathbed. And they built the great white church out on the point — the Salute, *salute,* it means *health,* it means *salvation* — to thank God for a mercy they had stolen through a mirror, and they never told a living soul, and the water has been coming up one centimetre a year for four hundred years ever since, because —" she spread her hands " — because there is no clean edit. Even for saints. Even to save a city. The bleed of that one beautiful forgery never healed, and it never will, and it is drowning us slow, and every mirror in this room is me and mine trying to darn a wound the size of Venice with a needle, one night at a time, forever, and *losing.*"

The workshop was very quiet. A mirror flinched somewhere, softly, in the dark.

"I'll tell you the part that I've never told anyone," Serafina said, and her voice dropped, "because you should know exactly what a forgery for the greater good actually looks like, up close, so you're never fool enough to think it's clean. When they scraped the true night away — the night the city fell — they didn't scrape a number. They scraped *people.* There were ten thousand who died in the true version and lived in the forged one, and that's the mercy, that's the miracle, that's the white church. But a forgery is a scraping, boy, and the old text always shows through — and every mirror-maker in my line, for four hundred years, has been able to *see them.* The scraped ones. The people who died in the true Venice and don't know they were ever dead. On the worst nights, in the oldest glass, I can still catch them — a face at a window that the forged city has no room for, a child in a doorway who is a little too grey, a little too still, a little too much like the old text under the new. My own grandmothers stole those people back from death, and I love them for it, and I have spent my whole life haunted by the ones they stepped on to do it, and I cannot tell you, to this hour, whether it was the most beautiful thing the Vigil ever did or the most terrible, and *that,* Sam — that not-knowing, forever — is the real price of the merciful sum. Not the water. The not-knowing." She wiped her eyes with the heel of her hand, roughly. "Do the sum if you must. But never, ever tell yourself it came out even."

The workshop was quiet again for a long moment. Even the mirrors held still.

"So you see," Serafina said, "why I would not let your father reach into a frozen catastrophe and *change* it, however good the reason. Because I know exactly where that road ends. It ends in a white church and a rising sea and an old woman with a needle. I have spent my whole life being the proof that *keep the record true* is not a rule the Vigil follows because we're better than the Palimpsest. It's a rule we follow because we *tried the other thing,* once, for the best reason there has ever been, and it is drowning us still. That is the difference between us and the pretty voice on the step. Not that we would never forge a moment. That we *did,* and we know exactly what it costs, and we have chosen to pay it forever rather than do it again." She looked at me. "Your father never understood that. He thought if the Vigil forged one moment to save a city, the rule was a fraud and he could forge his own. He couldn't see that the drowning *is* the rule. Do you see it, boy? Because if you don't, you'll do what he did, and I'll bury you the way I as good as buried him."

I opened my mouth to say I saw it. I want to tell you I saw it.

That's when the bells started.

---

Not the church bells. The other kind — the kind only I could hear, the kind Serafina could hear, the kind that made every covered mirror in the workshop shiver at once and then *ring,* a high thin dreadful note like a wet finger on glass, all of them together. Serafina was on her feet before I understood I was standing. Nico came pounding up the stairs three at a time with Fenna behind him, and Nico's cheerful face had gone the colour of the lagoon.

"The Salute," he gasped. "Serafina — the *church* — there is a bleed on the Salute —"

We went out onto the workshop's water-steps into the acqua alta and the dark, and across the wide mouth of the Grand Canal, out on its point where it has stood for four hundred years, the great white church was *wrong.* I could see it even from there, even across all that black water — the way you see a bleed, the way you can't un-see it once you know — the white dome swimming, the stone going soft at the edges, the whole enormous building flickering between the church that is there and something else, some other night, the old dark drowning night it had been built to write over, bleeding up through the paint of the world like the old text under a scraped page.

And standing on the church steps, tiny and far and perfectly still in a coat the colour of wet slate, was Lena. And she was not alone. Behind her, along the whole white front of the Salute, stood a row of grey coats — Scriveners, more than I'd ever seen, a dozen, two — and at the centre of them, where I could not see a face but could feel the *weight,* the way you feel a hole in the world, stood something that made even Lena look small, something the First Draft had sent, something strong enough to stand on a four-hundred-year forgery and start, very patiently, to *scrape.*

"They're not attacking the city," Serafina breathed, and I heard in her voice the exact moment a thing she'd feared her whole life stopped being a fear and started being a Tuesday. "They're going to *un-forge* it. They're going to tear open the one wound and let the true night come back up — let Venice fall the way it was always meant to fall, four hundred years late — and drown ten thousand people to prove one thing to the whole watching world." She turned to me, and her fierce old face was full of a horror that was almost calm. "That we are exactly what they are. That *keep the record true* was always a lie. They'll rip the mercy out of my city and let it drown, and they'll be *right,* and the only way to stop them —" she stopped.

"— is to send in a dreamer who can hold a frozen moment shut," I said. Because I'd learned that much. "One who can Anchor a whole night. One who can stand on your forgery and pin it down so hard they can't get a fingernail under it." The cold water was up around my ankles now, rising, flat and black and silent. "You need the door. You need me."

"God forgive me," said Serafina, who had spent her whole life making sure no one ever asked this of a child again. "Yes. I need Joris's boy to do the thing I could not forgive Joris for wanting. I need you to go into the worst night this city ever had, and hold its beautiful lie *true.*"

And out across the black water, on the steps of the white church, Lena lifted one hand and gave me a small, sad, unmistakable wave — just fingers, just for me — the way she had across the harbour on the very first night, when I still thought a wave was a friendly thing.

# Chapter Seven — Into the Worst Night

Here is how you break into four hundred years ago, in case you ever have to.

You need a door, which was me. You need someone who can read the moment so you go into the right night and not into the black between nights, which was Serafina. You need light you can trust, which is a harder thing than it sounds when every lamp in a frozen moment lies — and that turned out to be the lantern, my lantern, the warm lit brass lantern from my nightmare that had crossed the seam and burned on my sill, which Serafina took one look at and said, "*That* you brought and did not tell me — of course you did, you are your father's son to the bone," and made me carry, because a light that has crossed the line and stayed lit will burn true in any dream there is.

And you need someone to keep the record. Which was Fenna, and I fought it, and I lost, and I have never been so glad to lose a fight or so sorry.

"You are not coming," I said, in the workshop, while Serafina drew the great covered mirror out from the wall — not the forbidden one; a different one, an old cloudy oval the height of a man, the one she said opened onto the plague night. "Fen. It's the worst night this city ever had. There's *plague* in there. You're a Sleeper —"

"I'm a Sleeper who *knows,*" said Fenna, already lacing her boots, "which the ferry man said three times is the rarest thing, and which means the Lull can't get to me and I can't get lost in a boring story, which is exactly what happens to you the second something goes sideways — you reach for the easy version, Sam, it's your whole *problem,* you've been doing it since the lantern on the shelf. You need somebody in there whose head stays hard. That's me. That's the job. I wrote it down." She stood up. "Also you're not the boss of me and never were. Ruben's hat, remember. You owe me everything. I'm collecting."

Serafina looked at the two of us, and at Nico, who had simply picked up a boat-hook and taken up a position by the mirror as if the argument were already over, and she said something under her breath that I think was a prayer, and she pulled the last cloth off the cloudy oval, and it stopped being a mirror and became a doorway, and on the far side of it was the worst night Venice ever had, waiting for us, four hundred years deep.

"Hold hands," she said. "All of you, hold on, and do not let go inside, whatever you see, because what you see will be *true,* and the true things in there died a long time ago and some of them will want you to stay. Sam. Open the door."

I took the warm lantern in one hand and Fenna's hand in the other, and I leaned toward the frozen moment the way I'd leaned toward the Night Watch, and the seam went soft, softer than it ever went for anyone, a puddle, a warm bath — and I pulled the whole of us across into 1630.

---

I have to tell you about the smell first, because it's the thing that made it real, and I've never been able to make anyone understand it who wasn't there.

It was cold, and it was dark, and it was Venice — I knew the shape of the canals even four hundred years gone — but it was a Venice out of a bad dream: fires burning on the quays, not for warmth but for the smoke, because they thought the smoke kept the sickness off; doors marked with white crosses, house after house after house; and boats, low black boats, moving slow on the dark water with lanterns at their prows and a shape under a cloth in the middle of each one, and more of them than there were house-lights, a whole silent fleet of them going out toward the lagoon in the dark. And over all of it, the bells — not ringing a tune, just tolling, one at a time, all across the city, on and on, a bell for a death and then another bell for a death and then another, so many and so steady that after a minute your own heart started keeping their time.

"Don't look at the boats," Serafina said, low and hard, gripping my hand. "Look at the church. Look at where the church will be."

Out on the point, where the great white Salute would stand for four hundred years, there was nothing yet but a muddy spit of land and a few pilings driven into the black water — because we were *before,* we were in the night the forgery was made, the night her seven-grandmothers-back gathered the darners and decided to steal a mercy. And over that empty point, in the dark, the air was *wrong* the way the Salute had been wrong from across the canal — flickering, scraping, the seam being worked — because the Palimpsest were already here. They'd come into the same night from the other side, and I could see them now, down at the water's edge, grey coats in the firelight, and Lena among them, and at their centre the weight, the hole in the world, the strong one the First Draft had sent, standing with both hands raised toward the empty point where the church wasn't yet, patiently pulling the mercy apart before it could ever be made.

"They're going to stop the forgery from happening at all," Nico breathed. "Not un-write it after. *Prevent* it. If the darners never make the lie tonight, the church never stands, the plague never breaks —"

"— and ten thousand people who lived for four hundred years never lived," Serafina finished. "It all comes up at once. The whole true night, four centuries of it, drowning back in. Sam." She turned to me, and there was no fierceness left, only the terrible calm. "You cannot stop them scraping. You are not strong enough to fight that thing at the water's edge and neither am I. But you don't have to fight it. You have to do the other thing. The thing you did in the painting. Get to the point — get to where the darners are making the forgery — and *Anchor it.* Hold the mercy true. Pin it so deep they cannot get it loose, no matter how they scrape. Keep the record —" she stopped, and gave a short, awful laugh, four hundred years of irony in it " — keep the *forged* record true. That is what I have brought Joris's son across four centuries to do. Hold my lie shut. Go."

---

We ran.

Or we did the thing that passes for running in a frozen moment, which is a kind of terrible wading, because the night didn't want extra people in it and pushed back on us like deep water, and the dead-boats slid past silent on the canals, and the bells tolled, and twice a door with a white cross on it opened as we passed and something looked out that I will not describe to you because you are, I hope, younger than I was when I saw it, and Fenna's hand was crushing mine and her lips were moving, and I leaned in to hear what she was praying and she wasn't praying, she was *narrating,* fast and flat and steady under her breath — "*door with a cross, boat with a lantern, Sam's got the light, Nico's got the hook, Serafina's leading, we're going to the point, we are going to the point, we do not look at the boats*" — keeping the record, holding her own head hard with the only tool she had, which was the truth said out loud, and I understood in that moment exactly what Wessel had seen in her on the train, and exactly why the Lull had never really caught her: my best friend was a darner. She'd been one her whole life. She just hadn't had a seam to work on until now.

Halfway there the night tested us, the way Serafina had warned it would.

A boat drew alongside on the black canal — a dead-boat, low, a lantern at its prow, a shape under a cloth amidships — and the man poling it turned his head and looked at us, at the four living warm loud people wading through his frozen grief, and he *saw* us, which the dead in a held moment are not supposed to do. His face was grey and wet and hopeless and it fixed on Fenna, and he said something, low, in the old Venetian, and reached out one hand off his pole toward her — not cruel, that was the worst of it, just *asking,* the way a drowning man asks — and Fenna stopped narrating and stopped dead in the water and I felt her start to reach back, because that is who Fenna is, she reaches back, and Serafina hissed "*do not take his hand, girl, he has been dead four hundred years and he only wants company*" and Nico got his boat-hook across Fenna's chest and hauled her bodily back and the boat slid on into the dark with the grey man still reaching, still asking, until the fog took him.

Fenna was shaking. "He looked *right at me.*"

"You're a Sleeper who knows," Serafina said, hauling us on. "You're the warmest, most awake thing in this whole dead night — of course they see you. That's why you should not have come, and it's too late now, so keep your head *hard* and keep the record and do not, do not, give a dead man your hand, no matter how he asks. Move."

We came out onto the muddy point in the firelight, and there they were — the Vigil of 1630, seven or eight of them in the plain dark clothes of four hundred years ago, kneeling in the mud in a ring with their hands pressed flat to the ground, and at the center of the ring an old woman with white hair scraped back and a face I knew, a face I was holding by the hand, a face Serafina would wear in four hundred years — her own seven-grandmothers-back, mid-forgery, mid-theft, mid-mercy, holding the drowning city up out of the true night by main strength and about to lose it.

And between us and them, turning slowly, unhurried, rising up out of the grey-coated ranks at the water's edge, the weight, the hole in the world, the First Draft's strong one, lowered its raised hands from the empty point and turned them, instead, toward *me* — toward the boy who had just waded a whole living door into a frozen night — and a voice came out of it that was not one voice but many, dry and patient and pleased, the sound of a page being scraped clean:

"*There* he is," said the First Draft. "The little door. We didn't come for the church, boy. We never come for the thing you're looking at. We came for *you* — and your teacher has known it since the bells started, and brought you anyway, because a Venetian will always, always spend one child to save ten thousand. Ask her. Ask her what she promised us for the city. Go on." The many-voiced weight tilted, almost gently, toward Serafina, whose hand had gone slack and cold in mine. "Ask her why the door was left open behind you."

# Chapter Eight — What a Venetian Will Spend

"Tell me it's not true," I said to Serafina, in the firelight, in the worst night Venice ever had, with the bells tolling and the dead-boats sliding past and the First Draft standing patient in the mud waiting for her to answer for me. "Tell me you didn't."

And Serafina, who had never once been warm to me, who had grieved my father for forty years and could barely look at his face on mine — Serafina looked at me and did the bravest and worst thing I have ever watched a person do. She told me the truth.

"I did," she said.

The bells tolled. Fenna's hand tightened in mine.

"When the bleed came up on the Salute, they sent a Scrivener to me, before you woke, with an offer." Her voice was steady and her eyes were not. "They said: we will leave your city its mercy. We will let the forgery stand, let the ten thousand keep their four hundred years, let the water stop rising if it can. All we want is the door. Bring us the boy, open the deep moment, and step back. One child, Sam, for ten thousand souls and a city fifteen hundred years old." She drew a breath that shook. "And I have spent my whole life being the proof that *keep the record true* is the hard right rule, and I looked at that offer and I did the sum your Lena did in the painting, the sum every one of us swears we'd never do — I counted. One against ten thousand. And I said *yes.* I said yes, and I brought you across, and I left the door open behind you so they could take you clean, and I told myself it was the same terrible arithmetic my grandmother did on this very mud, and that if she could steal a mercy to save the city then I could sell a boy to keep it, and it is the *same sum,* Sam, that's the horror of it, it has always been the same sum —"

"— and that," said the First Draft, pleased, "is all we ever wanted anyone to see. Not the city. *The sum.* That your saints do it too. Thank you, old woman. You've un-forged something far bigger than a church tonight. You've un-forged the *Vigil.*"

And I understood, cold all the way down, that Lena had told me the truth on the wet step and I hadn't heard the shape of it. *This time it won't be forty dead men in a painting. This time it'll be somebody you brought with you.* The whole thing — the bleed on the Salute, the spectacle, the drowning threat — none of it had ever been about Venice. It was a machine built to make a good woman do a terrible sum in front of a witness, so the whole watching record would see the keepers of the true record spend a child, and know at last that there was no difference between the two sides, no *true,* nothing worth folding your arms over. It was the most patient, most beautiful, most evil thing I have ever stood inside, and it had already worked, the second Serafina said *yes* to a Scrivener in her workshop, hours ago, before I'd even woken.

And the worst part — I have to tell you the worst part, because I've promised you the ugly ones — is that standing in the mud of 1630 with the woman who'd sold me looking at me with her whole heart breaking, I could not even be *surprised.* I'd known, hadn't I. Not the details. But the shape. Lena had told me on the wet step: *this time it'll be somebody you brought with you.* The girl in the dream had spent six years warning me that both sides would want to use me and that kindness was the cheapest thing either of them had. Serafina had told me herself, plainly, that a Venetian would always spend one child to save ten thousand — she'd said it about her own grandmother and I'd heard it as history. I'd been *told,* over and over, by everyone who'd ever tried to help me, that the people who love you will do the merciful sum when it comes to it, and I'd nodded and filed it and gone right on trusting the next warm voice, because that is the one thing I have never learned, the thing that will probably be the end of me: I keep believing that the person in front of me will be the exception. That's not innocence. Innocence is not knowing. I *knew.* It's something worse than innocence, and I don't have a word for it, and it's the truest thing about me, and it's why I'm the one this all happened to.

---

Then Serafina broke the deal.

I want that on the record too, next to the betrayal, because both are true and neither cancels the other, and I have spent a long time refusing to let people tell me the simple version of her.

"I said yes," she said, and turned, and put herself between me and the First Draft, one tiny fierce old woman in the mud of four hundred years ago. "And I have had six hours to sit with it, and I find I cannot pay it. Not because the sum is wrong. The sum is *right,* God help me, one is less than ten thousand, it will always be less. But I did the sum once already tonight, from the other side, when I decided to bring him — and a woman who has spent her whole life darning the bleed of one beautiful forgery knows the one thing the arithmetic leaves out." She raised her hands, and every dead-boat on every canal behind her turned its prow, and the drowned city itself seemed to lean toward her. "There is no clean edit. Not of a church. Not of a boy. You do the merciful sum, you save the ten thousand, and the bleed of the one you spent comes up a centimeter a year forever. I will not start another four hundred years of rising water. Take your spectacle. You'll not have *him.* Run, Sam. RUN — go deep — I'll hold the—"

She never finished it.

The First Draft did not shout, did not strike, did nothing so kind as that. It simply *let go* of the thing it had been holding all along — not scraping the forgery now but the opposite, releasing the whole frozen moment it had been pinning open for us to stand in — and the worst night Venice ever had, four hundred years deep and never meant to hold this many living souls, began, all at once, to *collapse.*

And here is the thing the First Draft understood that Serafina, in all her sixty years of darning, had not let herself see: it didn't need to beat us. It didn't need to win the working on the point. It only needed to make the moment *fail* while four living people stood inside it — because a frozen moment that comes apart around you doesn't set you gently back on the shore. It drowns you in itself. The un-forging of Venice and the murder of us were the same act. It had never planned to do one without the other. That is what patience looks like, in the end, when it finally moves: it does everything at once, and none of it looks like an attack, and by the time you understand what's happening you're already up to your knees.

I'd felt a working collapse before. I'd felt the boat-hook go, on the ferry, and it had taken my father's hagelslag to pay for it. This was that, the size of a city, the size of a plague. The mud went soft. The firelight smeared. The bells all tolled at once and then stopped, and the silence that came after was the silence of the flooded square, the deep wrong silence of a place that has stopped being real — and the black water of 1630 began to come up, fast, over the point, over the mud, cold and salt and rising, exactly, *exactly* the water of my nightmare, up my legs, up past my knees, the whole dead night dissolving into the flood it had always been underneath —

"The mirror!" Nico was screaming, from somewhere behind, "back to the mirror, it's closing, it's *closing* —" and Serafina had my collar and was hauling me back the way we'd come, toward the cloudy oval hanging impossibly in the drowning dark, our one door home, already shrinking, already thinning, and I had the warm lantern in one hand throwing its true light across the rising black water and I had Fenna's hand in the other and I was pulling her, we were all wading, chest-deep now in the cold salt flood, toward the closing door —

and Fenna's hand was torn out of mine.

Not let go. *Torn* — the collapsing moment grabbed her, the way Serafina had said the true dead things would want to keep us, the flood surging between us in a black wall — and I turned, waist-deep, lantern up, and I saw my best friend go under. Not swept away. *Down.* The way my father went down. The black water of a dying city closing over Fenna's furious frightened face, her mouth still moving — still narrating, still keeping the record, *Sam's got the light, we're going to the—* — as the flood took her under and the door behind me shrank to the size of a coin and Serafina screamed at me the one word that has haunted me ever since, because it was the right word, and it was the wrong word, and I have never once been sure which:

"*Leave her!*" screamed Serafina, hauling at my collar toward the closing door. "She's a Sleeper, she's already gone, you cannot hold a whole night AND a drowning girl, you'll go under with her — one against the door, Sam, do the SUM, LEAVE HER —"

And close against my ear, from nowhere, calm and almost gentle, the way you speak to a boy about to touch a hot pan, the voice I had known for six years said:

"*Not yet.*"

# Chapter Nine — The Bigger Thing

*Not yet,* the girl said, and I want to tell you what those two words had always meant, before that night, so you understand what it cost me to break them.

For six years *not yet* had meant *don't.* Don't look at the light. Don't go where the diver keeps his places. Don't do the thing you're going to want to do. It was a hand on my chest, holding me back from the edge, and I had trusted it with my life, because it had been right every single time. In the painting she'd said *not yet* and I'd held the record true and it had been the hard right thing.

And now she was saying it again, calm and certain, the same hand on the same chest, and Serafina was screaming *leave her, do the sum,* and my best friend was going under in the black water of a dead city, still moving her mouth, still keeping the record, and the door home was shrinking to a coin.

And I did the thing I was going to want to do.

I'm not proud of it and I'm not sorry and those two facts have never once made peace in me, so I'll just tell you what happened.

I let go of Serafina's hand. I let go of the door. I turned my back on the only way home, and I waded down into the black flood after Fenna, with the warm lantern held up over my head throwing its one true light across water that had drowned ten thousand people, and I reached down into the place where she'd gone under, and I did not try to pull her.

I'd learned that much. You cannot pull a whole person the way you pull a coin — she was too big to hold whole, she'd come across wrong, fused, missing, warm where she should be cold, and the backwash would take us both down. Pulling wasn't the tool. I knew the tool now. Serafina had put it in my hands herself, over three days, one candlestick at a time.

I *Anchored.*

I reached into the collapsing night — into the true dead moment that was dissolving all around me back into the flood it had always been — and I did the thing the Vigil forbid, the thing that drowns cities, the thing my father did once and never came back from. I did not hold the moment true. I *changed* it. I found the black water closing over Fenna and I drove a different night down into it, pinned it deep, harder than I have ever held anything, a whole new true written over the old one by main strength: *the point did not flood here. The water did not take her. Fenna Bakker came up out of the worst night Venice ever had, alive, above the surface, breathing, and this is what happened, this is the record, this is TRUE* — and I held it, and I held it, and I felt the whole dead city push back against the lie with four hundred years of its own weight, and I held it anyway, twelve years old, waist-deep in a plague, and I *would not let it be false.*

And the water gave.

It went hard and true and certain under my open hands the way the clock in my dream went hard and true, the way the Night Watch went, except this time it wasn't the real night I was pinning shut. It was a night that never happened. And it happened. It *stuck.* The black flood drew back off the muddy point, off my legs, and Fenna came up out of the water gasping and swearing and alive, alive, in a version of 1630 that I had written with my own two hands and nailed to the floor of the world so hard that four hundred years couldn't lift it.

I had forged a moment to save one life.

I had done the exact thing that built a white church and drowns a city a centimetre a year. I had done the exact thing that Lena did for a dead chocolate family and called mercy, the exact thing Serafina's grandmother did and carried to God, the exact thing I had refused to do in the painting when it was forty men I didn't know — and I'd done it in a heartbeat, without a single second of the sum, the moment it was Fenna.

That's the thing about the sum, I understand now. Everyone can do it until it's someone they love. Then the arithmetic just... stops being arithmetic. Then it's Fenna's face going under, and there is no ten thousand in the world that weighs against it, and you find out exactly what kind of dreamer you are, and it is not the kind that keeps the record true. It is the kind both sides would burn the world to own.

Fenna, coughing, gripping my arm, looked at the water drawn back off the point, at the dry ground where a flood had been, at the new true I'd nailed over the old one, and she looked at my face, and — this is the thing I love her for, out of a whole life of things — she didn't say *thank you.* She didn't say *you saved me.* She looked at what I'd done, and what it had cost to do it, which she could see written all over me even if she couldn't yet name the coin, and she said, very quietly, in the middle of a dead city I'd rewritten to keep her breathing: "Sam. That's the thing you're not supposed to do. Isn't it. You just did the thing." And I said, "Yeah." And she held on tighter, not less, and said, "Okay. Then we did it together, and I'm going to write it down exactly, so nobody ever gets to tell you you did it for a bad reason." Which is the only absolution I have ever actually believed, and she gave it to me kneeling in four-hundred-year-old water with her hair full of ash, and I have never told her that either.

---

Then the backwash came for its coin, and it was arc-level, and so was the price.

I felt it reach into me, gentle as a grown-up taking a knife off a toddler, and I braced for it to take my father — the little I had left of him, the photograph over my heart, the *him* of him — because that's what it took last time and I thought I knew the shape of my own losses now.

It didn't take my father.

It reached past him, deeper, to something I had never once thought of as fragile because it had been there my whole life like the ceiling, like the canal, like breathing — and it took my mother's face.

Not her. Not that I have a mother, or her name, or her voice, or the too-careful clink of a bowl going into the cupboard when she's home tired from a night shift, or *gefeliciteerd, bijna-jarige,* or the money on the counter and the drawing of a carrot. It left me all of that. It reached in and it lifted out, clean, the way it took the hagelslag, the one thing a boy never thinks to guard: what my mother *looks like.* Her actual face. The smile she keeps just for me, tired all the way through but real. It took it, to pay for the night I forged to save my best friend, and where it had been there is now a smooth warm place my mind goes to and finds nothing, and I know she has a face, I know I love it, and I cannot, however I try, however I have tried every day since, call it up out of the dark.

I saved Fenna, whom I brought into this, and I paid for her with my mother, whom I left at home making stroopwafels, and I will go back to Amsterdam one day soon and my own mum will open the door and I will not know her face until she speaks, and that is the sum the world made me do instead of the one Serafina begged me to. There is no clean edit. Not even to save the person you love most. *Especially* not then.

---

Serafina got us out. She came back — she came *back* into the collapsing moment for us, which I have never forgotten and never will, an old woman wading into a drowning night she'd been ready to leave me in one minute before — and she got Nico and Fenna and me through the shrinking coin of the door and out onto the cold wet floor of her workshop as the mirror behind us went black and cracked corner to corner with a sound like a shot and would never open onto 1630 again.

And I lay on the floor of the mirror city, hollowed out, holding my best friend's cold hand while she coughed up four-hundred-year-old water and lived, and I looked up, and every covered mirror in the room had uncovered itself, all of them at once, every sheet and cloth and scrap of velvet slid to the floor — and every single mirror was showing me the same thing, the whole room of them, dozens of doorways all turned my way: a boy with a lantern, standing in black water, under a bruised purple-yellow sky, in a dead city of leaning pale stone, while the ash came down like snow that wasn't snow.

And from every mirror at once, in a warm delighted voice and a dry many-throated one, layered, the two sides of the war saying it together for the first and only time, I heard:

"*There* you are."

# Chapter Ten — Nobody Recruits a God

They came for me that same night, both of them, and I want you to notice that neither of them tried to be kind about it, and that this — the end of the kindness — was how I knew everything had changed.

Lena came up out of the black cracked mirror on the workshop floor, the one that would never open on 1630 again, stepping out of the ruin of it into the room like it was a puddle — a Vivid can follow where an arc-level door has been, and I'd blown that door so wide the whole seam of Venice was standing open. She was breathing hard and her slate coat was soaked to the waist with four-hundred-year-old water and for once she did not look delighted. She looked *afraid,* which I had seen on her exactly once before, kneeling in the woodsmoke of the Night Watch when I chose against her.

Behind her, filling the doorway, not entering, the weight — the First Draft, the many-throated hole in the world, standing in the acqua alta on the water-steps as if it could not quite be bothered to come in for me anymore, as if I were a thing it had already decided it owned.

"Well," said Lena, quietly. "You've done it now, little bell. You held a painting shut and both sides said *maybe.* You've just forged a night to save a Sleeper and made it *stick,* in front of the First Draft itself." She wiped water off her face. "There's no maybe left. There's no *recruiting* left. Do you understand what you've done? Nobody recruits a god, Sam. You don't send a charming girl with a photograph to *recruit* the one dreamer in a generation who can hold history steady and change it and make it hold. You just —" her voice did something I'd never heard it do; it wobbled " — you just take him. However you have to. Before the other side does."

"Then take me," I said. I was still on the floor, hollow, holding Fenna's hand. "Go on. You said it. Nobody recruits me anymore. So do the other thing."

And the strangest silence fell in that room of mirrors. Because neither of them moved.

---

I understand it now, though I didn't then. I'll explain it to you the way Serafina explained it to me later, dry and exhausted, because it's the shape of the whole war from here on and you'll want it for what comes next.

The reason neither of them took me off that floor is the same reason nobody had grabbed me in Amsterdam once I started ringing: I was too loud, and now I was *arc-level* loud. If the Palimpsest seized me, every darner in Europe would feel it and the Vigil would come down on them with everything they had left, because an arc-level dreamer in the First Draft's hands is the end of the world and they know it. If the Vigil locked me away, the Palimpsest would tear the continent apart to get me back, because an arc-level dreamer is the only thing that can un-drown their four hundred years of patient work. I had become, in one night, in one flooded moment, too valuable for either side to *hold* — because the instant either one held me, the other would go to open war to take me, and neither was sure it would win.

"You are a loaded gun on a table between two frightened people," Serafina said. "And nobody will pick you up, because whoever picks you up gets shot at by the other. So they will do the next thing. They will *guard* you, and watch you, and wait — each of them making sure the other doesn't get you — and they will circle each other around you for years if they must, until the one day comes when they *have* to use you, the one moment worth going to war over." She looked at me, and her fierce old eyes were wet. "And you already know when that day is, boy. You've had the date since your sixth birthday. That is the day they will stop circling. That is the day the whole war lands on one child in one place at one hour, and both sides throw everything they have into the water to own you, because whoever holds the arc-level door on *that* day writes the world." She said it very quietly. "The date is not your nightmare, Sam. The date is the battlefield. And you've been dreaming the battlefield your whole life."

I want you to sit with what that meant for a second, because I didn't, not for weeks, and it did me damage. I had spent a whole month terrified of being *taken.* Of a grey coat closing on my shoulder, of Lena's warm voice, of being used. And in one night I'd graduated past all of it into something lonelier: I was too dangerous to take, and that meant I was never going to be *rescued* either. Nobody was going to swoop in and carry me somewhere safe, because there was nowhere safe to carry a boy who lit up every mirror between Venice and the sea, and because the moment anyone tried, the war would land on whoever tried it. I wasn't a prize anymore. I was a weather system. People were going to arrange themselves around me — carefully, at a distance, watching each other over the top of me — and wait for the one day the storm made landfall. And I was twelve, and I wanted, more than I have ever wanted anything, for one single grown-up to be *in charge* of this, and there was no one, there was only me, standing in the water at the bottom of everyone's arithmetic.

Wessel, when he heard — Nico sent word north that same night, the Vigil have their ways — Wessel didn't come. He sent back one line, by the same silent channel, and Nico read it out, and it's the truest thing anyone said to me in that whole ruined week: *"Tell the boy I'm not staying away because I don't care. I'm staying away because if I stand next to him now, I'm just one more person the other side has to count. The kindest thing left is to be somewhere else. He'll understand that one day and hate it, and he'll be right."* I did understand it. I do hate it. He was right.

Lena did not deny it. That was the terrible confirmation. She stood in the ruined mirror-light, soaked and afraid, and she looked at me on the floor and she said the truest thing she ever said to me, and I've turned it over a thousand times.

"I came back to Venice half meaning to save you," she said. "Not for the First Draft. For *me.* Because when you held that painting shut and paid your father's face for it, you did the one thing I have never once managed in my whole clever life — you chose true when it cost you, and I have wanted, ever since, to be near the kind of person who can do that, the way you want to sit near a fire when you've been cold a long time." Her jaw set. "And instead I watched you forge a night to save your friend and I watched it stick, and now you're not a fire, Sam. Now you're the thing my masters and his mistress will burn the world to own, and I can't save you from that, because there's nowhere on earth to hide a boy who lights up every mirror between here and the sea." She turned to go, and stopped, and did not look back, and said: "I'll see you at the water, on the day. We'll both be there. Everyone will. And I don't know, this time, which side I'll be standing on — and that, God help me, is the nicest thing I've ever been able to say to anyone." And she stepped into the cracked black mirror and the First Draft folded up off the water-steps like a shadow at noon, and they were gone, and the acqua alta lapped the workshop floor, and it was just us again, the four of us, wet and wrecked in a room full of mirrors all showing the same dead city under the same bruised sky.

---

Fenna sat up, finally, and coughed the last of 1630 out of her, and looked around the room at the dozens of mirrors all showing the ash and the stone and the leaning pale streets, and then she looked at me, and she did not do a joke.

"That's your dream," she said. "Isn't it. That's the place under the water. It's in all of them now. Since you — since the thing you did for me." She swallowed. "You lit it up. Whatever you turned into, saving me, it lit up the place you've been trying to find all month, in every mirror in the city, all at once." She wiped her mouth with the back of her hand, and her voice was very small and very steady, my furious brave best friend who narrated her own drowning. "Sam. You said the backwash took something. Last time it took your dad's face. What did it take this time. You've gone a colour I don't like. What did it *take.*"

And I looked at Fenna, whom I had refused to leave, whom I had bought back out of a dead city with the only currency the world would take — and I found I could not tell her. Not because of the Lull. Because of shame. Because the answer was *my mother,* because I had spent my own mum's face to keep my best friend's, and Fenna would carry that for the rest of her life the way I was going to, and I could not — I could not do that to her, not after everything, not on top of everything.

So for the tenth time, and the last time, and the only time I have ever been glad of it, I did not tell Fenna the truth. But I didn't lull her either. I just looked at my best friend in the mirror-light and I said, "Nothing you have to carry," and I let her see that it was a wall and not a smoothing, a door I was holding shut on purpose and not one I was tucking her behind — and Fenna, who knew the difference now, who had a notebook full of the difference, looked at me for a long moment and then nodded, once, and let me keep it. Which is its own kind of love, and a harder one, and the one we had left.

Then Serafina came and stood over the two of us, over Joris's boy and the girl he'd broken every rule to save, in her ruined workshop under her uncovered mirrors, and she looked at the dead city burning in all that glass, and all the fight had gone out of her, and all the deflection, and all the forty years of holding a door shut, and she said, in a voice like the drowned church:

"All right. All right, Sam. I've one more truth to give you, and it's the worst one I own, and it's about your father, and it's about that place in the glass, and I have kept it four hundred — no." She stopped. Her old mouth twisted. "That's the lie I tell. I told you the mirror was four hundred years of my grief and it was a *deflection,* boy, the way you deflect, we're all of us the same — it isn't four hundred years old, my grief, it's *forty.* That mirror is your father's. And I know exactly what city that is, and what happened there, and why he could never come home, because I'm the one who forbade him to go, and he went anyway, and he did not come back the same, and if you're going to walk into it too — and you are, I can see the date on you like your father wore it — then you'll walk in knowing. Sit up. Both of you. And let me tell you about Pompeii."

# Chapter Eleven — The Diver

"There is one moment," Serafina said, "that every dreamer who can reach it is drawn to, the way water is drawn downhill, and it is the worst place in the whole record, and its name is Pompeii."

She'd made us tea by then — even in a ruined workshop full of burning mirrors, a Venetian will make tea — and Fenna had her notebook out, wet pages and all, and Nico sat very quiet on the stairs, and I held the warm lantern in both hands and listened to the last true thing my father's teacher had to give me.

"You know the story the tourists know. A town by the sea, under a mountain, on a bright morning near two thousand years ago. And the mountain — Vesuvius — comes apart. A day and a night of it. The sky goes black at noon and rains fire and stone, and the sea draws back and then comes up wrong, and by the second morning the town and everyone slow enough to still be in it are gone, sealed whole under the ash, mid-step, mid-word, mid-*everything* — a whole moment of the world frozen exactly as it fell." She wrapped her hands round her cup. "That is what a Sleeper knows. Here is what a darner knows. A catastrophe that sudden, that complete, that *witnessed* — it does not fade the way ordinary past fades. It *sets.* It becomes the hardest-frozen moment there is, harder than any painting, because it wasn't a genius holding it — it was ten thousand terrified people all holding the same instant at once, the way the Night Watch is forty men holding one, only it's a whole *city,* and the moment they're holding is their own end." She looked at me. "It is the single most anchored moment in human history. Which makes it, to the Palimpsest, the holiest site on earth. Because if you could get *inside* the moment Pompeii fell, and change it, and let ten thousand terrified anchors hold your change true forever — you would have rewritten history itself in the one place strong enough to make it stick. It's the Night Watch, Sam. It's the same crime. It's just ten thousand times bigger, and it's real, and it's the thing they have wanted for two thousand years, and it is the place in your nightmare."

The mirrors burned around us, all showing the leaning pale streets, the ash falling like grey snow, the bruised purple-yellow sky.

"But the water," I said. My voice had gone small. "In my dream there's water. A flooded square. A diver. Pompeii burned, it didn't —"

"Both," said Serafina. "That's why your dream is so hard to read — it layers them, the way the true moment layered them. The mountain fell, and the *sea* rose to meet it — the bay drew back and came up in a black wall, and the ash came down, and for a few hours the people of that coast were caught between a drowning and a burning at once, the water rising and the sky falling, and the boats —" she stopped. "There were boats, Sam. That's the part almost no one remembers. On the true day, while the town died, ships came *across the bay* toward it. Rescue. Men who saw the mountain fall and rowed *into* it, toward the dying town, to pull people off the shore, and most of them died doing it, and one of them — a great man, a famous one — walked up the beach with the ash coming down and the sea coming up, to help, and lay down on that shore and never rose. A man who crossed the water toward the catastrophe carrying a light, to save whoever he could." She held my eyes. "Does that sound like anyone, boy? A figure who wades across rising water, slow, patient, carrying a lantern, coming toward you out of the worst moment there is — not to hurt you. To *reach* you. To pull you out."

The lantern was very warm in my hands.

"The Lantern Man isn't hunting me," I whispered. It came up out of me the way the true things do. "He's — he's a rescuer. He's crossing the water to get to me."

"That," said Serafina, "is what I believe. And it is why your nightmare has always frightened me more than any monster could. Because a monster you run from. But a figure wading through fire and flood toward a child, carrying the only warm light in the world, patient as tide, *to save him* — you don't run from that, Sam. You *wait* for it. Your whole life. You let it come. And when it finally reaches you and lifts the light to your face —" She could not finish it.

---

"My father," I said. "He went to Pompeii. Didn't he. That's what you forbade. That's what he did anyway."

Serafina set down her cup, and forty years came up in her face, and she told me the last of it.

Joris had been the best she ever taught, better than her, better than his teacher — the one dreamer in a generation who could open the deep mirrors and stand in a frozen catastrophe and hold it steady. And he had come to believe, the way I'd nearly come to believe on that muddy point, that if the Vigil had forged one moment to save Venice, then the rule was a fraud, and he could go to the worst moment there is, the moment ten thousand people died in the ash, and he could *change* it. Save them. Undo Pompeii. The greatest darning there ever was, or the greatest forgery — he'd stopped being able to tell the difference, which is exactly how it takes you.

"I forbade it," Serafina said. "I told him what I told you: there is no clean edit, and the bleed of Pompeii would not be a centimetre of water a year, it would be the end of everything, because you cannot un-write the hardest-frozen moment in history without the whole record tearing. He didn't believe me. Or he did, and did it anyway, because he was your father and he collected impossible promises the way you do — I see it, boy, you got it from him, this dreadful gentleness that gets everyone killed." She wiped her eyes, furious, the way Fenna does. "He went into Pompeii. Alone. To save ten thousand strangers two thousand years dead. And he reached too deep, and held too much, and the moment took him the way the flood took your friend tonight — except there was no one who loved him waiting in the water to forge him back out. He came home. His body came home. But the man who could open the deep mirrors never surfaced from that dive, and a few years later the rest of him followed it down, and that is what *going under* is, Sam, in your father's case — it isn't sleep. It's a dive he never came up from. He's still down there. In Pompeii. In the worst moment there is. On the day the mountain falls." Her voice dropped to nothing. "On your date."

The room was silent but for the lap of the acqua alta and the soft burn of the mirrors.

"That's why the diver," I said, and I was crying and didn't care. "The brass suit. The helmet. It's not a Roman. It's — going that deep looks like that. Diving into the hardest moment there is. The suit is what a dreamer wears to go all the way down." I looked at the warm lantern, the one that had crossed the seam and stayed lit, the one thing my father's whole life had been about. "The Lantern Man is my dad. He's down there. He's been wading toward me across that moment for six years, carrying a light, trying to reach me — or trying to warn me — or trying to *pull me out before I come in after him,* and the girl keeps saying *not yet* because —"

"Because if you go in," Serafina said, "on the date, to save your father the way you saved your friend tonight — you will do the thing he did. You will reach into Pompeii and try to hold it and change it, and it will take you the way it took him, and there will be two Vermeers under the ash instead of one. Unless." She took my face in her two old hands, hard, the way you hold something you've decided to save. "Unless you are stronger than he was. Which, God forgive me for the hope of it, you are. You forged a night tonight and made it *stick,* which he never once managed. You might — you *might* — be the one dreamer who could go all the way down into Pompeii and come back up. And every soul on both sides of this war has just watched you prove it, in my mirrors, tonight. So now they know what I know." She let go. "They know that on your date, at the fourth hour, in the worst moment there is, the arc-level boy is going to dive for his drowning father — and whoever is standing in the water when you do gets to decide what you save, and what you change, and who writes the world that comes out the other side."

She stood, an old woman in a ruined room, and looked at the burning mirrors one last time, and then she walked down the row of them and, one by one, took each cloth up off the floor and covered them again, shutting the dead city away behind velvet and brown paper and sixty years of her own stubborn hands, until only the last one was left uncovered — the tall one, the forbidden one, my father's mirror, floor to ceiling, showing the leaning streets and the falling ash and, far off across the flooded stone, small and dark, a warm point of light beginning, very slowly, to swing.

"That one's yours now," Serafina said. "Heaven help us all. Look in it when you're ready, and not before. And when you're ready, Sam — you'll know the date, and now you'll know the place, and I'm sorry, because that's everything, that's the whole map, there's nothing left to keep you." She put her hand flat on my back, once, between the shoulder blades, heavy and still, and I nearly came apart, because I couldn't remember my mother's face but my body remembered exactly what that meant. "Go home to your mother while you still can. Book the train. It's coming, boy. The fourth hour's coming. And this time nobody's going to say *not yet.*"

# Chapter Twelve — The Fourth Hour Coming

We took the train home, the long way, over the ground, the way the Vigil do.

Venice let me sleep the whole journey north, the same strange mercy as before — a bell rings quiet in a city that's all bells, and rings quieter still going away from it — and I slept without the flooded square for the last easy nights I would get for a long while, with Fenna across the aisle keeping her notebook, writing down everything that had happened while it was still true, filling page after page in her small furious hand so that at least one true record of the mirror city would exist that no Lull could ever soften. She's still got them, those notebooks. When my own memory goes wrong — and it does, more and more, that's the tax, that's what all this costs — hers are the truest version there is. Truer than mine. I told you at the very start that when I say *this is what happened* I mean *this is the truest version I've still got.* Fenna's is truer. If you ever meet her, ask her. She'll tell you straight. She always did.

Nico came as far as the mainland to see us off, cheerful and wet-eyed at once, and made me promise to come back to Venice one day when it wasn't ending, and I promised, because I collect them, and because I meant it, and because I have learned that a promise you mean is worth making even when you can't keep it. Serafina didn't come to the station. She'd said her goodbye in the workshop, with her hand between my shoulder blades, and some goodbyes you don't improve by repeating. But she gave me two things to carry north, and I'll tell you both, because they're the last of Book Two and the first of what comes next.

The first was my father's mirror. The tall one. The forbidden one. Crated up and shipped by slow freight to a still-room in Amsterdam, because — she said — a thing like that shouldn't be in a city that's already drowning, and because it was mine now, my father's dive folded up in glass, and a boy should keep his own map even when it's a map he's afraid of.

The second was a sentence, murmured on the water-steps with the acqua alta round our ankles, that I didn't understand until much later and that I'll give you now to carry too: "The girl in your dream. The one who says *not yet.*" Serafina had looked at me strangely. "Your father heard her too, boy. On his dive. Same voice, same words, holding him back from the edge of Pompeii for years before he went in. We never knew who she was. But I'll tell you what your father came to believe, at the end, and it frightened him worse than the diver." She'd paused. "He thought she wasn't warning him *off* the moment. He thought she was warning him *toward* it. He thought *not yet* didn't mean *never.* It meant *not yet — but when it's time, you'll have to.* He thought she was on his side. And he could never work out how a girl could be waiting inside a moment two thousand years old, saying the same two words to a father and a son across forty years, unless —" and there Serafina had stopped, and shaken her head, and said only, "unless time is even thinner than the seam. Ask her yourself, one day. You'll get the chance. You're going to the one place she lives."

---

Here is where the war stands, now that you've come this far, so you'll understand the size of what's still ahead.

Both sides know what I am. Nobody recruits me anymore. The Palimpsest and the Vigil circle each other around me like two dogs around one bone, each making sure the other doesn't close its jaws, and they will keep circling — for months, Wessel thinks, maybe a year — right up until the day the circling has to stop. Because there is a date. It has been coming toward me since my sixth birthday, and now I know the place it's coming at, and the two of them together make a battlefield: a bright morning by the sea, two thousand years ago and also not yet, under a mountain that is about to come apart, in the worst and hardest-frozen moment in the whole record of the world. Pompeii. On the day the fourth hour finally arrives.

On that day I am going to go down into the ash and the rising water to pull my father out — because he's down there, the diver, wading toward me across the drowning town with a lantern for six years, and I cannot leave him, I have already proved to the whole watching world that I cannot leave the people I love in the water, whatever the sum says. And on that day both sides will be standing in the water with me. The Vigil, to stop me changing the hardest-frozen moment there is, because they know exactly what the bleed of that would cost — they have a white church and a drowning city to prove it. The Palimpsest, to *make* me change it, to use my father's face as the kindest-looking hook there is and get me to crack open the holiest moment on earth so they can write the whole world over inside it. And Lena, who does not yet know which side she'll be standing on, and neither do I, and that not-knowing is the only wild card I've got.

And me. Twelve years old, going on the age you have to be to do a thing like this, which is an age nobody ever is. With a lantern that crossed the seam and stayed lit. And no living memory of either of my parents' faces, only two photographs and a warm hand I can't picture. And the knowledge, which I would give anything to un-have, that to save my father I will have to become, completely and forever, the thing I chose against in a painting when I was younger and better: someone who reaches into a true and terrible moment and rewrites it, and makes it stick, and calls it love.

That's the thing I did, in the end, that made so many people so angry. Or it's the thing I *didn't* do. I've told you all along that I'd get to it, careful, and not out loud, and I still can't, not quite, not yet — but you can see it now, can't you, the shape of it, coming up out of the water like the old text under a scraped page. You know the choice. You've known it since the flooded square on my sixth birthday. Let the true and terrible thing stay true. Or rewrite it, and save him, and be exactly what I fought.

---

The night we got home to Amsterdam, my mum opened the door, and I didn't know her face until she said my name — *Sam* — and then I knew her all at once, the whole of her, everything but the picture of her, and I put my arms around my mother whom I'd spent to save my best friend, and I held on, and she held on, and neither of us said what we both knew, which is the family way, and she made hagelslag even though it was midnight, and I ate two slices pressed down hard so the sprinkles stick, which is the correct method, and I will not be discussing it further.

And then I went up to my room, where the lantern burns on the windowsill to this day, warm and lit and real, come all the way across the black water and the six years and the seam. And I lay down on my bed as the green numbers ticked toward four. And I could feel the dream coming up under me the way the tide comes up under the pont — but it's a different dream now. Now I know the place. Now I know it's my father wading toward me through the fire and the flood, patient as tide, carrying the only warm light in the world, coming to reach me before I come in after him. Now, when the diver lifts the lantern to the little fogged window in his helmet, and the breath blooms grey on the glass, and the face swims up behind it that I've known since I was six and never once could name —

I look. I make myself look. And I still can't tell whose face it is.

It's my father's. I'm nearly sure. It's the face in the two photographs, older, sadder, breathing on the glass.

Except sometimes, in the half-second before the fog thickens and the ripple smears it away, it isn't my father's face at all.

Sometimes it's mine. Older. Sadder. The face of a man who came back from the water changed, and has been trying to warn a boy across all the thin years since, in the only two words the seam will carry.

*Not yet.*

But soon.

# About the series

*Lull* is a middle-grade fantasy trilogy about Sam Vermeer, a boy from Amsterdam whose recurring 4 a.m. nightmare turns out to be a real place — and a real day, still coming. Across three fast, cliff-ended adventures, Sam is pulled between two secret societies of dream-walkers: the Palimpsest, who rewrite history for power, and the Vigil, who keep the record true. Slowly he learns that "keep the record true" and "make the world better" are not the same thing — and that the line between saving history and stealing it is as thin as the line between real and fake.

Each book is a complete adventure carrying one series mystery: what is the nightmare, when is the date, and who is the figure wading toward Sam through the water with a light.

To save everyone he loves, Sam will have to find a third thing neither faction ever found — and pay for it with the only currency big enough.
