**Content note:** A middle-grade fantasy adventure for readers roughly 9–13, with crossover appeal to older readers. Contains peril, grief and loss, a long-missing parent, and a costly, hard-won ending, all handled age-appropriately. A frightening-but-not-horror register: the catastrophe of Pompeii is kept as sky, ash, and rising water rather than shown through bodies, and there is no gore and no graphic violence. A parent returns changed rather than magically restored, and the hero pays a real, lasting price to bring everyone home. This is the third book of a trilogy; it reads best after Books One and Two.

# Chapter One — The Fourth Hour Comes

I promised you the date.

I've been promising it for two whole books now, dangling it and snatching it back, and the girl's right about me, it's a bad habit and I know exactly where I got it — *away,* *not yet,* *I'll tell you when I can tell it careful.* My whole family talks around the thing in the middle of the room. It's how we survive each other. But you've come all this way, down two floods and a mirror city, and you've earned it, and more than that — I've worked out, finally, that a date I keep un-said is a date I keep a little bit *pointed at me,* and I am so tired of it being pointed at me. So here it is. I'm going to write it down, and it's going to cost me, the way I always told you it would, and then it'll be yours and I'll be a little more free of it, and we can both stop flinching.

The date in the clock, the one that swam up out of the brass over the flooded square and went hard and true and would not slide — the date my father read the week before he went under, the date both sides have been circling like two dogs around one bone —

it's my birthday.

Of course it is. It was always going to be. The nightmare started at four in the morning on my sixth birthday and it has been counting, patient as tide, toward another one ever since. The fourth hour of a birthday. The exact hinge the Lull is built to close over a child — the year the seal is supposed to set, world.md would say, if world.md were a book you were allowed to read. My sixth birthday was the one where the seal didn't take. And the date at the bottom of the clock, the day the whole war lands in the water, is the fourth hour of my *thirteenth.* The morning I stop being twelve. Which, if you've been keeping count, and Fenna has, is tomorrow.

There. It's written. The specials board's out. I feel exactly as bad as I said I would and I'm not going to make a thing of it.

---

I want to tell you about the year, because a year happened, and books skip it and I won't, because it's the year I found out what it's like to be a weather system.

After Venice I came home to Amsterdam and my mum whom I couldn't picture opened the door and I knew her the second she said *Sam,* and we didn't discuss it, and I ate two slices of hagelslag pressed down hard, and then I went upstairs and lay under the burning lantern and waited for the war to come and grab me.

It didn't. That's the thing nobody warns you about being the most important person alive: mostly it's *waiting.* Serafina had told me the shape of it and she was right down to the grain. Nobody recruited me anymore and nobody took me, because whoever took me started the war, and neither side was sure it would win the war, so both sides did the next thing. They *watched.* And they made sure the other one was watching too, so that we all just — held. A whole year of holding.

You'd know a Vigil watcher by the tired eyes and the night-shift jobs: a woman who read a newspaper on the same bench by the Prinsengracht every single morning and never turned a page; the new caretaker at school who kept the corridors too clean and looked at me a half-second too long. You'd know a Palimpsest watcher because the Lull would go *soft* near them, sweet, like the air just before you fall asleep in a warm car — a man in a good coat who was always three shops down, whom I passed on a Tuesday and again on the Thursday and again the Tuesday after, and each time I felt that lovely dangerous drowse come up in me, *nothing's wrong, everything's fine, why don't you sit down.* I learned to bite the inside of my cheek. Pain holds against the Lull. Wessel taught me that, on the pont, in the grey. Bite down and the world goes true again and there's the man, three shops down, smiling, not there to hurt me. Just to *count* me. To make sure I was still on the table.

Wessel didn't come, most of that year. You remember why — *if I stand next to him now I'm just one more person the other side has to count.* He was right and I hated it, exactly as he said I would. He sent word sometimes, by the silent channel the Vigil have, a single line read out by whoever brought it. *Sleeping? Bite down. Not long now.* Once, near the end: *Your dad was proud of a thing he never got to be proud of. Ask your mother. I've said too much.* I asked my mother. She went very still at the sink, and said *later,* and *later* is a word my family uses to mean *when I'm braver,* and I let her have it, because I was learning that you can love someone by not making them be brave before they're ready.

And I got worse. I should tell you that plainly, because it matters for what comes. I got *worse,* the way my father did the year before he went under. My memory started going soft in patches — not the big losses, not the faces the backwash took clean, but small true things wearing thin the way a photo wears where a thumb keeps touching it. I'd remember a morning two different ways and not be able to tell which was the true one. Fenna started writing things down *for* me — not just her record now, mine too, little notes in her small furious hand pressed into my coat pocket. *You did eat. It's Thursday. Your mum's on nights. The man in the coat is not your friend.* I have those notes still. When you can't trust your own head, a friend who writes it down for you is the truest thing there is. Hold that. It's the whole answer, at the end, and I want you to have had it early, the way I didn't.

---

The dream changed, too, that year. I have to tell you how, because it's the thing that finally made me understand, and it frightened me worse than any of it.

For seven years I had stood in the flooded square and watched the Lantern Man wade toward me across the black glass, one slow brass boot at a time, and been unable to move, my feet grown down into the water, waiting for the light to reach my face.

Now, some nights, I was on the *other side of the lantern.*

I'd be down in the cold — not standing in the square but *coming across it,* looking out through a little round fogged window, my own breath blooming grey on the inside of the glass, and a heavy brass arm swinging a warm light ahead of me, and far off across the water, small and pale and frozen, a *child,* standing shin-deep in the flood in rocket pyjamas with a paper crown floating by his knee, watching me come and unable to move. And I was wading toward him, patient as tide, one enormous slow boot at a time, and I could not go any faster however I tried, and there was something I had to tell him before the fog thickened, some warning, some name, and I could never reach him in time, and I'd wake with my chest aching from a breath I'd been holding for a boy who was me.

I told Fenna. Of course I told Fenna. She wrote it down and then she put her pen in the crease and looked at me the way she does when she's about to say the true thing nobody's saying.

"So you're both of them now," she said. "In the dream. The scared kid in the water, and the diver coming for him." She tapped the notebook. "You know what that means, right. It means whoever the diver is — your dad, or you grown up, or both — he isn't coming to *get* you, Sam. Serafina said it. He's coming to *reach* you. He's got a warning he can't get out in time." She was quiet a second. "And now you're dreaming his side of it. Which means it's close. Which means you're nearly the diver too."

---

I want to end this part where the last two ended, in my own room, in the last of the dark before four, because I owe you the symmetry and because it's the last ordinary night I got.

The lantern burned on my sill, warm and lit and real, come all the way across the black water and the seven years and the seam. My father's mirror stood crated in a still-room across the city where Serafina had shipped it, waiting for me to be ready. Down on the counter my mum had left the money and a note, *werk, ik hou van je, bijna-jarige* — *work, I love you, almost-birthday-boy* — because she was on nights, and because in our house you leave love in writing when you can't leave it in person. I'd learned to keep every one of those notes.

I lay under the lantern and I could feel the dream coming up under me like the tide comes up under the pont. Tomorrow I would be thirteen. Tomorrow, at the fourth hour, the circling stopped and the whole war landed in one place at one moment, and I would go down into the ash and the rising water to pull my father out, and become, to save him, the thing I'd chosen against in a painting when I was younger and better.

I did not want to be brave about it and I wasn't. I lay there and I was just scared, a boy in rocket pyjamas he'd grown out of and kept anyway, and I bit down on the inside of my cheek to hold against the drowse, and I said out loud to the empty room, the way you do when you want a grown-up and there isn't one: "Not yet. Please. Not yet."

And close against my ear, from nowhere and no one, calm and almost gentle, the way you speak to a kid about to touch a hot pan, the girl I'd known for seven years said the thing she had never once said in all that time. She didn't say *not yet.*

She said, "It's time, Sam. I'm sorry. It's finally time."

And downstairs, three hours before my mother would be home, someone knocked on our front door in the dead middle of the night — three knocks, slow, patient, spaced like brass boots — and I heard the letterbox lift, and a rough tired voice I had missed more than I knew how to say came through it into the dark hall, low, so as not to wake a street.

"It's Wessel, kid. Get dressed. Bring the lantern. It's time."
# Chapter Two — Everyone at the Water

Wessel filled our hallway the way he'd always filled the wheelhouse of the pont — too big for the room, smelling of diesel and cold water and the strong black coffee he wasn't supposed to have — and when I came down the stairs with the lantern in one hand and my coat half on he looked at me for a long moment in the dark and did not say anything kind, which is how I knew it was as bad as I thought.

"You've grown," he said finally. It was an accusation. "You're nearly the height of him."

"I don't remember how tall he was," I said, because I don't, and because I've stopped pretending things about my father to spare other people. The face went first, in the museum. The height went sometime this year, quietly, the way the small things go.

Wessel's jaw worked. "No," he said. "You wouldn't." He looked at the lantern burning in my hand, and something crossed his tired face — grief, or fear, or the particular thing Wessel does that's both — and he said, "Right. Sit down at your own kitchen table, kid, because your mother's not on nights."

"She left a note. She said —"

"She's in the front room," said Wessel, "and she's been waiting up for both of us, and she has a thing to give you and a thing to tell you, and I'll not have you go south without hearing it, because I made your father a promise once that I broke, and I don't break two."

---

My mum was sitting in the dark front room with the lamp off, which is a thing she does when she's being brave, and on her lap was the shoebox from Venice, the one from under her bed, and next to it on the settee was a second thing I'd never seen: a canvas roll, oiled and old and heavy, tied with a leather cord gone black with handling.

"Gefeliciteerd," she said. *Happy birthday.* It was still an hour to four. It was still, just, the day before. But she said it anyway, and her voice cracked on it, because she knew — of course she knew, she'd married a darner, she'd always known everything — that she might not get another clean chance to say it to my face while she could still be sure I'd remember hers.

She patted the settee. I sat. Wessel stood by the door like a man guarding it, which he was.

"Your father made this," she said, and put her hand flat on the canvas roll, "and Serafina kept it, and she sent it north with his mirror, and I've had it a year and I couldn't — " she stopped. Started again. "It's his kit. A darner's kit. His needles, his salt, his true-glass, the little tools for mending a bleed. And the key." She untied the cord and folded the canvas open across both our laps and there they were, socketed in loops of leather: fine bright needles, a stoppered jar of grey salt, a disc of smoked glass, a folding knife, and a heavy old brass key, the shackle-key from the shoebox, the one that goes to nothing in this house. "He wore this open across his chest when he darned. The way you wear his lantern. I want you to have it now, because tomorrow you're going where he went, and you shouldn't go with empty hands."

I ran my thumb over the needles. They were cold, and true, and they did not swim when I looked away and back. My father had held these. My father, whose face I'd spent to keep forty dead men true, whose hands I'd spent to keep my best friend breathing.

"Mum," I said. "Wessel said — he said ask you. About a thing Dad was proud of that he never got to be proud of."

My mother closed her eyes.

---

Here is the thing my mother told me, in the dark, an hour before the fourth hour of my thirteenth birthday, and it is the last piece of my family I got before the war, so I'm going to give it to you carefully.

"When your father went under," she said, "for the first year I kept his face. I had it perfect. I'd close my eyes and there he was, every line of him. And it was *unbearable,* Sam, because a face you love perfectly on a man who's never coming back is not a comfort, it's a knife you can't put down. I couldn't sleep. I couldn't look at you, because you had his eyes and I couldn't stand it. I was going under myself, in the slow ordinary way people do, the way that hasn't got a magic name." She opened her eyes and they were wet and steady. "And one night I decided to let it go. His face. On purpose. I told myself: I will keep that he loved me, and that he loved you, and that he was gentle and reckless and collected impossible promises, and I will let go of the *look* of him, because I cannot carry the look of him and raise his son at the same time. And in the morning it was softer. And a week later it was gone. And I could breathe."

I sat very still. Because I knew that smooth warm place she was describing. I had two of them now, where my parents' faces used to be, and I'd thought the backwash put them there, the magic, the tax.

"You *chose* it," I said. "You did what the backwash does. Without any magic at all. You just — decided to forget his face, so you could keep going."

"Everybody does, liefje," my mother said, and she took my hand, the one with the lantern-warmth still in it. "That's the thing the magic only makes *loud.* It's the oldest kindness there is and the oldest theft, and they're the same act, and you can't always tell which one you're doing while you do it. The Lull didn't invent it. The Lull is just what it looks like when the whole world does it at once, to be able to bear itself." She squeezed. "I'm telling you this because tomorrow someone is going to offer you your father back, and someone else is going to tell you to let him stay lost for the greater good, and *both of them are going to be right,* and I don't want you to think either one makes you a monster or a saint. It's just the sum. Everyone does the sum. What matters is whether you do it with your eyes open, and whether you can live in the house you build after." She stood, and kissed the top of my head, heavy and still, the way — the way someone used to, a warm hand, a coffee smell, a gap I go to and find nothing. "Now go and get your father. And Sam." Her voice went to almost nothing. "If you can only bring one thing up out of that water, bring yourself. I've done the sum too. One son is not less than one husband. It's not even close. Come home."

---

Fenna was waiting on the corner with a rucksack and two notebooks and an expression that dared me to argue.

"Don't," she said, before I'd opened my mouth. "You brought me to Venice and I nearly drowned and I'd do it again, and if you think I'm letting you go to the worst moment in the history of the world without someone to write down what actually happens, you've forgotten who keeps the record around here." She hefted the bag. "I've got dry socks and a torch and my nan's rosary that I don't believe in and a whole notebook that's just for tomorrow. You do the — " she waved a hand — "the god stuff. I'll do the true stuff. That's the deal. That's always been the deal."

I have thought about arguing with Fenna the way you think about arguing with the tide, which is to say I've admired the idea and never once tried it. And there's a thing I understand now that I didn't then, standing on the corner in the last dark: that letting her come was the most important decision I made in the whole war, more important than any working, and I made it by not fighting her, which is the only way I ever get anything right.

"Okay," I said. "Then we did it together, and you write it exactly."

"I always do," said Fenna. "Truer than you. You know that."

"I know that," I said. It's the thing I'm surest of in the world.

---

We went south the way the Vigil go, over the ground, on trains, because a boy who lights up every mirror between the Netherlands and the sea cannot fly toward a thin place at four in the morning without the whole seam standing open behind him. Two days of it. Amsterdam to Basel to Milan to Naples, the flat grey giving way to mountains and then to a hard bright coast and a sea too blue to trust, and Fenna wrote it all down, every station, every changed clock, every stranger in a good coat who got on when we did and off when we did, and Wessel sat across from us the whole way with his cap over his eyes not sleeping, one hand on the strap of the bag that had my father's mirror in it, wrapped in a Vigil still-cloth so it wouldn't wake and start showing the dead city to a train full of tourists.

And on the second evening the train came round a headland and there it was, across a bay gone gold and then bruise-coloured in the falling light: a wide flat plain of broken pale stone under a mountain, a mountain with its top blown off two thousand years ago and grown green and quiet since, sitting over the ruins the way a grown-up stands over a child who's done a thing there isn't a rule for.

"Pompeii," said Wessel, without lifting his cap. "The hardest-frozen moment there is. Ten thousand people holding their own end steady since before anybody wrote anything down." He tipped the cap back and looked at it, and at me, and his tired eyes were the grimmest I ever saw them. "Everybody's already here, kid. Both sides. They came for the same reason you did. Get some sleep on the true ground tonight, because tomorrow at the fourth hour we all go into the water together, and only some of us are coming out, and I'll not lie to you about which."
# Chapter Three — The Holiest Site

You can buy a ticket to the worst moment in the history of the world. That's the thing nobody tells you. There's a car park and a café and a lady who checks your bag, and school groups in matching caps, and a man selling cold water and little plastic gladiators, and you walk in through a gate in a wall past a sign with opening hours on it, into Pompeii, which is a town — a whole real town, roofless and pale and enormous, streets you can walk down with grooves worn in the stone by cart-wheels two thousand years ago, ovens and counters and gardens and painted walls, all of it standing in the hard blue morning exactly where the people left it.

We went in with the first tickets, the three of us and Wessel, at eight in the ordinary morning, twenty hours before the fourth hour, and it was so bright and so full of pigeons and schoolchildren and normal life that for about a minute I let myself believe it might be all right.

Then I looked at a clock.

There's a modern one over the ticket office, a plain white face, and I looked at it the way you do, to know how long we had, and the hands drifted. The numbers swam like fish in a bowl. I looked at Fenna, and she'd seen my face and was already writing, and I looked back and the clock said four, straight up, both hands, hard and true and certain, at eight in the morning, in the sunshine, with the pigeons.

I want to tell you what it's like to walk through the busiest tourist site in Italy when you're the one person in it who can feel the four-o'clock underneath. Everyone else saw a ruin — pretty, sad, interesting, mind the step. I saw a lid. A bright, loud, crowded lid, held down by ten thousand cheerful living minds all reaching for the boring story at once, and under it, pressing up through the worn stone, through the cart-grooves and the painted walls and the little plastic gladiators, the deep cold four-o'clock silence of a moment that never stopped happening. I'd put my hand on a warm sunny wall and feel, half a heartbeat behind, the same wall cold and shivering under falling ash. I'd watch a school group laughing by an oven and see, laid over them like a wet cloth, a family frozen at that same oven with their arms up against a sky coming down. The Lull was working so hard there you could almost hear it, a great soft hum of *isn't it interesting, isn't it long ago, wasn't it sad, buy a cold water,* and I understood why the Palimpsest loved the place and why the Vigil feared it: it's the one spot on earth where the worst moment there is sits a single thin inch under the most ordinary afternoon you can imagine, and almost nobody alive can tell.

"It's thin here," I said. My voice had gone small. "It's thin here all the *time.* Not just at four. All the time."

"Course it is," said Wessel, low, steering us off the main path into the shade of a wall where the school groups didn't go. "You're standing in the single most anchored moment in human history, layered under a working town two thousand years newer. The seam here doesn't close. It never closes. That's why they keep the place open and full of people — a crowd Lulls faster, remember, all those minds reaching for the boring story, *isn't it interesting, mind the step, buy a gladiator* — the busiest tourist site in Italy is the best lid there is on the worst thing there is. Nobody who comes here at noon feels the four-o'clock underneath. But you do. And tonight, when they close the gates and the crowd goes home and the lid comes off — " he didn't finish it.

---

Serafina was waiting for us in the garden of a ruined house, sitting on a fallen column in the shade with her hands folded on a stick, older than Venice, and Nico was beside her, taller than I remembered and wearing a look I'd never seen on his cheerful face, which was fear held very still.

"Joris's boy," said Serafina, and looked at me the long way, the way she had in her workshop, seeing his face on mine, except this time she didn't break. She'd used up her breaking. "You came. Of course you came. You collect impossible promises like your father, and this is the most impossible one there is, and you were always going to keep it." She thumped the stick once on the old stone. "Sit. All of you. I have to tell you what the two sides mean to do tonight, and it's worse than you think, and then I have to tell you the last true thing I know, and it's about the girl, and I've saved it two thousand years so you'll forgive me if I take a moment."

We sat, in the garden of a house where a family had once grown lemons, and Serafina told us the shape of the battlefield.

"The Palimpsest means to *open* it," she said. "You know that. Pompeii is ten thousand people all holding one instant — their own end — steadier than any painter ever held a moment, because terror and love hold a thing harder than skill ever could. If the First Draft can get an arc-level door down into that instant and *crack it* — change one thing and let ten thousand anchors hold the change true forever — they don't rewrite a church, or a city. They rewrite the *rule.* They prove the past was always soft, always theirs, and they write the whole world over from inside the one moment strong enough to make it stick. They have wanted this for two thousand years, and they have never had a door strong enough to open it." Her fierce old eyes found me. "Until you."

"And the Vigil?" said Fenna, pen moving. "What do they mean to do?"

Serafina was quiet a moment. "The Vigil means to *shut it,*" she said. "Forever. So the Palimpsest can never open it. And they have worked out — the tired clever librarians, the darners, the old men who keep the true record — that the only way to shut the hardest moment there is, so hard it can never be cracked, is to *anchor it from the inside.* To put a dreamer down into it, an arc-level dreamer, and have them hold it true with their whole self, and *stay.*" She let that land. "They mean to shut the door with you inside it, boy. Not to hurt you. They'll be gentle and grateful and they'll weep while they do it. They think it's the hard right thing — one dreamer, to keep ten thousand true and the whole world safe from the First Draft forever. They think it's the noblest darning there ever was." Her voice dropped. "They've done the sum, same as everyone. And on the arithmetic, God help us, they're right."

"So one side wants me to open it and be a god," I said, and my mouth had gone dry, "and the other side wants me to shut it and be a — a cork. Forever. In the worst moment there is."

"Yes," said Serafina.

"And my dad's down there in the middle of it either way."

"Yes."

I looked at Wessel. Wessel looked at the ground. "That's why I stayed away all year," he said, roughly, not looking up. "Not just so I wasn't one more to count. Because I'm *Vigil,* kid. And the plan I just heard Serafina say out loud — I've known it for a year. It's my side's plan. And I couldn't stand next to you carrying it. Keep the record true — I've said it my whole life, I believe it in my *bones* — and this is where it takes us: to a garden in a dead town, asking a thirteen-year-old to be a cork in the drain of the world so nobody worse gets to pull the plug." He finally looked up, and his eyes were wet and furious. "I don't have a better plan. That's the worst of it. I came anyway, and I've got your father's kit and no better plan, and I'll tell you the one true thing I've got, which is: whatever you decide down there, I'm not letting them do it *to* you. If it happens it's because *you* chose it. That's the only line I've got left to hold, and I'll hold it against my own side, and it'll cost me everything I am, and I don't care."

It was the bravest thing anyone said to me in the whole war, and he said it like it was a small thing, a tired man in a garden, and I have never loved him more.

---

"Now," said Serafina, into the quiet. "The girl."

The pigeons had gone. I hadn't noticed them go. The garden had gotten very still, the four-o'clock still, even though the sun was high, and I understood that Serafina had thinned the Lull around us on purpose, a little clearing, so that what she said next couldn't be softened or overheard or filed away.

"Your father heard her too, I told you that. *Not yet,* forty years apart, a father and a son, the same two words. And I told you he came to believe she wasn't warning him *off* the moment but *toward* it — *not yet, but when it's time, you must.*" She leaned on her stick. "He was half right, which is the most dangerous thing to be. He heard *when it's time, you must* and he thought it meant *un-write Pompeii.* Save the ten thousand. Do the greatest darning there ever was." Her old face twisted. "It didn't mean that. I've had forty years to be sure of what it didn't mean, because it's what killed him. It didn't mean *save them.* It meant something else, and I don't know what, because I'm not arc-level and I can't reach her, but I know this much, boy, and it's the last thing I have to give you before the fourth hour." She took my face in her two hands, hard, the way you hold something you've decided to save. "*She is not on either side.* Not the Palimpsest, not the Vigil, not the opening or the shutting. She has spent two thousand years saying *not yet* to every dreamer that moment ever snagged, holding a door against all of them — and no faction taught her to do it and no faction can make her stop. Whatever she's waiting for, it isn't a god and it isn't a cork. It's a third thing. And your father never found it, and it took him. So when you go down tonight, and both sides are screaming their sums in the water, and the girl says her two words in your ear — you *listen to her,* Sam. She's the only one down there who has never once wanted to use you. Everything I have believes that. It's why I sent you her voice in a mirror instead of keeping you safe on dry land. God forgive me. Go and find out what she's been holding the door for. I think it's you. I think it's always been you."

The light was going. It goes fast, on that coast. The gates would close soon, and the crowd would thin, and the lid would come off the worst moment there is.

And that's when a warm, familiar, dangerous voice spoke out of the long shadow of the garden wall, where no one had been a moment before, and the Lull came up sweet around us like the air in a warm car, and I bit down hard on the inside of my cheek to hold against it, and Fenna's pen stopped moving.

"Room for one more at the water?" said Lena Marchetti, stepping out of the dark in her slate coat, more real than the real stones around her, and she looked tired, and frightened, and for once not delighted at all. "Don't. Don't do the thing your faces are doing. I haven't come to take him." She looked at me, and something moved in her that I'd only seen once, kneeling in woodsmoke. "I told you I'd see you at the water, and that I didn't know which side I'd be standing on. Well. It's the water. And I still don't know. And I came anyway, because I couldn't stand to find out which side I am from a safe distance." Her jaw set. "So. That's all of us, isn't it. Everybody the boy loves and everybody who wants to use him, in one dead garden, at the end. Shall we go down and find out who we really are?"
# Chapter Four — Down

They close Pompeii at sunset, and lock the gates, and the crowd flows out to the buses, and for a little while the dead town belongs to nobody, and then, at the fourth hour, it belongs to everybody it ever belonged to.

We waited it out in the ruined house with the lemon garden, and I want to tell you what the waiting was like, because it's the last of the ordinary and you should have it. Fenna made us all eat, which is a Fenna thing, dry bread and cheese and a chocolate bar broken into exactly equal pieces, and she gave the biggest piece to Nico because he was youngest and most scared, and she wrote our names in the front of the fresh notebook, all of us, *Sam, Fenna, Wessel, Serafina, Nico,* and after a moment, her mouth twisting, *Lena,* and under it she wrote the date, my birthday, and the place, and *the true record, in case,* and I didn't ask in case of what.

Wessel checked my father's kit over one more time, laying each piece out on the still-cloth in the lamplight — the needles, the salt, the smoked glass, the folding knife, the shackle-key that went to nothing — and naming them under his breath the way you say a prayer you don't believe in but say anyway because your hands know it. "Needles for the seam. Salt for the bleed. True-glass to tell the false from the real. Knife for — " he stopped. "Knife for whatever a knife's for." He rolled it back up and pressed it into my hands. "Wear it across your chest. Over the suit. The way he did. It won't help you and it might. Both of those are true at once, which is the whole of this business."

Lena sat apart, at the edge of the lamplight, and did not try to charm anybody, which frightened me more than charm would have. Once she caught me looking and said, quietly, "I keep waiting for it to tell me. The sum. I've done it my whole life and it's never once failed to tell me the answer and now it's — quiet. I look at you and I do the arithmetic and it just — stops." She almost smiled. "Your mother's voice does that in my head, and I've never met your mother. That's your fault. You broke my sum, little bell. I don't know what I am without it."

"Maybe that's the start of being okay," said Fenna, not looking up from the notebook.

"Maybe," said Lena. "Or maybe it's how you drown."

---

At half past three by a watch that still worked, Serafina unwrapped the mirror.

My father's mirror. The tall one, floor to ceiling, the forbidden one, shipped from Venice and carried down through a dead town by a tired ferryman, and she stood it up against the garden wall and drew off the still-cloth, and it woke.

You've seen it in the last book, through my telling — the leaning pale streets, the ash falling like grey snow that isn't snow, the bruised purple-yellow sky, and far off across flooded stone a small warm point of light beginning, very slowly, to swing. But there's a difference between hearing about a door and standing in front of one at four in the morning knowing you're about to go through it. The mirror didn't show a picture. It showed a *place,* deep and cold and real, the way the surface of black water shows you it has a bottom you can't see. And the fourth-hour still came off it like cold off a freezer, and the whole dead town leaned toward it, and the pigeons that had come back went up all at once out of the dark and did not come down.

"You go the way your father went," said Serafina. "In the suit. It's not a costume, boy — you've dreamed it your whole life and never understood it. To go all the way down, into the hardest-frozen moment there is, deeper than a painting, deeper than a plague-night, you have to *seal yourself,* the way a diver seals against the pressure of deep water. The brass and the canvas and the little window — that's what an arc-level dreamer *looks like* going that deep. It's how you carry your own true air down into a moment that will try to make you part of it. Without it, you don't visit Pompeii. You *become* Pompeii. You hold your last breath forever, like everyone else down there." She nodded at the mirror. "Your father's is on the other side, where he left it. Reach in and take it."

I reached into the cold glass — it gave like the skin of water, closing round my arm — and my hand found canvas and brass, heavy and real and waiting, and I drew out a diving suit two thousand years old and forty years old and no years old at all, the suit from my nightmare, and it fit me the way the lantern fit my hand, which is to say it had always been mine and I'd just never known it.

Fenna helped me into it, because that's a thing you can't do alone, and neither of us said anything, and when the great round helmet went on and the world shrank to a little fogged window and the sound of my own breath, I understood at last what I'd been seeing behind that glass for seven years. Not a monster's face. A *diver's* face. Mine. Anyone's. The face of a person going down into the worst thing there is for somebody they love, breathing on the glass, patient because there is no way to hurry a descent like that.

Fenna knocked twice on the top of the helmet, which is a thing she'd decided meant *I'm here,* and I heard it come through the brass all muffled and far away, and then she pressed her face close to the little window so I could see her through the fog of my own breath, and she held up the notebook and a pen, and mouthed, very clearly, so I couldn't miss it: *I write down what happens. You come back and tell me. That's the deal.* And I nodded, which inside a diving helmet is a whole-body thing, an idiot bobbing, and she almost laughed, and didn't, because you don't laugh at the edge of the worst moment there is, you just make sure the last thing your best friend sees before he goes down is a face that isn't frightened, even when it is. She did that for me. I'll never be able to pay it back.

"The lantern," said Serafina.

I took it off my hip, the little brass ship's lantern, warm and lit, the one thing that had crossed the seam the other way — up out of the dream and into my bedroom, seven years ago, and burned on my sill every night since. And now it was going back down. Prop-echo, Fenna would call it later, when she explained to me why my own life had a shape. The thing that came up is the thing that goes back down. The light the diver carries across the flood was always going to be *my* light, carried by *me,* down to the boy in the water.

"It'll light the true from the false down there," said Serafina. "It's the one thing in that whole moment that isn't held by terror. Your father made sure of it. Follow it home."

---

Here is how you go through a mirror into the hardest-frozen moment in the history of the world, in case you ever have to, which I hope with everything I've got left you never do.

You put your hand flat on the cold glass. It gives. You lean in, and the garden and the lamplight and your friends' frightened faces slide sideways and away, and there's a moment of nothing, the moment you never remember how you got there, and then —

Cold. First the cold, climbing up your legs. Then the silence, the deep wrong four-o'clock silence of a place that has stopped being *now.* Then, when your eyes learn the dark, the light-with-no-source that lit my dream every night for seven years — everything faintly, sourcelessly grey, the ash coming down slow as feathers, no clean shadow anywhere.

And then Pompeii.

Not the ruins. Not the roofless pale walls with the school groups. The *town,* whole, the night it died — leaning close-built houses with their lamps still lit in the windows, and the ash falling on everything, and the ground shivering, and beyond the low seaward roofs the bay drawn back and rising, a black wall of water coming up to meet the fire coming down. And the *people.* That's the thing I wasn't ready for, that nobody can be ready for. Ten thousand people, held. Mid-step. Mid-word. Mid-*everything.* A man in a doorway with his arm around a woman, both of them looking up at the mountain, frozen in the exact instant of understanding. A girl frozen running with a baby held against her. A dog. A baker frozen reaching for a shutter. All of them lit sourcelessly grey, all of them held at the last breath, all of them, I understood with my whole cold body, *real* — not statues, not ghosts, but people, caught in the one instant they'd all held together so hard it had never let go, for two thousand years.

I stood in the middle of them in my brass suit with my warm lantern and I was the intruder, the ghost, the thing that shouldn't be there, and I have never in my life felt so small or so wrong or so completely, unbearably sad.

"Not yet," said the girl, close in my ear, calm and almost gentle. But she didn't say it like a warning this time. She said it like *hello.* Like *you came.* Like a person who has been alone in a room for two thousand years hearing the door open at last.

I lifted the lantern. Its warm light swung across the frozen grey, across the held faces, across the falling ash — and there, at the edge of it, small and pale and not frozen, not held, standing in the ash and looking straight at me with eyes that moved, that were *awake,* was a girl.

About my age. Barefoot, in a rough tunic gone grey with ash, with her hair cut short and uneven like someone had done it in a hurry with a knife. And she looked at me and my lantern across two thousand years of the worst night there ever was, and she smiled the saddest, most relieved smile I have ever seen, and she said, in the voice I'd known since I was six:

"Hello, Sam. I've been holding the door for you. Come and see. But quietly — he's very close to the edge now, your father, and I can't hold him and the whole night both for much longer, and I've been so tired for such a long time."
# Chapter Five — The Girl Who Says Not Yet

Her name was Vibia.

She told me that first, before anything else, standing in the falling ash with the frozen town around us, and I understood why she led with it: because for two thousand years she had been a voice and no name, *the girl who says not yet,* a thing that happened to dreamers, and she was so hungry to be a person again that she said her own name the way you'd drink water after a desert. Vibia. She'd been thirteen. She was still thirteen. She had been thirteen for two thousand years.

"Don't take the helmet off," she said, when I reached for it, wanting to see her properly. "Not yet. That's a real *not yet,* Sam, the old kind — the suit is your air, and if you take it off in here you'll start to belong to the moment, and then you'll be like me, and one of us being like me is enough." She said it lightly, the way brave people say the heaviest things, and it went into me like a needle. "Walk with me. I'll tell you while we walk. I've had a long time to work out how I'd tell it, if anyone ever came who could hear."

So we walked through the last night of Pompeii, the awake girl and the boy in the brass suit, past the held people at their held last breaths, and she told me.

---

"I was a dreamer," Vibia said. "Like you. The rare kind — one to a city, my grandmother said, and she was one too, and she taught me the way your Serafina teaches, in secret, at night. We didn't have your words. We didn't have the Palimpsest or the Vigil, not by those names, though they were here, oh, they were already old even then, the ones who rewrite and the ones who keep. We just knew that some of us could wake in the dream and hold a moment steady, and that it was dangerous, and that you never, ever held a moment you were *inside of.*" She stepped over a frozen chicken. "And then the mountain came apart."

The ash fell. The ground shivered under us and did not stop.

"I was on the shore," she said. "With my little brother. Everyone was running for the boats, and the sea had gone wrong, drawn back and coming up black, and the sky was falling, fire and stone, and I had my brother's hand and we were going to die, Sam, we were *right there,* the last instant, the water and the fire coming together and no boat and no time — and I did the thing you must never do." She looked at me. "I woke up inside it. I woke up inside my own last moment, awake and knowing it was real, and I held it. To stop it. To keep the wave from coming down on us one more second, and one more, and one more. I held my own death steady with my whole self because I could not bear to let it finish."

"And it — held," I said. My voice was small in the helmet.

"It held," said Vibia. "That's the thing nobody understands about this place, that your factions have never worked out in all their arguing. Pompeii isn't held by ten thousand terrified people. That's the story, and it's *nearly* true, and nearly true is the most dangerous thing there is. It's held by ten thousand terrified people *and one awake girl who will not let go.* I'm the pin, Sam. I've been the pin for two thousand years. The moment sets around a dreamer the way a pearl sets around a grain of grit, and I'm the grit. I'm why it never faded like ordinary past. I'm why it's the hardest-frozen moment in the world." She said it without pride, the way you'd tell someone the depth of a well you'd fallen down. "I meant to hold it a second. I've held it two thousand years. You can't let go of a thing this size once you've caught it. If I let go now, all at once, it doesn't gently un-happen. It *finishes* — the wave comes down, the fire lands, ten thousand deaths all arrive at once, and everything that grew up on top of the moment for two thousand years, every dreamer whose snag ever caught here, tears loose with it. So I hold. And I've learned to hold it so it *rests* instead of screaming, which took me about four hundred years, and I whisper to the ones who come, because they always come, this moment pulls dreamers the way water pulls downhill." She stopped walking. "And every single one of them wants to *use* it. Open it, said the ones who rewrite. Shut it harder, said the ones who keep. And I said the same two words to every one of them for two thousand years, because it was true every single time."

"Not yet," I said.

"Not yet," said Vibia. "Which never meant *never.*"

She was quiet a moment, walking, and then she told me the thing about the two thousand years that I think about most, of everything anyone told me in the whole war, and I'll give it to you exactly.

"People think being alone that long would make you mad," she said. "And it nearly did, at the start — a hundred years, two hundred, screaming into a held moment where no one can hear because everyone's frozen at their own last breath. But you learn a thing, if you last. You learn that the moment you're holding isn't empty. It's full. It's ten thousand people at the truest instant of their lives, loving each other, holding each other, looking up together — and I got to *know* them, Sam. All of them. I've had two thousand years to walk this town and learn every held face, and I know the baker's name and the name of the dog and which two of the frozen lovers had quarrelled that morning and made it up, and I have kept them, in here — " she touched her own chest — "the only way anyone could, one witness holding the whole town's last day. That's what I was really doing all that time. Not just holding the door shut. *Keeping them.* Being the one who knew. I just never had anyone to tell." Her voice went very small. "That's the whole loneliness, in the end. Not being alone. Knowing something true and enormous and having no one to tell it to. If you ever want to know what the worst thing there is really is, Sam, it isn't the wave. It's that."

I have never forgotten it. It's half of why I do what I do now. But I'm getting ahead of the telling.

"It meant," she went on, "*not this way, and not by you, and not until the one comes who can do the third thing.* And I didn't know what the third thing was, Sam. I want to be honest with you, the way you're honest, I can hear that you're honest, it's why I could bear to keep the door for you. I've held this moment for two thousand years and until about a year ago I did not know what I was holding it *for.* I only knew it wasn't opening and it wasn't shutting-harder, because I'd watched both of those in dreamers' hearts and both of them are just me, forever, times ten thousand. Another pin. A bigger prison." Her ash-grey eyes found mine. "And then a year ago a boy in the north held a painting shut and paid his father's face for it, and rang so loud the whole record turned to look, and I felt it, all the way down here, and I thought: oh. Oh, there he is. The one my brother and I have been waiting for. And I started saying *not yet* to *you* differently. Not *don't.* *Soon.* *Get strong enough. Get old enough. Get here.*"

"Your brother," I said, because I'd caught it, the *my brother and I.*

Vibia's face did a thing I can't describe and won't try to. "He's here," she said. "Held. In the moment, at the last breath, where I caught him. That's the other half of why I never let go. Letting go finishes him too. I've had my little brother's hand in a held second for two thousand years, Sam. I can't wake him and I can't lose him and I can't put him down. That's what holding a moment true by force *is,* in the end, when you do it out of love. It's not noble. It's just a hand you can't unclench." She looked at me steadily. "Your Vigil want to make you into me. Your father tried to become the opposite of me and it took him. I've watched a thousand years of clever, brave, loving people arrive at exactly the two wrong answers. So I held the door. For someone who might find the third one. I'm so tired, Sam. I'm so tired I can't tell you. But I held it. Come and see your father. And then — please — I've earned the right to ask it — tell me you've got a third thing. Because I've been thirteen for two thousand years, and I would very much like to stop."

---

She led me down a leaning street toward the seaward edge of the town, where the frozen wall of black water hung against the sky, held mid-fall, a wave two thousand years from breaking. And I want you to understand what I was feeling, walking behind that brave tired girl in the ash, because it changed everything I did after.

I'd come down here to save my father. That was the whole plan. Pull Joris out of the water the way I'd pulled Fenna, forge whatever I had to forge, pay whatever it cost. Do the Palimpsest's own act, for love, and be the thing I fought, and never mind.

And now there was Vibia. And her little brother. And ten thousand held people. And the truth Serafina had reached for and never quite caught: that there were not two answers here, the god and the cork, the opening and the shutting. There was a third thing, and it was standing right in front of me being unbearably brave, and I did not yet know what it was, but I knew — the way you know the true things, the way I've always known them, up out of the middle of me before I can decide to say it —

I knew that whatever I did, I could not walk out of this moment and leave one more child holding a hand she couldn't unclench. Whatever saving my father meant, it had to be the *same* saving that let Vibia go. It was both of them or it was the wrong answer. That was the shape of the third thing, even before I knew its name.

"There," said Vibia softly, and stopped, and pointed with one ash-grey hand down the last street to the water's edge, where the black wave hung frozen over the shore.

And out on the drawn-back sea, waist-deep in the held black water, under the falling fire, was a man in a brass diving suit exactly like mine, wading toward the town one slow boot at a time, patient as tide, carrying a lantern that had gone out — a man who was not frozen and not held and not free either, a man caught halfway between the awake and the drowned, going under so slowly you could watch it happen, reaching for a shore he was never going to reach.

The Lantern Man. My father. Wading, forever, toward the town he'd come to save and couldn't, in the worst moment there is, on my birthday, at the fourth hour.

"He can't hear you," Vibia whispered. "He's too far under. He's been going under for forty years, Sam, one step at a time. But you're arc-level, and you've got air, and you've got a light that's still lit." She looked at me, and there were two thousand years of tiredness in it, and one year of hope. "Go on. Go and see your father's face. It's the last thing I can give you before the others come, and they're coming, I can feel the seam tearing open behind you — both sides, all at once, into the water. Go quickly. Look at him. And then you're going to have to be the third thing, and I don't know how, and neither do you, and it has to be in the next few minutes, and I'm sorry, I'm so sorry, I held it as long as I could."
# Chapter Six — The Diver

I waded out to my father through the held black water, and it did not part around my legs and close silent the way it does in the dream — it *pushed,* cold and real and heavy, because I was really there, in the suit, arc-level, carrying my own air down into the worst moment there is.

He was bigger than me. He was the height I'd forgotten, the height Wessel had accused me of nearly reaching. His suit was older than mine, the brass gone green, the canvas rotted soft at the seams where forty years of a slow drowning had worn it, and his lantern hung dead and dark and full of black water from his hand. And he was moving — that was the terrible thing, the thing I hadn't understood from watching him wade toward me all those years — he wasn't held like the ten thousand, frozen at a last breath. He was *going.* Still going. Still, after forty years, taking the next slow step toward a shore he would never reach, because he was an intruder like me, awake in a moment that wasn't his, and the moment had been pulling him under one step at a time for four decades and hadn't finished yet.

I got in front of him. I put my brass glove flat against his chest, the way you stop someone at the edge of a road, and I felt him lean into it, still walking, walking into my hand, unable to stop, and through the two little fogged windows, mine and his, an arm's length apart in the falling fire, I looked at my father's face.

---

I have to tell you what I saw, because people made me say things about this afterward that weren't true, and this is the true version, the truest I've got, and Fenna wrote down what I told her the moment I came up so I know I'm not softening it.

The window fogged, the way it always did. His breath, blooming grey on the inside of the glass, in and out, patient, the breathing of a man in no hurry because hurry had stopped meaning anything to him a long time ago. And through the fog, before it thickened, I saw a face.

And it was my father's. I knew it the way you know your own name. Not from the photographs — the photographs are of a man asleep, and this was a man awake, drowning slowly with his eyes open — but I knew it, the backwash had taken his face out of my memory and here it was in front of me being given back, older and sadder and more tired than any face should be allowed to get, and it was *him,* it was Joris, it was my dad, and I made a sound in the helmet that I'm not going to write down.

And then the fog shifted, the way it always did in the dream, and for half a second, before the ripple of my own breath smeared it, the face behind the glass wasn't my father's at all.

It was mine.

Older. Sadder. A grown man's face with my eyes in it, breathing on the glass, looking out at a boy in a brass suit with a lit lantern, trying to say something the fog wouldn't carry.

And I finally understood the thing the dream had been trying to tell me for seven years, the thing that had frightened me worse than the water, worse than the wave, worse than anything — the thing that had made me start dreaming *his* side of it, the diver's side, coming across the flood toward the frozen boy.

The face behind the fogged window was never one face. It was never *Joris or future-Sam,* one or the other, a mystery to solve. It was *both,* and it always would be, because they're the same face. A father going down into the worst thing there is to save his child, and a son going down into the worst thing there is to save his father, look *exactly the same* from the outside — a diver in a brass suit, wading across rising water, patient as tide, carrying the only warm light in the world toward someone they cannot bear to lose. The suit hides everything else. All you can see through the fog is the love, and love wears the same face across forty years, across the seam, across a father and a son. The nightmare was never asking me *who is the Lantern Man.* It was telling me *this is what you are made of.* This is what your family does. This is the shape of us: we put on the suit and we go down into the water for each other, and we look identical doing it, and it is the best and the most dangerous thing about us, and it is going to get us all killed, and I would not trade it.

I have to tell you what it does to you, standing that close to a parent you've grieved your whole life and finding out he's not dead, he's just *stuck,* he's been reaching for you the entire time and couldn't get through — because it isn't relief, not first. First it's rage. A hot ugly wave of it that surprised me. Forty years. Forty years of my mother alone at the sink, forty years of a smooth place where a face should be, forty years of *away,* the word my family uses so we don't have to say the real one — and it was all because a good gentle reckless man decided he was strong enough to un-write the worst thing there is, and wasn't, and got caught, and left us. I wanted to shout it into the fog. *You collected an impossible promise and it cost me my whole childhood.* And then the rage broke, the way a wave breaks, because the face behind the glass was so tired, and so sorry, and so exactly like the face I make when I've done a reckless loving thing that hurt somebody, and I understood that being angry at my father for diving into Pompeii to save ten thousand strangers was like being angry at my own reflection, and there's no bottom to that, so I let it go.

"Dad," I said, into the fog, knowing he couldn't hear, still walking into my hand. "Dad, it's Sam. I came. I read the date. I'm here. It's my birthday. I'm thirteen." My voice cracked all to pieces. "I've got your lantern and it's still lit. I've got your kit. Mum let go of your face on purpose so she could raise me and I got it back just now and it's *yours,* it's really yours, I'd know it anywhere." I pressed my glove harder against his chest to keep him from walking one more step under. "I'm going to get you out. I don't know how yet. But I'm not leaving you in the water. Nobody in this family leaves anybody in the water, that's the whole problem with us, and I'm not going to be the one who breaks it."

And behind the fog my father's face — or my own, older — did a thing that undid me. It smiled. Just barely. The exhausted smile of a man who has been trying to reach someone for forty years and has finally, at the very end, been reached instead. And his mouth moved, slow, forming two words the glass wouldn't carry, but I could read them, I'd read them my whole life on the other side of this exact window —

*not yet.*

He was saying it too. He'd always been saying it too. The warning he could never get out in time, wading across the flood toward the frozen boy — it was hers. It was always hers. *Not yet, son. Not this way. Don't do what I did. Wait for the third thing.*

---

And then the seam tore open behind me, and the war came into the water.

I felt it before I saw it — the moment shuddered, the held ash trembled, and the light-with-no-source flickered as two doors ripped open in the fabric of the frozen night, one cold and dry and many-throated, one warm and sweet as a car you fall asleep in.

Out of the first came the First Draft — the many-throated hole in the world, the inner circle of the Palimpsest, wearing a dozen faces and no face, wading into the held black water with the patient hunger of a thing that has waited two thousand years and can taste the end of the wait. And with it, at its shoulder, in her slate coat soaked to the waist, came Lena, and she would not look at me, and I could not tell, I truly could not tell, which side she'd walked in on.

Out of the second came the Vigil — not tired librarians now but the whole grim strength of them, darners in dark coats with their needles out and their salt ready, come to do the noblest terrible thing their doctrine had ever asked. And at the front of them, wading fast through the black water toward me with his cap gone and his face wet and set, came Wessel, my Wessel, holding my father's kit against his chest, having crossed the seam against his own side's plan to hold the one line he had left — *if it happens it's because you chose it* — and behind him, small and fierce and impossibly brave, holding a torch and a notebook above the rising water, came Fenna, who had refused to stay on dry land, who was already writing.

And from every direction at once, from the cold throats and the warm voice and the grim darners and the held wave hanging over all of us, the whole war looked at the boy in the brass suit standing between his drowning father and the awake girl who'd held the door for two thousand years, and the water began, very slowly, to rise, because Vibia's grip was finally, finally slipping, and everyone in the worst moment there is understood at the same instant that the circling was over, and the fourth hour had come, and it was time.

"Now, Sam," said the First Draft, in a dozen kind voices. "Open it."

"Now, Sam," said the Vigil, weeping, needles bright. "Let us shut it. With you inside. Forgive us."

And close in my ear, exhausted, at the very end of two thousand years, Vibia breathed: "*Now, Sam. The third thing. Whatever it is. Now or never — I can't hold it — now.*"
# Chapter Seven — The Sum, One Last Time

The water was at my knees and rising, and everyone in the world I loved and everyone who wanted to use me was standing in it with me, and the wave hung over all of us, two thousand years from breaking and getting closer, and they made me their offers, one last time, the two answers I'd been running from since I was six.

The First Draft spoke first, because the Palimpsest always speaks first — patient, warm, so reasonable it makes your teeth ache.

"Look at your father," it said, in a dozen gentle voices, and gestured at Joris, still walking, still going under against my glove. "Forty years in the water. Your mother alone. You with two smooth places where your parents' faces should be. And all of it — *all of it, Sam* — un-happens if you simply open the moment. You're already inside it. You're arc-level. You don't even have to reach far. Crack Pompeii, change the one thing, and let ten thousand anchors hold the change true forever. The mountain doesn't fall. The town lives. Your father never dives, because there's no catastrophe to dive into. He grows old in Amsterdam with your mother and drinks coffee he shouldn't and puts his hand on your back when you have a bad dream that never comes, because in the world you write, there was never anything to dream." Its voices dropped, sweet as sleep. "We are not asking you to hurt anyone. We are asking you to *save ten thousand people.* Your father's own dream. The greatest darning there ever was. Do the kind thing, Sam. You've always wanted to do the kind thing. It's your whole trouble. Do it."

And God help me, I wanted to. You have to understand that. It wasn't a cartoon of temptation. It was the best thing I could imagine, offered by the worst thing there is, and the worst thing there is had done its homework, and I stood in the rising black water with my hand on my drowning father's chest and I *wanted to open it,* and the wanting is the truest thing I'll ever confess to you.

I want you to sit in the wanting with me for a second, because if you skip past it you'll think I'm braver than I am, and I need you to know I'm not, so that when your turn comes you'll know that wanting it isn't the same as being weak. I saw the whole world it was offering. I saw a kitchen in Amsterdam with three chairs at the table instead of two. I saw my mother young again, un-widowed, laughing at something my father said. I saw a version of me who never woke at four in a flooded square, who never learned any of this, who got to be a boy who was just a boy — who read the whole cereal box on his sixth birthday and had a normal dream about it and forgot it by breakfast. It offered me *ordinary,* which is the one thing I'd wanted my whole life and never had, and it offered it for the price of a lie so beautiful I could barely find the seam in it. That's how the Palimpsest works. It doesn't tempt you with something wicked. It tempts you with the thing you'd have given anything for, and it's *right there,* and all you have to do is agree that the past was always soft.

Then the Vigil spoke, and it was worse, because the Vigil was crying.

"Don't listen, boy," said the darner at the front — an old woman with Wessel's kind of tiredness, needles bright in her wet hands. "You know the rule. There is no clean edit. Open this moment and the bleed isn't a centimetre of water a year. It's *everything.* Ten thousand deaths un-written and the whole record downstream of them tears loose — every life that ever grew in the ground Pompeii made, every story, every one of us, gone soft and rewritten in the First Draft's hand, the whole world a palimpsest with the old text bleeding through forever. You'd not be saving ten thousand. You'd be ending everyone, so the Palimpsest could rule what's left." She stepped closer through the water, weeping, holding out her hand. "There's only one way to keep it safe. Shut it. So hard it can never be opened. And the only thing strong enough to shut the hardest moment there is, is *you,* holding it true from the inside, forever. We'll be gentle. We'll be grateful. We'll keep your name in the true record till the end of the world. One dreamer, Sam, to keep ten thousand true and the whole world safe. It's the noblest darning there ever was. Your father would —"

"*Don't,*" I said, "tell me what my father would." And my father, under my glove, in his rotted suit, mouthed *not yet,* and I loved the darner for crying and I could not do what she asked. "You want to make me into Vibia. You want me to be a hand that can never unclench. You've done the sum, I know you have, one against ten thousand, and on the arithmetic you're *right* — " I was shaking, the water at my thighs now — "and I still won't. Because I know something you don't. Because I've *met* the cork you want me to be, she's standing right here, she's been thirteen for two thousand years holding her little brother's hand, and it isn't noble, it's just *sad,* it's the saddest thing there is, and I will not do that to Vibia and I will not do it to me and I will not let you tell yourselves it's the hard right thing when it's just the same theft as theirs with a nicer story on it."

"There you are," said the First Draft, delighted, to the weeping Vigil. "Do you hear him? He sees it. He sees there's no difference between us. He's arc-level and he's *seen the sum,* and the sum has no true side. So he'll do the kind thing instead. He'll open it." A dozen faces turned to me, hungry. "Won't you, Sam."

And here's the trap they'd built, and it was beautiful, and it nearly had me: they were both right about each other. That was the whole thing. The Palimpsest was right that the Vigil did the merciful sum too — Serafina proved it in Venice, one boy for a city. And the Vigil was right that opening Pompeii would end the world. They'd walked me to a place where both of my teachers were liars and both of my enemies were truth-tellers and the only two doors were *be a god* or *be a cork,* and every clever loving person for two thousand years had walked into that same room and picked one of those two doors, and every single one of them was still holding a hand they couldn't unclench. My father picked *god* and drowned. Vibia picked *cork* and went thirteen for two millennia. And the room was built so well that standing in it, with the water at my chest, I genuinely could not see a third door, and I want you to know that, because when they build that room for you — and they will — you are going to stand in it and see only two doors too, and you are going to have to trust that there's a third one even when you can't see it, on nothing but the word of a girl who says *not yet* and a friend with a notebook.

---

And that's the moment Lena chose.

She'd been standing at the First Draft's shoulder the whole time, soaked and silent, not looking at me, and I want to tell you exactly how it happened because it's the bravest thing I ever watched a person do and I've watched some brave people.

She looked at me. Finally. Across the rising black water, in the falling fire, with the wave hanging over all of us. And she did the thing she'd told me in Venice she'd never once managed in her whole clever life: she chose true when it cost her everything.

"He's not going to open it," said Lena. Quietly. To the First Draft. To her own masters. To the thing that had made her and taught her and given her the warm voice and the slate coat and the terrible arithmetic. "And he's not going to shut it either. I've been trying to work out for a year which side I'd stand on at the water, and I've just this second worked out the answer, and the answer is *neither,* the answer is *his,* the boy who broke my sum, because I have done the arithmetic every way there is and it always ends in a child holding a hand she can't let go of, and I am *done.*" Her voice broke, and steadied, and I have never heard anything braver. "I'm done, Sam. I'm standing with you. I don't know what the third thing is. But I know it's yours, and I know it isn't ours, and if I'm going to be near one person who chooses true when it costs them, I'm going to have to *be* one first." She turned her back on the First Draft — on the whole Palimpsest, two thousand years of it — and waded through the black water to stand at my shoulder, opposite my father, facing the wave. "So. Whatever you've got. I'll hold the seam for you. It's the one thing I'm better at than anyone alive. Do your third thing, little bell. I've bet my whole self on you having one."

The First Draft did not shout. It never does. It simply began, in a dozen cold voices, to let go — the way it had in Venice, the merciful murderer's trick, releasing the frozen moment it had helped Vibia pin so that the whole night would collapse around us and drown everyone in it and un-write itself in the falling. The wave shuddered. The held ten thousand trembled. The water surged up to my chest, cold and salt and rising fast, and Vibia cried out somewhere behind me, her two-thousand-year grip tearing loose all at once, and Joris went under to his shoulders against my glove, and Fenna was screaming from the dry edge, torch high, and there was no time, there was no time at all.

"NOW, Sam!" cried everyone at once — the Palimpsest *open it,* the Vigil *shut it,* Lena *do it,* Vibia *the third thing, now, NOW* —

And I didn't know what the third thing was.

I want to be honest, the way I promised, all the way down to the bottom. In the water, at the fourth hour, with the wave coming down and my father drowning and a girl's two-thousand-year grip tearing loose, I did not have a plan and I did not have an answer and I was thirteen years old and I was going to fail everyone I loved in the next four seconds.

And then, from the dry edge of the worst moment there is, over the roar of the collapsing night, my best friend Fenna Bakker held up a soaked notebook in the torchlight and screamed the truest thing anyone has ever said to me, the thing that saved the world, the thing she'd been trying to tell me since we were six years old on an ordinary morning that turned out to be a dream:

"SAM! YOU DON'T HAVE TO HOLD IT! YOU JUST HAVE TO *WRITE IT DOWN!*"
# Chapter Eight — What Fenna Knew

Here is the thing Fenna had known since we were six, that the Palimpsest never understood in two thousand years and the Vigil forgot four hundred years ago in a white church, that my father died not knowing and Vibia held a door open her whole endless life waiting for someone to remember.

You cannot keep a moment true by holding it.

That's the mistake. That's the whole mistake, both sides of it. The Palimpsest holds a moment down and *scrapes* it and writes over it. The Vigil holds a moment down and *pins* it so it can never be scraped. Vibia held her own death down so it could never finish. My father tried to hold Pompeii down and un-write it. Everyone, on every side, was *holding* — by force, with their whole self, forever — because they all believed the same wrong thing: that a moment is only as true as the grip on it, and that if you let go, it becomes soft, and someone can steal it.

And Fenna, who is a Sleeper, who has no reach, who cannot pull a coin or shadow-step or hold a painting shut — Fenna had spent seven years proving the opposite with a biro and a stack of cheap notebooks.

Because here's what the Lull can't touch. The Lull works on memory. It softens what happened into *you must have dreamed it,* and it does that to minds, one at a time, and it does it best to a mind that's alone and unsure and reaching for the boring story. But a thing that has been *witnessed, and told true, and written down, and freely remembered by people who choose to keep it* — that thing is not soft. That thing does not go *you must have dreamed it,* because you didn't dream it, there's the notebook, there's your friend who was there, there's the true record freely made and freely held. You can't scrape what a hundred people choose to remember on purpose. You can't un-write what's been told out loud and believed and passed on. Testimony is the one true thing the Lull was never built to soften, because testimony isn't a grip on the past — it's the past made a *shared choice,* and you cannot backwash a choice out of ten thousand people who go on choosing it.

The Vigil hoarded photographs and called them the true record and never understood their own archive. A photograph slips a Lulled mind's grip because a photo is just a thing, and a lonely mind rationalizes a thing. But a photo that a *community keeps and tells the story of* — that a grandmother shows a child, saying *this happened, remember it, tell your own children* — that's not a thing anymore. That's a covenant. That's the record kept true by love and telling, the way it was always meant to be kept, the way Fenna kept mine when my own head went soft, the way she'd been trying to teach me since we were small: *You did eat. It's Thursday. The man in the coat is not your friend.* Not holding my memory down by force. Just — telling me the truth, and letting me choose to keep it, so no Lull could take it.

I should have seen it years ago. It was in front of me the whole time, from the very first night, and I told it to you in Book One without understanding what I was telling you. Remember my sixth birthday? My father came into my room, and he didn't say *it's just a dream.* He sat on the edge of the bed and he put his hand on my back and he *listened* — he let me tell him the whole thing, the water and the man and the light, and he took it seriously, like it was the news. He didn't hold my fear down. He didn't smooth it over. He let me *say* it, out loud, to someone who believed me, and it got smaller, not because he pinned it but because it was witnessed. That's the darning that actually works. That's the only kind there ever was. My father knew it once, before Pompeii took it out of him — he just forgot it in the water, the way the Vigil forgot it in a white church, the way everyone forgets it the moment they get frightened enough to grab. The answer was never a stronger grip. The answer was always: *tell someone true, and let them help you carry it.* A six-year-old knows this. It takes a lifetime and a war to forget it, and it took Fenna screaming across a flood to give it back to me.

"YOU DON'T HAVE TO HOLD IT," Fenna screamed, over the collapsing night, "YOU JUST HAVE TO *WRITE IT DOWN!*"

And I finally, finally understood the third thing.

---

I let go of holding.

That's the part nobody believes, afterward, on either side, and it's why they were all so angry — I'll get to the anger, it's coming, it's the thing I promised you at the very start. But in the water, at the fourth hour, with the wave coming down, I did the thing no arc-level dreamer had ever once done at Pompeii: I stopped trying to grip it.

I didn't open it. I didn't shut it. I opened my whole self the way you open your arms, and instead of pushing the moment down, I *witnessed* it. All of it. Every one of the ten thousand. The man in the doorway with his arm round the woman, looking up, understanding. The girl running with the baby. The baker reaching for the shutter. Vibia's little brother, frozen at the last breath with his hand in hers. My father, drowning slow. Every held face at every held last moment — I *saw* them, truly, one at a time and all at once, not as a thing to keep or a thing to change but as a thing that *happened,* that was real, that mattered, that deserved to be *known* exactly as it was — and I held nothing down, I gripped nothing, I just bore witness to the whole terrible true moment with everything I had.

And Fenna, on the dry edge, wrote it down. Out loud, as she wrote, screaming it across the water so it would be *told* and not just held: "Ten thousand people. The mountain came apart on a bright morning and the sea came up and the sky came down and they held each other and looked up and it was real and it happened and we will not un-happen it and we will not forget it and we will *tell* it — " and Lena, at my shoulder, took it up, her warm voice that could push a whole crowd's Lull now pushing the *opposite,* a clearing, holding the seam wide so the truth could be *seen* — and Wessel, in the water, and the weeping Vigil darners, and Nico from the edge, and even, at the very last, some of the held people themselves seeming to lean toward it, toward being *known,* every witness the moment had ever wanted and never gotten —

and the moment did not collapse.

It didn't need Vibia's grip anymore. That's the thing. A moment held down by a lonely child in fear will finish the instant she lets go. But a moment *witnessed,* known, told true and freely kept by people who choose to remember it — that moment doesn't need a pin, because it isn't loose. It's *held by everyone who knows it,* the way the true record was always meant to be held, and there's no edge for the First Draft to get a fingernail under, because you cannot scrape a thing ten thousand living people have chosen to keep.

Pompeii went hard and true and certain under my open, empty, un-gripping hands — the way the clock in my dream went hard and true — not because I pinned it, but because for the first time in two thousand years it was *seen* whole and told true and no one was fighting over it. It just — was. The truest thing there is. A real and terrible morning, known at last, and safe forever, not because it was locked, but because it was *loved into the record* by people who refused to let it be either stolen or forgotten.

---

"Oh," said Vibia, behind me, in a voice I'll hear for the rest of my life. "*Oh.* That's — that's it. That's the third thing. You don't hold it. You *know* it. You let it be true out loud." She laughed, a cracked, astonished, two-thousand-year laugh. "I could have let go any time. Any time at all. I just had to have someone to tell it to first. I just had to not be *alone* with it." Her voice filled with tears. "I've been holding my brother's hand in the dark for two thousand years, Sam, because I thought if I let go he'd be forgotten and gone. And all I ever needed was one person to *know.* To write it down. To say *Vibia had a little brother and she loved him and they were on the shore and it was real.* That's all. That's all it ever was."

"It's written," I said. My own voice was gone to pieces. Fenna was writing it. Lena was saying it. It was in the record now, true and told and freely kept, and no Lull would ever soften it and no Palimpsest would ever scrape it, because that's what it means to keep the record true — not to hold it, but to *tell* it, together, on purpose, forever. "Vibia. It's written. He's known. You can let go."

The wave hung over us. The water lapped at my chest. My father drowned slow against my glove, still an intruder in the moment, still not part of it, still going under. And the First Draft, understanding at last what was happening, that its two-thousand-year prize was slipping shut in a way it could never crack — because you cannot rewrite what has been *witnessed and told* — began, in a dozen cold furious voices, to do the last thing it had, the thing I should have seen coming, the thing that made me pay the price the frame has been hinting at since the very first page.

"Then if we cannot *open* it," said the First Draft, in twelve voices gone to ice, "and we cannot *hold* it — we will *drown* it. All of you. The witnesses. The record only stays true, boy, if the ones who keep it *live.* Kill the tellers and the telling stops and the moment goes soft again, in a generation, in an ordinary way, no magic required — and then it's ours after all." The wave shuddered, began, at last, to fall. "You've made the record depend on *people.* How fragile. How *kind* of you. Let's see how the record does when we take the people."

And the black wall of water two thousand years from breaking finished breaking, and came down on all of us at once.
# Chapter Nine — The Fourth Hour

The wave came down, and I did the only thing left, and I'm going to tell it to you plainly because it's the middle of everything and you've earned the plain version.

I didn't hold the wave. I'd just learned better than that. You can't hold the ocean and you can't hold the truth by force and I wasn't going to try either one again.

I *sorted.*

Here's the thing about that moment that took me weeks to be able to explain to Fenna, who wrote it down anyway while I was still stammering. Pompeii, the true moment, the ten thousand — that was real. That happened. It didn't need saving because it was already true, witnessed now, kept forever. The wave falling on the town was *part of the true moment,* and I let it fall, because that's what keeping the record true means: you don't get to un-write the terrible true thing, not even now, not even to be kind. The ten thousand died. They died on a bright morning under a mountain and it was real and I let it be real, and that was the hardest thing I have ever done, harder than holding the Night Watch, harder than forging a night for Fenna, because every gentle reckless Vermeer bone in me screamed to reach out and stop the water and I *didn't,* I let ten thousand people I'd just come to know die true, and I will carry that for the rest of my life, and it was right.

But there were people in that water who were *not* part of the true moment. Intruders. Trespassers in a moment that wasn't theirs. My father, who'd waded in forty years ago and never belonged there. Vibia, who'd been thirteen for two thousand years and belonged to a life the wave had taken long ago. Her little brother, held at a last breath that had already, truly, happened — but held by his sister's grip, not by his own true death, which meant he was, right now, in this instant, *hers to release.* And Fenna, and Wessel, and Lena, and Nico, and me — the living, the tellers, the ones the First Draft meant to drown so the telling would stop.

And I have to stop here and tell you the hardest true thing in the whole three books, because Fenna made me promise I wouldn't skip it to make myself look kinder, and she's right, and she wrote down that I promised.

There was a moment — half a second, less — where I could have saved more. Where the reach was open all the way and the wave was falling slow the way everything falls slow when you're holding a moment thinned, and I could feel, at the edge of what I could hold, the ten thousand. Not as history. As *people,* right there, each one a held last breath I could have reached for. And I wanted to. Every gentle reckless bone. I could have tried to Anchor a mother and her baby out of the true wave the way I'd Anchored Fenna out of 1630 — one, then another, then another — I could have tried to empty the shore.

And it would have been a forgery. It would have been exactly what my father did, exactly what killed him, exactly what drowns Venice. Because those ten thousand *belonged* to the moment. It was their true death. To pull them out wasn't a rescue — it was a rewrite, one life at a time, and each one would have torn the record a little more, and the tearing wouldn't have stopped at ten thousand, it would have run downstream through two thousand years of everyone who ever came after, and I'd have saved a mother on a shore and un-made a hundred thousand strangers to do it, and never known their names. That's the difference nobody in the war could ever hold in their head at once, and I had to hold it in mine in half a second with the water at my chest: *you can pull out the trespasser and you cannot pull out the true.* You cannot un-write a real death, not even a terrible one, not even a child's, not even to be kind — because kindness that rewrites the true record isn't kindness, it's the Palimpsest wearing your own face. I let them go. Ten thousand. I let the true wave take everyone it truly took, and I have never once been sure it was right, only that it was *true,* and by now you know those aren't the same thing and that I've spent my whole life learning to choose the second one when they part.

I couldn't stop the wave. So I pulled everyone who didn't belong to it *out of its way.*

Not a forgery. Not an edit. I didn't change one grain of what happened to Pompeii. I did the thing my father should have done and never learned how — the thing that is the whole difference between a rescue and a revision, the difference the entire war had never once understood: I reached into the true moment and I took out the people who weren't supposed to be in it, the guests, the trespassers, the ones love had dragged in, and I lifted them clear, and I let the true thing happen to everyone it truly happened to, and I saved everyone it never should have taken. That's a rescue. A rescue leaves the record true and pulls the living out of the water. A revision rewrites the water. My father drowned for forty years because he tried to rewrite the water. I just opened my arms and lifted out everyone the water was never meant to have.

---

I Anchored them to the living side. All of them at once, which is more than any dreamer has ever held, and it cost what it cost, and I'll tell you the cost in a minute because it's coming and it's the price the frame promised you.

I pinned my father to *now* — to a version of the world where Joris Vermeer came up out of the water, alive, an old tired man who'd been gone forty years and was coming home — and I did not rewrite one thing about Pompeii to do it, I just took the trespasser out of the flood and nailed him to the shore of the living, and he came loose from the moment with a sound like a held breath finally let go, and his rotted brass suit fell away, and he was just my dad, gasping, real, forty years older and blinking in the light of my lantern.

I pinned Vibia and her brother to *rest* — not to now, they'd been gone two thousand years, you can't Anchor a Roman child into a Naples morning, that would be the exact revision I refused — but *out of the held moment,* out of the endless last breath, into whatever comes after for people who died a long time ago and finally get to stop holding on. I don't know what that is. I'm not going to pretend I do, to make you feel better or worse. But I felt them go — Vibia and the little brother whose hand she'd held for two thousand years — I felt her let go of the door and the grip and the whole endless watch, and I felt her *rest,* at last, the way you feel a fever break, and the last thing she said, going, was not *not yet.*

It was: "*Now.* Oh, Sam. It's finally *now.* Thank you. Tell them I had a brother and I loved him. Tell them it was real."

I'm telling you. It's written. It was real.

And I pinned the living — Fenna, Wessel, Lena, Nico — to the dry edge, out of the wave's reach, out of the First Draft's murder, and I held the seam wide for them with the last of what I had while they scrambled clear onto the true ground of a dead town at four in the morning.

---

And the door shut.

Not because I held it. Because there was nothing left to hold. The moment was witnessed, told, true, and safe. The trespassers were out. The living were clear. The dead were let rest. And Pompeii — the real, terrible, true Pompeii, the hardest-frozen moment in the history of the world — simply *closed,* the way a book closes when the story's done, not pinned, not scraped, just *finished being fought over,* true forever because it was finally, only, *known.*

The First Draft screamed then. All twelve throats. I'd love to tell you it was frightened but it wasn't, it was *furious,* the specific fury of a thing that has waited two thousand years for a door and watched a thirteen-year-old close it in a way that can never be opened again — because you cannot crack a moment that no one is holding down, that lives now in testimony instead of in a grip, that a whole record of living people have chosen to keep true on purpose. There was no edge left. There would never be an edge again. Pompeii was out of the war forever, not locked, but *loved into the true record,* which is the one place the Palimpsest can never go.

"You've ruined it," it said, in twelve cold voices, thinning, its warm sweet Lull curdling to something like grief. "Two thousand years. And you didn't win it, boy, you didn't take it, you didn't even *keep* it — you gave it *away.* To everyone. You made it belong to anyone who'll remember it." A pause, and then, in a voice almost like respect, almost like the thing Lena used to have: "We'll never forgive you. Not because you beat us. Because you proved we were never necessary. That the record was always keeping *itself,* in the telling, and all our scraping and all their pinning was two old men wrestling over a book that a child could just — *read aloud.*" It began to fold up, out of the water, out of the shut moment, a many-throated shadow going home defeated for the first time in two thousand years. "You've made a great many people very angry, Sam Vermeer. And some of us will be angry forever. Which is a long time. When you consider what we can do to time."

And it was gone.

---

And then the backwash came for its coin.

I felt it reach into me, gentle as a grown-up taking a knife off a toddler — and I braced, the way I always brace now, for it to take a face, my father's, my mother's, Fenna's, some warm thing I'd never get back. Because I'd held more true at once than any dreamer ever had. I'd witnessed ten thousand deaths and Anchored a father out of forty years and let a Roman girl rest and held the seam for four living people, all in the space of one falling wave, and I knew — the way you know the true things — that a working that size charges a price that size, and I was about to pay the most anyone had ever paid.

The water drew back off the shore. My father lay gasping in the ash, alive, real, coming home. Fenna had her notebook. Vibia was at rest. The door was shut forever. Everyone I loved was out of the water.

And the backwash reached past all their faces, deeper, to the thing I had never once thought of as fragile because it had been there my whole life like the ceiling, like the canal, like breathing —

and it took hold of the one thing big enough to pay for all of it.

I'll tell you what it was in the next part, because it's the whole cost, and it's the thing that made *me* angry, for a while, before I understood it was the best trade I ever made. But I'll tell you the shape of it now, standing in the drawn-back water with my father breathing at my feet and the fourth hour finally, finally passing:

it did not take a face.

It took the water itself.
# Chapter Ten — The Cost

It took the water.

I don't mean the flood. I mean *the water* — the thing in me that could reach it. The seam. The reach. The whole of what I was.

I need to explain this properly, because it's the thing that made *me* angry, for about a year, and it's the price the frame promised you from the very first page, and I don't want you to think it was taken from me. I *chose* it. In the half-second the backwash reached in, I understood what it meant to reach for, and I could have flinched it toward a face instead, and I didn't. I steered it. Because I'd finally worked out the one thing that would make the door stay shut forever, and it was the only thing big enough to pay for a working that size, and it was the same thing, and that's how I knew it was right.

Here's the problem I hadn't said out loud yet, even to Fenna. Pompeii was safe *now* — witnessed, told, kept true by living people. But I was still arc-level. I was still the one dreamer in a generation who could open the hardest moment there is. And the First Draft was gone but not dead, and the Vigil was still out there with its needles and its terrible noble sums, and as long as *I* existed — a boy who could crack any moment, hold any night, forge any lie and make it stick — the war didn't end. It just moved. To the next thin place. The next held moment. Me, forever, a loaded gun on a table, both sides circling until the next day worth a war, for the rest of my life, and my children's, because a thing like me doesn't get to be safe and doesn't get to make anyone near me safe either.

Unless the gun unloaded itself.

So when the backwash reached in for its coin, I gave it the reach. All of it. I let it take the thing that let me wake inside a dream, the thing that let me pull a coin and shadow-step and hold a painting shut and Anchor a father out of forty years — I let it take *the dreamer,* clean, the way it had taken my father's face, and I paid for the rescue of everyone I loved with the entire fact of what I was.

I felt it go. I felt the water in me drain out through the hole where an arc-level dreamer used to be, and I felt the seam close over me, all the way, tighter than the Begijnhof, tighter than Serafina's darned mirrors — closed, sealed, *done* — and I understood, kneeling in the ash next to my gasping father, that I would never dream a true dream again. That I was a Sleeper now. Less than a Sleeper. Someone the seam has shut its door on for good, so completely that there is nothing left in me for either faction to want, nothing to recruit, nothing to seize, nothing to fear. The gun unloaded. The war, for me and mine, over — not because I won it, but because I spent the only thing that kept it going, which was me.

The door to Pompeii is shut forever, and here is the lock: it can only be opened by an arc-level dreamer, and I was the last one anyone knew of, and I am one no longer, and I made very sure, on the way out, to close it behind me and throw the reach away so I could never be made to open it again. That's the true version. That's what I spent. Not a face. *The magic.* The whole wondrous terrible gift. I was twelve when I found out dreams were real and thirteen when I gave up ever visiting them again, and I did it in a heartbeat, without the sum, the moment it was everyone I loved — which is, I've come to think, the only good reason anyone's ever done anything.

---

And then — and this is the part I didn't expect, the grace I hadn't earned and got anyway — the true record came home.

Because here's the thing about a backwash. It's a debt. Every edit I'd ever forced across the line had charged me a true memory to pay for a false thing held. My father's face, to pay for forty men held true in a painting. My mother's face, to pay for a night forged to save Fenna. The record in *me* had gone soft in patches all year, worn thin, unreliable, because I'd spent so much of it holding lies steady.

But I hadn't forged anything at Pompeii. I hadn't held one false thing. I'd witnessed the true moment and let it be true and pulled the living clear and paid with my reach — and a reach, once you give it up, doesn't owe anything. There was no lie left in me needing a true memory to pay for it. The debts closed. And the smooth warm places where my parents' faces used to be — the gaps my mind kept going to like a tongue to a missing tooth —

they filled back in.

My mother's face came back first, kneeling in the ash, before I'd even seen her, just — *there,* in my head, whole, the tired real smile she keeps just for me. And then my father's, which I was looking straight at, gasping and alive and forty years older and *home,* clicked true and stayed, and I knew it, and I knew I'd keep knowing it, because there was nothing false left in me to charge against it. I gave up every dream I'd ever have, and I got back every true thing I'd ever lost. That's the trade. That's the whole trade. The magic for the memory. The wonder for the faces. And I would do it again, and I'd do it faster.

---

I should tell you about the anger, because I promised you at the very start that I made a great many people very angry, and now you can see all of it, up out of the water like the old text under a scraped page.

The Palimpsest is angry forever, because I closed their holy site in a way that can never be opened, and worse, because I proved they were never necessary — that the record was always keeping itself in the telling, and two thousand years of their scraping was a lie about a book that anyone could just read aloud. That's a thing you don't forgive. Not when you can do what they can do to time.

The Vigil is angry too, and that's the one that took me longest to bear, because they were the good ones, I *chose* them, and I unmade them. Because if the record is kept true by testimony — by witnessing and telling and choosing to remember — then everything the Vigil ever did by *force* was not just unnecessary, it was the same crime as the Palimpsest's, wearing a kinder face. Four hundred years of drowning Venice to hold a forgery shut. A plan to make a thirteen-year-old a cork in the drain of the world. All of it — *all of it* — never needed. They could have just *told the true story* of 1630, freely, kept it alive in the telling, and let the forged night go soft and heal. They held instead. They pinned. They sacrificed. And I stood in the water and proved, in front of all of them, that keeping the record true never once required a single one of their beautiful terrible sacrifices, and some of them have never forgiven me for making four hundred years of grief unnecessary, because unnecessary grief is the hardest kind to have carried.

I need to say one more thing about the anger, because it's the one that's mine, and I've been circling it for three books the way my whole family circles the thing in the middle of the room.

I was angry too. At myself. Not for the dive — I'd do the dive again. For the *after.* Because giving up the reach sounds noble when I write it out like this, clean, *I chose it, I steered it,* and all of that's true. But there's a version of the truth I told Fenna at three in the morning about a month later that's truer, and she wrote it down, so I can't pretend I didn't say it: I mourned the magic. I *mourned* it, for a year, the way you mourn a person. I'd been the most important dreamer in a generation, the one both sides would burn the world for, the boy who could hold history steady in his open arms — and I traded it for hagelslag and a father who doesn't always know the year and a mother whose face I can finally picture, and every one of those is worth more than the magic, I know that, I knew it when I chose it, and I *still* woke up some mornings that year with my hands aching to reach for a seam that wasn't there anymore, grieving a wonder I'd thrown away on purpose, feeling like a boy who'd sold the only interesting thing about himself. That's the ugly true part. You don't get to make a beautiful sacrifice and also get to feel good about it. You make it, and then you live in the house you built, and some days the house is just a house, and you loved the palace, and the palace is gone, and you did it, nobody did it to you. Fenna read me that back once, exactly as I'd said it, and then she said, "Yeah. And?" And I said, "And I'd still choose the house." And she wrote *that* down too, underneath, because it was also true, and both of them are the record, and you don't get to keep only the noble half.

But some of them — the best of them — wept, and put down their needles, and went home to Venice, and did a thing that had not been done in four hundred years. I'll tell you about it in the next part. It's the best thing that came out of all this, better than my father, and it's Serafina's, and it's the reason the water's going down in a city you can still go and see.
# Chapter Eleven — Coming Up

We came up out of the mirror into the lemon garden a little after four in the morning, on my thirteenth birthday, six of us who'd gone down and — I have to write this carefully, because it's the count that matters — six of us who came back, and one man more.

My father came up last, through the cold glass, and Wessel and I caught him under the arms, and he came out into the garden of a dead town gasping and blinking, an old tired stranger with my eyes, forty years gone from a world that had kept turning without him, and he looked around at the ruined walls and the lamplight and the frightened faces, and then he looked at me, at the boy holding him up who had his eyes and was wearing his lantern and his kit, and his mouth worked, and the first thing my father said in forty years, the first thing I ever heard him say that I'll actually remember, was:

"Sam?" A whisper, wrecked, disbelieving. "You're — you got so *big.*" And then, breaking all to pieces the way I break, the way he taught me across a seam without ever being there to teach me: "You came down. Into the water. For me. Oh, no. Oh, Sam, you weren't supposed to — that's the whole thing I've been trying to — for years I've been trying to *warn* you —"

"I know, Dad," I said. "You said *not yet.* I heard you. The whole time. I heard you." And I put my hand flat on his chest, the way I'd stopped him drowning, the way someone once put a hand flat on my back between the shoulder blades and said *I've got you.* "But I didn't do what you did. I promise. I didn't rewrite the water. I just took you out of it. It's okay. You can stop trying to reach me now. I reached you."

And my father, Joris Vermeer, who went under before I was old enough to keep him, put his forehead down against the top of my head, heavy and still, and he was warm, and he smelled of cold salt and, underneath, faintly, impossibly, of coffee, and he stayed there, and I stood in a dead garden holding my father up at the fourth hour of my birthday, and I want to be honest with you the way I promised: it was not a clean happy ending. He was forty years strange. He'd lost a world. He had a wife who'd let go of his face on purpose and a son who was a stranger and a gift at once. It was going to be hard for a long time. But he was *up,* and he was *true,* and no one had been rewritten to do it. It was a rescue. It was the good kind. It was the only kind I'd allow.

---

I'll tell you where everyone landed, because you've carried them this far and you deserve to set them down gently.

**Vibia** is at rest. I told you that and I meant it and I won't spoil it by pretending I know what rest is for a girl two thousand years dead. But I know she let go, and I know her last word was *now,* and I know her brother went with her, and I know that somewhere in Fenna's notebook — in the true record, freely told, freely kept — there is a page that says *Vibia had a little brother and she loved him and they were on the shore at Pompeii and it was real,* and as long as that's told and kept and passed on, she is *known,* which is the only thing she ever needed and the only kind of forever any of us really gets. I say her name sometimes, out loud, on purpose. You can too. That's how it works. That's the whole magic, in the end, the only one I've got left.

**Lena** walked out of the Palimpsest that night and never walked back in, which is a thing that had, as far as anyone knows, never once been done. She has no faction now and no sum and, for a while, no idea who she was without either. She came north with us. She and my mother get on alarmingly well, which frightens me. She's teaching Fenna Italian and Fenna's teaching her how to write a true thing down without making it prettier than it was, which Lena finds almost impossibly hard and is getting better at. She told me once, drying dishes in our kitchen, "I spent my whole life being the warmest voice in the room, little bell, and it turns out the trick was never warmth. It was *telling the truth even when it's cold.* You did that to me. I'll never forgive you." She was smiling when she said it. She meant it both ways. That's Lena.

**Nico** you'll meet again in a moment, so I'll only say here that of everyone who went down into the water that night, he's the one it changed for the better in the simplest way: a cheerful frightened boy who found out that the thing he was good at — telling a true story so it stays told — was the thing that mattered most all along, and got to spend his life doing it instead of darning by force in the dark. Not everyone comes out of the worst moment there is lighter than they went in. Nico did. I'm glad. He earned it holding a torch over rising water for a boy he barely knew.

**Fenna** — I could write a whole book about Fenna and one day I might, except she'd correct every page, which is exactly why it would be a better book than this one. She kept the record. She kept *me.* She went down into Pompeii with no reach and no magic and no way to protect herself and the only tool she had was the truth and a biro, and it turned out the truth and a biro were the strongest things in the water. She's studying to be — she'll hate that I told you this, so tell no one — a historian. Someone who keeps the true record of what really happened, out loud, on purpose, so it can't be stolen or softened. She says it's not related to any of this. She's lying, and she knows I know, and she lets me know she knows, and that's the whole friendship in one sentence.

**Wessel** went back to the pont. He stands in the wheelhouse in the grey and loads bikes and keeps the record true the honest way now, the way he always half-knew was the real way — he *tells* it, to the ones who need telling, carefully, in a safe room, with the door shut. He and my father sit on the ferry sometimes and don't talk, which is how tired brave men are friends. He said one thing to me, the morning after, that I've kept: "You unmade my whole life's work, kid. Forty years of darning by force, and you proved a girl with a notebook could've done it better." He took a pull of coffee. "Best thing anyone ever did to me. Now I don't have to hold it all myself. Now I can just *tell* someone and go to sleep." I think that's the closest Wessel ever came to saying he was happy.

---

And **Serafina.** I saved her for last, and the best of it, because it's the thing that outlived all the rest and you can go and see it.

She went home to Venice, and she called the darners together — the old ones, the tired ones, the keepers of the forged night of 1630 that built a white church and drowns the city a centimetre a year — and she told them what happened at Pompeii. That the record isn't kept true by holding. That four hundred years of darning the bleed had been the same mistake, the same grip, the same fear of letting go. And then Serafina did the bravest thing of anyone in this whole story, braver than my dive, and I'll fight you if you say otherwise:

She *told the true story of 1630.*

Out loud. The whole thing. That the Venetian Vigil forged the plague-night to save the city, and that it was a theft dressed as a mercy, and that the water has been rising ever since to pay for it. She wrote it down and she made it *known* — not hidden, not held, not darned into silence — she put the true record into the telling, into the hands of people who would choose to keep it and pass it on. And she stopped holding the forgery shut.

And the bleed began to heal.

Not all at once. Not a miracle. But a forged moment that everyone has agreed to hold down by force keeps bleeding forever, and a true story that people freely tell and keep — even a shameful one, *especially* a shameful one — that story doesn't bleed, because it isn't a lie being pinned, it's a truth being carried. The water in Venice is going down. Slowly. A little each year. You can look it up, if you know how to look, which now you do. They think it's the new pumps. It isn't the pumps. It's an old woman who finally told the truth and let a four-hundred-year-old grip unclench, and a city that's learning, one told story at a time, that you keep the record true by *keeping* it — out loud, together, on purpose — and not by drowning yourself to hold it down.

Serafina died last winter. Old, and at peace, in a city that had started to come up out of the water because of her. Fenna and I went to Venice for it — the first time I'd been back — and Nico met us at the station, taller still, a darner in his own right now, doing it the new way, the telling way. And at the end, Serafina had left me one line, murmured to Nico to pass on, and it's the last thing my father's teacher ever said to me, and I'll give it to you to carry:

"Tell Joris's boy he found the third thing. Tell him his father can rest now too, even the parts of him that came home. And tell him — " Nico's voice had caught here — "tell him the girl in the water was right about him all along. It was always him. It was always going to be him. *Not yet* was just her way of saying *soon.*"
# Chapter Twelve — Not Yet

I've been telling you this whole story for three books now, and I've never once told you who you are.

That was on purpose, the way most of what I've kept back was on purpose, and now it's the end, and you've earned it, and it's the last thing I've got. So. Here's the truth about you, and about me, and about why I've told it the way I've told it — careful, and not out loud, and in a safe room, with the door shut.

You're six. Or near enough. And a few weeks ago, on a birthday, you woke up at four in the morning from a nightmare that felt too real, and it had water in it, or fire, or a long empty street where the letters wouldn't hold still, and a light swinging toward you across the dark. And you told a grown-up, and they said *it's just a dream, go back to sleep,* which never once helped anybody. And it happened again. And you started to notice things other people unsee — a man who ducks round a corner that isn't there, a clock that won't keep a number, a dog barking at nothing that isn't nothing.

And somebody who loves you got frightened for you, the good kind of frightened, and they brought you to me. Because they'd heard there was a man who knew about this. A man who came back. And they did the thing nobody did for me: they *chose to tell you,* carefully, before you found out by accident, with your whole face, the way I did.

That's who you are. You're the next one. You're a beacon, ringing at four in the morning, and both sides can hear you, and they're already circling, and you have a choice coming that no six-year-old should have to make and you're going to have to make it anyway. And I couldn't stop that. Nobody can. But I could do the one thing that was done wrong for me. I could sit you down in a room with the door shut, and tell you the truth, all of it, carefully, so that when it comes — and it's coming, it comes for everyone who wakes at four — you'll know the rules. You'll know the tells. You'll know the Lull, and how to bite down against it. You'll know that both sides will offer you the world and both of them will be lying in the exact same way, and that the third thing is real, and that its name is *keep the record true, out loud, together, and never by force.*

That's why I told you. And now you know why it had to stay a secret, too — why not out loud, why never written large.

---

Because a story told too loud becomes a held thing. You understand that now; you've come all this way. A moment that everyone points at, that gets carved in stone and made *official* and pinned down hard — that's a moment the Palimpsest can find, and reach, and try to crack. If I stood in a square and shouted what I did at Pompeii, if I made it *famous,* I'd be making it an anchor, a fixed loud moment with an edge someone could get a fingernail under. The way you keep a true thing safe forever isn't to shout it. It's to *tell* it, quietly, to one person at a time, in a safe room, and let them choose to keep it and tell the next one. Soft. Alive. Passed hand to hand. Un-scrapeable precisely because it was never pinned. That's the whole trick. That's the trick I bought with everything I had. A story kept soft and true and freely handed on can never be stolen, because there's no one place to steal it *from.* So I've told it to you the only way it's safe to tell — as a confession, in a quiet room, from one who woke at four to the next one who did.

And there's one more reason it's a confession, and I owe it to you straight, because I promised at the very start that when I say *this is what happened* I mean *this is the truest version I've still got,* and you should know why the record's soft even in me.

I can't check it anymore.

I gave up the reach at Pompeii. I told you that. What I didn't tell you is what it's like, after, to be a person who used to be able to go down into the true moment and *see,* and now can't. My dreams are just dreams now — the little films, the leftover worry and cheese and whatever I saw on the tram. I don't visit anymore. I can't go back and look. So all I've got left of the truest years of my life is *memory,* ordinary soft human memory, wearing thin where my thumb keeps touching it, and the notebooks. Fenna's notebooks. When I get it wrong — and I do, more every year, I've told a version of the Rijksmuseum three different ways and I genuinely don't know which is true — I go to Fenna, and she reads me back what she wrote down while it was still happening, and I say *oh, yes, that's right,* and I keep it a little longer. She's the true record. I'm just the one who tells it. I always was. That's not a sad thing. It's the whole point. You keep the record true by *needing each other to keep it* — one who dives and one who writes it down, one who does the god stuff and one who does the true stuff, and neither is more important, and I promise you, on everything, the one with the notebook is the one who saves the world. Find yours early. Don't wait like I did.

---

You'll have noticed, if you've been the kind of reader Fenna would approve of, that I never did tell you my father's whole story from the inside — what forty years in the water was actually like, down there, going under one step at a time. That's because I can't. I gave up the reach; I can't go and ask the moment; and my father, when I ask him, goes quiet and far away and then says, "It was cold, Sam, and I was always nearly home," and that's all he's got, or all he'll give, and I've learned not to make the people I love be brave before they're ready. So there's a hole in this record right where my father's forty years should be. I'm leaving it a hole. A true record has holes in it — the honest ones do, the ones kept by people instead of forced by force. The Palimpsest never has holes; it writes over every gap with something smooth and total, and that's how you know it's lying. If you ever meet a story with no holes in it, no *I don't know,* no *he wouldn't say* — run. It's being held down by someone. The true ones breathe.

My father is downstairs. I can hear him, faintly, doing the dishes with Lena, the two of them arguing cheerfully about the correct method for something. He's still forty years strange and probably always will be, and some mornings he doesn't know what year it is, and my mother takes his hand and tells him, out loud, on purpose — *it's this year, Joris, and I love you, and here's your son, and here's the coffee you shouldn't have* — and he keeps it a little longer. We keep each other true. It's what a family is. I understand that now. It was always the same magic as the big one; it just doesn't have a name because everybody's already doing it.

The lantern's on my windowsill. It burns still — warm, and lit, and real, come all the way across the black water and the years and the seam, down into the worst moment there is and back up again, and it never goes out and no match in my house can light it. I don't need it to see the true from the false anymore; I gave up the eyes that could. But I keep it burning, because a boy in the water dreamed it toward himself when he was six, and a diver carried it across a flood, and it is the only warm thing that made it all the way through, and I'll not let it go dark now.

And you, six years old, waking at four, at the start of the whole long thing — I'll give you the last of it, the two words I carried for seven years and finally got to trade in.

For seven years a girl said *not yet* in my ear. She wasn't warning me off. She was holding a door. She was buying me time to grow up enough, and get strong enough, and get *here,* so I could do the third thing and let her rest. *Not yet* never meant *never.* It meant *soon — and when it's time, you'll be ready, and you won't be alone.*

I'm not going to say *not yet* to you. Your door's already open. You woke at four; it's already begun. But I'll say the other half of it, the half she never got to say to me, the half I get to say to you instead, in this quiet room, with the door shut, as the truest gift I've got:

It's time. You're ready. And you won't be alone.

Now go and find the person who's going to write it all down for you. And when you're older, and it's your turn — tell the next one. Carefully. Out loud, but not too loud. In a safe room, with the door shut.

Keep the record true.

*Not yet,* the girl always said.

And then, at the very end, after two thousand years, she got to say the other word, and so do I, and so will you:

*Now.*

THE END

With this book the trilogy is **complete** — every question the series has carried, from the very first 4 a.m., is answered here.
