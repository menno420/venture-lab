# Chapter Nine — The Fourth Hour

*Lull, Book Three · draft*

The wave came down, and I did the only thing left, and I'm going to tell it to you plainly because it's the middle of everything and you've earned the plain version.

I didn't hold the wave. I'd just learned better than that. You can't hold the ocean and you can't hold the truth by force and I wasn't going to try either one again.

I *sorted.*

Here's the thing about that moment that took me weeks to be able to explain to Fenna, who wrote it down anyway while I was still stammering. Pompeii, the true moment, the ten thousand — that was real. That happened. It didn't need saving because it was already true, witnessed now, kept forever. The wave falling on the town was *part of the true moment,* and I let it fall, because that's what keeping the record true means: you don't get to un-write the terrible true thing, not even now, not even to be kind. The ten thousand died. They died on a bright morning under a mountain and it was real and I let it be real, and that was the hardest thing I have ever done, harder than holding the Night Watch, harder than forging a night for Fenna, because every gentle reckless Vermeer bone in me screamed to reach out and stop the water and I *didn't,* I let ten thousand people I'd just come to know die true, and I will carry that for the rest of my life, and it was right.

But there were people in that water who were *not* part of the true moment. Intruders. Trespassers in a moment that wasn't theirs. My father, who'd waded in forty years ago and never belonged there. Vibia, who'd been thirteen for two thousand years and belonged to a life the wave had taken long ago. Her little brother, held at a last breath that had already, truly, happened — but held by his sister's grip, not by his own true death, which meant he was, right now, in this instant, *hers to release.* And Fenna, and Wessel, and Lena, and Nico, and me — the living, the tellers, the ones the First Draft meant to drown so the telling would stop.

And I have to stop here and tell you the hardest true thing in the whole three books, because Fenna made me promise I wouldn't skip it to make myself look kinder, and she's right, and she wrote down that I promised.

There was a moment — half a second, less — where I could have saved more. Where the reach was open all the way and the wave was falling slow the way everything falls slow when you're holding a moment thinned, and I could feel, at the edge of what I could hold, the ten thousand. Not as history. As *people,* right there, each one a held last breath I could have reached for. And I wanted to. Every gentle reckless bone. I could have tried to Anchor a mother and her baby out of the true wave the way I'd Anchored Fenna out of 1630 — one, then another, then another — I could have tried to empty the shore.

And it would have been a forgery. It would have been exactly what my father did, exactly what killed him, exactly what drowns Venice. Because those ten thousand *belonged* to the moment. It was their true death. To pull them out wasn't a rescue — it was a rewrite, one life at a time, and each one would have torn the record a little more, and the tearing wouldn't have stopped at ten thousand, it would have run downstream through two thousand years of everyone who ever came after, and I'd have saved a mother on a shore and un-made a hundred thousand strangers to do it, and never known their names. That's the difference nobody in the war could ever hold in their head at once, and I had to hold it in mine in half a second with the water at my chest: *you can pull out the trespasser and you cannot pull out the true.* You cannot un-write a real death, not even a terrible one, not even a child's, not even to be kind — because kindness that rewrites the true record isn't kindness, it's the Palimpsest wearing your own face. I let them go. Ten thousand. I let the true wave take everyone it truly took, and I have never once been sure it was right, only that it was *true,* and by now you know those aren't the same thing and that I've spent my whole life learning to choose the second one when they part.

I couldn't stop the wave. So I pulled everyone who didn't belong to it *out of its way.*

Not a forgery. Not an edit. I didn't change one grain of what happened to Pompeii. I did the thing my father should have done and never learned how — the thing that is the whole difference between a rescue and a revision, the difference the entire war had never once understood: I reached into the true moment and I took out the people who weren't supposed to be in it, the guests, the trespassers, the ones love had dragged in, and I lifted them clear, and I let the true thing happen to everyone it truly happened to, and I saved everyone it never should have taken. That's a rescue. A rescue leaves the record true and pulls the living out of the water. A revision rewrites the water. My father drowned for forty years because he tried to rewrite the water. I just opened my arms and lifted out everyone the water was never meant to have.

---

I Anchored them to the living side. All of them at once, which is more than any dreamer has ever held, and it cost what it cost, and I'll tell you the cost in a minute because it's coming and it's the price the frame promised you.

I pinned my father to *now* — to a version of the world where Joris Vermeer came up out of the water, alive, an old tired man who'd been gone forty years and was coming home — and I did not rewrite one thing about Pompeii to do it, I just took the trespasser out of the flood and nailed him to the shore of the living, and he came loose from the moment with a sound like a held breath finally let go, and his rotted brass suit fell away, and he was just my dad, gasping, real, forty years older and blinking in the light of my lantern.

I pinned Vibia and her brother to *rest* — not to now, they'd been gone two thousand years, you can't Anchor a Roman child into a Naples morning, that would be the exact revision I refused — but *out of the held moment,* out of the endless last breath, into whatever comes after for people who died a long time ago and finally get to stop holding on. I don't know what that is. I'm not going to pretend I do, to make you feel better or worse. But I felt them go — Vibia and the little brother whose hand she'd held for two thousand years — I felt her let go of the door and the grip and the whole endless watch, and I felt her *rest,* at last, the way you feel a fever break, and the last thing she said, going, was not *not yet.*

It was: "*Now.* Oh, Sam. It's finally *now.* Thank you. Tell them I had a brother and I loved him. Tell them it was real."

I'm telling you. It's written. It was real.

And I pinned the living — Fenna, Wessel, Lena, Nico — to the dry edge, out of the wave's reach, out of the First Draft's murder, and I held the seam wide for them with the last of what I had while they scrambled clear onto the true ground of a dead town at four in the morning.

---

And the door shut.

Not because I held it. Because there was nothing left to hold. The moment was witnessed, told, true, and safe. The trespassers were out. The living were clear. The dead were let rest. And Pompeii — the real, terrible, true Pompeii, the hardest-frozen moment in the history of the world — simply *closed,* the way a book closes when the story's done, not pinned, not scraped, just *finished being fought over,* true forever because it was finally, only, *known.*

The First Draft screamed then. All twelve throats. I'd love to tell you it was frightened but it wasn't, it was *furious,* the specific fury of a thing that has waited two thousand years for a door and watched a thirteen-year-old close it in a way that can never be opened again — because you cannot crack a moment that no one is holding down, that lives now in testimony instead of in a grip, that a whole record of living people have chosen to keep true on purpose. There was no edge left. There would never be an edge again. Pompeii was out of the war forever, not locked, but *loved into the true record,* which is the one place the Palimpsest can never go.

"You've ruined it," it said, in twelve cold voices, thinning, its warm sweet Lull curdling to something like grief. "Two thousand years. And you didn't win it, boy, you didn't take it, you didn't even *keep* it — you gave it *away.* To everyone. You made it belong to anyone who'll remember it." A pause, and then, in a voice almost like respect, almost like the thing Lena used to have: "We'll never forgive you. Not because you beat us. Because you proved we were never necessary. That the record was always keeping *itself,* in the telling, and all our scraping and all their pinning was two old men wrestling over a book that a child could just — *read aloud.*" It began to fold up, out of the water, out of the shut moment, a many-throated shadow going home defeated for the first time in two thousand years. "You've made a great many people very angry, Sam Vermeer. And some of us will be angry forever. Which is a long time. When you consider what we can do to time."

And it was gone.

---

And then the backwash came for its coin.

I felt it reach into me, gentle as a grown-up taking a knife off a toddler — and I braced, the way I always brace now, for it to take a face, my father's, my mother's, Fenna's, some warm thing I'd never get back. Because I'd held more true at once than any dreamer ever had. I'd witnessed ten thousand deaths and Anchored a father out of forty years and let a Roman girl rest and held the seam for four living people, all in the space of one falling wave, and I knew — the way you know the true things — that a working that size charges a price that size, and I was about to pay the most anyone had ever paid.

The water drew back off the shore. My father lay gasping in the ash, alive, real, coming home. Fenna had her notebook. Vibia was at rest. The door was shut forever. Everyone I loved was out of the water.

And the backwash reached past all their faces, deeper, to the thing I had never once thought of as fragile because it had been there my whole life like the ceiling, like the canal, like breathing —

and it took hold of the one thing big enough to pay for all of it.

I'll tell you what it was in the next part, because it's the whole cost, and it's the thing that made *me* angry, for a while, before I understood it was the best trade I ever made. But I'll tell you the shape of it now, standing in the drawn-back water with my father breathing at my feet and the fourth hour finally, finally passing:

it did not take a face.

It took the water itself.
