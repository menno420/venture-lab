# Chapter Ten — The Cost

*Lull, Book Three · draft*

It took the water.

I don't mean the flood. I mean *the water* — the thing in me that could reach it. The seam. The reach. The whole of what I was.

I need to explain this properly, because it's the thing that made *me* angry, for about a year, and it's the price the frame promised you from the very first page, and I don't want you to think it was taken from me. I *chose* it. In the half-second the backwash reached in, I understood what it meant to reach for, and I could have flinched it toward a face instead, and I didn't. I steered it. Because I'd finally worked out the one thing that would make the door stay shut forever, and it was the only thing big enough to pay for a working that size, and it was the same thing, and that's how I knew it was right.

Here's the problem I hadn't said out loud yet, even to Fenna. Pompeii was safe *now* — witnessed, told, kept true by living people. But I was still arc-level. I was still the one dreamer in a generation who could open the hardest moment there is. And the First Draft was gone but not dead, and the Vigil was still out there with its needles and its terrible noble sums, and as long as *I* existed — a boy who could crack any moment, hold any night, forge any lie and make it stick — the war didn't end. It just moved. To the next thin place. The next held moment. Me, forever, a loaded gun on a table, both sides circling until the next day worth a war, for the rest of my life, and my children's, because a thing like me doesn't get to be safe and doesn't get to make anyone near me safe either.

Unless the gun unloaded itself.

So when the backwash reached in for its coin, I gave it the reach. All of it. I let it take the thing that let me wake inside a dream, the thing that let me pull a coin and shadow-step and hold a painting shut and Anchor a father out of forty years — I let it take *the dreamer,* clean, the way it had taken my father's face, and I paid for the rescue of everyone I loved with the entire fact of what I was.

I felt it go. I felt the water in me drain out through the hole where an arc-level dreamer used to be, and I felt the seam close over me, all the way, tighter than the Begijnhof, tighter than Serafina's darned mirrors — closed, sealed, *done* — and I understood, kneeling in the ash next to my gasping father, that I would never dream a true dream again. That I was a Sleeper now. Less than a Sleeper. Someone the seam has shut its door on for good, so completely that there is nothing left in me for either faction to want, nothing to recruit, nothing to seize, nothing to fear. The gun unloaded. The war, for me and mine, over — not because I won it, but because I spent the only thing that kept it going, which was me.

The door to Pompeii is shut forever, and here is the lock: it can only be opened by an arc-level dreamer, and I was the last one anyone knew of, and I am one no longer, and I made very sure, on the way out, to close it behind me and throw the reach away so I could never be made to open it again. That's the true version. That's what I spent. Not a face. *The magic.* The whole wondrous terrible gift. I was twelve when I found out dreams were real and thirteen when I gave up ever visiting them again, and I did it in a heartbeat, without the sum, the moment it was everyone I loved — which is, I've come to think, the only good reason anyone's ever done anything.

---

And then — and this is the part I didn't expect, the grace I hadn't earned and got anyway — the true record came home.

Because here's the thing about a backwash. It's a debt. Every edit I'd ever forced across the line had charged me a true memory to pay for a false thing held. My father's face, to pay for forty men held true in a painting. My mother's face, to pay for a night forged to save Fenna. The record in *me* had gone soft in patches all year, worn thin, unreliable, because I'd spent so much of it holding lies steady.

But I hadn't forged anything at Pompeii. I hadn't held one false thing. I'd witnessed the true moment and let it be true and pulled the living clear and paid with my reach — and a reach, once you give it up, doesn't owe anything. There was no lie left in me needing a true memory to pay for it. The debts closed. And the smooth warm places where my parents' faces used to be — the gaps my mind kept going to like a tongue to a missing tooth —

they filled back in.

My mother's face came back first, kneeling in the ash, before I'd even seen her, just — *there,* in my head, whole, the tired real smile she keeps just for me. And then my father's, which I was looking straight at, gasping and alive and forty years older and *home,* clicked true and stayed, and I knew it, and I knew I'd keep knowing it, because there was nothing false left in me to charge against it. I gave up every dream I'd ever have, and I got back every true thing I'd ever lost. That's the trade. That's the whole trade. The magic for the memory. The wonder for the faces. And I would do it again, and I'd do it faster.

---

I should tell you about the anger, because I promised you at the very start that I made a great many people very angry, and now you can see all of it, up out of the water like the old text under a scraped page.

The Palimpsest is angry forever, because I closed their holy site in a way that can never be opened, and worse, because I proved they were never necessary — that the record was always keeping itself in the telling, and two thousand years of their scraping was a lie about a book that anyone could just read aloud. That's a thing you don't forgive. Not when you can do what they can do to time.

The Vigil is angry too, and that's the one that took me longest to bear, because they were the good ones, I *chose* them, and I unmade them. Because if the record is kept true by testimony — by witnessing and telling and choosing to remember — then everything the Vigil ever did by *force* was not just unnecessary, it was the same crime as the Palimpsest's, wearing a kinder face. Four hundred years of drowning Venice to hold a forgery shut. A plan to make a thirteen-year-old a cork in the drain of the world. All of it — *all of it* — never needed. They could have just *told the true story* of 1630, freely, kept it alive in the telling, and let the forged night go soft and heal. They held instead. They pinned. They sacrificed. And I stood in the water and proved, in front of all of them, that keeping the record true never once required a single one of their beautiful terrible sacrifices, and some of them have never forgiven me for making four hundred years of grief unnecessary, because unnecessary grief is the hardest kind to have carried.

I need to say one more thing about the anger, because it's the one that's mine, and I've been circling it for three books the way my whole family circles the thing in the middle of the room.

I was angry too. At myself. Not for the dive — I'd do the dive again. For the *after.* Because giving up the reach sounds noble when I write it out like this, clean, *I chose it, I steered it,* and all of that's true. But there's a version of the truth I told Fenna at three in the morning about a month later that's truer, and she wrote it down, so I can't pretend I didn't say it: I mourned the magic. I *mourned* it, for a year, the way you mourn a person. I'd been the most important dreamer in a generation, the one both sides would burn the world for, the boy who could hold history steady in his open arms — and I traded it for hagelslag and a father who doesn't always know the year and a mother whose face I can finally picture, and every one of those is worth more than the magic, I know that, I knew it when I chose it, and I *still* woke up some mornings that year with my hands aching to reach for a seam that wasn't there anymore, grieving a wonder I'd thrown away on purpose, feeling like a boy who'd sold the only interesting thing about himself. That's the ugly true part. You don't get to make a beautiful sacrifice and also get to feel good about it. You make it, and then you live in the house you built, and some days the house is just a house, and you loved the palace, and the palace is gone, and you did it, nobody did it to you. Fenna read me that back once, exactly as I'd said it, and then she said, "Yeah. And?" And I said, "And I'd still choose the house." And she wrote *that* down too, underneath, because it was also true, and both of them are the record, and you don't get to keep only the noble half.

But some of them — the best of them — wept, and put down their needles, and went home to Venice, and did a thing that had not been done in four hundred years. I'll tell you about it in the next part. It's the best thing that came out of all this, better than my father, and it's Serafina's, and it's the reason the water's going down in a city you can still go and see.
