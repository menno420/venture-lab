# Chapter Seven — The Sum, One Last Time

*Lull, Book Three · draft*

The water was at my knees and rising, and everyone in the world I loved and everyone who wanted to use me was standing in it with me, and the wave hung over all of us, two thousand years from breaking and getting closer, and they made me their offers, one last time, the two answers I'd been running from since I was six.

The First Draft spoke first, because the Palimpsest always speaks first — patient, warm, so reasonable it makes your teeth ache.

"Look at your father," it said, in a dozen gentle voices, and gestured at Joris, still walking, still going under against my glove. "Forty years in the water. Your mother alone. You with two smooth places where your parents' faces should be. And all of it — *all of it, Sam* — un-happens if you simply open the moment. You're already inside it. You're arc-level. You don't even have to reach far. Crack Pompeii, change the one thing, and let ten thousand anchors hold the change true forever. The mountain doesn't fall. The town lives. Your father never dives, because there's no catastrophe to dive into. He grows old in Amsterdam with your mother and drinks coffee he shouldn't and puts his hand on your back when you have a bad dream that never comes, because in the world you write, there was never anything to dream." Its voices dropped, sweet as sleep. "We are not asking you to hurt anyone. We are asking you to *save ten thousand people.* Your father's own dream. The greatest darning there ever was. Do the kind thing, Sam. You've always wanted to do the kind thing. It's your whole trouble. Do it."

And God help me, I wanted to. You have to understand that. It wasn't a cartoon of temptation. It was the best thing I could imagine, offered by the worst thing there is, and the worst thing there is had done its homework, and I stood in the rising black water with my hand on my drowning father's chest and I *wanted to open it,* and the wanting is the truest thing I'll ever confess to you.

I want you to sit in the wanting with me for a second, because if you skip past it you'll think I'm braver than I am, and I need you to know I'm not, so that when your turn comes you'll know that wanting it isn't the same as being weak. I saw the whole world it was offering. I saw a kitchen in Amsterdam with three chairs at the table instead of two. I saw my mother young again, un-widowed, laughing at something my father said. I saw a version of me who never woke at four in a flooded square, who never learned any of this, who got to be a boy who was just a boy — who read the whole cereal box on his sixth birthday and had a normal dream about it and forgot it by breakfast. It offered me *ordinary,* which is the one thing I'd wanted my whole life and never had, and it offered it for the price of a lie so beautiful I could barely find the seam in it. That's how the Palimpsest works. It doesn't tempt you with something wicked. It tempts you with the thing you'd have given anything for, and it's *right there,* and all you have to do is agree that the past was always soft.

Then the Vigil spoke, and it was worse, because the Vigil was crying.

"Don't listen, boy," said the darner at the front — an old woman with Wessel's kind of tiredness, needles bright in her wet hands. "You know the rule. There is no clean edit. Open this moment and the bleed isn't a centimetre of water a year. It's *everything.* Ten thousand deaths un-written and the whole record downstream of them tears loose — every life that ever grew in the ground Pompeii made, every story, every one of us, gone soft and rewritten in the First Draft's hand, the whole world a palimpsest with the old text bleeding through forever. You'd not be saving ten thousand. You'd be ending everyone, so the Palimpsest could rule what's left." She stepped closer through the water, weeping, holding out her hand. "There's only one way to keep it safe. Shut it. So hard it can never be opened. And the only thing strong enough to shut the hardest moment there is, is *you,* holding it true from the inside, forever. We'll be gentle. We'll be grateful. We'll keep your name in the true record till the end of the world. One dreamer, Sam, to keep ten thousand true and the whole world safe. It's the noblest darning there ever was. Your father would —"

"*Don't,*" I said, "tell me what my father would." And my father, under my glove, in his rotted suit, mouthed *not yet,* and I loved the darner for crying and I could not do what she asked. "You want to make me into Vibia. You want me to be a hand that can never unclench. You've done the sum, I know you have, one against ten thousand, and on the arithmetic you're *right* — " I was shaking, the water at my thighs now — "and I still won't. Because I know something you don't. Because I've *met* the cork you want me to be, she's standing right here, she's been thirteen for two thousand years holding her little brother's hand, and it isn't noble, it's just *sad,* it's the saddest thing there is, and I will not do that to Vibia and I will not do it to me and I will not let you tell yourselves it's the hard right thing when it's just the same theft as theirs with a nicer story on it."

"There you are," said the First Draft, delighted, to the weeping Vigil. "Do you hear him? He sees it. He sees there's no difference between us. He's arc-level and he's *seen the sum,* and the sum has no true side. So he'll do the kind thing instead. He'll open it." A dozen faces turned to me, hungry. "Won't you, Sam."

And here's the trap they'd built, and it was beautiful, and it nearly had me: they were both right about each other. That was the whole thing. The Palimpsest was right that the Vigil did the merciful sum too — Serafina proved it in Venice, one boy for a city. And the Vigil was right that opening Pompeii would end the world. They'd walked me to a place where both of my teachers were liars and both of my enemies were truth-tellers and the only two doors were *be a god* or *be a cork,* and every clever loving person for two thousand years had walked into that same room and picked one of those two doors, and every single one of them was still holding a hand they couldn't unclench. My father picked *god* and drowned. Vibia picked *cork* and went thirteen for two millennia. And the room was built so well that standing in it, with the water at my chest, I genuinely could not see a third door, and I want you to know that, because when they build that room for you — and they will — you are going to stand in it and see only two doors too, and you are going to have to trust that there's a third one even when you can't see it, on nothing but the word of a girl who says *not yet* and a friend with a notebook.

---

And that's the moment Lena chose.

She'd been standing at the First Draft's shoulder the whole time, soaked and silent, not looking at me, and I want to tell you exactly how it happened because it's the bravest thing I ever watched a person do and I've watched some brave people.

She looked at me. Finally. Across the rising black water, in the falling fire, with the wave hanging over all of us. And she did the thing she'd told me in Venice she'd never once managed in her whole clever life: she chose true when it cost her everything.

"He's not going to open it," said Lena. Quietly. To the First Draft. To her own masters. To the thing that had made her and taught her and given her the warm voice and the slate coat and the terrible arithmetic. "And he's not going to shut it either. I've been trying to work out for a year which side I'd stand on at the water, and I've just this second worked out the answer, and the answer is *neither,* the answer is *his,* the boy who broke my sum, because I have done the arithmetic every way there is and it always ends in a child holding a hand she can't let go of, and I am *done.*" Her voice broke, and steadied, and I have never heard anything braver. "I'm done, Sam. I'm standing with you. I don't know what the third thing is. But I know it's yours, and I know it isn't ours, and if I'm going to be near one person who chooses true when it costs them, I'm going to have to *be* one first." She turned her back on the First Draft — on the whole Palimpsest, two thousand years of it — and waded through the black water to stand at my shoulder, opposite my father, facing the wave. "So. Whatever you've got. I'll hold the seam for you. It's the one thing I'm better at than anyone alive. Do your third thing, little bell. I've bet my whole self on you having one."

The First Draft did not shout. It never does. It simply began, in a dozen cold voices, to let go — the way it had in Venice, the merciful murderer's trick, releasing the frozen moment it had helped Vibia pin so that the whole night would collapse around us and drown everyone in it and un-write itself in the falling. The wave shuddered. The held ten thousand trembled. The water surged up to my chest, cold and salt and rising fast, and Vibia cried out somewhere behind me, her two-thousand-year grip tearing loose all at once, and Joris went under to his shoulders against my glove, and Fenna was screaming from the dry edge, torch high, and there was no time, there was no time at all.

"NOW, Sam!" cried everyone at once — the Palimpsest *open it,* the Vigil *shut it,* Lena *do it,* Vibia *the third thing, now, NOW* —

And I didn't know what the third thing was.

I want to be honest, the way I promised, all the way down to the bottom. In the water, at the fourth hour, with the wave coming down and my father drowning and a girl's two-thousand-year grip tearing loose, I did not have a plan and I did not have an answer and I was thirteen years old and I was going to fail everyone I loved in the next four seconds.

And then, from the dry edge of the worst moment there is, over the roar of the collapsing night, my best friend Fenna Bakker held up a soaked notebook in the torchlight and screamed the truest thing anyone has ever said to me, the thing that saved the world, the thing she'd been trying to tell me since we were six years old on an ordinary morning that turned out to be a dream:

"SAM! YOU DON'T HAVE TO HOLD IT! YOU JUST HAVE TO *WRITE IT DOWN!*"
