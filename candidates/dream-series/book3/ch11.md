# Chapter Eleven — Coming Up

*Lull, Book Three · draft*

We came up out of the mirror into the lemon garden a little after four in the morning, on my thirteenth birthday, six of us who'd gone down and — I have to write this carefully, because it's the count that matters — six of us who came back, and one man more.

My father came up last, through the cold glass, and Wessel and I caught him under the arms, and he came out into the garden of a dead town gasping and blinking, an old tired stranger with my eyes, forty years gone from a world that had kept turning without him, and he looked around at the ruined walls and the lamplight and the frightened faces, and then he looked at me, at the boy holding him up who had his eyes and was wearing his lantern and his kit, and his mouth worked, and the first thing my father said in forty years, the first thing I ever heard him say that I'll actually remember, was:

"Sam?" A whisper, wrecked, disbelieving. "You're — you got so *big.*" And then, breaking all to pieces the way I break, the way he taught me across a seam without ever being there to teach me: "You came down. Into the water. For me. Oh, no. Oh, Sam, you weren't supposed to — that's the whole thing I've been trying to — for years I've been trying to *warn* you —"

"I know, Dad," I said. "You said *not yet.* I heard you. The whole time. I heard you." And I put my hand flat on his chest, the way I'd stopped him drowning, the way someone once put a hand flat on my back between the shoulder blades and said *I've got you.* "But I didn't do what you did. I promise. I didn't rewrite the water. I just took you out of it. It's okay. You can stop trying to reach me now. I reached you."

And my father, Joris Vermeer, who went under before I was old enough to keep him, put his forehead down against the top of my head, heavy and still, and he was warm, and he smelled of cold salt and, underneath, faintly, impossibly, of coffee, and he stayed there, and I stood in a dead garden holding my father up at the fourth hour of my birthday, and I want to be honest with you the way I promised: it was not a clean happy ending. He was forty years strange. He'd lost a world. He had a wife who'd let go of his face on purpose and a son who was a stranger and a gift at once. It was going to be hard for a long time. But he was *up,* and he was *true,* and no one had been rewritten to do it. It was a rescue. It was the good kind. It was the only kind I'd allow.

---

I'll tell you where everyone landed, because you've carried them this far and you deserve to set them down gently.

**Vibia** is at rest. I told you that and I meant it and I won't spoil it by pretending I know what rest is for a girl two thousand years dead. But I know she let go, and I know her last word was *now,* and I know her brother went with her, and I know that somewhere in Fenna's notebook — in the true record, freely told, freely kept — there is a page that says *Vibia had a little brother and she loved him and they were on the shore at Pompeii and it was real,* and as long as that's told and kept and passed on, she is *known,* which is the only thing she ever needed and the only kind of forever any of us really gets. I say her name sometimes, out loud, on purpose. You can too. That's how it works. That's the whole magic, in the end, the only one I've got left.

**Lena** walked out of the Palimpsest that night and never walked back in, which is a thing that had, as far as anyone knows, never once been done. She has no faction now and no sum and, for a while, no idea who she was without either. She came north with us. She and my mother get on alarmingly well, which frightens me. She's teaching Fenna Italian and Fenna's teaching her how to write a true thing down without making it prettier than it was, which Lena finds almost impossibly hard and is getting better at. She told me once, drying dishes in our kitchen, "I spent my whole life being the warmest voice in the room, little bell, and it turns out the trick was never warmth. It was *telling the truth even when it's cold.* You did that to me. I'll never forgive you." She was smiling when she said it. She meant it both ways. That's Lena.

**Nico** you'll meet again in a moment, so I'll only say here that of everyone who went down into the water that night, he's the one it changed for the better in the simplest way: a cheerful frightened boy who found out that the thing he was good at — telling a true story so it stays told — was the thing that mattered most all along, and got to spend his life doing it instead of darning by force in the dark. Not everyone comes out of the worst moment there is lighter than they went in. Nico did. I'm glad. He earned it holding a torch over rising water for a boy he barely knew.

**Fenna** — I could write a whole book about Fenna and one day I might, except she'd correct every page, which is exactly why it would be a better book than this one. She kept the record. She kept *me.* She went down into Pompeii with no reach and no magic and no way to protect herself and the only tool she had was the truth and a biro, and it turned out the truth and a biro were the strongest things in the water. She's studying to be — she'll hate that I told you this, so tell no one — a historian. Someone who keeps the true record of what really happened, out loud, on purpose, so it can't be stolen or softened. She says it's not related to any of this. She's lying, and she knows I know, and she lets me know she knows, and that's the whole friendship in one sentence.

**Wessel** went back to the pont. He stands in the wheelhouse in the grey and loads bikes and keeps the record true the honest way now, the way he always half-knew was the real way — he *tells* it, to the ones who need telling, carefully, in a safe room, with the door shut. He and my father sit on the ferry sometimes and don't talk, which is how tired brave men are friends. He said one thing to me, the morning after, that I've kept: "You unmade my whole life's work, kid. Forty years of darning by force, and you proved a girl with a notebook could've done it better." He took a pull of coffee. "Best thing anyone ever did to me. Now I don't have to hold it all myself. Now I can just *tell* someone and go to sleep." I think that's the closest Wessel ever came to saying he was happy.

---

And **Serafina.** I saved her for last, and the best of it, because it's the thing that outlived all the rest and you can go and see it.

She went home to Venice, and she called the darners together — the old ones, the tired ones, the keepers of the forged night of 1630 that built a white church and drowns the city a centimetre a year — and she told them what happened at Pompeii. That the record isn't kept true by holding. That four hundred years of darning the bleed had been the same mistake, the same grip, the same fear of letting go. And then Serafina did the bravest thing of anyone in this whole story, braver than my dive, and I'll fight you if you say otherwise:

She *told the true story of 1630.*

Out loud. The whole thing. That the Venetian Vigil forged the plague-night to save the city, and that it was a theft dressed as a mercy, and that the water has been rising ever since to pay for it. She wrote it down and she made it *known* — not hidden, not held, not darned into silence — she put the true record into the telling, into the hands of people who would choose to keep it and pass it on. And she stopped holding the forgery shut.

And the bleed began to heal.

Not all at once. Not a miracle. But a forged moment that everyone has agreed to hold down by force keeps bleeding forever, and a true story that people freely tell and keep — even a shameful one, *especially* a shameful one — that story doesn't bleed, because it isn't a lie being pinned, it's a truth being carried. The water in Venice is going down. Slowly. A little each year. You can look it up, if you know how to look, which now you do. They think it's the new pumps. It isn't the pumps. It's an old woman who finally told the truth and let a four-hundred-year-old grip unclench, and a city that's learning, one told story at a time, that you keep the record true by *keeping* it — out loud, together, on purpose — and not by drowning yourself to hold it down.

Serafina died last winter. Old, and at peace, in a city that had started to come up out of the water because of her. Fenna and I went to Venice for it — the first time I'd been back — and Nico met us at the station, taller still, a darner in his own right now, doing it the new way, the telling way. And at the end, Serafina had left me one line, murmured to Nico to pass on, and it's the last thing my father's teacher ever said to me, and I'll give it to you to carry:

"Tell Joris's boy he found the third thing. Tell him his father can rest now too, even the parts of him that came home. And tell him — " Nico's voice had caught here — "tell him the girl in the water was right about him all along. It was always him. It was always going to be him. *Not yet* was just her way of saying *soon.*"
