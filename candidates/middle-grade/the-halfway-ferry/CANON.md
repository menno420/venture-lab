# Series Canon — The Halfway Ferry

One-page canon sheet per the night-kiln precedent (`.sessions/2026-07-13-night-kiln-book-2.md` 💡: names/ages, laws verbatim, artifacts, open hooks with where they were planted — so book N+1's canon recovery is an O(1) lookup, not a reread). Update in the same PR as each book lands.

## Laws (byte-exact; the three rules are load-bearing series text)

1. **"Fares are never money."** A fare is something true and something the passenger can spare — barely. Paid at the fare box (or to the fog). Money "costs nothing true."
2. **"The fog decides the route."** The captain steers *for between*; the fog routes. Arguing with it "is like arguing with Tuesday."
3. **"Always count the passengers twice."** Once at the gangway, once when the fog has us. Out loud, by faces, not by hats. Deckhand counts, cat checks. More is one kind of trouble; fewer is another. Never stop mid-count. Theme corollary (Book 1 climax): a count squares an account — being counted is belonging; everybody counts.

## Mechanics

- The **fog** is a *sorter*: it reads fares at the box ("the box is the fog's ear"), routes each passenger to the far bank that squares their account, and collects tolls at the **Fogline** (iron-colored wall mid-river; a seam of bright water under it opens when the account squares). Not cruel, always gentle, "collects on silence." It bowed to Wren once (Book 1, ch. 13).
- The **far bank "isn't anywhere in particular"** — beyond the Line the river braids into channels; each passenger lands somewhere exactly particular to them.
- "**Halfway**" lore (Book 1, ch. 3): the boat is *called* the Halfway Ferry — a description, not a name. Halfway — the in-between of every crossing — is her whole route.
- **Fare box**: dark wood, brass corners, slot too wide for any coin; opens to a flat left hand and "knows whose daughter you are" (Tolliver hands recognized). Sticks on the left, in spring.
- **Ship's log**: green spine, ruled for names; records crossings of the Line (including the *Harebell*'s); crew sign the log, passengers pay the box. **Ship's bell**: clapperless; rings itself at the Line.
- **Crew pay**: sixpence a crossing (real money — wages are wages), hot supper, an education. The cat is paid in stories, in installments, always stopped "at the best bit."

## Geography

- **Little Slake** — river town on the **Sedge** (~140 yd wide at town); three jetties (eel-boats / coal-barge Thursdays / the condemned far jetty = the *Harebell*'s jetty, ferry's berth, "some doors are better kept looking broken"); riverwall (five lamps); Slake Street; Tolliver's Rope & Tin (four generations); chapel with brass plate for the *Harebell*, vestry photograph of all three jetties working, clock 11 minutes fast "out of civic optimism"; wagtails nesting behind the CONDEMNED notice.
- **Great Slake** — upriver, bigger, smug, has the road bridge.
- **The Hatch** — downriver landmark; sixty years ago the fog's notice said the Line would move past it, taking Little Slake off the route.
- **Overslake** — the *Harebell* town on the far side of the Line: three working jetties, seven riverwall lamps, its own chapel clock also 11 fast, toffee recipe diverged sixty years; bell rung every first Tuesday for sixty years.

## Cast

- **Wren Tolliver**, 11 — deckhand (hired end of Book 1; dawn Tuesdays + whenever the fog docks); keeps a SUNDRIES stock book; counted the eel-boats twice, unasked; initials in the *Harebell*'s log.
- **Captain Marl** — "not my whole name"; ~100+ years in business; **never given a pronoun on the page** (deliberate; hold this line series-wide); pays Tar in serialized stories.
- **Tar** — black harbor cat of Little Slake; talks ("a small door with sand in the hinge"); checks the counts; has been wrong twice in a hundred years, per the captain.
- **Wren's mother** — unnamed on the page so far; splices better than she prices; owes the fare box a fare, "keeping mine back for thirty years." **Father** — dredger *Persistent*, home Sundays ("Always count yourself. First rule of mud."). **Great-gran** — paid the box "a whole rope-walk."
- **Tam Rush** — deck-boy of the *Harebell*, ~13; rang the last bell, uncounted for sixty years, signed aboard/ashore at Overslake with his mother **Margaret Rush** (ship's cook) and **Captain Weck** (m., *Harebell*, corrected his log's only mistake).
- Book 1 passengers (reusable townsfolk): Mr. Ollery (umbrella returned), Miss Vane (retired teacher, relocated across), Iva & Pearl Tanner (twins), Mr. Dunnock (beekeeper), Mrs. Grebe (baker), Old Nol (retired lock-keeper; stayed across at the lock landing).

## Open hooks (planted → for future books)

1. **The Tolliver open-dated fare** — copperplate log entry: *"Received of Tolliver, one fare, paid in full. Crossing open-dated, at the holder's need. Balance: kept."* Whose? For where? (ch. 13.)
2. **The ferry's name was somebody's fare** — taken off her bows letter by letter; the account it squared was "large"; answer promised "about four winters" of deckhand seniority away (ch. 13, per Tar).
3. **The mother's held-back fare** (thirty years) and the family's history with the box (ch. 14, ch. 4).
4. **The *Tollgate*** — another ferry, "one of them you'd best never race" (ch. 13). "There are other ferries. There always are."
5. **The stepping-stone story** — the bridgekeeper's daughter, the third stepping-stone, "what was under it, keeping the toll" — the captain's serial for Tar, twice interrupted at the best bit (ch. 2, ch. 14).
6. **The crossing-song** — last verse belongs to the deck-boy; Wren carried it home phonetically; the near bank "ought to have it again" (ch. 12).
7. **The Line moves** — the fog re-opened Little Slake; "word travels"; a standing queue now exists (ch. 14). Why the fog closed the route sixty years ago is unexplained on the page.

## Timeline & style constraints
