# THE HALFWAY FERRY

*The Halfway Ferry, Book 1: The Fogline Crossing*

> Everyone in Little Slake knows the last jetty is condemned. The notice says so, and the notice has hung there so long it could use a notice of its own. So when Wren Tolliver sees a ferry tied up there at dawn — a ferry with no name, no timetable, and a captain who pays the harbor cat in stories — she does the sensible thing: she asks for a job. Rule one: fares are never money. Rule two: the fog decides the route. Rule three: always count the passengers twice. Nobody tells her what happens when the count comes out wrong. Book 1 of The Halfway Ferry, a series about the crossings between here and everywhere else.

---

**Content note:** Middle-grade fantasy, ages 8–12. Contains gentle peril (a stubborn fog, a passenger too many), a sixty-year-old case of homesickness set right, and one cat with strong opinions about payment. No violence, no romance, nothing that follows a reader past the last page. Ends warm, with supper.

---

# Chapter One — The Jetty Nobody Mended

Little Slake had three jetties, the way some people have three teapots: one for every day, one for best, and one that was broken but too full of history to throw away.

The everyday jetty belonged to the eel-boats. The best jetty belonged to the coal barge, which came on Thursdays and thought very well of itself. And the third jetty — the far one, the last one, the one that leaned into the river Sedge like an old man listening for gossip — belonged to nobody at all, because it was condemned.

There was a notice that said so. CONDEMNED, it said. DO NOT WALK, STAND, SIT, FISH, OR LOITER. The notice had hung there so long that the paint had gone the color of weak tea, and last spring a pair of wagtails had built a nest behind it, which Wren Tolliver felt undermined its authority considerably.

Wren was eleven, and she kept a notebook.

It was a stock book, really — a leftover from her mother's shop, with SUNDRIES printed on the cover — and in it she wrote down everything in Little Slake that needed mending, in order of how much it needed it. The chapel clock, which ran eleven minutes fast out of what the sexton called civic optimism, was number fourteen. The school gate, which shrieked in E flat, was number nine. The far jetty had been number one for three years running, and nobody had ever once asked to see the list.

She was up at dawn that Tuesday for a sensible reason, which is the best kind. The gutter above her window had come loose, and it dripped — not always, only when the wind sulked round to the east — and it dripped onto the sill exactly like a tap being tapped by a very patient finger. You cannot sleep through a thing like that. You can only lie in the dark being eleven at it.

So she got up. She put on her coat over her nightgown and her boots over bare feet, took the notebook (gutter: number six, moving up), and let herself onto the riverwall, where the air smelled of mud and morning and somewhere, faintly, of toast.

The fog was in.

Fog on the Sedge was not weather. Weather comes and goes and apologizes for itself. The fog came upriver like something arriving on purpose, and it stood — that was the word for it, stood — in a long gray bank below the town, where it had stood every day of Wren's life and, by all accounts, every day of everyone else's. The charts called it the Fogline and drew a polite dotted ring around it, the way you might draw a ring around a sleeping dog. Boats went around. Boats had always gone around.

Except that this morning, out of the standing fog, with no sound at all — no engine, no bell, no fuss — came a ferry.

Wren knew every boat on the river the way other people know their cousins. This was none of them. It was a proper double-ended ferry, wide in the beam, with a wheelhouse like a sentry box and a stovepipe breathing one thin thread of smoke, and it was old — old the way the chapel was old, kept old, polished old. Where its name should have been painted there was a long clean blank, as if the boat had been asked a question and was still thinking it over.

It did not go to the everyday jetty. It did not go to the best jetty.

It came alongside the condemned jetty — the leaning one, the do-not-walk-stand-sit-fish-or-loiter one, number one on Wren's list for three years running — and it tied up there as gently as a cup being set on a saucer.

Wren looked at the notice. The notice went on saying CONDEMNED to nobody in particular.

A gangplank came out. A figure in a long gray coat stepped down it, sure-footed on planks that Wren knew for a fact would not hold the weight of a determined duck. And the harbor cat — Tar, the black one, who belonged to no household and inspected all of them, who had not hurried for anything in living memory — came down the riverwall at a dead run, tail up like a flag, and sat at the bottom of the gangplank as if he had an appointment.

The figure in the gray coat sat down on a bollard, unhurried, and began — Wren could see it plainly, though the sense of it took much longer to arrive — to pay the cat.

Not with fish. Not with anything from a pocket.

The figure leaned down, and talked, and the cat listened the way nobody in Little Slake had ever listened to anything: ears forward, eyes half shut, entirely still, like a saucer being filled to the exact brim and not one drop more.

Wren stood on the riverwall in her coat and her nightgown with the fog lying quiet along the river, and she did what she always did when the world produced something that made no sense.

She got out her notebook, and she started a fresh page.

*Ferry,* she wrote. *No name. No timetable. Docked at Jetty No. 3 (CONDEMNED) at dawn. Paid the cat.*

She read it back. It made no more sense written down, which was, in Wren's experience, how you could tell it mattered.

⁂

Her mother was laying the shop fire when Wren came down properly dressed, notebook buttoned into her coat pocket like a passport.

Tolliver's Rope & Tin had stood on Slake Street for four generations, selling rope, tin, tar, nails, wicks, hooks, and advice, the advice being free and therefore the most argued-over stock in the shop. Wren's mother could splice an eye in a mooring line while conducting two disagreements and a sale. Her father was upriver on the dredger *Persistent*, being home on Sundays and enormous the rest of the week in letters.

"There's a boat at the far jetty," Wren said, in the voice she kept for facts.

"Mm," said her mother, which was shopkeeper for *I have heard you and am saving my opinion in case of a rush on opinions later.*

"It came out of the Fogline."

Her mother stopped laying the fire. It was only for a moment, and if you had not spent eleven years watching her lay fires you would have missed it entirely. Then the kindling went on going where kindling goes.

"Eat your breakfast," her mother said. "And Wren —"

"Mm," said Wren, who had learned it from her.

"Mind the notice," said her mother, which was not, Wren noticed — and she noticed it the way you notice a stair missing — the same thing at all as *there is no boat.*

# Chapter Two — Paid in Stories

School could not reasonably be avoided, but school ended, and the tide of children went up Slake Street, and one particular drop of the tide went the other way.

The ferry was still there. All day it had sat at the condemned jetty in plain sight, and all day Little Slake had done the remarkable thing that towns do with impossibilities, which is to walk past them slightly faster than usual. Mrs. Grebe the baker had gone by with her eyes fixed on the middle distance. Old Nol, the retired lock-keeper, had stopped, looked, taken out his pipe, put it back, and gone home without lighting it. The wagtails behind the notice had strong views but kept them to the nest.

Wren came down the riverwall slowly, the way you approach a horse or a question, and stopped where the good stones ended and the condemned planking began.

The captain was sitting on the bollard again, and Tar the harbor cat was sitting in front of the captain, and Wren arrived in the middle of the paying.

"— so the bridgekeeper's daughter," the captain was saying, "who had never in her life been wrong where water could see her, looked at the third stepping-stone. And the third stepping-stone looked back."

The cat's ears were forward. The cat's eyes were shut to slits of green. The fog stood down the river and seemed — although fog has no ears, Wren told the notebook later, fog has no ears and this observation is not evidence — to be leaning very slightly closer.

"And that," said the captain, "is where I stop, because that is a two-evening story, and you, sir, have had your fare's worth."

The cat opened his eyes and made a remark. Anyone in Little Slake would have called it a meow. Standing this close, Wren heard it hit the air the way a foreign word hits it when you suddenly know the language: not a meow at all, but, unmistakably, in a voice like a small door with sand in the hinge:

"*Cheat.*"

"Never once," said the captain comfortably, "in one hundred years of doing business, and never with a cat, which is the only customer who checks."

The captain looked up, then. Under a gray cap was a weathered face of the sort that could have been fifty or seventy or something with three digits, and eyes of no particular color, the way the river is no particular color and yet you would know it anywhere.

"You've been standing there," the captain said, "for the length of one stepping-stone. Most of your town has walked past me all day at a very brave speed. You stopped. Why?"

There were a great many answers, and later Wren thought of all of them. What she said was the true one at the front.

"Your jetty's condemned," she said. "It's number one in my book. It's been number one for three years. Nothing that leans like that should be holding a boat like this, and it *is*, and I want to know what's holding it up."

The cat turned around and looked at her with both eyes, which from Tar was a form of applause.

"She keeps a book," Tar said, to the captain, in the tone of one presenting credentials.

"I heard," said the captain.

"She counted the eel-boats after the March flood," said the cat. "All of them. Twice. Nobody asked her to."

Wren had not known that anyone had seen that, least of all a cat, and she felt the tips of her ears go hot.

"Twice," said the captain, in a new voice — a slow, weighing voice, a voice like a hand testing a rope. "Twice, unasked. Well." The captain stood up off the bollard, and the ferry sat quiet behind, and the blank where the name should have been caught the low sun and gave nothing back. "Then you may as well ask me the thing you actually came down here to ask, because my tea is stewing."

And Wren — eleven years old, boots on the very edge of the condemned planking, notebook buttoned over her heart, too practical to be scared and too curious to have ever once in her life gone home when it mattered — took a breath and did the sensible thing.

She asked for a job.

⁂

Afterward, the whole town agreed that somebody ought to have stopped her, and the whole town was quite sure it would certainly have done so itself, if it had not been busy that week.

What actually happened was this: the captain looked at her for the length of time it takes a kettle to decide, and then said, "Can you carry a rope, count past thirty, and hold your tongue in fog?"

"Yes," said Wren. "Yes. And I don't know, because I've never been in fog. Nobody in Little Slake has. It doesn't come up to the town. It stays where it is."

"That," said the captain, "is the first honest answer this jetty has heard in sixty years. Come aboard, and mind the third plank; she's a liar."

"The plank?"

"The plank."

Wren came up the gangplank of the ferry with no name, past the third plank, which creaked in a way that suggested it was perfectly sound and would later, when she trod on it in fog with her arms full of rope, prove to be nothing of the kind.

The deck was scrubbed pale. There were two long benches, a fare box on a post by the gangway — old dark wood, brass-cornered, with a slot too wide for any coin ever minted — and a ship's bell with no clapper, and the wheelhouse with its window-glass gone slightly liquid with age, and a small iron stove somewhere below sending up its one thread of smoke, and the whole boat had the feel of a room in which somebody very calm has just stopped speaking.

"Tea," said the captain, "and terms."

# Chapter Three — Three Rules and a Trial

The tea was the color of the river and surprisingly good, which was also, Wren would come to learn, a fair description of most things aboard.

They sat in the wheelhouse, the captain on a stool worn to the shape of the captain, Wren on an upturned crate that had FRAGILE stenciled on it in a language she almost knew, and Tar along the sill of the liquid window with his tail hanging down like a bell-rope.

"Terms," said the captain. "I am Captain Marl. That is not my whole name, and you will not get the rest of it by asking sideways, so save your sideways. The boat has no name. That is not the same as never having had one. You are Wren Tolliver, of the rope shop, daughter of the woman who splices better than she prices, and you talk to your notebook the way my last deckhand talked to himself, which is to say usefully. I take passengers across the river."

Wren looked out the wheelhouse window at the Sedge, which at Little Slake was wide enough to be respected but had, as every child knew, another side — Great Slake's side, reeds and heron country, reachable by the road bridge upriver if you had a morning to waste.

"Across is twenty minutes by rowboat," she said. "There's nothing over there but reeds and the back of Great Slake's smugness."

"Across the *river*," said Captain Marl, "is not always across the *Sedge.* The river this boat crosses is wider than the one your charts have met. The far bank isn't anywhere in particular. It is wherever the passenger is actually going, which is almost never where they bought passage to, and the sooner you stop finding that strange, the better deckhand you'll make. I don't explain it. I operate it. There's a difference, and the difference is what I pay wages out of."

Wren wrote *far bank not anywhere in particular* in her notebook, and found that her hand had put a small firm tick beside it, as if some part of her had checked the fact against something and found it good.

"Wages," she said, because you do not grow up over a shop and forget the order of business.

"Sixpence a crossing, hot supper, and an education," said the captain. "The sixpence is real money. Wages are wages. Fares are another thing entirely, which brings us to the rules, of which there are three, and here I will ask for your book, because I want them written in your hand, where you'll believe them."

Wren opened the notebook to a fresh page and dated it.

"Rule one," said Captain Marl. "*Fares are never money.* Not ever. Not once. A crossing takes a person from where they are to where they're going, and no coin ever minted knows the way. A fare is something true and something the passenger can spare — barely. They pay it into the fare box or they pay it to the fog, and it had far better be the box. You will learn to see fares the way your mother sees a bad shilling. That takes about ten years, so begin tonight."

*Fares are never money,* wrote Wren, and underlined *never*, and the ink went down willingly.

"Rule two. *The fog decides the route.* Not the wheel, not the chart, not the captain, and not — young Tolliver — the deckhand, however sensible. I steer. The fog chooses. If the crossing is meant to be short, it is short. If the fog takes us the long way round, then the long way round is the route, and there is a reason, and the reason is not always shown to us, and we go anyway. Arguing with the fog is like arguing with Tuesday. You may do it as loudly as you please and it will still be Tuesday."

*The fog decides the route,* wrote Wren. Beside it her hand put a small question mark, and she let it stay. It seemed honest.

"Rule three." And here Captain Marl set down the tea, and Tar came down off the sill and sat upright on the boards with his tail wrapped round his feet, and the wheelhouse went as quiet as the middle of a word. "*Always count the passengers twice.* Once at the gangway when they board, and once when the fog has us. Out loud. By faces, not by hats. You count them, and the cat checks you, and if the two counts agree, you say so, and if they do not agree, you come to me at the wheel — walking, not running — and you tell me which way the count is wrong. More is one kind of trouble. Fewer is another. You do not shout, you do not point, and you do not, whatever you see, stop counting until you have finished."

Wren wrote it down. *Always count the passengers twice.* Her pen stopped after it, waiting for the reason the way a held-out hand waits.

"Why twice?" she said. "Nobody miscounts a ferry-load walking up one gangplank."

"Not at the gangplank, no," said the captain, and picked the tea back up, and that was the whole answer Wren got on her first evening.

"Trial," said Captain Marl, when the tea was gone. "One crossing. We board at first light. If you're any good, the job's yours and I'll square it with your mother myself, which will go easier than you expect, for reasons that are her business. If you're no good, I'll put you ashore with your sixpence and a story worth more, and the notice on that jetty will go back to being the truth. Well, deckhand? Look at the terms all the way round, the way you'd look at a used kettle. Any dents?"

Wren looked at the terms all the way round.

"One," she said. "The cat gets paid in stories. I've seen it. What do I do if he checks my count and I think he's wrong?"

Tar's tail unwrapped and rewrapped the other way about, which she would learn was the punctuation of an interested cat.

"In one hundred years," said Captain Marl, "that has happened twice, and both times were the most instructive afternoons of my working life. If you think the cat's wrong, say so out loud with the fog listening. Nothing on this river respects anything the way the fog respects a person who'll say *I think the cat is wrong* out loud." The captain stood, and the stool sighed. "First light, Wren Tolliver. Wear wool. Bring the book."

Wren went home along the riverwall in the blue end of the evening. The fog stood in its place downriver, the way it had stood all her life, and she was nearly to the shop door before she realized what was different, and had to stop, and hold the sill of her own front window, and let the fact climb all the way into her.

All her life, the fog had been a thing on the river.

The whole way home, it had felt like a thing on the river *facing her.*

⁂

Her mother was in the back room with the lamp lit and a mooring line across her knees, and she listened to the entire account — job, terms, sixpence, rules, first light — without dropping a single tuck of the splice.

Then she said the thing that Wren turned over for years afterward, the way you turn over a coin from a country you cannot find on any map.

"Ask the captain," her mother said, "whether the fare box still sticks."

# Chapter Four — Fares

First light on the condemned jetty, and Little Slake had, in the overnight way of towns, decided the ferry could be seen after all.

Wren came down the riverwall at half past too-early, in wool, with the notebook and a heel of bread and her heart going like the school gate in a gale, and found a queue.

It was a small queue, and it stood on the good stones short of the notice, in the particular manner of people who have each privately decided to be somewhere and are each startled to find they have company. Wren knew every face — that was Little Slake for you; strangers were a thing that happened to other towns — and she wrote them into the book as the gangplank went down, because writing things down was the job, or if it wasn't yet, she intended it to be.

Mr. Ollery was first, in his good coat, holding an umbrella that Wren had never once seen him without and had never once seen open. He was going, he told the captain, to return it. It had been borrowed forty years ago from a man across the water, and every year it had gotten harder to bring back, umbrellas gathering weight in a way that scales don't register.

"Fare," said Captain Marl, at the fare box.

"I had an excuse ready," said Mr. Ollery. "For when I gave it back. I've had it ready forty years. It's a beautiful excuse. It's got weather in it, and a dog that really existed, and it's — it's absolutely watertight, and I've polished it so long I could say it singing." He stopped. The morning stood still around him the way mornings will when something is actually happening. "That's the fare, isn't it."

"That's the fare," said the captain.

Mr. Ollery leaned to the wide brass slot, and said his excuse into the fare box, all of it, quietly, the whole beautiful watertight forty-year excuse, and Wren, standing at the gangway with her book, heard the box take it the way dry ground takes rain. When he straightened up he looked lighter and slightly alarmed, like a man whose pockets have been picked of stones he'd sworn were savings.

"One," said Wren, and wrote him down: *Ollery, umbrella, excuse (paid).*

Miss Vane came next, the schoolteacher, retired these ten years, upright as a Monday. She was going to see a former pupil. She did not say which, and the captain did not ask, and her fare was a sentence: she put her face near the slot and said, in her clear dictation voice, "It is too late," and the box took the sentence out of her like a splinter, clean, all of it. She stood there a moment, testing where it had been, the way your tongue tests the gap where a tooth was.

"It appears," she said, to nobody in particular, in a voice ten years younger, "that it is Tuesday, and early."

"Two," said Wren.

The Tanner twins were three and four, Iva and Pearl, going across to their grandmother's, escorted as far as the gangplank by their entire remaining family, who all talked at once. The twins paid together, one head on either side of the slot like a secret being told down a well: the ingredient — the actual ingredient, the sworn one, the one they had promised on the millpond never to tell — of their grandmother's toffee. The box received it. The twins looked at each other, wildly, and then began, helplessly, both at once, to laugh: they had told, and told safely, and the telling was gone somewhere no one could ever make them repeat it.

"Three and four," said Wren. "Stop laughing or I'll count you as one."

Mr. Dunnock the beekeeper was five, with a humming box of bees on his shoulder, taking a swarm to somebody who needed starting over. His fare was a dream he'd had once of flying — a true one, he said, the kind you have exactly one of — and he paid it in with his eyes shut, and afterward he stood taller, like a man who has finally set down a ladder he'd been carrying in case.

Mrs. Grebe the baker was six, with a basket for a christening across the water, and she paid the smell of her best loaf, which came off the basket and went into the slot in a long visible curl of gold, and the whole jetty was briefly and unbearably hungry.

And Old Nol was seven, the retired lock-keeper, who had said nothing in the queue and carried nothing at all. He stumped up the plank, put his face to the box, and paid — Wren wrote it exactly, because exactness was all she could hold onto by then — *one hour of his best luck, the hour to be chosen by the river.* The captain looked at him a long moment over that one.

"You've ridden before," said Captain Marl.

"Once," said Old Nol. "Boy and man, once." And he went and sat in the bow, facing the fog, holding his cap in both hands like the start of an apology.

Wren stood at the head of the gangway and counted, out loud, by faces, the way she'd been told: Ollery under his umbrella, Miss Vane bolt upright, Iva, Pearl, Dunnock with the humming shoulder, Mrs. Grebe, Old Nol in the bow.

"Seven aboard," said Wren.

Tar walked the benches end to end, unhurried, whiskers forward, like an auditor with a pension at stake, and sat, and said, "Seven."

"Counts agree," said Captain Marl, from the wheel. "Log it. Stand by the line, deckhand. And Wren Tolliver —" The engine woke below like a big dog dreaming; the fog, downriver, stood up off the water in a way fog does not. "— your mother wants to know if the fare box sticks. Tell her: only on the left, only in spring, and she still owes it a fare. Cast off."

Wren cast off.

The line came aboard through her hands, wet and alive; the condemned jetty let them go with a creak like a word; and the ferry with no name swung its blunt bow downriver and went, without any hurry at all, straight at the standing fog.

⁂

The last thing Wren saw of Little Slake was the wagtails coming off the notice to watch, and the first thing she learned about the Fogline was that it has a door.

Not a door you could draw. A door you could *feel* — a place where the gray ahead stopped being weather-that-was-thick and became, between one breath and the next breath, somewhere with an inside.

"Fog's got us," said the captain, easy, both hands on the wheel. "Welcome to the river, deckhand."

And the fog closed behind them, soft as a shop door on a Sunday, and Little Slake — riverwall, chapel, gutter, mother, notice, and all — was somewhere else entirely.

# Chapter Five — The Fog Decides

Inside the Fogline, the river was wider.

Wren established this professionally. She paced the deck from bow to stern coiling the mooring line as instructed — over the elbow, thumb out, none of your shopkeeper's hanks, deckhand — and while she paced she measured, because measuring is what you do with your eyes when your eyes cannot otherwise be trusted. The Sedge at Little Slake was a hundred and forty yards bank to bank; her father had chained it once for a wager. Here there were no banks. There was pewter-colored water going out flat in every direction until the fog chose to end it, and the fog chose differently on each side, and once — she wrote this down with the line still over her elbow — it chose differently on the *same* side twice in one minute.

Sounds arrived without addresses. A dog barked, once, somewhere that had no somewhere. A church rang a half hour that belonged to no clock Wren knew, and the chapel at Little Slake would have been mortified to be so accurate. Underneath everything the engine kept up its big warm dreaming, and the water went past the hull with a sound like pages.

At the wheel, Captain Marl steered the way you stir soup you trust.

"We're not steering for anywhere," Wren said, arriving at the wheelhouse door with the coiled line worn like a bandolier, because if you are going to ask impertinent questions you should look as employed as possible while doing it.

"I'm steering," said the captain, "for *between*. The fog does the rest. Look at your wake."

Wren looked. The wake went out behind them, white and orderly, and then — she made herself watch it happen — it bent. No current bent it. It bent the way a sentence bends when the speaker changes their mind halfway, and where it bent, the fog stood a little denser, a little more deliberate, like a hand laid on the water.

"It's reading us," she said.

"It's *routing* us," said Captain Marl. "Reading came earlier. It read the fares at the box — that's what the box is for; the box is the fog's ear, or near enough. Seven fares, seven destinations, and none of those people going where they think. Ollery thinks he's going across the water to give back an umbrella. The fog heard a forty-year excuse paid in whole, and it will land him — you watch — somewhere a good deal more precise than *across the water.* The fog is many things, and I'll thank you not to list them aloud while we're inside it, but above everything it is a *sorter.* It takes what's paid and it finds the far bank that squares the account. Our work is only to keep the boat sound and the count true while it does it."

Wren stood in the wheelhouse door and watched the gray go by, and asked the question that had been sitting in her chest since the queue.

"What does it do with the fares? Mr. Ollery's excuse. Miss Vane's *too late.* The twins' toffee secret. Where do they go?"

"Ah," said Captain Marl, and was quiet for the length of three pages of water. "Somewhere they're needed, is the honest edge of what I know. The fog doesn't eat them, if that's your fear. It's not a customs shed, it's — " the captain's hands moved on the wheel, quarter of a spoke, back again " — it's more like your mother's shop, deckhand. Things come in that one person can spare, and things go out that another person can't do without, and the shop holds them in between, and the shop is very old, and the shop does not explain its stock to the errand girl. Now walk your rounds. Passengers get strange in fog, and strange passengers need a calm face going past at regular intervals, and yours is the calmest face I've hired in a century, which is why I hired it."

So Wren walked her rounds, calm-faced, with everything under the face going like the dredger's flywheel.

The passengers were strange in seven different ways. Mr. Ollery had discovered that his umbrella would now open — it had opened for him at the first shake, after forty shut years — and he sat under it in the windless fog, delighted and bereft, a man whose excuse was gone and whose errand had therefore gone simple. Miss Vane had her gloves off and was writing, very fast, on the backs of old attendance slips, the words *well done* visible even upside down, in a hand that got younger as it went down the page. The twins knelt up backward on a bench trying to out-stare the fog, which was a fair fight. Mr. Dunnock's bees had gone quiet in their humming box — asleep, he said; *listening*, said the box's silence. Mrs. Grebe held her scentless basket on her knees like a hymnal.

Only Old Nol was unchanged. He sat in the bow exactly as he had sat since Little Slake, cap in his two hands, facing forward, and when Wren's rounds brought her past him for the fourth time he said, without turning, in a voice like a lock gate easing:

"You'll be counting us again soon, girl."

"When the fog has us," Wren agreed. "It has us."

"It has us," said Old Nol. He turned his cap over once, as if checking the luck hadn't spilled out of it early. "I was aboard a ferry on this river the last time a count came out wrong. Sixty years back, come autumn." He faced forward again, at the fog. "Count slow," he said. "Count kind. That's all. Count kind."

Wren wrote it down — she could do nothing else with it — and went walking, calm-faced, to the stern and back.

And it was on that return, past the fare box, past the clapperless bell, that the lamps along the deck rail — which nobody had lit, which were nevertheless burning the small brave yellow of shop windows at closing — all leaned their flames, together, toward the aft bench on the port side.

The way flames lean when a door has opened somewhere.

# Chapter Six — The Second Count

Wren did not stop walking. Afterward she was proudest of that: her boots kept their exact errand-pace, past the leaning lamps, up the deck, to the wheelhouse, where she stepped inside, and stood, and said — walking-voiced, not running-voiced —

"Captain. It's time I did the second count."

Captain Marl looked at her for exactly one breath, and something went over the weathered face that was gone before it could be named, though later, with the notebook open, Wren tried: *proud,* she wrote, *and sorry, both at once, like weather doing two things.*

"Then count," said the captain, and turned back to the wheel, and took hold of it slightly differently than before — the way you hold a ladder someone is about to climb.

Wren went out on deck. The fog stood close around the boat now on every side, an audience of it, and the lamps burned yellow and leaning, and Tar came down off the wheelhouse roof and fell in beside her, black as the inside of a pocket, saying nothing.

"Second count," Wren called, in the voice she'd heard her mother use for stocktaking, a voice that made an ordinary thing of whatever it landed on. "Sit easy, all. It's only counting."

She started at the bow, by faces, not by hats.

"One." Old Nol, cap in hands, who did not look at her, and whose lips moved with her, counting along.

"Two." Mrs. Grebe and her basket.

"Three." Mr. Dunnock, one hand flat on the quiet hive.

"Four. Five." Iva and Pearl, kneeling the right way round now, subdued at last, shoulder against shoulder.

"Six." Miss Vane, her attendance slips squared into a tidy deck, her eyes up and steady on Wren's — a teacher recognizing, from the far side of the desk, the sensation of being examined.

"Seven." Mr. Ollery, under his open umbrella.

And Wren's boots took her on, because the rule was that you did not stop until you had finished, and the deck did not end at Mr. Ollery, and neither did the passengers.

"Eight," said Wren, at the aft bench on the port side.

He sat where the lamplight leaned. A boy. Bigger than her — thirteen, maybe, or the start of fourteen — in a deckhand's canvas jacket of a cut she had seen in exactly one place before: the brown photograph in the chapel vestry, the one of the jetties when all three worked. He sat very straight, feet flat, hands on knees, like a boy minding his manners in a parlor, and he was gray. Not sick-gray. Weather-gray. The gray of the fog, wearing a boy the way fog wears a hedge at the bottom of a field — entirely there, and made of elsewhere.

He looked up at her and his face was young and worried and hopeful and drenched-through with waiting, and he said, in a voice that came from a long way inside the morning:

"Is this the ferry?"

"Yes," said Wren, because it was.

"Then I'm not too late," said the boy, and smiled — and his smile had the whole of sixty years in it, though she didn't know that number yet — "I'm never too late. I'm always exactly this early."

Wren finished the rule. She turned, walked her errand-pace up the whole length of the listening deck to the wheelhouse, stepped in, and said:

"Captain. The count is eight. It was seven at the gangway. The wrong way is *more.*" And then, because the rest of the rule was done and her knees chose that moment to mention everything her face had been declining to discuss: "He's sitting on the aft bench. He's gray like the fog's gray. He asked me if this is the ferry, and captain, there's no fare for him in the box — I know it the way I know when mother's till is out. The count is eight and the fares are seven."

Tar flowed up onto the chart shelf. "Eight," the cat confirmed. "And the eighth weighs nothing. The bench doesn't know he's on it."

Captain Marl let out a long breath, and did a thing Wren had not yet seen: reached up and took off the gray cap, and held it, the way you do at certain doors.

"Sixty years," the captain said, quietly, to the fog ahead. "You might have handed him up gentler than mid-crossing, you old sorter. But you never do a kindness the easy way, do you."

The wheel came a half-spoke over. The engine dreamed on. Somewhere below and ahead — felt through the deck sooner than heard — the river changed its sound, from pages to a held breath, and the fog in front of the bow began, slowly, ceremonially, to darken like steeping tea.

"Deckhand," said the captain, cap back on, both hands on the wheel. "The passengers will want their calm face. Go and learn our stowaway's name. I'd say you have about an hour."

"Before what?"

"Before the Fogline," said Captain Marl, "asks to see our tickets."

# Chapter Seven — The Boy Made of Weather

Nobody remembered him boarding. Wren checked, bench by bench, in her stocktaking voice, and it was worse than that: they could not quite manage to remember him *now.* Miss Vane looked straight at the aft bench and her eyes came away with nothing to declare. Mrs. Grebe offered him bread from her basket and forgot the offer with her hand still out, and stared at her own arm as if it had said something odd. Only Old Nol, in the bow, would not look aft at all — would not, rather than could not, his neck stiff as new rope — and only the twins, whose eyes were younger than forgetting, waved. The boy waved back the way you wave when your whole arm is grateful.

Wren sat down on the aft bench, opened the notebook, and did an inventory, because kindness comes in every shape and hers had columns.

"Wren Tolliver," she said. "Deckhand. What's your name?"

"Tam Rush," said the boy, promptly, and then said it again, slower, with relief, like a man patting his pocket and finding his key still in it. "Tam Rush. Deck-boy." A pause. "Of the *Harebell.*"

The pen stopped on the page. Every child of Little Slake knew that name the way you know a scar. It was on the brass plate in the chapel. It was the answer to why the far jetty leaned unmended: *the Harebell's jetty,* the old people called it, when they called it anything.

"The *Harebell,*" said Wren, evenly, "sailed sixty years ago."

"It sails today," Tam corrected her, gently, the way you'd correct a younger child about something lovely. "The last run. Fog's notice went up Tuesday: one more crossing, all who are going, and after that the Line moves down past the Hatch and Little Slake is — " his gray hand moved, describing it " — off the route. Whole town's argued all week. Forty are going across to stay. Rest are stopping home." He looked at her, worried, hopeful, drenched with waiting. "I counted the forty aboard. Twice, mind — I always count twice, Captain Weck says a count you won't say twice is a count you don't believe once. Forty, and forty, and the counts agreed, and then the captain sends me back up the jetty to ring the bell. Last bell from the Little Slake side, see. Ever. Somebody had to, and I'm deck-boy; bell's mine." He said it with a pride so old and so unrusted that Wren's throat did something unhelpful. "So I rang it. Proper. Full minute. And when I turned round —"

The fog around the boat stood very still.

"— the fog had come up over the jetty," said Tam Rush, "between me and the boat. That's all. It came up quick. And I've been just about to catch them ever since. It's fine —" as if she'd argued; she hadn't moved "— it's *fine,* they'd never sail without me, I'm the count. I'm the one that counts. They'll have laid to just inside the Line, waiting on their deck-boy, and this boat's going the very way, so I got on. It's all worked out. I'm not even tired." He sat with his hands on his knees, straight-backed, minding his manners in the parlor of sixty years. "I'm just cold," he said, in a smaller voice, "sometimes," and the lamps down the whole deck leaned toward him like a row of people who cannot bear a thing and cannot look away from it.

Wren wrote: *Tam Rush. Deck-boy, Harebell. Rang the bell. Fog closed. Counted everyone twice —*

And her pen stopped, and she read her own line back, and the whole shape of it went through her like the cold of the sill under her fingers that first evening.

*Counted everyone twice. Except.*

"Tam," she said. "When you counted the forty. Twice, at the gangway, like you do." She kept her voice on its stocktaking rails, and it took every rail she had. "Did you ever count *yourself?*"

Tam Rush looked at her, and blinked, and it was clear — clear as the river on a frost morning — that in sixty years of waiting, in all that gray, this had never once occurred to him.

"Crew's not passengers," he said. But he said it slowly.

"The manifest closed at forty," said Wren, "and you weren't in it. You weren't in the count, Tam. The fog sorts by the count — it's a sorter, it squares accounts — and you weren't *in* the account. It didn't shut you out." She had his hand somehow; it was cold the way the notebook's cover was cold, a thing left outside overnight. "It couldn't see you at all. Nobody ever counted you aboard anything, so it's held you at the gangway. For sixty years you've been standing on the jetty in the middle of ringing the bell."

The fog was very close now, and darkening ahead, tea-dark, and through the deck came the long slow feeling of the river holding its breath, and from the wheelhouse Captain Marl called, in the voice captains keep folded away for exactly one drawer of occasions:

"Fogline, all! Deckhand to the box! They're asking for the tickets!"

# Chapter Eight — The Toll

The Fogline from inside was a wall the color of iron kettles, and it went up out of sight, and the river ran along the foot of it and under, through a seam of dark water that shone like the gap under a door.

The ferry came to the wall and stopped without being stopped — the engine dreaming on, the water sliding past, the boat itself held as gently and absolutely as a word held on the tip of a tongue. The clapperless bell rang once. Nothing rang it. It was a beautiful sound and Wren never learned to like it.

"Fares to the Line!" said Captain Marl, at the wheelhouse door. "Deckhand opens the box. Passengers stand easy. It takes what's paid and nothing else — it has never once taken else, and it won't begin tonight if everybody minds their count. Wren Tolliver: the key is the whole of your left hand, flat on the lid, and it will know whose daughter you are, and your mother can explain that or not as suits her. Open it up."

Wren put her left hand flat on the cold brass lid of the fare box, and the box unlocked with a sound like a till at closing time, satisfied and final.

What came out was the morning's queue, made visible. The fares rose from the box one at a time, each in its own kind of light, and went over the rail into the iron-dark wall, and the wall took each one the way the box had, the way dry ground takes rain: Mr. Ollery's excuse first, a beautiful complicated shimmer with weather in it and a dog that really existed, gone into the gray with all its polish on. Miss Vane's *it is too late,* a small black sentence that the fog swallowed whole, and served it right. The toffee secret, giggling even now. The flying dream, which tried, at the last, to fly, and did, and was allowed to be magnificent for the length of the deck before the wall received it like an old teacher. The gold smell of bread. And last, deliberate as its owner, one round untouched hour of a lock-keeper's best luck, which crossed the rail glowing like a sovereign — *fares are never money,* Wren thought, *but luck comes minted* — and was gone.

Six lights and a sentence and an hour. Seven fares.

The seam of bright water under the wall began, by inches, to widen.

And then it stopped widening.

The bell rang once more, uninvited. Along the rail every lamp bent flat as combed grass, all pointing aft. The wall of fog leaned out over the boat the way the third stepping-stone had looked at the bridgekeeper's daughter, and out of it came pressing a long cold attention, moving down the deck bench by bench, counting — you could feel it counting, a finger of winter going *one, two, three* down the row of them — and where it arrived at last, at the aft bench, at a gray boy sitting very straight with his cold hands on his knees, it stopped, and it stayed, and the whole enormous patient weight of the Fogline gathered over Tam Rush like a question that has waited sixty years to be asked with the right person in the room.

*Eight aboard,* said the fog. It did not say it in words. It said it in the water and the lamp-flames and the small bones of Wren's ears, and every passenger heard it, and even Miss Vane's eyes, this time, found the bench and held it. *Eight aboard. Seven paid. The Line opens for the account that squares.*

"There it is," said Captain Marl, quietly, from the wheel, cap in hand again. "Sixty years the old sorter's held him, and it means to settle him tonight, one way or the other way. Deckhand —" and the captain's no-particular-color eyes came down the deck to Wren, steady as a handed rope "— you found him. You count him. What does he have?"

Wren stood by the open empty fare box with her notebook against her chest, and looked at Tam Rush, deck-boy of the *Harebell,* who owned a canvas jacket, a rung bell, a pride in a straight count, and sixty gray years of being just about to catch them — and the fog waited, and the seam of bright water under the door of the world stood a hand's width open, and did not widen, and did not close.

# Chapter Nine — Stock-Taking

Wren Tolliver had grown up over a shop, and there is a thing that shops know that nowhere else quite knows, and it is this: *everybody has stock. The trick is the counting of it.*

She crossed the deck and sat down on the aft bench beside the boy the whole night was leaning on, and she opened the notebook to a fresh page, and she dated it, because the date was the first thing she could do for him and she meant to do him every single thing she could.

"Tam Rush," she said. "We're going to take inventory."

"I haven't anything," said Tam. He said it simply, without self-pity, a deck-boy reporting an empty hold. "That's the whole trouble, isn't it. I heard what the fares were, this morning. Excuses and secrets and dreams and luck. I've been standing on a jetty since before your mother was born, Wren Tolliver. Everything I had's been sixty years somewhere else. My kit sailed with the boat. My — " the smallest hitch, the first one " — my folk sailed with the boat. I've a jacket and a cold and half a bell-rope's worth of blister, and you can't pay a fog with a blister."

"That," said Wren, "is what everyone says at the start of stocktaking. My mother says the first count of any shop is always *nothing, we're ruined,* and the second count is the truth. We're doing the second count. Sit up. Answer plainly."

Behind her, unseen, Captain Marl leaned in the wheelhouse door and did not interrupt, and Tar sat on the fare box with his tail wrapped, watching the smallest shop in the world open its books; and the great iron wall of the Fogline waited, which was the one thing it knew best how to do.

"Item," said Wren. "One canvas jacket, deck-boy's, sixty years old, still fits. That means something here. Things that stay true have weight — I watched an umbrella get heavier for forty years. Item: one rung bell. You rang the last bell ever rung on the Little Slake side, a full proper minute of it, alone, with your boat leaving. Nobody else in the history of the town has that. Item: one count. Forty souls counted aboard the *Harebell,* twice, counts agreeing, by a boy the captain trusted with the whole town's leaving." Her pen was flying now. "Item —"

"Those aren't things I *have,*" said Tam. "Those are things I *did.*"

"Same shelf," said Wren, and heard, behind her, the captain make a small sound like a swallowed word. "And item," she said, and here she slowed down, because the pen had gotten ahead of her and written it already, and she had to catch up to her own hand, and when she did her voice came out quiet: "Item. One waiting. Sixty years long. Never once set down."

The lamps stood straight up.

"You said it yourself," said Wren. "*They'd never sail without me. They'll have laid to just inside the Line. I'm just about to catch them.* You've held that, without putting it down, without once letting go of the handle, for sixty years, Tam. In the cold. In the gray. All by yourself on a condemned jetty. It's the biggest thing anybody's carried up this gangplank all day — it's bigger than the excuse and the secret and the dream and the hour of luck *put together* — and it's true, and it's yours, and I think —" she looked up from the page, and found she was fierce, and let herself be — "I think it's exactly the kind of thing the fog trades in. I think your fare is the waiting."

Tam Rush went very still, the particular stillness of a boy on a jetty between one turn of his head and the next.

"If I pay that," he said, at last, and his voice had gone down to the small cold place it kept for *sometimes.* "If I put it in the box. Then it's — done, isn't it. Paid means spent. It'd mean the *Harebell* sailed. Sixty years ago. Without me. It'd mean they didn't lay to inside the Line and wait, it'd mean nobody's — " and the fog all around the boat was utterly still, and every lamp was leaning, and Miss Vane's hand was pressed flat to her collarbone as if holding a sentence in " — it'd mean I *missed it,*" said Tam Rush, and there it was, out on the deck at last, sixty years old and dressed as a boy: the thing under the waiting. "I'd rather be cold," he said, barely, "than late."

Wren looked down at her notebook, at *count kind* written in her own hand in the middle of the river with the fog listening.

"Tam," she said. "When you count a boatload twice, and the counts agree — what does Captain Weck say the second count is for? You told me this morning. A count you won't say twice —"

" — is a count you don't believe once," whispered Tam.

"You believed the first count all this time," said Wren. "*They'd never sail without me.* You've said it sixty years. But you never said the second count, the checking one, out loud, with the fog listening. And the second count isn't the enemy of the first, Tam, it's the *making sure of it.* So make sure. Say the true count of that morning, all of it, yourself included, and see what the fog does with a squared account." She held out the notebook and the pen. "You're crew," she said. "Crew aren't passengers. You don't pay at the box like a passenger. Crew sign the log."

From the wheelhouse door, Captain Marl spoke, and the captain's voice had the fold shaken out of it now, warm as the galley stove: "She has it right, deck-boy. Sixty years I've carried a blank line in my log and never known whose it was. Come sign your name where it's been waiting."

# Chapter Ten — All Counted

They did it properly, because Tam Rush had been raised aboard a working ferry and would not have survived doing it any other way.

The log came out of the wheelhouse: a ledger with a green spine, older than the notice on the jetty, older than the chapel clock, its pages ruled for names. Captain Marl opened it flat on the fare box, and there — Wren saw it and her arms went to gooseflesh — halfway down a page dated sixty autumns back, between *Weck, Capt., the Harebell, 40 souls, last crossing* and the next day's business, ran one ruled line, empty, that every entry since had stepped around. Sixty years of ink, and not one word had ever sat on that line, the way nobody sits in a chair that everyone somehow knows is saved.

"Stand at the rail, all," said Captain Marl. "This is a counting. First and second, gangway and fog, and the deck-boy of the *Harebell* has the call."

Tam Rush stood at the fare box with the pen in his cold gray fingers, facing the iron wall, and called the roll of a morning sixty years gone. He called it from memory, and his memory of it was the freshest thing aboard, because he had been standing inside that morning all this time: forty names, the *Harebell*'s last manifest, and he gave each one its trade and its street the way a bell gives the hour — *Hollet the wheelwright and his four. The Misses Prynne, both, with the linnet in the cage. Weck, Captain, at the wheel. Rush, Margaret, ship's cook —* and here his voice went carefully, wonderfully steady, in the way of a boy being brave in front of his mother's name — *my mam. Forty aboard. That was the first count, and I made it at the gangway, and it was true.*

"Say the second," said Captain Marl, quiet.

"Second count," said Tam Rush, to the Fogline, out loud, with the fog listening. "Made this night, from the deck of the ferry with no name, by Tam Rush, deck-boy, who rang the bell." He took one breath, sixty years deep. "Forty aboard the *Harebell* — and one on the jetty. Forty-one souls, all told, at the last crossing of Little Slake. The counts don't agree." His voice cracked exactly once, on the next thing, and he said it anyway, and Wren decided in that moment that she would know this boy her whole life: "*The boat sailed. I missed it. I was ringing the bell, and it was worth ringing, and I missed my boat.* That's the true count. All told. All —"

He stopped. Wren put her hand flat on the log beside his, the way you steady a page in wind.

"— all counted," said Tam Rush, and signed the empty line.

What happened then, Wren wrote three separate times in the notebook and crossed out twice, and the version she let stand was this:

*The waiting went over the rail.*

It rose off him like fog off a field when the sun finally attends to it — sixty years of it, gray and cold and faithfully carried, more of it than the boy it came off of, and for one moment it hung above the deck in the shape of everything it had been: a jetty, a bell-note, a boy turning round; and the Fogline leaned out and took it — not the way dry ground takes rain, this time, but the way a very old shopkeeper takes the last payment of a long account: carefully, both hands, with respect.

And under the iron wall, the seam of bright water swung wide as a shop door on market day.

Color came up the deck like a tide. Tam Rush stood at the fare box in a canvas jacket that was brown — plain, worn, honest brown — and his hands on the ledger were a boy's hands in November, chapped and pink and cold-nipped and altogether the colors of the living, and he looked down at them, and up at Wren, and laughed the way the twins had laughed at the gangway: helplessly, at the enormous safety of a thing finally told.

"All counted," said the fog — in the water, in the lamp-flames, in the small bones of everyone's ears, and there was nothing in the sound of it of iron at all now; there was something in it, Wren would have sworn, of a bell. "*All counted. The account squares. The Line is open.*"

"Wren Tolliver," said Captain Marl, at the wheel, in the drawer-voice, but the drawer had sixpence and supper in it now, "log your count."

"Eight aboard!" called Wren, and her voice went out over the bright water like a wagtail. "Seven passengers, paid — and one crew, *signed!*"

"Eight," said Tar, from the fare box, and put one black paw, briefly, on Tam Rush's sleeve, which from Tar was a knighthood.

"Counts agree," said Captain Marl. "Hold on to your hats, all. Sixty years late is still tonight on this river."

And the ferry with no name went through the door in the wall of the world with its lamps all burning upright, and every soul aboard felt the crossing the way you feel the first stair down in the dark — that lovely dreadful step onto something that is exactly, after all, where it should be —

and the fog on the far side wasn't gray at all.

# Chapter Eleven — Everywhere Else

The far side of the Fogline was lit like the inside of a pearl, and the river braided.

That was the only word Wren's notebook would accept for it: the one broad water divided ahead of them into channel after channel after channel, each between its own soft walls of shining, each with its own light down at the end the way a hallway has its own door, and there were more channels than she could count — she tried; Tar watched her try with open approval — and the ferry threaded them at a walking pace while Captain Marl called the stops.

There was no calling them ahead of time. That was the thing to understand, the captain said, and Wren never afterward could make anyone at home understand it: the far bank *isn't anywhere in particular.* It is somewhere very particular indeed, one passenger at a time.

"First stop!" called Captain Marl, and the ferry slid down a channel that smelled suddenly and exactly of wet slate roofs, and came alongside a small stone landing under a lit window, where a very old man stood leaning on a stick with his housekeeper holding an anxious lamp, and Mr. Ollery rose from his bench with the umbrella rolled and his heart in his good coat, and went down the gangplank forty years and no seconds late.

They did not hear the excuse he didn't give. They heard, as the ferry backed away, the two old men begin to laugh, and the laugh followed them out of the channel like the smell of the bread.

Miss Vane's landing was a school gate — not the shrieking kind — in the blue of an evening somewhere with hills, where a stout gray-haired woman was locking up a schoolhouse of her own, and turned at the sound of the engine, and put her hand over her mouth; and Miss Vane went down the plank with her attendance slips squared in her glove, upright as a Monday, ten years younger than too late.

The twins' channel smelled of toffee from a quarter mile out, which everyone aboard agreed was the fog showing off. Their grandmother's cottage stood on a green bank with the door already open, and their grandmother came down to the landing at a pace unbecoming a grandmother, and the last that ferry heard of Iva and Pearl Tanner was the pair of them, mid-hug, beginning — helplessly, both at once — to tell her the secret ingredient, which they now safely and forever did not know.

Mr. Dunnock was set down at an orchard landing where a young man in black stood alone by three empty hives, in the particular stillness of somebody starting over; the humming box went ashore awake. Mrs. Grebe's landing was a chapel porch full of christening guests who all turned at once, and her basket, Wren noticed as she helped it over the rail, smelled of nothing at all right up until the moment her feet touched the far stones, when the whole channel went warm with gold.

And Old Nol stood up in the bow at a channel mouth that looked, of all of them, the most ordinary: a river lock, upcountry somewhere, lamplit, with the gates standing patiently open and a lock-keeper's cottage above it and washing on the line in the evening air. He put his cap on. He looked a long time.

"Sixty years back," he said, to Wren, at the rail, "I stood on the everyday jetty and watched the *Harebell* go, and my best friend on her deck, and I was fourteen and too proud to pay what the crossing asked, and I've wondered every day since what my luck was for." He nodded down the shining channel at the little lock, at the cottage, at a door in the door-light where a figure had come out onto the step, shading her eyes at the sound of an engine sixty years overdue. "Now I know. It was for choosing the hour. Evening, girl. Mind the count."

He went down the plank like a man arriving on time.

Then the benches were empty, all of them, and the braided light stood quiet around the boat, and there was one channel left ahead — the widest one, the oldest-looking one, with a light down at its end that flickered, very slightly, in a rhythm Wren's ears knew before she did.

A bell. Somebody down that channel was ringing a bell.

Tam Rush came forward to the bow with his brown jacket buttoned, and stood beside her, and did not say anything at all, and his chapped living hands held the rail the way you hold the counter of a shop on the morning it finally, finally opens.

"Deckhand," said Captain Marl, easing the wheel over, "call the stop."

"It's not my — " Wren began, and then understood, and her eyes stung, and she called it in her wagtail voice down the whole bright length of the last channel: "*Harebell landing! All ashore that's sixty years going ashore!*"

# Chapter Twelve — Overslake

The town at the end of the channel was Little Slake.

It was Little Slake the way the brown photograph in the chapel vestry was Little Slake: three jetties and all three working, a riverwall with the same seven lamps her own riverwall had five of, the chapel with its clock — Wren checked, of course she checked — running eleven minutes fast, out of civic optimism, on the far side of the door under the world. The houses had settled differently, and the trees had had sixty years of their own opinions, and over the door of a rope-and-tin shop on what was unmistakably Slake Street hung a sign that said, unmistakably, OVERSLAKE.

"They named it," said Captain Marl, bringing the wheel over a last quarter-spoke, "the way you name the far end of anything. Over-the-water Slake. Forty souls came across on the *Harebell*'s last run, and the fog set them down here, all together — that was the fare the whole town's leaving had paid, though none of them knew they'd paid it: a town, spared. And they built their landing, and they kept their boat painted, and one of them, deckhand, has rung the bell on the first Tuesday of every month for sixty years, on the chance the Line might open. You may tell your chapel sexton, when you're home, that somewhere the habit of hopeless punctuality is a *sacrament.*"

The *Harebell* lay at the landing.

She was small — smaller than Wren had built her in her head, the way everything in the vestry photograph was smaller than its legend — clinker-built and honest, and sixty years painted: you could see the years of paint on her the way you can see rings in a stump, layer on faithful layer, harebell blue. Her brass was bright. Her bell was swinging, live, in a rope-hardened old hand.

And the landing was full. Word travels fast in a town of forty souls and their sixty years of children; there were lanterns coming down every lane of Overslake, and the crowd stood along the water in their nightshirts and coats the way crowds had once stood, on the other side, to see off the coal barge — except silent, all of them, dead silent, watching the ferry with no name come up the channel with a boy in the bow.

Tam Rush stood at the rail where the gangway would go, and his manners finally failed him, and no one aboard held it against him: he was up on the sheer-strake with one hand on a stanchion, leaning out over the bright water like a figurehead of some country whose flag was a canvas jacket, searching the lantern-light face by face, counting — Wren could see his lips move — counting the way he did everything, and his count kept snagging and starting over, because the faces kept being almost, almost, almost —

"*TAM!*"

An old woman came through the crowd the way Mrs. Grebe's bread-smell had gone into the box: parting everything, golden, unstoppable. She was small and white-haired and she had a wooden spoon in her hand — she had, it would later be established, been stirring something, and would carry the spoon through the entire ensuing hour without once noticing — and the crowd made way for her because the crowd knew, every lantern of it, exactly who she was and exactly who was standing on the incoming rail.

Ship's cook of the *Harebell.* Sixty years of first Tuesdays.

Tam Rush said, "Mam," in a voice that was no particular age at all, and did not wait for the plank, which Wren logged in the book as *conduct unbecoming, thoroughly becoming,* and jumped.

He landed in the shallows of everywhere else, drenched to the knee in bright water, three strides from sixty years, and Margaret Rush dropped her spoon after all and met him at the second stride, and the lanterns closed around the two of them, and the whole crowd of Overslake made one sound together. It was not a cheer. A cheer is thinner. It was the sound a town makes when its last account squares — when the one line stepped-around in every ledger of every family in it is finally signed — and Wren Tolliver stood at the rail of the ferry with her notebook shut, because there are counts you keep and counts you only witness, and Tar sat beside her with his tail wrapped round his feet, purring like a slow engine, and even Captain Marl, at the wheel, cap in hand, was — Wren logged this too, and stood by it — leaking, quietly, in a weathered and dignified fashion, at the eyes.

An old man came down the landing through the lanterns, straight-backed, in a captain's coat sixty years out of fashion and utterly unimpeached, and stood at the water's edge, and looked a long time at the boy in the shallows. Then he looked up at the ferry with no name, and at Captain Marl, and touched his cap-brim, one working captain to another across whatever it is that lies between here and everywhere else.

"You'd be the one that carries them now," said Captain Weck of the *Harebell.* His voice was like the log's green spine. "We heard the Line open. Sixty years we've told them — the young ones — the Line answers a squared account, and sixty years it's been a story for first Tuesdays." He looked at Tam, hauled now onto the landing proper and being wept on in relays. "Who found the fare? He never had a thing on him but the jacket. We knew. It's why we couldn't — " and there sixty years of a captain's arithmetic showed for a moment in his face, the long unforgiving sum of a boat sailed on time " — it's why the Line never opened to *our* knocking. The debt wasn't ours to pay. Who counted him?"

"My deckhand," said Captain Marl, and did not say anything further, and did not need to; and the old captain of the *Harebell* looked at Wren Tolliver, eleven, of the rope shop, in wool, with a stock book buttoned over her heart, and gave her the whole cap-brim, all the way off the head, the salute Wren would not learn the size of for years, until she had stood at a wheel or two herself.

"Deck-boy Rush," called Captain Weck, without turning, in a voice that had called it across weather Wren hoped never to meet, "you're out of uniform and your count's overdue."

And Tam Rush, drenched, laughing, alive, mothered half to death, came up straight as a Monday out of the crowd and gave back, cracked and joyful, the answer he'd been keeping warm the whole of a cold sixty years:

"All aboard that's coming aboard, captain! Forty-one souls, all told —" and the bell of the *Harebell* rang out over the bright water, live and swinging, rung by somebody's grandchild, one full minute, proper — "*all counted!*"

⁂

They stayed an hour, by the chapel clock of Overslake, which is to say: nobody ever agreed afterward how long they stayed.

There was toffee. There was a great deal of toffee; the twins' grandmother, it turned out, was not the only keeper of the recipe on this side, and Overslake's version had had sixty years to diverge, and feelings about the divergence ran hot and delicious in both directions. There was Margaret Rush holding Wren's face in two floury hands and saying nothing at all, which said it. There was the *Harebell*'s log fetched out, and the two green ledgers laid open side by side on a trestle under the lanterns, sixty years apart, at two versions of one morning, and Captain Weck drawing his pen down at last through the entry that read *40 souls* and writing above it, in the careful uphill hand of a man correcting the only mistake of his career: *41. Deck-boy rejoined. Line squared. W.T., deckhand, per the count.*

Wren Tolliver's initials, in the log of the *Harebell,* on the far side of the fog.

"Ledgers," said Captain Weck, blotting it, "are patient. That's all a ledger is, girl. Patience, ruled."

And when the ferry with no name backed off the Overslake landing — engine dreaming, lamps upright, Tar aloofly permitting a dockside crowd of children to admire him — the whole town stood along the bright water to see them out of the channel, and Tam Rush stood at the front of it, between his mother and his captain, one hand raised, not waving: *holding,* the way you hold a thing you mean to keep; and the last Wren saw of him, before the pearl-light folded the channel closed, he had turned to look up at Captain Weck, and he was talking, both hands going, and Wren knew exactly what he was doing, because it was what she would have been doing.

He was telling the story.

# Chapter Thirteen — The Way Back Is Shorter

The way back was shorter. Captain Marl said it always is, and declined, at the wheel, in the pearl-colored quiet between the walls of shining, to say one word more about it; so Wren took the question to the fare box, where Tar sat on the brass-cornered lid washing an ear with the industry of a cat who has recently been publicly admired.

"Why is the way back shorter?"

"Because," said Tar, "you weigh less." The ear achieved perfection. "Everyone does, going home. You paid attention the whole way out, and attention's heavy. Any other questions, deckhand, while my rates are low?"

Wren had approximately four hundred, and an open notebook, and the sense — new tonight, and never to leave her — of standing at the very beginning of a very long stock book. She picked the question at the front.

"The boat had a name. The captain said so, my first evening: *that's not the same as never having had one.* What was it?"

Tar stopped washing.

"That," said the cat, after a moment, "is not a low-rate question." He looked forward, at the wheelhouse, where the captain's gray shape stood easy against the pearl-light, and his voice did the thing voices do when they decide to hand you something breakable. "Here's what a cat will say for free. Fares are never money, and names are never free, and once — long before your town had a notice to hang — this boat's name was somebody's fare. Paid at that box. Taken clean off her bows, letter by letter, and over the rail, and the account it squared was —" the tail tightened round the feet "— large. She's run nameless since, and the captain's run with her, and if you want the rest of it, you'll want to be the kind of deckhand a captain tells things to on a slow crossing about four winters from now. I'd start," said Tar, resuming the ear, "by keeping the tea coming and the counts clean."

*Four winters,* wrote Wren, in the notebook, under a fresh heading she'd opened that had begun, already, to fill: *OWED TO ME BY THIS RIVER (patience, ruled).*

Under it were three entries now, and she read them over while the bright channels braided themselves back toward the iron-colored dark of the near side of the Line:

*Item: the boat's name. Somebody's fare. Whose? — four winters, per cat.*

*Item: the captain squared it with Mother "for reasons that are her business." The fare box knows our left hands. Mother knows the box sticks. Mother said "mind the notice," not "there's no boat." Query the entire history of my family, at tea, repeatedly, forever.*

*Item —*

The third entry was the newest, and she had written it back at the Overslake trestle, fast, while two ledgers lay open in lantern-light. Because Captain Marl, turning the pages of the nameless ferry's own green log to find Tam's signed line, had turned one page too many, and Wren — trained over a shop, cursed and blessed to read all ledgers upside down — had seen an entry, old, older than the *Harebell* page, in faded formal copperplate:

*Received of Tolliver, one fare, paid in full. Crossing open-dated, at the holder's need. Balance: kept.*

*Item: somebody named Tolliver has a paid-up crossing in that box. Open-dated. Uncollected. — WHO? For WHERE? And why does nobody in my family ride ferries?*

She was still looking at that entry when the light changed — pearl to iron, iron to plain honest night-gray — and the ferry came through the standing wall the ordinary way, without ceremony, the way you come out of a shop with the bell jingling behind you; and there, upriver, small and lamplit and dear and running eleven minutes fast, was the true and only original Little Slake, fast asleep on both banks of the ordinary Sedge.

"Second count," said Captain Marl, from the wheel, without turning round.

"Captain?"

"Passengers, deckhand. The rule is the rule, outbound and home."

Wren stood in the middle of the empty deck, and counted the benches, out loud, by faces, of which there were none; and the fog stood off the stern, watching, the way it had watched her on the riverwall the first evening — *facing her* — and she understood, at last, standing in the lamplight of the emptied boat, what the second count was actually for.

"None aboard," called Wren Tolliver, to her captain, and to the cat, and to the listening fog, and to the river between here and everywhere else. "Seven carried, seven landed. One found, one signed, one *home.* Nobody lost, nobody left, nobody standing on any jetty anywhere in the middle of ringing a bell." Her voice wobbled precisely once, on the last of it, and she let it, because the fog respects a person who'll say the thing out loud: "All counted."

"Counts agree," said Captain Marl, and Tar purred like a slow engine, and the fog — Wren logged it, and stood by the log entry all her life — *bowed;* the whole standing bank of it inclining, once, slightly, the way the third stepping-stone might finally nod to the bridgekeeper's daughter; and then it was only weather again, gray and enormous and minding its own business, all the way down the Sedge.

The condemned jetty took their line with a creak like a welcome. Far up Slake Street a light was on over a rope-and-tin shop, at half past very-late indeed, and somebody's shape was in the window, not anxious, exactly. Keeping a count of her own.

"Trial's over," said Captain Marl, at the gangplank, and put into Wren's palm a sixpence — real money; wages are wages — and held the hand a moment after, rope-hard and steady. "You counted a boy the whole river had lost, and you found the fare in him when he'd have sworn to empty pockets, and you said *all counted* out loud with the fog listening, and it bowed. I've had deckhands forty years who never got a nod out of it." The no-particular-color eyes were, Wren decided, the color of the river after all: you'd know them anywhere. "The job's yours, Wren Tolliver. Dawn Tuesdays, and any morning the fog docks us at your notice. Bring the book. Mind the third plank."

"She's a liar," said Wren.

"You'll do," said the captain.

# Chapter Fourteen — Home Before the Kettle

Her mother was at the shop door before Wren's boots found the good stones, and what happened there is family business, but it involved the kind of hug that re-counts a person, rib by rib, twice.

There was cocoa. There was, remarkably, no scolding — there was instead her mother turning the sixpence over in the lamplight with an expression Wren had last seen her use on a genuinely rare knot, and saying, "Left-hand lid and all, I suppose," and pouring a second cocoa without being asked, as if a certain conversation had been standing in a queue for years and had finally, resignedly, shuffled forward.

"The captain says the box still sticks," said Wren. "Only on the left. Only in spring. And that you still owe it a fare."

"Mm," said her mother, which was shopkeeper for *later.* But she said the rest into the fire, quietly, and Wren wrote it in the book before sleep, word for word, under ITEM TWO: "*Everyone in this family pays that box eventually, my wren. Your great-gran paid it a whole rope-walk. I've been keeping mine back for thirty years, and it can wait a few more, and so can you.*" A pause; a poker settling a log. "*Tuesdays, is it. You'll want thicker socks.*"

Which was, as declarations of unconditional surrender go, the finest in the history of Slake Street.

⁂

The days after went the way days go: school, shop, gutter (mended — her father came home Sunday and did it in an hour, and heard the whole story twice, once plain and once with the questions, and said at the end of it, being a dredger man, "*Always* count yourself. First rule of mud."). The wagtails kept the nest. The chapel clock kept its eleven optimistic minutes. The notice went on saying CONDEMNED, and Wren went on leaving it at number one in the book, partly from honesty — the planking really was scandalous — and partly because she had begun to suspect the lean and the notice were the ferry's idea of a locked door, and that some doors are better kept looking broken.

And on the first Tuesday of the month, at dawn, with fog standing on the river and thicker socks standing on the third plank, deckhand Tolliver reported aboard, and found the captain on the bollard, and the harbor cat sitting at attention with his tail wrapped, being paid.

"— and the bridgekeeper's daughter," Captain Marl was saying, "looked at the third stepping-stone, and the third stepping-stone looked back. And what she said to it — and it to her — and what was under it, keeping the toll — " the captain stood, unhurried, leaving the story open on the morning air like a lid " — is a tale for the next slow water, sir, because here's my crew, and we've a queue."

There was a queue. There would always be a queue now, Wren understood; the fog had opened something and word was traveling, the slow sure way word travels along a river. Six this morning: faces she knew, bundles she could inventory at a glance, fares she was already, ten years too early, beginning to guess at.

Tar fell in beside her boots at the gangway, disgusted and content.

"He *stops,*" said the cat, "at the best bit. Every time. One of these mornings, deckhand, I will get the end of a story out of that captain in one piece, and the river will run backward from shock."

"Pay more," said Wren.

"*Cheat,*" said Tar, comfortably, and took his post on the fare box.

Wren Tolliver stood at the head of the gangway of the ferry with no name, at the condemned jetty of Little Slake, with her notebook open to a fresh page and the fog standing up off the water like a shop door opening, and the whole river between here and everywhere else went out ahead of her, channel after channel after channel, wider than any chart had ever met.

"Good morning," she said, in her mother's stocktaking voice, to the queue, to the morning, to all of it. "Boarding now. Have your fares ready. Fares are never money."

And she counted them aboard, one by one, by faces.

Twice, when the fog had them. But that's the rule, and by now you know it: that one's not for the boarding.

That one's to be sure that everybody — *everybody* — counts.
