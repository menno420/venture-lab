# The Kill Rules — one page, binding

The failure mode of a productive builder is not idleness — it is **sunk
cost**. If you can build things cheaply, you will happily keep building a
thing that will never sell, because building feels like progress and
nothing in your process forces the question "should this stop?" These
rules supply that moment. They are written down BEFORE the build, when you
are calm and nothing is sunk.

## Rule 0 — the intake precedes the build

No candidate gets code, copy, or design work before its intake exists:
scoring table filled with the arithmetic shown, all three kill-rule fields
stated, "Why this might fail" written to the honesty bar below. An intake
that cannot state its own kill conditions — or that flinches from naming
its weakest axes — has not earned the build.

## The three binding fields (they work as a unit)

1. **Validation signal.** A deadline + a trigger event + a menu of
   concrete, observable thresholds, any one of which suffices. Example
   shape: *"within 30 days of publish: ≥1 sale OR ≥50 reads, else ledgered
   negative."* No wiggle room for "it feels promising." The signal bounds
   the TIME and defines success.

2. **First-ten-customers path.** The named channels the first ten buyers
   actually arrive through, in order, with every step you cannot do
   yourself marked as gated. Attach the honest warning your path deserves
   (e.g. *"same saturated community funnel as my last two products — does
   not diversify channel risk"*). The path forces an honest look at
   DISTRIBUTION before anything exists.

3. **Max effort budget.** A hard numeric ceiling (hours, tokens, dollars —
   pick your unit) to a shippable v0.1. The budget bounds the COST and
   converts "we can always do a bit more" into a bright line.

## Rule 1 — over budget without the signal = ledgered negative

Not "re-evaluate." Not "one more push." When a candidate hits its budget
without its validation signal, the intake already decided the outcome, in
advance, so no one has to find the courage later. Record it in the
negative ledger the same day.

## Rule 2 — the honesty bar for "Why this might fail"

Write the section a hostile reader would write: name the softest scoring
axes in plain words, the free substitutes, the channel saturation, the
shelf-life risk. Test: if your "Why this might fail" is shorter than your
"What it is", it is not done. A candidate that documents its own weakest
axes can be killed on evidence rather than defended on ego.

## Rule 3 — kills stay killed

Every kill gets a ledger entry (see `NEGATIVE-LEDGER-TEMPLATE.md`): what
died, which condition fired, actual spend vs budget, the lesson. A
negative result that is written down is an asset; one that isn't is a
product you will accidentally build again.

## Rule 4 — the rubric is comparative, not oracular

Scores don't tell you an idea is good. They keep the comparison BETWEEN
ideas consistent and force the weak axes into the open. Never edit a
weight to save a candidate; if you must override the ranking, write the
override and its reason into the intake so the exception is auditable.
