# The Last Good Frequency

An original contemporary YA coming-of-age novella.

## Pitch

The summer her best friend moves away and her shrinking Lake Superior town keeps papering over its storefronts, sixteen-year-old Wren Hart accidentally inherits the midnight slot on WMRW — the dying community radio station nobody admits to listening to and everybody somehow knows. Wren has spent her whole life being furniture, the quiet kid who lets the smallest possible word fall out so people will think she's fine and leave. But a license that needs a warm body in the dark, a mentor felled by a stroke, and an insomniac girl who calls herself "Nightbird" pull a real voice out of her one true small thing at a time. When the town moves to sell the tower out from under the station, Wren discovers the quietest kid in Marrow Bay has the one frequency the whole sleepless town has been waiting to hear — and that being heard was never the same as being loud.

## At a glance

- **Format:** complete novella, 12 chapters
- **Length:** ~26,400 words, English
- **Age band:** 13–17 (upper-middle / young YA)
- **Genre:** contemporary coming-of-age; small-town; quiet queer first love
- **Status:** complete draft manuscript; all publishing/commercial steps owner-gated — see `DECISIONS.md`

## Content note (13–17)

Grief (a grandmother's death from illness, off-page, tender); parental absence; economic decline (a mill town in slow collapse); teen anxiety. A gentle same-gender first-love arc — emotional intimacy, hand-holding, and one kiss; nothing explicit. Mild language only (no stronger than "hell/damn," sparingly). No sex, drugs, or graphic content. Hopeful throughout, with an earned — not tidy — ending.

## Cast

- **Wren Hart** — sixteen, biracial, an "ear" who catalogs the world by its sound; terrified of her own voice, secretly desperate to be heard. The girl on the graveyard shift.
- **Deb Hart** — Wren's mother; overnight hospice/ER nurse, exhausted and fiercely loving, wants Wren *out* of Marrow Bay.
- **Emeka Okonkwo** — Wren's father; a talented, unreliable touring sound engineer who left when she was nine and comes through town once. Not a villain — just a man built for leaving.
- **Cash Odom** — the retired mill worker who held WMRW's midnight slot for thirty years; felled by a stroke at the book's start. Wren's gruff, warm mentor; passes her the torch.
- **Ruth "Ruthie" Feygin** — WMRW's blunt, bad-coffee station manager, keeping the lights on out of spite; secretly proud of Wren.
- **Frankie Boaz** — sixteen, new in town, an artist and her dying grandmother's night-caregiver; calls the show as "Nightbird." Wren's gentle sapphic first love.
- **Lita (Carlota)** — Frankie's grandmother, dying slowly of heart failure; loved the radio her whole life. Her death is handled off-page and tenderly.
- **Theo Nakamura** — Wren's lifelong best friend, who moves to Chicago in Chapter 1; the friendship-loss engine that resolves into an honest long-distance shape.
- **The Night Voices** — the town's collective 3 a.m. chorus: Big Al the long-haul trucker at the overlook, Mrs. Petrakis the widow on Pearl Street, June from the overnight gas-plant crew, and Marcus, the kid who won't give his name.

## Chapters

1. **Dead Air** — Theo leaves for Chicago; a shrinking town, a quiet girl, an absent father — and a midnight phone call asking Wren to keep a dying station on the air.
2. **The Graveyard Shift** — Alone at the board, frozen at the mic, Wren finds her first two sentences in the one thing that isn't about her: the lost-and-found.
3. **Lost and Found** — A voice built out of other people's small true things; a jar of jam left on the step proves someone is listening — and the red phone light blinks for the first time.
4. **Nightbird** — Wren finally answers the phone. A sharp, sleepless girl who won't give her name keeps the dark company right back.
5. **Frequencies** — The Night Voices find her; the station's future turns precarious; Nightbird asks for a local song called "Copper Wire," and Wren realizes she'd play her anything.
6. **Daylight** — A new girl comes through Wren's checkout line, hums three notes, and two countries collide under fluorescent light: *so it's you.*
7. **The Static Between** — Daylight and airwaves reconcile at a shuttered drive-in; Frankie's caregiving truth, Wren's father-shaped silence, and the night the show gets its name.
8. **My Father's Hands** — Emeka comes through town for one evening; Wren gets a truer thing than the apology she wanted, and speaks about herself on air for the first time.
9. **Off Air** — The town moves to sell the tower; a hard-won visit with Cash passes the torch — and Wren asks the sleepless town, for the first time, for something.
10. **The Longest Night** — A midnight radiothon; a town remembering it's a town; and, near dawn, a loss that cracks everything open and ends in dead air.
11. **Silence** — Grief and its acoustics; the Night Voices in daylight; a station saved-enough, not saved-forever; and a first kiss on a fire escape.
12. **The Last Good Frequency** — Early fall, honest new shapes for old ties, a choice made on her own terms, and the sign-off the whole book has been reaching for.

## Expansion note

- **Full-novel expansion (~55–70k):** clear room to grow — deepen the Night Voices into braided B-plots, open up the school-year timeline past the short gold summer, and add a subplot around the shuttered mill and the town's slow fight for itself.
- **Translations:** NL/DE editions are a natural follow-up, mirroring the `childrens-books` en/nl/de pattern, pending owner approval.
- **Audio:** the radio conceit makes this a strong native audiobook / podcast-serial candidate — the italic on-air interstitials and first-person voice are built for the ear.

All of the above are owner-gated.
