# The Last Good Frequency

*An original contemporary young-adult coming-of-age novella.*
*A Marrow Bay novel.*

**Age band:** 13–17  ·  **Length:** ~26,400 words (novella, 12 chapters)  ·  **Language:** English

---

## Chapter 1 — Dead Air

*— GOOD MORNING TO NOBODY. IT'S 3 A.M. IN MARROW BAY AND THE LAKE IS DOING THAT THING WHERE IT SOUNDS LIKE IT'S BREATHING. STAY WARM. —*

Theo left on a Tuesday, which felt like an insult, because nothing important has ever happened on a Tuesday.

His dad's hatchback was so full of boxes that the back window was just a wall of cardboard with THEO — SUMMER CLOTHES written on it in my handwriting, because I'd packed that one. We stood in the driveway while the gulls argued over something dead down by the marina, and Theo did the thing he does when he's about to cry, which is talk very fast about nothing.

"You'll come down at Thanksgiving," he said. "Chicago's got, like, actual buildings. And trains. You'd hate the trains, they're so loud, but you'd love hating them."

I said okay. I am very good at saying okay. It's a whole skill. You just open your mouth and let the smallest possible word fall out, and then people think you're fine, and then they leave.

Here is a thing about Marrow Bay that you learn early: everyone is always leaving. The mill left first, six years ago, and took half the town with it like a tide going out and forgetting to come back. Then the leaving became a habit. You could set your watch by it. U-Hauls in spring. FOR SALE signs gone gray by fall.

Theo hugged me too hard and for too long, which was correct. He smelled like the inside of his house, which is a smell I have known since I was four and now might never smell again. I filed it away. That's what I do — I keep things by their sound and their smell, the way other people keep photos. The click of Theo's retainer case. The specific creak of the third porch step at his place, a note somewhere between a C and the sound a door makes in a dream.

"Say something," he said into my hair. "You're doing the quiet thing. Don't do the quiet thing at me, Hart, I'm about to be gone."

So I said, "Text me when you see the first billboard that's just a lawyer's face."

He laughed, wet and surprised, and that was the last real thing before his dad started the engine and the hatchback backed out over the exact patch of gravel where Theo and I had once buried a time capsule that we forgot to dig up, so it's still there, a peanut butter jar full of 2016, and now only I know.

Then the car was small, then it was gone, and the street did what our street does, which is nothing. A wind chime three houses down. The foghorn out on the point, low, one long syllable, the sound the whole town falls asleep to whether it wants to or not.

I stood in the driveway and listened to my best friend not be there anymore.

---

My mom was asleep when I came inside, which is the correct verb for my mother at eleven in the morning. Deb Hart works nights at the county clinic — hospice, mostly, and whatever comes through the ER doors after the bars close, which in Marrow Bay is not much and is always somebody's cousin. She sleeps in the day with a fan running for the white noise and a folded washcloth over her eyes like she's a woman in an old movie fainting on a very long schedule.

I moved through the kitchen the way I've trained myself to, avoiding the two floor tiles that pop and the drawer that shrieks. I got the milk out without letting the fridge door suck and thunk. I have made an art of not waking my mother. It's the only performance I'm good at: the performance of not being there.

On the counter was a note in her nurse's handwriting, which is all capitals and no patience. BACK ON AT 7. THERE'S CHICKEN. THEO GET OFF OK? LOVE YOU MORE THAN THE LAKE. That last part is a thing we say. It doesn't mean much except everything.

My phone buzzed. For half a second — the stupid, animal half-second — I thought it was Theo already, but it was my dad.

happy summer wren!! kill it out there 🎸🎸

That was it. Emeka Okonkwo, everybody. He does this. Not birthdays exactly, though he gets those too, usually a day late, from a green room in a city I have to look up. Random texts, all exclamation points and guitars, like a man waving at his own daughter from a moving train. He left when I was nine. He's a sound guy, a session player — he sets up the invisible machinery that makes other people's voices sound like they matter, and then he tears it down at 2 a.m. and drives to the next town. There's a poetry to it I refuse to enjoy.

I didn't answer. I put the phone face-down on the counter, which is a thing you do to a phone when you want to teach it a lesson it will not learn.

Then I stood in my quiet kitchen with my quiet milk and did the math on the summer. Ninety-something days. No Theo. Mom asleep by day, gone by night. My shifts at the Save-A-Lot, bagging groceries for people who've watched me grow up and still call me "Debbie's girl," dropping cans of beans into plastic in a rhythm I could do in a coma. The lake. The fog. The gulls. The specific silence of a town that used to be louder.

I want to tell you I had a plan. I had a shift at four.

---

The Save-A-Lot smells like cold cardboard and the ghost of a bakery they shut down in 2019. My register faces the front window, so I get the whole gray theater of Cargill Street: the papered-over windows where the shoe store was, the pharmacy was, the diner that everyone still calls "the diner" even though it's been a tanning salon and then nothing. There's a mural on the brick wall of the old hardware store, a fisherman hauling a net, and the paint's peeled so his face is gone. Just a man made of net and weather, pulling something up out of the ground.

I bagged for three hours. Heavy on the bottom, bread on the top, don't let Mr. Lindqvist's frozen peas touch his newspaper or he tells you about it. Denny Voss came through buying only hot dogs and lottery tickets, which is the most honest grocery order in America. Mrs. Petrakis from Pearl Street bought cat food and one peach and counted out change from a coin purse, slow, her hands not what they were, and she said, "You've gotten tall, Debbie's girl," and I said okay, which wasn't even an answer, and she didn't notice, because nobody ever does.

That's the thing about being quiet in a small town. You become furniture. You become the sound the room makes when it thinks no one's listening. And here's the part I never said out loud, not to Theo, not to anyone: I liked being furniture and I hated it, both, at the same time, all the time. I liked disappearing. I wanted, so badly it scared me, for one single person to turn around and hear me.

I clocked out at seven. Walked home the long way, past the marina, because the long way is where the water is. The lake was flat and iron-colored and cold even to look at, and out past the breakwater the fog was coming in the way it does on summer evenings up here, not dramatic, just a slow forgetting, the far shore erasing itself, then the near buoys, then the sound of the buoys, until it was just me and the smell of it — wet stone and pine and rust, the smell that means home whether I've made peace with that or not.

Above the marina, on the second floor of what used to be Kessler's Bait & Tackle, one window was lit. A little amber square in all that gray. WMRW, 90.3, the community station, the one nobody I know actually admits to listening to and everybody somehow knows the jingles from. My dad used to have it on in the garage. Cash Odom's voice coming out of the dark like a hand on your shoulder. I hadn't thought about that in years, and I didn't want to think about it now, so I looked back at the water and kept walking home to my chicken and my empty house.

I ate at the counter. I texted Theo a picture of the peeled fisherman mural and he sent back a photo of a Chicago building so tall it didn't fit in the frame, and under it he wrote insane right?? and I wrote insane, and there was a little pause in the typing bubble, and then he wrote miss you already and I looked at that for a long time and wrote back miss you too, which was true, and then neither of us knew what to do with the truth so we let it sit there.

I went to bed at ten and lay in the specific dark of my room listening to the house. The fridge cycling. The fan in Mom's empty room, still running because she'd forgotten it. A moth against the screen. The foghorn, patient, spacing out its one word across the whole bay. I have never in my life been able to fall asleep before I've heard everything in a room. Theo used to say I was like those dogs that hear the earthquake coming. He said it like a compliment. I never knew if it was.

The phone rang at 11:52.

Not my cell — the house phone, the landline my mom keeps because half her patients' families only have landlines, a fat beige thing on the wall that rings maybe twice a month, always bad news, always for her. I nearly didn't get it. But she was at work, and a ringing phone in an empty house at midnight is a sound you cannot leave alone, it's a splinter in the quiet, so I picked up and said hello in the small careful voice I use with strangers.

"Deb?" A woman. Gravel and cigarettes and something running underneath it, fast, frayed. "Deb, it's Ruth Feygin, I know it's late—"

"This is Wren. Her daughter. She's at the clinic till seven."

A pause. In the pause I could hear a room behind her — a hum, a hiss, the specific electric breath of equipment left on, and under it, faint, a song playing to nobody. "Wren," Ruth said, like she was placing me. "The Hart girl. You used to come up here with your dad. You carried records for Cash. Little thing, big ears, never said a word."

"I don't—" I started, and then I did remember, all at once: the smell of that room, dust and warm tubes and old vinyl, the weight of a milk crate of records against my nine-year-old chest, Cash Odom's laugh like a truck downshifting, saying to my dad, that one listens, Emeka, watch her, she listens with her whole face.

"Something's happened to Cash," Ruth said, and her voice did a thing then, cracked and caught itself. "He had a stroke. This afternoon. He's up at the county — your mother's probably three doors down from him right now, God. He's alive. He's — they don't know yet, with the talking, they don't—" She stopped. Breathed. "Wren, honey, I've got nobody. I've got a license that says this station broadcasts through the night or we lose the whole frequency, and I've got Cash's shift starting in eight minutes and no Cash, and I have called every warm body in this town and they are all asleep because they have lives." A hiss of static swelled and ebbed behind her. "You busy nights, kid?"

Out my window the fog had eaten the streetlight. The foghorn said its one word. Somewhere three doors down from my mother, a man who once told her I listened with my whole face was learning whether he could still speak.

I have never been busy nights. I have never been busy any time. I am the least busy person in the least busy town on the coldest edge of the largest lake in the world.

"Okay," I said.

## Chapter 2 — The Graveyard Shift

*— THIS IS 90.3 WMRW, MARROW BAY COMMUNITY RADIO. THE AUTOMATED STATION IDENTIFICATION YOU JUST HEARD WAS RECORDED IN 1998 BY A MAN WHO IS NO LONGER HERE. STAND BY. —*

The stairs up to WMRW were the same stairs, and I mean the exact same — that dead spot on the fourth one that gives half an inch under your foot like the whole building is deciding whether to keep you. Kessler's Bait & Tackle sold minnows and hooks down here until I was ten, and the ground floor still smelled faintly of it, of cold water and something that used to be alive. I climbed up toward the amber light with my heart going like a rabbit's and Ruthie's key sweaty in my hand, because she'd had to leave one under the mat, because she'd had to go straight to the hospital, because she is not a fool and she was not going to sit in that room while a man she'd known for forty years found out about his own mouth.

Here is what the room was.

It was small and it was warm, warmer than the June night, because everything in it ran hot — a wall of gear breathing out its low electric hum, red and green pinpoints of light like the eyes of very patient animals. Two turntables, both older than my mother. A broadcast board the length of a coffin, every slider taped over with a strip of masking tape, every strip labeled in a heavy black hand: MIC. CD-A. CD-B. PHONE. AIR. Some of the labels were labeled twice, a newer word over an older ghost. Against the far wall, floor to ceiling, the records — vinyl and CDs and a few things I didn't have names for, thousands of them, alphabetized by a system that turned out to be not alphabet at all but something private, moods maybe, weather.

And taped everywhere, on the board and the monitor and the frame of the window that looked down on the black marina — notes. Cash's notes. DON'T RIDE THE RED SLIDER PAST 7 OR SHE SQUEALS. IF PHONE LINE POPS, IT'S THE FURNACE, IGNORE. COFFEE'S NOT COFFEE, IT'S CHICORY, RUTH DOESN'T KNOW THE DIFFERENCE, DON'T TELL HER. And one, older than the others, curling at the corners, gone soft as cloth, right above the microphone where you couldn't help but read it: SLOW DOWN. THEY'RE NOT GOING ANYWHERE. YOU'RE NOT EITHER.

The microphone hung there on its arm like a question.

Ruthie had given me the crash course by phone on the drive over, then eleven more minutes in person, talking the whole time she pulled her coat back on. She's sixty-something, built like a fire hydrant, hair the orange of a color that does not occur in nature, and she does not have a single soft edge that I could find, which under the circumstances I was grateful for. She showed me the slider for the mic and the two for the CD decks and the fader marked AIR that sends the whole thing up the hill to the tower on Gault and out into the dark. She showed me the emergency binder. She showed me the automated ID that fires on its own at the top of the hour so the FCC knows we exist. She showed me the red button on the phone that I would not be needing.

"You don't have to say anything," she told me, halfway out the door, keys already in her fist. "You hear me? Not one word. Cash used to talk because Cash liked the sound of his own gravel, God love him. But the license doesn't care about talking. The license cares that this frequency isn't dead air between midnight and dawn. So you sit, you play his records — he stacks 'em on the shelf under the board, in order, the man was a machine — and you let the ID do the ID, and at six the morning guy takes over, if he shows, which he will, because he's a Methodist. That's it. That's the whole job." She stopped in the doorway. The stairwell light made her look older and then she looked at me and something in her face went, for one second, almost kind. "He's going to be okay, your Cash. He's too mean to go." And then she was gone down the fourth stair, half an inch, and I was alone with the largest lake in the world and a microphone.

---

The record on the turntable finished at 12:07 and the room fell into a silence so total I heard my own blood.

There is silence and there is on-air silence, and they are not the same animal. Regular quiet is a room not doing anything. On-air silence is a room screaming. Every second that the needle wasn't down was a second of nothing pouring out of that window, up the hill, out of the tower, into every kitchen radio and truck cab and hospital room in Marrow Bay — a second of me, of my not-speaking, broadcast at the speed of light in every direction. Dead air. The one thing you cannot have. The thing Ruthie drove into the dark to prevent.

I lunged for the stack.

Cash's records were exactly where she'd said, a leaning tower of them under the lip of the board, and he had numbered them — little pencil digits on the corner of each sleeve, 1 through however far the night went. I slid number one out and it was a scratched-soft LP with no cover I recognized, some old thing, warm brass and a woman's voice like a lamp left on in a window, and I dropped the needle the way I'd done a thousand times at nine years old and never once since, and the crackle came up and then the music, and the room exhaled, and so did I.

For one entire song I was fine.

Then the song ended, and the whole terrible arithmetic started again, and I understood the mic wasn't optional the way Ruthie said. Between songs there's supposed to be a voice. That's what the graveyard shift is. That's what Cash was, for thirty years — the voice between the songs, the one that says it's 2 a.m. and you're not alone. Without it, all I was doing was playing records into a hole. Anyone still awake out there would hear the seam, the raw edge where the person should be, and know that the person wasn't there.

I looked at the mic. I put my hand on the slider Ruthie had labeled MIC in her own hand over Cash's older ghost of it. I pushed it up two fingers' width.

The room changed. There's a light on the board that goes red when the mic is live, and it went red, and a little needle on a dial jumped and settled, waiting for me, hungry for me, and I opened my mouth and —

Nothing.

Nothing came out. Not okay, not even that. My throat closed like a fist. My whole body went cold and then hot, the way it does when you stand up too fast, except I was sitting down and the thing rushing away from me wasn't blood, it was air, it was every word I have ever swallowed, all of them at once, jammed in the door. I have been afraid of a lot of things. I have been afraid of my mother's face when she's too tired to hide it and afraid of the sound my dad's texts make and afraid, always, underneath, of wanting to be heard and getting it and finding out I had nothing worth the hearing. But I had never been afraid like this, with a red light on and a whole sleepless town on the other side of it and my own voice refusing, flatly, to be a voice.

I shoved the slider back down. The red light died. I sat there with my hands shaking and hated myself in a thorough, professional way.

And then I did the only thing I could do, which was keep the records coming. Number two. Number three. I let the automated ID do its 1998 magic at the top of the hour, that dead man's cheerful WMRW ninety point three, and I let the music be the voice, because it was a better voice than mine, and the night went on the way the lake goes on, indifferent and enormous, and I was so ashamed I could have walked into it.

---

Somewhere around four the ashamed part burned down to ash and left something quieter behind. The fog had come all the way in and the marina lights were just smears. I got up and walked the wall of records with my fingertips, reading Cash's private order, and on the corkboard by the door I found it: the log.

LOST & FOUND, it said, in his hand, and under it a taped sheet of legal paper going yellow, a running list in a dozen inks and years. FOUND: SET OF KEYS, GAULT HILL LOT, JAN. FOUND: RED MITTEN, CHILD'S, BY THE BREAKWATER. FOUND: ONE GOOD HEARING AID, PARKING LOT OF THE SAVE-A-LOT, INQUIRE. And near the bottom, this week's, fresh ink: FOUND — DOG, BROWN, PART BEAGLE PART OPINION, ANSWERS TO NOTHING, TIED UP AT THE MARINA OFFICE. And under that: FOUND — ONE CLASS RING. MARROW BAY HIGH, CLASS OF '69. INITIALS INSIDE THE BAND, C.O. HELD AT THE STATION. IF IT'S YOURS YOU KNOW WHAT IT IS.

I read that list four times. It was the only thing in the whole room that wasn't about me. It was small and it was true and it belonged to strangers, and something about that — that these were real lost things belonging to real people who were maybe, somewhere out in the fog, still hoping — made a thing in my chest come loose.

At 5:40 the light was going gray-blue at the edges of the window and the last of Cash's stack was in my hand, number nine, and I knew the Methodist would be here in twenty minutes to save me. And I don't fully know why I did it. Maybe because it was almost over and the almost made me brave. Maybe because the list wasn't about me. I sat down at the board and I pushed the MIC slider up, two fingers, and the red light came on, and I did not try to say okay or hello or my name or anything that was mine. I looked at the log and I read what was written there, quiet, barely, my voice coming out of me like something I was carrying carefully in both hands so as not to spill it.

"There's a dog," I said, to the dark, to the fog, to nobody, to whoever. "Down at the marina. Brown. Part beagle." I had to stop. The red light waited, patient as Cash's note, they're not going anywhere. "And a class ring. Nineteen sixty-nine. If it's yours, it's here."

Then I pulled the slider down before the shaking could get into it, and I sat in the enormous ringing after, my ears roaring, and I felt — I don't have the right word. I felt like a person who had jumped into the lake in June, which is a thing you do exactly once because the cold is a hand that grabs your whole body and says now you know you're alive, this is what it costs.

The Methodist came at 5:58, apologetic, holding two coffees, and startled to find a girl. I gave him the board and I did not explain myself, because I couldn't have. I went down the stairs, fourth one, half an inch, out into a morning the color of the inside of a shell.

---

I drove Mom's car home through a town waking up wrong-way-round, me going to bed while the bakery-that's-gone smell got replaced by somebody's actual bacon, while the gulls clocked in, while a light came on in the diner-that's-nothing and a man I didn't know unlocked a door to a business I didn't know he had. The lake was pink. My hands still hummed on the wheel like the board's low hum had gotten into them.

Mom's coat was over the kitchen chair, which meant she was home, which meant she was already asleep with the fan on and the washcloth over her eyes and no idea her daughter had been on the radio, sort of, badly, for six hours. There was a new note under the old one. YOU WEREN'T HERE. WHERE WERE YOU. Not angry. Just a nurse noticing. I didn't answer it yet. I couldn't figure out how to make it small enough.

I lay down in my clothes on top of the covers with the light coming up gold and impossible around the blind, and I waited for sleep the way I always do, by listening to the room. Fridge. Fan. A robin, absurd, cheerful, wrong for the hour. The house settling.

But it was different now, the listening. All those years I'd lain here cataloguing the quiet like a girl counting change she'd never spend. Now, under the fridge and the fan and the ridiculous robin, I could hear the other thing — the shape of my own two sentences going out over the water, out past the buoys, into the ears of people I'd never meet, and the terrible enormous silence where I could have said more and hadn't. It wasn't peaceful. It was the opposite of peaceful. It was a door I'd found in a room I'd lived in my whole life, and now I knew it was there, and I was never going to be able to un-know it.

I did not sleep for a long time. I lay in the gold and listened to the silence like it owed me something.

## Chapter 3 — Lost and Found

*— IT'S 2:11 IN MARROW BAY. THE FOG'S IN OFF THE LAKE AND THE BREAKWATER'S GONE. IF YOU'RE OUT DRIVING GAULT HILL ROAD, GO SLOW. THEY'RE NOT GOING ANYWHERE. NEITHER ARE YOU. —*

I went back the next night. I told myself it was because Ruthie had asked and Cash was still in the county hospital learning his own mouth again and there was no one else and the license needed its warm body in its seat. All of that was true. None of it was why.

Why was the door I'd found in my own room. I couldn't leave it alone. You can't find a door like that and then just go back to sleep.

The second night I read the lost-and-found again, and I added a weather line, because I'd been listening to the marine forecast on the drive over and the man on it said the swells so plainly, four to six feet, winds out of the northwest, like the lake was just a thing that had moods and you could report on them, and I thought I could do that. I could report on things that weren't me. So at some hour with no name I pushed the slider up and I said, in my careful two-handed voice, "It's four to six feet out there tonight, so if you've got a boat in the water, you already know." And I pulled it down. And my heart didn't try to kill me quite as hard as the first time.

That's how it went. I talked around myself. I found out you can build a whole voice out of things that belong to other people. The lost-and-found. The weather off the lake. The shipping notes Ruthie left me in a folder — the ore boats coming through the locks, the *Adele Marchetti* riding low with taconite, the *Point Iroquois* light and northbound, big steel names moving through the dark that somebody, somewhere, a wife or a dispatcher or a kid, wanted to know the position of. I read the boats out like a lullaby. I was, it turned out, extremely good at saying true small things about the world as long as not one of them was about me.

The third night Ruthie left me a note taped over Cash's note: MRS. ILVES ON HARBOR ST. PASSED SUNDAY. FAMILY WANTS IT READ. And a little paragraph in her blunt hand — eighty-eight years old, taught fourth grade at the school for forty of them, played the organ at the Lutheran church, is survived by. I'd never read an obituary out loud. I did it slow. They're not going anywhere. I found out you could hold a whole life in your mouth for thirty seconds and set it down gently at the end and it was the most that little room had ever asked of me and I could do it. I could do that. I was surprised how much I wanted to do it right.

I did not, any of those nights, say who I was. I was a voice reading the lost things. That was all. That was plenty.

---

On the fourth morning there was a jar on the station's bottom step.

I nearly stepped on it in the shell-colored light coming down the stairs. A pint jar, the old kind with the wire bail, full of something dark and red, and tied to the bail with kitchen string was a square of paper torn off a grocery bag, and on it, in pencil, in a hand that shook a little, it said: FOR THE GIRL WHO READS THE LOST DOGS. THIS IS THIMBLEBERRY. FROM UP BY THE OLD MILL. THANK YOU FOR THE VOICE AT NIGHT.

I stood on the fourth stair, the one that gives, and I held that jar in both hands the way I'd been holding my own words, and I want to be honest with you about what happened, which is that I sat down on the step and I cried, not a lot, not the movie kind, just a hot quiet leak I couldn't do anything about, into the fog, over a jar of jam.

Somebody had heard me. Not seen me — nobody had seen me, that was the whole miracle of it. Somebody old, somebody up by the ruin of the mill in the middle of the night with a radio on, had heard a girl who wouldn't say her name read out a list of lost dogs and boats and dead schoolteachers, and had gotten up in the morning and gone into a cellar and found last summer's thimbleberry and walked it down to a bait shop above a marina to say thank you to a voice. To me. To the sound the room makes when it thinks no one is listening.

Somebody was listening.

I brought the jar home. I put it on the counter where my dad's texts don't reach, and when my mom got up at two in the afternoon she found it before she found me.

"Where'd this come from," she said. She had the washcloth still in one hand and her hair was doing what it does after a shift, which is give up. She turned the jar in the window light. She read the note through twice, I watched her read it twice, and then she looked at me the way she looks at a chart when the numbers don't add up to the patient in front of her.

"The girl who reads the lost dogs," she said.

So I told her. Not all of it. I don't have the machinery to tell all of anything. But I told her about Ruthie's midnight phone call and Cash's stroke and the license and the seat that needed a warm body in it, and the whole time she stood there with the jar in her hand and her face doing several things at once, and when I finished she set the jar down very carefully like it was a thing that could break, which it could.

"You're going up there alone," she said. "In the middle of the night. Above the marina. Sixteen years old."

"It's Ruthie's building. It's got a lock. Nobody's out there, Mom, it's Marrow Bay, the most dangerous thing between here and the station is a raccoon with a grudge."

She didn't laugh, but the muscle by her mouth thought about it. She was quiet for a while, and I let her be, because I've learned my mother thinks in the same silence I do, we're a whole household of people running the numbers before we'll say the answer.

"Cash Odom," she said finally, and something went soft and far-off in her voice. "He's three doors down from my station right now. He can't get his mouth to say *water* yet. Yesterday he got out *cold* and *record* and he cried about it, and I held his hand, because that's the job." She looked at me. "That man's been talking this town to sleep since before you were born. Since before *I* was born, practically. When your grandmother was dying in the back bedroom on Pearl, before you, she had his voice on all night. The graveyard shift." She shook her head, slow. "And now it's my kid. God help us."

"I don't talk," I said. "Not really. I just read the lost-and-found."

My mother looked at me for a long moment, and I braced for the practical thing, the safety thing, the *absolutely not*. She works with dying people. She has watched this town leave and knows exactly what it costs to love a place that's disappearing under you. She has spent my whole life trying to keep me from becoming furniture in a house with the lights going off one by one.

"You slept all day," she said. "Both days."

"I know."

"You'll take my car and you'll text me when you get there and when you leave and you will not, I mean it, Wren, you will not walk down that stairwell in the dark without your phone light on, that fourth step is a broken hip waiting to happen and I have seen enough of those to last me." She picked the jar back up, turned it in her hands. "I taught you to bag groceries so you'd have money to leave this town. I did not teach you to do that." She was quiet. "Reading the lost dogs. Huh." And then, so low I almost missed it, so low I've kept it: "Your grandmother would've liked that." She put the jar in the cupboard where the good glasses go. "For the girl who reads the lost dogs. Well." And she went to make her coffee, and that was the whole permission, wrapped in a warning, the way my mother wraps everything.

---

That night the fog was the thickest it had been, a wet wall pressed against the window so the marina simply wasn't there, and I read the boats and the weather and one lost-and-found — FOUND, A SINGLE OAR, HAND-CARVED, INITIALS ON THE HANDLE, DRIFTED UP ON THE NORTH BEACH — and I thought about all the single things in this town that had lost their pair, and I almost said that out loud, and I didn't, because that would have been about me.

I was three fingers into Cash's stack, some slow piano thing he'd numbered fourteen, when the red light started to blink.

Not the mic light. The phone.

I want to describe it exactly, because I have thought about it a hundred times since. The board has a whole constellation of lights, and I knew them all by now — the green pinpoints of the gear at rest, the amber level meters, the flat unblinking red of the mic when it's live. This was none of those. This was the phone slider, the one Ruthie taped PHONE, and above it a red light I had never once seen do anything, and it was blinking. Slow. On, off. On, off. Patient as the foghorn. Patient as Cash's note. Somewhere out in the fog, in a house I would never see, a person had picked up a telephone and dialed seven numbers into the dark, on purpose, to reach the voice above the marina.

To reach me.

The piano played. The light blinked. I sat in the warm humming room with my hand halfway to the slider and stopped it there, hanging in the air, the way my whole life hangs in the air right before I decide not to. Because here is the thing about the lost-and-found and the boats and the weather and the dead schoolteachers: they could not talk back. That was the entire deal I'd made with the microphone. I would say true small things that belonged to other people, and the dark would take them, and nobody would ever ask me a single question I would have to answer in my own voice, as myself, out loud, in real time, with a red light on.

The phone was a question I would have to answer.

The light blinked. On. Off. On.

I looked at it the way you look at the lake in June, knowing exactly what the cold is going to do to you, knowing it's the only thing that's going to tell you you're alive, and knowing, too, that you are not, tonight, going to jump.

I didn't pick up.

I let it blink. I let the piano finish and I put on number fifteen and I let the person in the fog hold their telephone against their ear and listen to it ring into a room where the girl was too scared to be a girl and not just a voice, and eventually — I don't know when, I couldn't watch it — the light stopped. They'd hung up. Out there, somewhere, someone had reached for me and I had let it ring.

I sat with that until the window went gray-blue and the Methodist came with his two coffees, and I drove home through the waking town with my hands humming on the wheel, and I lay down in the gold and did not sleep, and I listened, the new way, to everything.

And under the fridge and the fan and the ridiculous robin, I could hear it. Not a sound, exactly. The memory of one. A phone, ringing into the dark, waiting for a girl to be brave enough to say hello.

Tomorrow, I told the ceiling. I didn't believe me. But I said it anyway, quiet, in my own voice, to nobody, which was starting, a little, to feel like practice.

## Chapter 4 — Nightbird

*— YOU'RE LISTENING TO 90.3 WMRW, MARROW BAY COMMUNITY RADIO. IT'S 2:04 IN THE MORNING. IF YOU'RE UP, YOU'RE UP FOR A REASON, AND I'M NOT GOING TO ASK WHAT IT IS. —*

The red light had been blinking for eleven minutes.

I know it was eleven, because I'd watched the clock do it. That's the kind of thing I do instead of the brave thing. The song I'd put on was four and a half minutes long — a slow, salt-worn ballad off one of Cash's stacked records, all upright bass and a woman who sounded like she was singing into a coat — and it had ended, and I'd started another, and that one had ended too, and the whole time the little red bulb on the phone box kept pulsing at me like a heartbeat somebody had left running in an empty room.

The night before, it had blinked once and gone dark before I could decide. I'd told myself that was the end of it. Wrong number. A drunk thumb. The universe testing a door it didn't actually want to walk through.

But here it was again. Same time. Patient in a way wrong numbers aren't.

The station makes its own kind of quiet when a record's spinning. Not silence — the turntable has a hush to it, a warm rushing under the music like the inside of a shell — and over that, the building itself: the space heater ticking, the fridge in the back with its bad compressor doing its impression of a boat motor, the lake three floors of stairs and one thin wall away, breathing the way it does. I sat in all of it and looked at the light and thought about my mother.

Deb answers phones for a living. Not the fun part of a phone — the part where somebody on the other end is having the worst night of their life and needs a voice that won't crack. She picks up on the first ring, every time, in a tone so level you could set a marble on it and it wouldn't roll. *County clinic, this is Deborah.* I have heard her do it at 4 a.m. with her eyes closed, standing in our kitchen in one sock.

I am not my mother. I'm the daughter who lets the smallest possible word fall out and lets people leave.

I picked up the phone.

I want to tell you I said something. A DJ thing. *WMRW, you're on the air.* Cash used to answer callers like they'd caught him mid-laugh, like they were the exact person he'd been hoping would call. I opened my mouth and what came out was breath. Just breath, into a receiver, at two in the morning.

There was a pause on the other end. Then a girl's voice, low and amused, said, "Wow. Okay. So it *is* haunted."

I made a sound. Not a word. A sound like a door hinge deciding whether to open.

"No, don't — I like it," she said. "Most stations, you call in, some guy goes *You're live, caller, what's your requessst* —" she did a voice, oily and radio-slick, and I felt something in my chest do a small unfamiliar thing — "and it's terrible. You just breathing menacingly is a real improvement. Public radio should be more like this. More heavy breathing. Fewer pledge drives."

"I'm not," I said, and stopped, because I didn't know how the sentence ended. *I'm not haunted. I'm not on the air right now. I'm not a real DJ, I'm a girl who bags groceries and I got here by accident and I've been terrified for three weeks.*

"You're not what?"

"Supposed to answer," I said. "The phone. I don't — usually."

"So why'd you answer mine?"

The song was winding down behind me, the woman singing into her coat getting quieter. In about forty seconds there'd be dead air unless I did something. That's the whole job. Do something before the silence.

"It kept blinking," I said. "It was distracting me."

She laughed. It was a real one — surprised out of her, a little too loud for the hour, like she'd forgotten to keep her own volume down. Then, quieter, like she'd remembered: "Sorry. Okay. I'll let you go do your — thing. Your spooky station thing."

"You don't have to," I said.

I don't know where it came from. It came out before the part of me that vetoes things could get to the switch. And then it was in the room, hanging there, and I couldn't take it back, and I found I didn't want to.

There was a silence, but a different kind. A listening one.

"Yeah?" she said.

"Give me a second," I said, and set the phone down on the desk, and slid the next record out of the sleeve I'd already lined up, and dropped the needle, and it caught, and something old and bright and full of horns filled the room, and I brought the fader up with the flat of my thumb the way Ruthie had shown me on my first night when my hands were shaking so hard I'd nearly dropped a turntable arm. And I sat back down and picked up the phone and said, "Okay."

"'Okay,'" she repeated. "You say okay like it's the last okay in the world and you're rationing it."

"That's — yeah," I said. "That's pretty much it."

---

Her name was not a name. I asked — eventually, an hour in, when it felt like the kind of thing you could ask — and she said, "Let's not," so easy and firm at once that I didn't push. "Names are for daytime. This is a nighttime thing." A pause. "You can call me — I don't know. Something. What do you call the person who calls the radio at two a.m. and won't hang up."

"A problem," I said.

She laughed again, and this time she kept it down, held it close to the phone so it came through warm and cracked. "Rude. I take it back, you're not haunted, you're just mean."

"Nightbird," I said. I don't know why. The window over the board looks out at the marina, and there'd been a gull out there earlier, one of the big gray ones, walking the rail in the dark like it had somewhere to be, and the word had just been sitting in my mouth. "You're up. You're loud. You're a pain in the neck at two in the morning. Nightbird."

"Nightbird," she said, trying it, and I could hear her deciding she liked it. "Okay. Yeah. And you're — what. Radio Ghost. The Voice. What are you."

"The girl who plays the records," I said.

"That's not a name, that's a job description."

"It's what the log calls me," I said, which was almost true — the log didn't call me anything, the log was a spiral notebook where Cash had written LOST/FOUND in block letters thirty years ago — but I liked how it sounded, like I was a person a log had a word for.

"Fine. Girl who plays the records." She yawned, enormous, unbothered. "God. Sorry. I've been up since — what day is it."

"Thursday. Technically Friday."

"I've been up since Thursday. Wednesday, if we're being honest, but there was a nap in there that I'm choosing to believe counts as sleep." Something shifted on her end — the phone moving, a door somewhere, careful — and her voice dropped, softer, closer. "My grandmother doesn't sleep. So I don't. That's the whole — that's it. That's the mystery of the two a.m. phone calls, solved. She's up, so I'm up, and I ran out of things to do in this house about a week ago, and then I found you. Down at the bottom of the dial. The last thing on the radio before it turns to static."

I looked at the board. The little numbers. 90.3, taped over so many times the ink had gone furry. Below us on the FM band there was nothing. Wind. The hiss between stations that Cash used to call the sound of the lake talking to the sky.

"The last good frequency," I said, mostly to myself.

"What?"

"Nothing." The horns behind me were building toward something. "You keep her company. Your grandmother."

"I keep her *awake* company," she said. "There's a difference. She sleeps in the daytime now, mostly, so somebody had to volunteer for the vampire shift, and I lost. Or won. I haven't decided." A beat. "She likes the radio. She had it on all the time, back — before we moved here. She can't really follow it anymore, the words go too fast for her, but she likes the *sound*. The company of it. So I put it on low, and I sit, and I —" She stopped. "I'm talking a lot. You're very quiet. Is that a technique? Are you a therapist? Is this a therapy station?"

"No," I said.

"You're a good listener, is what I'm saying. That's rare. Most people are just waiting for their turn to talk."

Nobody had ever called it a good thing before. Quiet. All my life it had been a thing done *to* me, a fact people said with their eyebrows up — *Wren's just shy, Wren doesn't say much, is Wren okay?* — like a diagnosis they were being polite about. She'd said it like it was a skill. Like I was good at something on purpose.

I didn't tell her that. I wasn't ready to hand her that. But I sat there holding it, this warm strange coin, and outside the window the fog was coming up off the water the way it does before dawn, softening the streetlights down at the marina into blurs, and I felt — I want to be accurate about this, because it matters later — I felt *found*. Which is a stupid thing to feel about a stranger on a phone whose name you don't know. But there it is. That's the log entry. Two a.m., Marrow Bay, one girl found, condition: unknown.

---

We talked until the sky started doing its trick.

That's the thing about the graveyard shift that nobody warns you about — the light comes back so gradually you don't notice it happening, and then all at once the window that was black is gray, and the gulls start up, and the world remembers itself. She noticed before I did. "It's getting light where you are," she said. "I can hear it."

"You can't hear light."

"You can. The birds go weird. And you slowed down. You've got a nighttime voice and a morning voice and the morning one just showed up." A pause. "I like the nighttime one better. No offense to the morning one."

"None taken." I looked at the clock. 4:51. I had a sign-off to do, and a drive home, and my mother's car in the driveway meaning she was already back from her shift, meaning I'd have to be quiet on the stairs. And I didn't want any of it. I wanted the phone and the fog and this.

"Hey," she said. "Girl who plays the records."

"Yeah."

"Same time tomorrow, DJ?"

And here is the thing about the word *okay* that I've been telling you all along — how it's the smallest one, how I let it fall out of my mouth so people will think I'm fine and leave. I'd said it to Theo in a driveway with the gulls arguing and meant *please don't go.* I'd said it to Ruthie on the phone the night Cash had his stroke and meant *I'm terrified.* I'd said it my whole life meaning everything except what it says.

"Okay," I said.

And this time it meant *yes.* It meant *yes, and don't you dare not call, and I will sit here in this bait shop over the marina at two in the morning every night for the rest of the summer if it means the red light blinks and it's you.* It meant all of that, and I put every bit of it into two syllables, and I think — I hope — some of it made it down the line.

There was a smile in her voice when she answered. I couldn't see it. I didn't need to.

"Okay," she said back. "Fly safe, Radio Ghost."

The line clicked and went to the long tone, and I sat there with it against my ear a second longer than I needed to, listening to nothing, which is a thing I am extremely good at, and which for the first time in my life felt less like being alone and more like a room I'd just watched someone leave — the shape of them still warm in the air.

Then I brought the fader up, and leaned into the mic, and for the first time in three weeks I wanted to.

## Chapter 5 — Frequencies

*— GOOD MORNING TO THE OVERNIGHT SHIFT. TO THE ONES ON THE ROAD AND THE ONES WHO CAN'T SLEEP AND THE ONE WHO KNOWS WHO SHE IS. IT'S 1:40 AND THE FOG'S IN. STAY WARM. —*

Here is how you learn a town: you talk to it at two in the morning and you find out who talks back.

I didn't set out to have listeners. That would have required believing I was worth listening to, which was not yet a thing I believed — it was a thing that was being slowly done to me, from the outside, like weather. It went like this. I'd read the lost-and-found, and the weather off the lake, and the shipping notes for the ore boats coming through the locks, and I'd play Cash's records, and somewhere in the third week I stopped announcing them like a hostage reading a ransom note and started just — saying things. About the songs. About the fog. Small true things into the dark. And the dark started saying them back.

Big Al came first. Big Al drives a truck — long-haul, refrigerated, he told me once, "which means I haul lettuce across three states so people who've never seen a farm can have a sad salad in January." He parks nights at the overlook up on the county road where the cell signal dies and the only thing that reaches him clean is us, 90.3, bouncing off Gault Hill and out over the water. He called the first time to correct me. I'd said a boat was the *Edmund Freeling* and it was the *Edna Freeling*, and he could not let it stand. "Edna," he said, gravelly, offended on behalf of the boat. "My uncle sailed her. Edna. Get it right, radio." After that he called most nights just to say he was there. "Rig's parked. Coffee's bad. Carry on." Like clocking in. Like the show didn't fully start until Big Al confirmed the overlook was still attached to the earth.

Then the Widow on Pearl Street. Mrs. Petrakis. She didn't say she was a widow — I worked that out from the shape of her nights, the way a person's aloneness has a grammar to it. She called about the music, always the old stuff, and she'd tell me things sideways. "My husband proposed to me during a song like this one," she said once, and then, before I could say anything, "Play something else. That's enough of that." She was eighty-one and lived alone in a house on Pearl with a porch light she left on "for nobody, for the principle of it," and she told me I read the obituaries "like I actually knew them, which is more than the paper does."

The no-name kid I never got a name for, which felt only fair, given. Young — younger than me, I think, though it's hard to tell at two a.m. when a voice is trying to sound older than it is. He'd call and not say much. Ask what a song was. Go quiet. Once he said, "Is it weird to call a radio station," and I said, "You're asking the wrong girl about weird," and he laughed, this cracked surprised kid-laugh, and hung up. He called back the next night, though. They always called back. That was the thing I was learning. You leave a light on, and it turns out the whole town has been driving around in the dark looking for one.

And June from the gas plant, out on the overnight, watching gauges in a cinderblock room by the water treatment ponds. "Twelve hours of nothing happening," she said, "and my whole job is to notice the second it stops being nothing." I told her that was my job too. She said, "Then we're the same, you and me. Professional noticers. Somebody's got to."

Ruthie noticed too, in her way.

She'd started leaving me notes on the board in her furious little handwriting — SIGNAL SOUNDED HOT LAST NIGHT, PULL THE GAIN BACK, and once, terribly, taped over the mic: KID. THE PHONE'S RINGING BECAUSE PEOPLE ARE LISTENING. THAT'S THE POINT. STOP LOOKING SO SURPRISED. She came in one evening while I was setting up, hauling the sack of donated CDs somebody'd left at the door, and she stood in the middle of the station with her bad coffee and looked at me for a long moment like she was doing math.

"They've got a name for you now," she said. "You hear that?"

"For me?"

"Down at the diner. Marge told me. Somebody was in for the early breakfast going on about *the girl on the graveyard shift.*" She said it in quotes, with her eyebrows, so I'd know it wasn't hers. "Like you're a weather system. Like you're a thing that happens to the town at night." She sipped the coffee, grimaced at it the way she always did, faithful to her disappointment. "Cash was the graveyard shift for thirty years. Now there's a *girl* on the graveyard shift. Progress. Or the end of civilization. Jury's out."

I didn't know what to do with my face, so I did what I always do, which is nothing.

"Don't get a big head," she said, which from Ruthie was practically a hug. Then, quieter, tired in a way she didn't usually let show: "Enjoy it while it lasts, is my advice."

"While what lasts?"

She waved a hand at all of it — the taped-over board, the wall of vinyl, the window and the fog and the marina and the little red light. "This. The whole racket." She set the coffee down. "You didn't hear it from me, but the council's got the Gault Hill land on a list. The tower parcel. Somebody upstate thinks it'd make a nice — I don't know. Something with a view. And there's a vote coming on the community grant, the little bit of money that keeps the lights on up here, and there's a faction on that council that has been trying to zero us out since before you were tall." She shrugged, but it wasn't a real shrug; it was a shrug holding something heavier down. "We broadcast on borrowed everything, kid. Borrowed money, borrowed gear, borrowed hill. Someday somebody's going to call the loan." She picked the coffee back up. "So. Enjoy it. And pull the gain back, you're clipping."

She left. I sat with it. The Gault Hill tower — I'd never once thought of it as a thing that could be *sold*, any more than you think the sky can be sold, and then of course you remember that everything here can be, the mill was, half of Cargill Street is, the whole town is a thing being quietly put on a list somewhere by someone who's never had to notice the second it stops being nothing.

But that was a daytime worry, and I was a nighttime thing now. So I put it away for later. Which is a skill I have. Ask my father.

---

Speaking of.

My phone buzzed on the drive home one morning, gray dawn, gulls, and I glanced at it at the stop sign by the shuttered pharmacy and it was him. Emeka. My father texts the way a comet arrives — rarely, brightly, on a schedule known only to itself.

*hey Wren-song. touring through the midwest end of August, we've got dates in Duluth and the Soo, I'm looking at the map and Marrow Bay's basically right there, been thinking I could swing by, would love that, would love to see you. how's your mom. how's you.*

*Wren-song.* He hasn't earned that name in seven years and he uses it like the meter's still running on it.

I read it three times at the stop sign, until a truck came up behind me and I had to go. Then I drove home and did the thing I do, which is nothing. I didn't answer. I put the phone face-down on my nightstand and lay on top of my covers in the morning light listening to my mother sleep-breathe through the wall, and I thought: he's *looking at the map.* He found us on a map. That's what we are to him, a dot that's *basically right there*, a convenient point between two better gigs. *Would love that.* Love is a thing he does at a distance, over a satellite, on his way somewhere. I know because I inherited it. I do it too. I just don't have a tour to blame it on.

I didn't answer that day. Or the next. The message sat there face-down like a stone I'd have to pick up eventually, and knew it, and left anyway.

Music was his. That was the deal I'd made with myself at nine, in the driveway, watching a different full car pull away. He'd left a house full of instruments and I'd shut the door on all of it. I didn't sing. I didn't play. I didn't even really listen, not on purpose, not the way you're supposed to, with your chest. And here I was, spinning records over a dying town at two in the morning, and I want you to understand I did not think of that as music. I thought of it as *company.* I thought of it as leaving a light on. Those are different things. I needed them to be different things. If they were the same thing, then I was doing his thing, in his medium, with his ear, and I could not afford to know that yet.

So I didn't. That's a skill too.

---

She called at 2:04, like always. Nightbird. My red light, my two-in-the-morning problem, the girl with the name that wasn't one.

We'd fallen into it by then — the shape of the nights. She'd tell me about her grandmother, the small news of a shrinking world: a good day, a spoon of soup finished, a story told twice. I'd tell her about the town without telling her where I was, which had become a game we both knew we were playing and neither of us wanted to win. She knew I was a girl in a room by the water. She didn't know which room, which girl. I liked it that way. I liked being a voice she'd built a person around out of nothing but sound. It felt safer than being *Wren Hart, sixteen, bags your groceries, doesn't say much, is she okay.* On the phone I was funny. On the phone I was quick. On the phone I was the girl on the graveyard shift, and I was starting to suspect that girl might be the real one and the grocery-bagger the disguise.

"Play me something," she said, this particular night, near three. Her voice had gone soft the way it did when the house was fully asleep around her, when it was just her and the machines and the low radio and me. "Not the sad-lady-in-a-coat stuff. Something from *here.* You're always playing me the whole rest of the world. Play me where you are."

"You don't know where I am."

"I know it's a *here,*" she said. "Everybody's a here to somebody. Play me yours."

And I thought about it, and I went to the wall, the local shelf, the one Cash had built out of records nobody upstate would ever press again — church-basement pressings, one-summer bands, the sound of Marrow Bay when Marrow Bay still made a sound. And near the end of it, warped a little, sleeve soft as cloth from handling, was a 45 by a band that doesn't exist anymore, that broke up when the singer left town the way everyone leaves this town, and the song was called "Copper Wire," and it's about — I didn't know yet, that night, what it was about. I just knew the title had a hum in it.

"There's a local one," I said. "Old band. The Gault Hill Foghorns. They're gone now — the singer left, the rest scattered. But they made this one thing. 'Copper Wire.'"

There was a silence on the line. Not the listening kind. A caught kind.

"Play it," she said. Quiet. Almost nothing. "Please. Play me 'Copper Wire.'"

And I did. I slid it out and set it down and dropped the needle, and it caught, and the room filled up with something — a guitar like a screen door, a voice singing about a thing you string between two houses so you can hear each other after everyone's gone home — and I sat back down and picked up the phone to say something clever, some noticing thing, professional noticer that I am.

I didn't. I just listened to her listen. To the little catch in her breath at the second verse. To the fog outside and the town asleep and this girl I'd never seen holding still somewhere with a dying woman and a low light, both of us inside the same three minutes of a song by people who'd already left.

And I understood, sitting there, plainly, the way you understand the temperature of a room: I would play her anything. Anything she asked for. I would drive up Gault Hill in the dark and hold the antenna in my two hands if that's what it took to get one more song down the wire to her. I'd have played her the whole wall. I'd have played her every last good frequency there was.

That should have scared me more than it did.

## Chapter 6 — Daylight

*— THAT WAS THE LAST ONE, FRIENDS. THE SUN'S DOING ITS THING OVER THE BREAKWATER AND I'M HANDING YOU BACK TO THE DAYTIME. IT'S 5:58. GO EASY OUT THERE. YOU ARE NOT ALONE. STAY WARM. —*

Daylight is a different country and I've never had a visa for it.

I mean it. At night I was the girl on the graveyard shift, quick and warm and made of sound. At 3:15 on a Tuesday afternoon I was a girl in a maroon polyester vest with SAVE-A-LOT stitched over the pocket and a name tag that said HART, standing at the end of register two, bagging groceries for a town that mostly did not look at me, and I liked it that way, and both of those girls were me, and the trick of my whole life was keeping them from ever having to be in the same room.

The Save-A-Lot on a summer afternoon has an acoustic all its own. The freezer cases hum in B-flat — I checked once, against the pitch pipe in the school music room, back when I still went in there. The scanners beep. The wonky wheel on the third cart squeaks down aisle six and back. Somebody's kid is always crying two lanes over in that bored, tuneless way kids cry when nothing's wrong, they just want the day to be a different day. Old Mr. Lindqvist buys the same four things every afternoon — soup, bread, a lottery ticket, a can of cat food for a cat I've never confirmed exists — and tells me the same thing about the weather, and I bag it the same way, soft things on top. It's a song. A boring one. I know all the words.

I was three hours into the shift and thinking about nothing, which is the whole appeal of the shift, when she came through the line.

I clocked the cart before I clocked her, because that's how I'm built. It was a strange little cargo — a hopeful, sad cart, the kind that tells you a whole story if you've bagged long enough to read them. Two boxes of tea, the good loose kind we barely stock. A soft blanket, lilac, still folded in its plastic. A four-pack of those flameless candles, the ones that flicker on a little motor so you can have the light without the fire. A packet of pipe cleaners and a bag of cotton balls, which is a craft-project cart, a keep-someone's-hands-busy cart. And a set of drawing pencils — the real ones, tin case, the range of grays from a whisper to a bruise — and a fat spiral pad of the heavy paper.

"You're going to tell me the tea's on sale," she said, "and I'm going to be so happy about it that it'll be embarrassing for both of us."

I looked up.

Girl my age. Dark hair pushed back with a pencil actually stuck through it, which I'd have made fun of if I were a person who made fun of strangers out loud. A henley with paint or charcoal ground into one cuff. Tired around the eyes in a specific way I knew, though I couldn't have told you from where — the tired of someone who keeps different hours than the daytime wants them to. She had a smile already loaded, aimed at me, easy, like we were mid-conversation and I'd just walked in late.

"It's not on sale," I said, and scanned it. Beep. "But nobody buys it, so it's about to expire, so." Beep. "Basically the same thing."

"Grim," she said, delighted. "'Buy it, it's dying.' You should do the ads."

"We can't afford ads."

She laughed, and I bagged the tea — soft things, a habit — and reached for the blanket, and she watched my hands do it, the sorting, the soft-on-top, and something crossed her face that I didn't have time to read because Mr. Lindqvist's cat food was in my head reminding me the line was long.

"That's a nice system you've got," she said. "The bagging. Very — considered."

"It's not a system, it's just physics. Eggs don't go under the milk. You'd be amazed."

"I would not be amazed, I would be furious, I've *had* my eggs go under the milk, and let me tell you —" She stopped herself, waved it off, still grinning. "Sorry. I'm a lot in the daytime. I don't get out much. My social skills are all — theoretical."

*I don't get out much.* It went past me. I want that on the record. It went right past me, because I was reading the cart and not the girl, because the two countries don't overlap, because it does not occur to you that the voice you have been building a person around in the dark could be standing at register two in a henley telling you your bagging is considered.

"The pencils are good ones," I said, ringing them through. "These. People buy the cheap ones and then get mad they can't get a dark dark."

She went still. Just slightly. "You draw?"

"No," I said. "I bag. But I notice." I set the tin in the bag, on top, careful. "Somebody in your house draws."

"Somebody in my house," she said slowly, "does a lot of drawing lately. Yeah." She touched the spiral pad on the belt, a small protective touch, the way Mrs. Petrakis's voice touched the word *husband.* "It's for — my grandmother's room. She doesn't do much anymore, so I. Make her things. Put stuff on the walls she can look at." The candles went by. Beep. "The candles are because they won't let us do real ones. On the oxygen. Understandably. But she likes a flame, so." She shrugged, and the shrug was doing the same work Ruthie's had done, holding something heavier down. "Fake flame. Modern problems."

And I should have known then. Everything was there. The grandmother who doesn't sleep, who likes the sound of company, the different hours, the tired eyes, the whole architecture of a night I'd been sitting inside for weeks — it was all right there on the belt at register two under the B-flat hum of the freezers, and I bagged it and did not see it, because I am a professional noticer who cannot, it turns out, notice the one thing that's about to change her life while it's happening.

"Forty-one twelve," I said.

She dug for her card, and while she dug, she did the thing people do when a silence gets a beat too long in a checkout line: she filled it. Not with words. She hummed.

Low. Absent. Not performing it — the opposite, the kind of humming you don't know you're doing, that leaks out of you when your hands are busy and your guard's down. A guitar like a screen door. A voice singing about a thing you string between two houses.

"Copper Wire."

I want to describe what happened to me but the truth is the store went away. The freezers stopped humming. The crying kid, the squeaky cart, the scanners — all of it dropped out, the whole daytime country, and there was just the sound, three notes of it, hummed under the breath of a girl at my register, and it was *her.* It was the catch in the second verse. It was two in the morning. It was the fog on the marina and the low light in a dying woman's room and the whole rest of the world I'd been playing her, and here it was, wearing a henley, buying flameless candles, standing four feet from my face in the middle of the afternoon.

I made a sound. Not a word. A sound like a door hinge deciding whether to open.

She looked up.

I watched it happen to her too. I watched it land — watched her hear the sound I'd made, the exact useless breath-sound I make instead of the brave thing, the one I'd made into a telephone at 2:04 in the morning three weeks ago, the one she'd said was a real improvement over the oily radio voice. I watched her face do the thing a face does when two countries slam together. Her mouth came open. The card stayed forgotten in her hand.

"No," she said. Quietly. To herself, almost. "*No.*"

"Copper Wire," I said. It was all I had. It came out cracked. "By the Gault Hill Foghorns."

"'The singer left,'" she said, "'the rest scattered' —" she was quoting me, quoting me back to myself, the exact words I'd said down the line, and her voice had gone up at the end, wondering, "'—but they made this one thing.'"

"You caught your breath," I said. "At the second verse. I heard you."

"*Radio Ghost,*" she said.

"Nightbird," I said.

And then neither of us said anything at all, which for two people who talk to each other in the dark for four hours a night was a genuinely remarkable thing, a silence so total you could have heard the freezers come back on, which they did, in B-flat, faithful to their note. The line behind her had grown. Somebody's cart squeaked. The daytime country reassembled itself around us and we stood in the middle of it having just discovered we were the same two people we'd been all along, and had no earthly idea what to do about it in the light.

Her face had gone pink. Mine, I have no doubt, had gone whatever the opposite of pink is on me, some gray hot terrified color. She was looking at my name tag. HART. She was reading it like a lost-and-found entry. *Found: one voice. One girl. Class of whatever. Initials —*

"You have a name," she said, wonderingly, like she was accusing me of it. "A daytime name."

"You do too," I said. "You cheated. You had mine right there on my chest the whole time."

"I wasn't *reading your chest* —" she started, mortified, and I said, "My name *tag,*" and she said, "I *know,*" and covered her face with the hand that wasn't holding the card, and made a sound that was half a laugh and half something else, and the whole thing was so far outside anything my nighttime voice knew how to handle that I just stood there and let the register do the talking. FORTY-ONE TWELVE, it said, in green. INSERT OR TAP CARD.

She tapped the card. The machine thought about it. Behind her, Mr. Lindqvist cleared his throat in the eternal patience of a man who has soup at home and nowhere he needs to be.

"I have to —" She gestured at the line, at the bags, at the enormous impossible daylight. "I'm holding up the — there's a whole —"

"Soft things are on top," I said, which was not a thing a person says, which was just the last true sentence I had left, and I pushed the bags toward her across the steel.

She took them. She looked at me one more second, and there was something in her face I would spend the whole rest of the night at the board taking apart and putting back together — not just the shock, but under it, something that looked a lot like the thing I'd felt on the very first night, the *found* thing, the two-a.m. one-girl-found condition-unknown thing — and then the machine printed the receipt, and she caught it before I could, and she pulled the pencil out of her hair.

She wrote fast, standing there, using the steel bagging shelf as a desk, her tongue between her teeth. It took ten seconds. The line waited. The whole town waited, as far as I was concerned. Then she set the receipt down on the shelf, face-up, weighted under the corner of the lilac blanket so the door-draft wouldn't take it, picked up her bags, and walked backward two steps toward the exit, watching me, still pink, a smile breaking over the shock like the sun does over the breakwater at 5:58.

"Same time tomorrow, DJ," she said.

The automatic doors sighed open behind her and the afternoon light came in white and enormous and she went out into it and was gone, and Mr. Lindqvist put his soup on the belt and said something about the weather.

I picked up the receipt.

She'd drawn a bird. Fast, sure, four or five lines — a gull, but not the mean gray marina kind, a *flying* one, wings out, going somewhere on purpose. And under it, in pencil pressed dark enough to prove she'd bought the good ones:

*so it's you.*

I stood at register two with a soup can in one hand and a receipt in the other, and the freezers hummed their B-flat, and somewhere two lanes over the bored kid finally stopped crying, and I did not float and I did not exactly panic — it was both, at once, the same way *okay* can mean *no* and mean *yes*, the same way a girl can be two countries and a song can be about a wire you string between two houses so you can still hear each other after everyone's gone home.

"Terrible for June, all this fog," said Mr. Lindqvist.

"Yeah," I said, and bagged his soup, soft things on top, and put the receipt in my vest pocket over my heart like it was the most important thing I'd ever found in the lost-and-found, which — condition: confirmed — it was.

## Chapter 7 — The Static Between

*— IT'S 12:52 IN MARROW BAY AND THERE'S FOG COMING OFF THE LAKE LIKE THE WHOLE TOWN'S BREATH FOGGED UP A WINDOW. I'M NOT GOING TO TELL YOU IT'LL BURN OFF BY MORNING. I'M JUST GOING TO SIT HERE WITH YOU WHILE IT DOESN'T. —*

The receipt lived in my back pocket for two days before I let myself look at it in daylight.

I'd looked at it in the dark plenty. Under the register light that first night, when Frankie walked out and left it face-up on the belt like a dropped card in a magic trick. Later at the station, propped against the pot-metal microphone Cash had wrapped in electrical tape so it wouldn't buzz, while dead air I was too rattled to fill went out over the whole sleepless county. In bed, phone flashlight, blanket over my head like I was nine and reading past lights-out.

*so it's you,* it said. And under that, a little bird drawn in three strokes — no body, really, just the shape a bird makes when it's already leaving. She'd used the pen chained to the debit machine, so the ink was that anemic register blue that gives up halfway through your signature.

*So it's you.* Four days of me being a voice in her dark and her being a voice in mine, and it turned out we'd been standing eight feet apart the whole time under lights the color of a headache, and neither of us had known. Or she'd known. She'd hummed "Copper Wire" at me on purpose and watched my face do whatever my face did.

Here is a thing nobody warns you about being an ear: you can know exactly what a person sounds like and still not know them at all. I knew Nightbird's laugh — the real one, the one that got away from her, versus the polite one she used to fill space. I knew she talked faster when she was scared and slower when she was lying and that she'd never once, in four nights, told me her name. What I did not know was how tall she was, or that she bit the skin next to her thumbnail, or that in the flat fluorescent daylight of the Save-A-Lot her eyes were the brown of the lake in October, which is a brown with something moving under it.

So on my day off I texted the number she'd finally, finally given me — a daylight number, a real one — and I said: *the drive-in. do you know it.*

*everybody knows it,* she wrote back. *it's been dead since before we got here.*

*that's what makes it good,* I said, and my thumbs did that before my brain could talk me out of it, and then I stared at the little bubble that meant she was typing, stopping, typing.

*ok DJ,* it said finally. *daylight though. weird.*

---

The Starlite drive-in closed the same year the mill did, which is to say the town lost its work and its make-believe in the same tide. The screen still stood at the end of a field gone to chicory and Queen Anne's lace, a big blank rectangle the weather had bleached to the gray of a thing left out too long. Swallows lived in the projection booth. The speaker poles marched off into the weeds in crooked rows, most of them capped with the amputated stumps where the speakers used to hang, a couple still wearing them like the field was a room full of people all holding old telephones up to nothing.

Frankie was already there when I pulled in, sitting on the hood of a rust-freckled hatchback with a sketchbook open on her knees. She had a pencil behind her ear and one in her hand and she didn't look up right away, which I understood was a mercy — it gave us both somewhere to put the first thirty seconds.

"You draw the screen," I said, walking up. Not a question. I could see it from here, the big pale rectangle going down onto her page in careful gray.

"I draw whatever'll hold still." She turned the book so I could see. She'd made the screen look like what it was — a held breath, a thing waiting for a light that wasn't coming — and gotten the swallows in as three-stroke birds, the same three strokes as the receipt. Her whole hand knew that shape. "Grandmothers hold still," she said. "Screens. You, right now, standing there like I'm going to bite you."

"You bite the skin by your thumb," I said, because I am not always in charge of my own mouth.

She looked at her thumb. Then at me. Something crossed her face — the little rearrangement of a person realizing they'd been seen closer than they'd budgeted for. "God," she said. "You really do listen. On the phone I thought maybe you were just — good at radio. Making everybody feel listened-to. Like a — a thing you do."

"I'm bad at radio," I said. "I froze on the mic the first three nights. There's a tape somewhere of me not talking for eleven minutes."

"I know. I heard it." She said it plain, not a jab. "I was up with Lita. The station was just this box of quiet with your breathing in it. I kept the radio on because the quiet in our house is a different quiet, it's got a — it's got a sound to it I don't like. And yours had somebody in it." She closed the sketchbook. "Longest eleven minutes of my life, honestly. I wanted to reach through the radio and go, kid, just say the lost dogs, you can do the lost dogs."

I laughed. It got away from me, the real one, and she caught it and grinned like she'd been fishing for exactly that.

---

We walked the field. It's the thing to do at the Starlite — walk the rows of dead speaker poles like a cemetery of the town's attention span. The sun was doing the short-gold-summer thing, laying itself down flat and warm over the chicory, and the lake was somewhere off past the trees making its enormous patient sound, and for a while we just talked the way we talked at two in the morning, easy, overlapping, except now I could watch her hands do it.

That was the whole strange gift of the afternoon: the daylight didn't kill the night thing. I'd been braced for it to. I figured the voice-in-the-dark and the girl-in-the-flannel were two different people who'd have nothing to say to each other with the sun up. But it turned out they were the same person leaking into each other, and the awkward part — the *so it's you* part — burned off like she'd promised the fog wouldn't, right around the time she told me about her grandmother.

She told it flat, walking, not looking at me, which is how people tell the true hard ones. Lita's heart was failing by inches. Not the fast kind, the kind that would've been almost merciful — the slow kind, the kind that turns a person into a schedule. Pills at ten. Oxygen line that hissed. The two-a.m. wake-up because Lita got scared of the dark now, at eighty-one, after a whole life of not being scared of anything, and only Frankie could talk her back down. That was the caregiving. That was why the family had moved here, to the town at the edge of the water where the rent was nothing because everybody was leaving — so Frankie's mom could nurse at the county clinic and Frankie could take the nights.

"That's why I'm up," she said. "That's the whole mystery of Nightbird. There's no mystery. I'm just the person who's awake at the hour when somebody's dying slow, so she doesn't have to do it in a quiet house." She snapped a stalk of Queen Anne's lace off at the root and didn't do anything with it. "Your show came on and it was like — okay. Somebody else is up. Somebody else is sitting in the dark on purpose. It made the hallway shorter."

I didn't say I'm sorry, because I'd learned from my mom's job that I'm sorry is a door you close, and I wanted to leave the door open. So I told her mine instead. Trade for trade. It's the only currency that works at that hour, even when the hour is four in the afternoon.

I told her about Emeka. That my dad played guitar and ran sound for bands nobody's heard of, that he had the best ear in any room and used it to leave rooms, that he'd been gone since I was nine and I could measure the distance in birthday texts — one a year, thumbs-up emoji, sometimes a song title he thought I'd like, as if the man could recommend me a feeling instead of showing up to have one. I told her I didn't do music. Wouldn't. That I'd quit piano the month he left and never told my mom the two things were the same thing. That music was his and I wasn't going to want a thing he'd used to leave with.

"But you do radio," Frankie said.

"Radio's not music. Radio's just — company. Talking to the dark."

She stopped walking. We were between two speaker poles, one capped, one still wearing its dead speaker, and she looked at me for a second too long, the October-lake look, the brown with something moving under it.

"You know that's the same thing, right?" she said, gentle, like she was talking Lita down. "Music. Company. Talking to the dark so somebody hears it." She reached over — I watched her decide to, watched her hand cross the eight inches of gold air between us — and she almost took my hand. Almost. Her fingers came to rest on the back of my wrist instead, light, right over the pulse, and neither of us moved, and the lake breathed, and a swallow cut across the blank gray screen behind her, and I thought: *this is a held note. This is the part of the song where nobody plays anything and that's the whole point.*

Then a truck backfired out on the county road and we both jumped and laughed and the note broke, on purpose, both of us breaking it because we weren't ready to find out what came after it.

But she'd left her hand a second longer than the jump. I counted it. It's a skill.

---

Driving home I named it.

Not the thing with Frankie — I didn't have a name for that yet, and wouldn't for a while, and that was okay. The show. Cash had never named the show; the town called it "the graveyard shift" because Cash called himself the graveyard man and let the rest of it be nameless, a slot, an hour, a light left on. But it wasn't a slot anymore. It had gotten to be a place. A place people went when the house-quiet had the wrong sound to it.

I thought about the receipt. About all the frequencies in the air over Marrow Bay — the trucker's CB, the marine channel out on the water, the county dispatch, the mill's dead intercoms rusting in a dead building — and how ours was the only one anybody talked *back* on. The last one with a person in it at that hour. The last good one.

That night at 12:52 I said it out loud for the first time, into the tape-wrapped mic, with the fog doing its thing and the whole sleepless county listening or not listening the way you listen or don't to a light left on.

"This is The Last Good Frequency," I said. "That's what it's called now. In case you needed to know what to call the light."

And then, near two, when the red phone hadn't blinked yet and the record was spinning down and my heart was going like a moth at a screen, I did the bravest thing I'd done out loud in my whole life, which was smaller than it sounds and bigger than I can say.

"This next one," I said, "goes out to the Nightbird. Wherever you're keeping the dark company tonight. You know who you are."

I dropped the needle. The whole town heard me do it. And somewhere across the fog, in a house with the wrong kind of quiet, I knew — the way you know a voice in the dark — that she was smiling at a radio.

## Chapter 8 — My Father's Hands

*— YOU'RE LISTENING TO THE LAST GOOD FREQUENCY, 90.3 WMRW, AND IT'S 1:20 IN THE MORNING. SOMEWHERE OUT THERE A MAN IS DRIVING TOWARD YOU OR AWAY FROM YOU. THE HEADLIGHTS LOOK THE SAME EITHER WAY. STAY WARM. —*

The text came on a Tuesday, and I should have known from that. Nothing important ever happens on a Tuesday, except when it does, and then it's the kind of important that's really just a Tuesday wearing a coat.

*Hey Wren. Playing the Anchor Room in Iron Harbor Thursday, two towns over from you if my map's right. Off Friday. Could swing through Marrow Bay in the afternoon. Been too long. Would love to see you. — Dad*

He signed his texts *Dad* the way you'd sign a card to a coworker's baby shower. A little formal. A little unsure of his standing. I read it four times in the walk-in cooler at the Save-A-Lot with the compressor humming its one endless note, and each time the same two things happened in my chest at once, which is the whole trouble with my father — the door in me that flies open before I can hold it, and the hand that slams it, both of them mine.

I didn't answer for a day. Then I answered *ok* — my great skill, the smallest word — and he sent back a thumbs-up and a guitar emoji, and my mom, when I told her, went very still at the kitchen table with her scrubs on inside-out because she'd come off a fourteen-hour and didn't have the energy left to face her own clothes.

"You don't owe him an afternoon," she said.

"I know."

"You don't owe him the show, either. He doesn't get to know about the show unless you decide he does." She said it careful, and I understood she was giving me something — the thing she wished somebody had given her, back when she was the one waiting on him to swing through. She reached over and fixed my collar even though nothing was wrong with my collar. "But if you want to see him, you see him. I'll be asleep by four. I work nights so I don't have to be awake for the men who leave." She heard herself say it and almost smiled. "That's a bad joke. Don't put it on the radio."

I put it on the radio eventually. But not that week.

---

He pulled up outside the marina at four-fifteen in a van that had been white once, with a trailer behind it and a bumper sticker for a festival that had happened in another state in a year that was over. And then he got out, and here is the thing I hate: he was beautiful. My father is one of those men that time is polite to. Gray coming in at the temples like it had been styled there. That easy long-limbed way of standing, one hand already reaching to shake, the other holding a coffee he'd bought at a gas station and would forget somewhere. He had my mouth. Or I had his. It's disorienting to watch your own mouth smile at you across a parking lot, deploying its charm, and to feel yours want to smile back.

"Wren," he said, like the name was a good song he hadn't heard in years. "God. You got tall. You got — you look like your mother and you look like my sister and you look like nobody, you look like yourself." He hugged me. He smelled like van and cold coffee and, underneath, the specific smell of guitar cases, that felted-plush-and-old-wood smell that lived in the back of my nine-year-old memory like a splinter that never worked its way out. I let him. I am good at letting things happen to me and then examining them later.

We walked the marina. He was good at this part — the coming-through part, the afternoon part. He was funny. He noticed things. He noticed the foghorn was flat, actually flat, a quarter-tone under where it used to be. "They must've replaced the diaphragm with a cheaper unit," he said. "Hear it? It used to be a B-flat. That's an A and change. Whole town's foghorn went out of tune and nobody's got the ear to be annoyed." And I did hear it, I'd been hearing it my whole life and never had the words, and the fact that he handed me the words for free, casual, like it cost him nothing — I hated how much I wanted more of them.

Because that's the con of a talented absent father. He gives you the ear and takes the man. You get the thing that lets you hear the whole world and you get to use it, mostly, to hear him not being there.

We stood at the end of the pier where the fog was coming in low over the marina, and he told me about the Anchor Room in Iron Harbor, the band he was running sound for — kids, he said, good kids, a singer with a voice like a cracked bell, which he meant as a compliment. He talked about the road the way other men talk about a woman they can't quit. Reverent and a little ashamed. He named towns I'd never heard of like they were rooms in a house he actually lived in, and I understood, standing there watching my own mouth make his shapes, that the road was where he kept the staying he couldn't spend on people. He'd give a load-out his whole heart at two in the morning. He just couldn't give it to a Tuesday in Marrow Bay.

"You ever miss it?" I asked. "Here. Us." I hadn't meant to say *us*. It fell out, the small word's dangerous cousin.

He was quiet for a second, and the flat foghorn went off, that A-and-change, and he winced at it out of pure reflex, the ear working even when the heart was somewhere it shouldn't be. "Every day," he said. "That's the part nobody tells you. Leaving doesn't turn the missing off. It just moves it somewhere you can't do anything about it." He looked at me. "I'm not saying that so you feel sorry for me. I'm saying it so you know it wasn't ever that I didn't want you. Wanting was never the problem. I've got wanting to spare. It's the showing up I was born without."

---

He came to the station. I didn't invite him. He asked what I did with my nights now — small talk, filling the walk back to his van — and I said I ran the graveyard slot at WMRW and he stopped walking.

"You're on the radio," he said.

"It's nothing. It's a license thing. They needed a warm body for the FCC hours."

"Wren." He was looking at me the way Frankie looked at me over the sketchbook, the seen-closer-than-you-budgeted-for look, except on him it opened up something raw and quick that I wasn't ready for. "Can I hear it. Not — I don't want to make it a thing. Can I just come sit in the back and hear it."

I should have said no. I said ok.

So my father sat in the folding chair against the wall of vinyl at one in the morning, in the converted bait shop above the marina, with the board Cash had labeled in his own hand and the mic wrapped in electrical tape, and he watched me work. He didn't touch anything. That surprised me — I'd braced for the man to reach for the board, to fix my levels, to make it his. He kept his hands in his lap. My father's hands, which I'd spent seven years measuring the distance in, folded and still.

I did the show. I read the lost-and-found — a set of keys, a child's rain boot found on Cargill Street, one boot, always one, this town losing things by ones. I gave the weather off the lake. I took a call from Big Al up at the overlook, who wanted to know if I'd ever seen the northern lights or just heard people describe them, and I said heard, and he said that was probably better, and my dad in the corner made a small sound that I clocked as a laugh he'd tried to swallow.

Near two I did the thing I'd started doing since the drive-in. I talked, a little, around the edges of a true thing. Not Frankie's name. Never a name. Just — company for the dark. And I felt my father listening the way I listen, all the way in, and when I cut to a record and pulled the headphones down around my neck he was leaning forward with his elbows on his knees and his face was wet.

"That's my ear," he said, very quiet, wrecked. "In your head. You got my ear and you did something with it I never — I ran sound for other people's voices my whole life, Wren. I made other people sound like they were worth hearing. And you just — you *are* the voice. You're the one they're up listening for." He wiped his face with the flat of his hand, unembarrassed, the way musicians are unembarrassed about crying because it's just another thing the body does with sound. "I'm so proud I could — I don't have the right to be this proud. I know that. I know I don't get to."

And there it was, the grace note, the real one, the thing I'd wanted so long I'd built a whole locked room to keep from admitting I wanted it. My father, recognizing me. Handing me back my own voice and calling it good.

I waited for the rest of it. You know the rest of it you're waiting for. The *I'm sorry.* The *I'll come back.* The *let's fix this, let's start now, I'll get a place two towns over, I'll come Thursdays.*

He looked at the clock on the board. It was 2:40. And I watched it happen on my own borrowed mouth — the little rearrangement, the reaching for the coffee that wasn't there, the man remembering he had a load-out in Iron Harbor at eight and four hundred miles after that.

"I gotta get on the road by four to make the next one," he said. "I wish — Wren, I wish it was different. I'm not built for the staying. I found that out too late to not hurt anybody with it." He said it like a diagnosis. Like the flat foghorn. Just a true thing about how a machine was made. "I'm not going to promise you Thursdays. You've had enough men promise you Thursdays."

And the strange thing — the thing I'm still turning over — is that it was the truest, kindest thing he could have said, and it broke my heart cleanly, one clean break instead of the ragged waiting kind, and a clean break is a thing that can actually heal.

I didn't cry. I said, "Okay." But this time the word wasn't the small one falling out so people would think I was fine and leave. This time I meant it as a full sentence. *Okay. I hear you. You're a man who leaves, and I'm the girl on the graveyard shift, and those are two true things that don't cancel each other out.*

He hugged me at the door. He said the ear was mine now, free and clear, no lien on it. He got in the white van and the taillights went up Cargill Street and out toward the county road, and they looked exactly like headlights, exactly like a man driving toward you, right up until they turned and were gone.

---

I went back in and sat down at the board.

It was 3 a.m. — the voice-anchor hour, the good-morning-to-nobody hour, the hour I usually hid inside other people's small lost things. And I pulled the tape-wrapped mic close, and for the first time in eight weeks of talking to the dark I didn't talk around myself. I talked about myself. Plainly. To the county.

I said my father had come through town tonight. I said he was a beautiful, talented man who was not built for staying, and that I'd spent seven years thinking his leaving was a thing that had been done to me, a wound with my name on it, and tonight I'd found out it was just weather. Just how a machine was made. I said I had his ear. I said I was keeping the ear and letting go of the ache, and that they were two different objects and you could set one down without dropping the other. I said if anybody out there was up tonight because of a man who left — a father, or anybody — then this hour was for them, and they were not alone, and the ache was allowed but it didn't have to be the last word.

I stopped. My hands were shaking on the board.

And the red phone light came on.

Then it came on again, the little relay clicking, a second line, which I hadn't even known we had. Then a third. The whole dead switchboard lighting up like a runway in the dark, the town holding me the way I'd been holding them, all those weeks, one small true thing at a time.

I put my hand over the receiver and let it ring one more time before I picked up. Just to hear it. Just to be, for one second, the person somebody stays up for.

"You're on the Last Good Frequency," I said. "You are not alone. Go ahead."

## Chapter 9 — Off Air

*— IT'S 1:03 IN MARROW BAY AND I WANT TO TELL YOU SOMETHING BEFORE I LOSE MY NERVE, SO STAY ON THE LINE. THE TOWER UP ON GAULT HILL HAS BEEN TALKING TO YOU FOR SIXTY YEARS. IT MAY NOT GET TO FINISH ITS SENTENCE. —*

Ruthie told me with the coffee pot in her hand, which is how I knew it was bad, because Ruthie always had the coffee pot in her hand but she never forgot to pour it.

She just stood there in the little kitchenette off the studio holding the pot over the mug and not pouring, watching the black stream that wasn't coming, until I said "Ruthie," and she looked at the pot like it had betrayed her and set it down.

"Council's got a vote next Thursday," she said. "There's a buyer wants the Gault Hill parcel. The tower land. Some outfit that puts up storage units — climate-controlled storage, if you can feature it, in a town that's leaving. Storage for what. For everybody's boxes on the way out of here." She laughed, no air in it. "And the county's cutting the community-media line item. The grant. Same meeting. Two knives, one throat. Without the land and without the grant we can't keep the tower and we can't keep the lights and we sure as hell can't keep the license." She sat down on the stool by the board, and I'd never seen Ruthie sit like that, folded, sixty-odd years old and out of fumes. "We go dark, kid. End of summer. I've been running this place on spite and bad coffee for eleven years and the spite finally ran out before the coffee did."

"We can fight it," I said.

"With what." Not a question. She said it the way my dad said the foghorn was flat. A true thing about how a machine was made. "We got no money, Wren. We got a wall of records nobody plays and a phone with one working line and a girl who found her voice about six weeks too late to save the thing she found it in." She said it kind. That was the worst part. She reached over and squeezed my wrist, right over the pulse, the same spot Frankie had found at the drive-in, like the whole town knew where to reach for me. "I'm not giving up on *you*. I'm giving up on the tower. There's a difference and I want you to hear it. You take that voice and you get out of Marrow Bay like your mother's been praying for since you were born."

I didn't say anything. I am good at not saying anything.

But something had lit in my chest that wasn't the door-flinging-open and it wasn't the hand slamming it shut. Something new. A third thing, with its own weather.

---

I went to see Cash on Sunday.

The rehab wing at the county clinic smelled like my mother's scrubs when she came off shift — that hospital smell, disinfectant with something human underneath it that the disinfectant is losing to. Deb had told me which room. She'd also told me, careful, the way she tells me the hard true ones: *he's better than he was. But the stroke took the left side and it took some of his words. Don't rush him. The words are in there. They just have to come the long way around now.*

Cash Odom was by the window in a chair, a blanket over his knees even though it was August, looking smaller than the voice I'd known my whole life. The graveyard man. Thirty years of the town falling asleep to him, and here he was in the daylight he'd spent three decades staying up against, with a physical therapist's rubber ball in his good hand and the other hand curled in his lap like a thing set down.

"Mr. Odom," I said. "It's Wren. Wren Hart. I used to carry your records."

His face did a slow enormous work, the whole left side lagging behind the right like a radio signal arriving late, and then he got there, he got all the way to a smile, and it was worth the wait, it was worth thirty years of waiting.

"The." He stopped. Gathered it. The words coming the long way around. "The girl. On the. Graveyard." A long breath. "Heard you." He tapped his good ear with one finger. "They. Bring me a radio. One a.m." Another breath, this one shaking. "I stay up. For you. Now."

I had to look out the window for a second. Out at Gault Hill, where you could just see the tower over the trees, red light blinking on top, patient, the way it had blinked over this town my whole life and Cash's whole life and would blink for one more week.

I told him. I don't know why. Maybe because he was the only one who'd understand it all the way down. I told him the council and the buyer and the storage units and the grant, two knives one throat, and Ruthie sitting folded on the stool with the spite run dry. I told him it was going dark.

His good hand closed hard around the rubber ball.

"No," he said. Clear as anything. The word came all at once, no long way around, like it had been waiting by the door. Then slower, hunting: "Listen. Wren. Listen." And I did the thing he'd taught the whole town to do without knowing he was teaching it. I listened. I gave him the eleven minutes if he needed them.

"It was." He pressed his fist to his own chest. "Never. Mine." Breath. "Thirty years. I thought. My. Show. My. Slot." He shook his head, slow, the left side lagging. "Wasn't. It was. Theirs." He moved the fist from his chest and opened it toward the window, toward the town, toward all of them out there — Big Al at the overlook and the Widow on Pearl Street and June at the gas plant and the no-name kid and Nightbird in a house with the wrong kind of quiet. "The light. Belongs. To the. People. Awake." A long, long breath, and then the whole sentence came out of him in one hard-won piece, the most words he'd said since the stroke, I could see what it cost him and I could see he'd pay it again: "It was never mine either, girl. You just keep it lit."

I couldn't talk. So I did the other thing.

I'd brought it in my jacket pocket, the way it had lived in the lost-and-found drawer at the station for who knows how many years before I ever read it out on the air my second night — a set of keys, a child's boot, and a class ring. Marrow Bay High, Class of '69. Gold gone dull, a red stone chipped at one corner. Initials scratched inside the band, worn nearly smooth, but you could still read them if you knew what you were looking for.

C.O.

I'd known for weeks. I'd known since I read it out loud at four in the morning to a town that was mostly asleep, that the ring had been sitting in a drawer waiting for its man the same way the tower had been blinking over the hill waiting for somebody to be up. I put it in his good hand. I closed his fingers around it because his fingers couldn't quite do it themselves.

He looked at it for a long time. His face did the slow enormous work again, and this time the water came, running down the side that worked and the side that didn't both.

"Lost this," he said. "Homecoming. 'Sixty. Eight." A wet laugh. "Fifty. Years. Thought it was. Gone. For good." He closed his eyes. "Nothing's. Gone for good. If somebody's. Still. Reading the. Lost and found."

We sat with that a while. Outside the window a gull worked the parking lot for something dropped. Cash turned the ring in his good fingers, slow, learning it again, the way you relearn a word after a stroke — the long way around, back to a thing that used to be automatic. Then he pressed it into his palm and held it there against the rubber therapy ball, two small stubborn objects, and closed his eyes, and I thought he'd gone to sleep until he said, without opening them, "Don't. Let Ruthie. Quit." A breath. "She quits. Loud. Like me." The corner of his mouth that worked pulled up. "Make her. Stay mad. Mad. Is fuel." He tapped his chest. "I ran. Thirty years. On mad. And. Company." His eyes came open and found me, clear all the way down. "You got. Both. Girl. Go use them."

---

I drove home along the lake with the windows down and the flat foghorn going its quarter-tone under, and the third thing in my chest with its own weather got louder than the two old things, loud enough to hear over the water.

Ruthie was right that we had no money. She was right that spite runs out. But she was wrong about the arithmetic, because she was doing the mill's arithmetic — the leaving arithmetic, the FOR SALE-sign-gray-by-fall arithmetic that this town had been doing to itself for six years until leaving became the only sum it knew how to carry.

Here's the arithmetic I had. I had a microphone wrapped in electrical tape. I had a red phone light that came on now, three lines, a runway in the dark. I had a trucker and a widow and a woman at a gas plant and a no-name kid and a girl who kept the dark company for a dying grandmother, and every one of them was awake at an hour when the council members were asleep. I had a town that had forgotten it was a town — that had come to think of itself as a waiting room, a place you sit until your U-Haul's ready — and I had, at one in the morning, the only frequency any of them still talked back on.

I called Frankie from the driveway before I went in. She picked up on the second ring, quiet, the kitchen sounds of a house on the night schedule behind her — the oxygen line's little hiss, a kettle. I told her about the tower and the vote and Cash and the ring, all of it, fast, the way you tell the true ones. She was quiet a long time after.

"You're going to fight it," she said. Not a question. She knew my voice better than I did.

"I don't know how to fight anything, Frankie. I froze on a microphone for eleven minutes."

"You didn't freeze tonight." I heard her move, heard a chair, heard her settle in the dark of her own vigil. "Lita listens to you, you know. She can't follow all of it anymore but she likes the sound. She calls you *la de la radio*, the radio one. Last night she asked if the radio one was going to be there tomorrow." A pause. "Fight it, Wren. Some of us don't have a lot of tomorrows left to be kept company through. Ask the town. They'll come. People come, when somebody finally asks. That's the thing nobody in this town has figured out — everybody's been waiting to be asked."

That was the sentence I drove into the studio on.

Cash gave the light to the people awake. Nobody had ever asked the people awake for anything. They just got read to. They just got kept company. But company is a debt that runs both directions, and it was time to call it in.

That night at 1:03 I pulled the tape-wrapped mic close and told them about the tower. All of it. The vote, the buyer, the storage units, the grant, Ruthie folded on her stool, the light that had blinked over Gault Hill for sixty years and might not get to finish its sentence. I told them what Cash told me, that the light was never his and never mine, that it belonged to the people awake. My hands were steady this time. That surprised me.

And then I did the thing I had never once done in ten weeks of talking to the dark — the thing I'd been more scared of than the microphone, more scared of than my father, more scared of than my own voice. I didn't read them a small lost thing. I didn't keep them company. I asked them for something.

"So here's what I need," I said, and my voice didn't shake, and the whole sleepless county heard the girl on the graveyard shift ask, for the first time, out loud, for help. "I need you awake. All of you. Next Thursday, seven p.m., the council meets in the old library on Cargill Street — daylight, I know, I'm sorry, I'm asking you to come out of the dark for this one. I need you to be a town again for one night. Not a waiting room. A town. Big Al, I know you're rolling through — I need you here. Mrs. Petrakis. June. The kid who never gives me a name — you especially, whoever you are. I need every one of you who ever stayed up because this frequency was the last good one. Come be a body in a room. Come be loud in the daylight for the thing that keeps you company in the dark."

The red light came on before I finished. All three lines. Then the fourth one I hadn't known we had.

"You're on the Last Good Frequency," I said. "You are not alone. Tell me you'll come."

## Chapter 10 — The Longest Night

*— YOU'RE LISTENING TO 90.3 WMRW, MARROW BAY COMMUNITY RADIO, AND IT'S FIVE MINUTES PAST MIDNIGHT ON THE LONGEST NIGHT I'VE EVER SAT THROUGH. THE RED LIGHT'S BEEN ON SO LONG I FORGOT IT COULD BE OFF. IF YOU'RE OUT THERE — AND I KNOW YOU'RE OUT THERE, I CAN HEAR YOU BREATHING — STAY ON THE LINE. STAY WARM. —*

The phone rang before I finished the sentence.

I'd asked, the night before, on air, in a voice that shook so bad I nearly hung up on myself — I'd asked the town to help me save the station. To call. To pledge whatever they had, a dollar, a jar of anything. To show up Thursday at the council chamber and sit in the folding chairs until the men who wanted to sell Gault Hill out from under us had to look at every single face they were switching off.

I hadn't known if anyone would.

That's the thing nobody tells you about asking for something. It's not the no that scares you. It's the silence after, the two full seconds where the whole dark could just — not answer. I have lived my whole life in that two seconds. I have made a home in it.

The phone rang at 12:06.

"WMRW," I said. "You're on."

"It's Al." Gravel and diesel, the sound of a man calling from a cab parked at the overlook with the heater ticking. "Big Al. Listen, girl on the graveyard shift — I got forty bucks I was gonna spend on nothing. Chew and lottery scratchers and nothing. Put it in the pot."

I looked at the pot. The pot was a coffee can Ruthie had labeled STATION in marker that had gone brown with age, sitting on the board next to the mic like it had always been there. It had eleven dollars in it. It now, in my head, had fifty-one.

"Forty dollars from Big Al at the overlook," I said, and I heard my own voice do a thing it had never done on air. It went up. It got bright at the edge. "Al, that's — thank you. That's real."

"Yeah, well." A pause, the sound of a man deciding whether to say a soft thing into a CB he's held for thirty years. "You keep the light on up there, I know which exit's mine. That tower's how I know I'm almost somewhere. Don't sell my somewhere, kid."

The line clicked. The red light died and lit again, instantly, a new call stacked behind it like planes at a runway.

I want to tell you what the next four hours were, but I don't have a word big enough that isn't also a lie. Joy is too small and too clean. This was messier than joy.

Mrs. Petrakis called from the house on Pearl Street she'd been alone in for nine years. "I don't have money, sweetheart, I have a widow's checkbook, but I have my late husband's stamp collection and I will sell every last one and bring you the cash in an envelope, and don't you dare argue with an old woman at one in the morning." I didn't argue. I read out her husband's name — Constantine — because she asked me to, because she said nobody had said it out loud in a room in a long time and the radio counted as a room. I read it like it mattered, because it did, and somewhere a man named Constantine got to be in the air over the lake for a second, and that is the closest thing to church I have ever done.

June called from the gas plant on her break, wind screaming behind her, and pledged twenty and then read me the whole list of the overnight crew who'd chipped in — Darnell and the two Petes and a guy she only called Fireball — a hundred and ten dollars from men and women who make things go at three in the morning so the rest of the town can sleep through it. "We listen every night," June said. "You're the only thing on that's awake with us. Don't make us go back to the hum of the machines, girl. That hum'll eat a person."

The no-name kid called. Didn't say much. Never does. "I'm fifteen," the kid said, and I could hear a bedroom, a fan, a house holding its breath around a kid who couldn't sleep in it. "I got six dollars. Is six dollars stupid?"

"Six dollars is not stupid," I said. "Six dollars is a vote. Tell me your first name. Just the first."

A long quiet. Then, so soft I almost lost it under the static: "Marcus."

"Marcus put six dollars in the can," I said to the whole sleepless town. "Everybody hear that? That's Marcus. He's fifteen and he's still up and he's ours."

I heard him laugh — this shocked little bark, like nobody had put his name in the good part of a sentence in a while — and then he hung up before either of us could ruin it.

Ruthie came in around two. I hadn't asked her to. She stood in the doorway in her terrible coat with two cups of the worst coffee in the western hemisphere and she looked at the coffee can, and she looked at the phone light going and going, and she didn't say anything smart. Ruthie always says something smart. She set a cup by my hand and she sat down on the crate she sits on and she put her face in her hands for a second, and when she took them away her eyes were wet and furious about it.

"Two hundred and eighteen dollars," she said, hoarse. "Kid. Two hundred and eighteen dollars and a stamp collection and a promise of pierogi. That doesn't save us."

"I know," I said.

"The grant's twelve thousand. The land's worth more than this whole town's got."

"I know, Ruthie."

"So why," she said, and her voice cracked right down the middle, "does it feel like we're winning."

I didn't have the word for that either. But I knew it was the same word as the one for the four hours. So I keyed the mic and I told the town Ruthie Feygin had been keeping this station alive on bad coffee and spite for eleven years, and the phone lit up so hard she had to leave the room.

By three the plan had built itself out of all those voices, the way a nest gets built, one grabbed thing at a time. Thursday. The council chamber. Everybody there — Al swapping his run, June trading a shift, Mrs. Petrakis bringing the whole Pearl Street bridge club, Marcus swearing he'd get his mom to drive him. We would not be loud. That was mine, and I said it and heard it land. We would not yell. We would just be there, all our faces, every person who'd ever been alone at 3 a.m. with only this frequency for company, and we would make them cut the light off while we watched.

I was so far up inside that feeling that it took me until almost four to notice.

Frankie hadn't called.

Nightbird called every night. Nightbird had called every night since a June that felt like a hundred years ago, since before she was Frankie, since she was just a girl who wouldn't give her name and I was just a girl who couldn't say her own. On a night like this — the biggest night, my asking made real, the town roaring up out of the dark to catch me — there was no possible world where Frankie Boaz did not call.

I checked the phone. Working. The red light bright and dumb and empty.

I told myself she was with Lita. Of course she was with Lita. Some nights Lita was bad and Frankie couldn't get to the phone, and those nights I felt her absence like a cold spot in the studio and I filled it with the town. I filled it now. Al and June and Marcus and Constantine's widow. I let them be the warm.

But the cold spot didn't close.

I texted her at 4:15. *biggest night ever. town showed up. where are you, nightbird.* The little checkmark went gray. Delivered. Not read.

I texted her at 4:40. *frankie?*

Gray. Gray. Gray.

The sky over the lake started doing the thing it does before dawn, that dirty pewter, the fog going from black to nothing. The calls thinned out. That's how you know night's ending — the town starts going to sleep in the exact order it woke up, the plant crew first, then the truckers, then the old women, until it's just you and the automated ID and the gulls starting up down at the marina like an argument they never finished.

At 5:52 my phone lit. Not a call. A text. From Frankie.

Four words.

*lita passed. i can't.*

I read it four times. I read it until the words came apart and stopped being words. *lita passed. i can't.*

I don't want to tell you what I did next, because it isn't good, and I've had a long time now to know it isn't good.

I should have texted back one thing. *I'm here.* Or *I'm coming.* Or nothing — nothing would have been fine, nothing would have been a held breath and not a slammed door.

Instead I sat there in the last of the dark with the whole night still ringing in my ears, two hundred and eighteen dollars in a coffee can, a town roaring my name, so tired I could feel my own heartbeat in my teeth — and something old and ugly in me woke up. The thing that has watched every person I've ever loved get in a car and fill the back window with boxes. My father's brake lights. Theo's hatchback. The tide going out and forgetting.

*i can't* — she was already leaving. That's what the old ugly thing said, in my father's exact voice. She's going into a place you can't follow and she's shutting the door, and this is what always happens, this is the only thing that ever happens, they love you and then they go quiet and the quiet is the leaving.

I typed: *don't do this.*

I typed: *you don't get to just go silent. not you. not tonight.*

I hit send before the better part of me could get to the room.

The gray checkmarks. Delivered.

Not read.

And then — nothing. No dots. No answer. The phone in my hand as dead as the tower would be if I lost this fight, and I understood, sitting there, that I had just done the exact thing I was most afraid of having done to me. I had heard someone in the worst pain of her life reach out with the last of what she had, and I had grabbed her by the wrist and made it about me.

The automated ID clicked over. Ninety-point-three WMRW, Marrow Bay. The record I'd cued hours ago had long since run out.

I keyed the mic. I don't know why. Habit. The town was asleep. There was no one out there but the gulls and the fog and June's terrible hum starting back up in the machines.

I opened my mouth to say the thing I say. You are not alone. Stay warm.

And I couldn't.

Because it wasn't true. Somewhere across town a girl was sitting in a house with her grandmother's body cooling in it, and I had made her more alone, not less, and the whole reason this frequency existed — the only thing it was ever for — was a promise I had just personally broken to the one person who taught me it could be kept.

I let go of the key.

The board hummed. The needle sat in the dead groove. Outside, the pewter went the sick gold of a Marrow Bay dawn, and the light came up over Gault Hill and touched the tower we'd maybe just saved, and I sat there under it and did the one thing you are never, ever supposed to do on the graveyard shift.

I let it go silent.

Dead air. The worst sound in radio. The sound of nobody home.

I sat in it a long time. It was the loudest thing I'd ever heard.

## Chapter 11 — Silence

*— IT'S 90.3 WMRW AND WE'RE PLAYING NOTHING TONIGHT. NO, THE GEAR'S FINE. SOMEBODY WE LOVED WENT QUIET THIS WEEK AND WE'RE GOING TO SIT WITH IT FOR A SONG'S LENGTH, YOU AND ME, AND JUST LET THE LAKE TALK. HER NAME WAS CARLOTA. SHE LIKED THE RADIO. STAY WARM. —*

For three days I did not go on air, and I learned that grief has acoustics.

It's not silent. That's the thing nobody tells you. A quiet house isn't quiet — it's full of the refrigerator, the pipes, the specific creak of the third stair, all the sounds a place makes when there's no talking to cover them. Loss doesn't turn the volume down. It turns everything else up. I sat in my own kitchen with Deb asleep upstairs after a double, and I heard the whole house going about its business around the hole where I'd put my phone face-down on the table, and I understood for the first time in my life exactly what Frankie's nights had sounded like. A person keeping vigil in a house that won't stop making noise.

She didn't answer my texts. I stopped sending them after the second day, because I finally reread the ones I'd sent that dawn — *don't do this, you don't get to just go silent* — and I had to go outside and stand by the marina in the cold until I could breathe. I had sent those. To her. On that night. There is no version of an apology text that survives being read next to what you're apologizing for, so I didn't send one. I just carried it.

Deb found out from Frankie's mom at the clinic. They work the same floor, my mother and Frankie's — that's how this whole town is stitched, everybody's overnight sewn to everybody else's. Deb came home Wednesday and sat down across from me still in her scrubs, smelling like the hand sanitizer that means she's been somewhere sad, and she told me there'd be a small thing Saturday. A memorial. Not a church — Lita hadn't wanted the church. Just the little community room over the library, the one with the folding chairs and the coffee urn, the one that smells like every potluck since 1988.

"You should go," Deb said.

"She hasn't answered me since—" I stopped. "I did something. To Frankie. That night."

Deb didn't ask what. That's a thing about my mother I don't say enough. She doesn't make you narrate the wound before she'll look at it. She reached across the table and turned my phone over, face-up, and slid it back to me.

"People who love you go quiet sometimes," she said. "It's not always leaving." She looked at me a beat too long, and I knew she was thinking about herself, about the overnights, about all the years she'd gone quiet into a shift so she could keep a roof over a kid who thought quiet meant gone. "You'll learn the difference. Took me forty years. You've got a head start."

The memorial was at two on Saturday, which meant the Night Voices came out in daylight.

I don't know how I hadn't understood they were real. I knew they were real — I'd heard them breathing into the phone at 3 a.m. for a whole summer — but real the way weather is real, or the lake, a thing you live inside and never expect to shake hands with. And then I walked into the library room and there they were, in their bodies, in the flat gray afternoon light, and it broke something open in my chest.

Big Al was enormous. I don't know why that surprised me — the voice should've told me — but there he was, a mountain of a man in a clean flannel he'd clearly ironed, holding a foil-covered dish with both hands like it might get away from him, looking lost the way big men look in small rooms. Mrs. Petrakis was tiny and immaculate and had brought, of course, an entire tray of something involving phyllo, and she'd brought the bridge club, four old women in their good coats who arranged themselves in the folding chairs like they were guarding it. June was there in a plant-crew hoodie, out of place and not leaving. And near the door, hanging back, a skinny kid with a fan of dark hair over one eye, fifteen and terrified, standing next to a woman who had to be his mother.

Marcus. Six dollars. He caught me looking and went red and lifted one hand about two inches, and I lifted mine back, and neither of us said anything, because we'd never once in our lives spoken with our actual mouths and this didn't seem like the day to start.

They had all come. For a woman most of them had never met. For a girl they knew only as a voice on a phone, the way they knew me. Because that's what the frequency had done — it had made us a town again, a real one, the invisible kind that only exists at night, and now here it was standing in a library room in the daylight holding casseroles because one of its own was grieving.

Frankie stood at the front with her mother. She looked older. Grief does that in the worst way, ages you a decade in a week and then you get most of it back but not all. She was thinner and her eyes were swollen down to almost nothing and she was wearing a dress I'd never seen, dark green, and she was holding herself the way you hold a full glass you're scared of spilling. She saw me come in. She saw me. And then she looked away, and I felt it land in my sternum, and I went and sat in the back with Deb and I did not try to make it about me. I had learned that much. I sat in the back and I let it be hers.

There were a few words. Frankie's mom spoke, in Spanish and then in English, about her mother — how Lita had crossed a border at nineteen and buried a husband at fifty and never once, not one single time, let anyone in her house go to bed cold or unfed. How she'd loved the radio her whole life, any radio, static and all, because in the worst year of her life a voice on a station had kept her company through the nights and she never forgot it. How, this last summer, she'd made Frankie tune the little kitchen radio to 90.3 every single night, and she'd fall asleep to it, to a girl reading out lost dogs and dead men's names, and she'd told Frankie — near the end, when she wasn't saying much — she'd said, *that one. She's got a good voice. She knows what it's for.*

I stopped breathing.

I hadn't known Lita heard me. All summer I'd been talking to the dark, and the dark had a name, and the name was Carlota, and she was leaving me a message on her way out that I only got to hear at her funeral.

I put my face in my hands the way Ruthie had, and Deb put her arm around me, and I cried in the back of a library room for a woman I'd never met who'd fallen asleep to my voice.

Ruthie found me by the coffee urn afterward. She looked wrecked and she looked, underneath it, like she was carrying something she didn't know where to set down. "Council met Thursday," she said. She'd been there. They'd all been there — Al and June and the bridge club, packing the folding chairs while I stood at the front of a different sad room, so I'd missed it. "You want the news."

"I don't know," I said honestly.

"They didn't sell." She said it flat, like she didn't trust it to hold weight. "Not — they didn't save us either, kid, don't get the wrong idea. They voted to hold the land. Two years. Somebody stood up — Petrakis, if you can believe it, five foot nothing — and read out the pledges, and the coffee can, and the list of names, and then June's whole plant crew stood up at once, and a councilman I won't name looked at a room that full and decided he'd like to get reelected." A crack of something almost like a smile. "There's a thing now. Community license. We'd own it — the town would, all of us, if we can figure out how a town owns a radio station. There's paperwork. There's always paperwork. But the tower stays lit two more years, minimum, and after that it's ours to keep or lose."

Two years. Not forever. Not a miracle. A stay of execution and a mountain of paperwork and a coffee can with two hundred and eighteen dollars in it.

It was the best news I'd ever heard.

"We're not dark," I said.

"We're not dark," Ruthie agreed, and blew her nose like a foghorn.

I found Frankie on the fire-escape landing off the back of the library, where the smokers used to go, looking out at the gray roofs down toward the water. I didn't wait for her to be ready for me. That was the whole thing I'd figured out, standing in the back of that room — I'd spent a summer waiting for the red light to blink, for people to come to me, and this was the one time in my life I could not afford to wait to be called.

I went to her.

"You don't have to talk to me," I said. "I just — I couldn't sit in there and wait for you to come find me. Not this time."

She didn't turn around. For a second I thought that was it, I'd get the back of a dark green dress and the gulls and that would be the answer.

Then she said, low, "You told me I didn't get to go silent."

"I know."

"On the worst night of my whole life, you—"

"I know, Frankie." My voice cracked. I let it. "I was scared and I was tired and I have this — thing, this broken thing, where the second someone I love goes quiet I hear my dad's car pulling away, and I put that on you. On the one night. I'm not asking you to forgive it. I just needed you to know I know exactly what I did."

She turned around then. Her face was doing several things at once. "You think I left," she said. It wasn't a question. "You thought I was leaving you."

"Everybody leaves," I said. It came out smaller than I wanted. "That's the — that's the only thing this town has ever reliably done."

"I wasn't leaving." She stepped closer, and her voice broke all the way. "I was drowning, Wren. There's a difference. I went quiet because I couldn't get my head above the water long enough to make a sound, and I needed — I needed the one person who knows what silence is for to just — let it be quiet with me. And instead you—" She stopped. Wiped her face with the heel of her hand, angry at the tears, the exact way Ruthie is. "But you came out here. You didn't wait for the phone to ring. You came out here."

"I'll always come out here," I said. "I'm done waiting for the light."

She looked at me for a long time, out there on the fire escape with the whole gray shrinking town below us and the tower up on Gault Hill catching what little sun there was. Then she reached out and took a fistful of the front of my jacket, not gently, and pulled me in, and kissed me.

It was cold and her face was wet and mine was too and it tasted like salt and library coffee and it was, without any competition at all, the single best sound I have ever not-heard in my life — the sound of two people going quiet at the exact same time, on purpose, together, the good kind, the kind that isn't leaving.

When we came apart she kept the fist in my jacket. Didn't let go.

"Fly safe," I said, stupidly, because I didn't know what else, because it was hers.

She laughed — this wrecked, wet, real laugh — and put her forehead against mine, and we stood there on the fire escape not talking, and the gulls argued down at the marina, and the lake did its breathing thing, and for once in my life the quiet had somebody in it.

It was full. Not empty.

I hadn't known, until right then, that it could be both.

## Chapter 12 — The Last Good Frequency

*— GOOD EVENING, MARROW BAY. IT'S THE GIRL ON THE GRAVEYARD SHIFT, AND THE LEAVES ON CARGILL STREET FINALLY GAVE UP AND WENT GOLD, WHICH MEANS SUMMER'S OVER AND WE MADE IT ANYWAY. THE TOWER'S STILL LIT. YOU'RE STILL HERE. SO AM I. STAY WARM. —*

Theo called on a Sunday in September, which was correct, because by then we both knew that nothing important between us was going to happen on a Tuesday ever again.

We hadn't really talked in weeks. That's the truth of it. The texts had gone from paragraphs to sentences to those single stupid reaction-hearts you tap when you can't think of what a person is anymore. All summer I'd felt it happening and told myself it was temporary, the way you tell yourself the mill's coming back. And then he called, actually called, my phone lighting up with a photo of the two of us at eleven with our faces sunburned off, and I sat down on the back steps to answer it.

"Hart," he said, and his voice was different. Not bad. Different. Chicago had gotten into it somehow, some flatness, some speed. "Okay, don't be weird, I just — I listened to your thing. Your show. My mom found it on the internet, they stream it now or whatever, and I stayed up and I listened to the whole—" He stopped. "You talk now."

"I talk now," I said.

"Since when do you talk. You didn't talk to me for like a year in eighth grade."

"I know."

"There was a girl calling in. Nightbird." A pause that had a whole grin in it. "Is that—"

"Yeah."

"HART." Genuine, delighted, the old Theo surfacing through the new flat voice like a fish coming up. "Okay. Okay, I need everything, but first I need you to know I always knew. I called it in fourth grade. I want that on the record."

We talked for an hour. And here's the thing I didn't expect: it was good, and it was over, both at once. We weren't the two kids in the sunburned photo anymore. He had new people, a whole city of them, an entire life with trains in it that I would in fact hate. I had a station and a town and a girl and a voice. We'd grown apart the way two branches grow apart — same tree, and then just further and further from each other every year, and no villain in it, nobody's fault, just the growing.

But we were the same tree. When I told him about Lita, about the fire escape, about the coffee can and the two-year stay, he got quiet in the exact way he used to, and he said, "I'm really far away, aren't I," and I said, "Yeah," and neither of us pretended it wasn't true, and somehow that was the closest we'd been all year. We stopped lying about the distance. That's what let us keep the rest.

"Thanksgiving," he said at the end. "For real this time. I'll come up. I want to see the bait shop. I want to meet Nightbird."

"You'd hate the drive," I said. "It's four hours of nothing and then more nothing."

"I'd love hating it," he said, which was a callback, which meant he remembered exactly who we used to be even though we weren't it anymore, and that was the whole gift. We hung up okay. A new shape. Further apart and still, somehow, true.

I called my father the next day.

I want you to understand I did it on purpose, in the daylight, sitting up at the station in the afternoon with the gear off and the lake white in the window, because I wanted to be the one holding the phone this time. All my life Emeka had been a thing that happened to me — a text on my birthday, a car in August, a voice that came through and then went. I'd never once dialed him first. I dialed him first.

"Wren?" He sounded startled. Somewhere with a lot of echo behind him, a venue, a load-in, the clatter of somebody else's night getting built. "Hey — hey, is everything—"

"Everything's fine," I said. "I'm not calling because something's wrong. I just wanted to tell you a thing and then I'm going to go."

"Okay." Wary. Hopeful. Both. "Okay, I'm listening."

"I named the show," I said. "It's called The Last Good Frequency. And we almost lost the station and the town saved it — not all the way, but enough — and I did that. With my voice. The one you said sounded like yours." I breathed. "It doesn't. Sound like yours. It sounds like mine. I just wanted you to hear it say that."

There was a long echoey silence on his end, the sound of a man in a room full of other people's equipment, a thousand miles from a bait shop, hearing his kid tell him something he'd waited a long time to deserve and hadn't.

"It sounds like yours," he agreed, quiet. "You're right. It's yours." And then, because he is who he is, unreliable to the last, sad and charming and already somewhere else: "Can I listen? Online? Sometimes?"

"Yeah," I said. "It's a free country and it's a free frequency. Listen whenever."

"I love you, Wren."

"I know," I said, which is not the same as saying it back, and both of us knew that too, and it was okay. I hung up first. I sat with the phone warm in my hand and the lake white in the window and I felt — not fixed. Not healed, not any of the tidy words. I felt like a girl who'd finally put a heavy thing down on her own terms and walked out of the room without it. Lighter by exactly its weight.

Deb was at the table when I got home, in her civvies for once, a day off, drinking a beer she was making last.

"Frankie's mom says you're steady," she said, which is how my mother asks a question. "You and Frankie."

"We're steady," I said. Then, because it was true and I'd learned that summer to say the true things out loud: "For as long as we've got. She's not leaving — her mom's staying on at the clinic, the family's here now, Lita's here now." Buried up on the hill in the little cemetery you can see the tower from. "We're a we. However long that lasts. I'm not going to pretend forever, but I'm not going to pretend it's nothing either."

Deb nodded slowly. Turned her bottle a quarter-turn on the table, the way she does when she's about to say the hard thing.

"There's a program," she said. "Frankie's mom saw the flyer at the clinic, of all places. Some college downstate, a summer thing — youth audio, they call it. Radio, podcasts, sound. For kids who—" She stopped. Started over, softer. "For kids like you. You'd go for six weeks in July. You'd have to apply. It's a real thing, Wren, with real people who do this for a living, and it is downstate, and it is not Marrow Bay."

Here it was. The oldest fight in my house, the one that isn't even a fight, just my mother's whole heart pointed at the door and my whole heart digging its nails into the floor. Get out. Get out of this shrinking town before it shrinks you. I'd braced for it every day of my life.

But she didn't say get out. She looked at me across the table with her tired ER eyes and she said, "Six weeks. And then you come home to your shift. If you want. That's the part I finally figured out, kid, and it took me too long — leaving isn't the only way out, and staying isn't the only way to disappear. You can do both. You can go learn the thing and come back and be the thing. Nobody said you had to pick." Her voice went thick. "I just spent sixteen years wanting you gone from here, and it turns out what I wanted was for you to be somebody who chooses. That's all. Just — choose it. Don't let it choose you like it chose me."

I applied that night. Six weeks downstate in July, and my name on the WMRW schedule for every other night of the year, and the tenth-grade textbooks I hadn't cracked yet stacked on my desk. All of it. I didn't pick. I chose the whole shape of it, on purpose, my frequency, mine.

The last thing happened on a Friday.

I'd taken to visiting Cash on Fridays. He was better — better enough to be furious about the parts that weren't. The stroke had left his words loose in his mouth, and he hated it, a man who'd talked for a living for thirty years now hunting for nouns like they were coins under the couch. But his hands were good. We'd sit in the day room at the rehab place and he'd hold court without half his words, and I'd fill the gaps, and he'd let me.

That Friday he was wearing the ring.

I'd put it back in his hand in August, in the hospital — the class ring gone dull, Marrow Bay High, Class of '69, initials C.O. scratched inside the band, the one I'd read out on air my second terrified night without knowing whose it was. It had taken me all summer to understand it was his. Cassius Odom, Class of '69, who'd lost it half a century back and thought it gone for good. Now it rode loose on his good hand, too big for a finger the stroke had thinned, so he kept it on and kept turning it, a man learning an old thing the long way around.

He caught me looking at it. His face did a thing I won't try to describe because I'd wreck it. He got exactly one word out, and it was the right one, and it cost him everything, and it was: "Home."

"It found its way back," I said. "Took a while. Things do, up here."

He closed his fist around it and he looked at me, this man who'd kept the whole town company through thirty years of its worst hours, who'd handed me a microphone he swore was never his, and he tapped his chest twice with the fist that held the ring, and then he pointed at me, and I understood him perfectly. *You keep it lit.*

"I keep it lit," I said.

That night I did the sign-off I'd been carrying all summer without knowing it.

It was late. It was the good late, the deep part, the part that belongs to the ones who can't sleep. The board hummed. The red light was dark but I knew it would come. The lake was doing its breathing thing outside the bait-shop window and up on Gault Hill the tower stood there lit and stubborn against two more years of dark, and I pulled the mic in close, the way Cash used to, the way I do now, and I said the truest thing I know how to say.

*This is 90.3 WMRW, Marrow Bay community radio, and this is The Last Good Frequency. It's late. If you're up, you're up with me.*

*I want to say some names. Big Al, out at the overlook, watching for his exit — the tower's still lit, you're almost somewhere. June and the whole overnight crew at the gas plant, keeping the town running while it sleeps — I hear your machines and I hear you over them. Mrs. Petrakis on Pearl Street, and Constantine, who I said out loud and will say again. Marcus, fifteen, six dollars, still up — you're not stupid and you're not alone, kid, put your name in the good part of the sentence anytime you want. Cash Odom, who held this shift for thirty years and taught me it was never his to own — it's back in your hand, Cash. It found its way home.*

*And Nightbird. Fly safe. I'll see you when the light comes up.*

I let the quiet sit for one breath. The whole town on the other end of it, all those sleepless rooms, all those people who'd been alone in the dark until a frequency found them.

*Here's the thing this station is for,* I said. *Here's the only thing it was ever for. It's three in the morning and the lake sounds like it's breathing and half this town is boarded up and everybody you love is going to leave someday or stay someday and you can't ever tell in advance which one it'll be. And none of that — none of it — means you have to sit in the dark by yourself. You've got a light on Gault Hill and a voice in your kitchen radio and a whole invisible town awake right alongside you, every single night, for as long as we've got.*

I reached for the fader. The red phone light blinked on — the first call of the night, somebody out there needing the company. I let it wait one more second.

*You are not alone,* I said. *This is the last good frequency, and it's yours, and I'm right here.*

*Stay warm.*

And I picked up the phone.
