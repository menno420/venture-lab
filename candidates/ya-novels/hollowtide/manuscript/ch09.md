# Chapter 9 — Morren

The light did not come to her. She had to go to it.

Wren waded across the drowned cavern with her lantern held high and her heart going hard, and the small steady flame ahead grew as she came on until she could see what held it: a figure, seated, on a shelf of black rock that rose just clear of the water at the cavern's heart. An island the size of a hearthstone. And on it, cross-legged, still as the stone itself, an old woman cupping a single flame in her two hands.

Except *old* was not the word, and *woman* was only half of it.

Wren stopped a few feet off, standing to her waist in the black water, and made herself look. The figure was made of two things that should not have been in one body. Part of her was a person — a face, ancient beyond any face in Nethercote, the skin drawn thin as wet paper over the bones, a fall of hair gone past white to the grey of old ash. And part of her was the dark itself. Where the flame's small light didn't reach her she seemed to go on into the cold, to have no edge, to be of a piece with the drowned black around them, so that Wren could not have said where the old woman ended and the Deepings began. Ash-moths sat on her shoulders and did not stir. And between her cupped hands burned the flame — small, honest, and failing, guttering low and steadying and guttering again, the way a wick burns when there is almost nothing left to feed it.

"You know my name," Wren said. Her voice came out steadier than she felt. "Say yours."

Something moved across the ancient face that might once have been a smile. "It's a long time since anyone came down far enough to be owed it." The voice was dry as moth-wings, and it came as much from the dark as from the mouth. "I am Morren Cawley. I lit the first vigil in Nethercote, when there was a Nethercote to light. I came down this stair to hold the dark so the town above could keep its warmth." The flame in her hands dipped, and she cupped it closer, tender, a mother with a cold child. "That was — I have lost the count of it. A great many dark-tides ago."

Wren's own light shook in her fist. *Morren Cawley.* She'd heard the name in Gran's terrible voice, in the ledger's first pages — the First Lamplighter, the founding, the reason the Cawleys carried the bargain. She'd thought it a story to frighten children into their own hearths. And here it sat on a rock in the drowned dark, holding a flame that had not gone out in a hundred lifetimes, and it knew her by her face.

"You're — but you'd be dead," Wren said. "Centuries dead."

"Yes." Morren said it without weight, the way you'd agree it was cold. "I would. That is the trouble." She turned her cupped hands a little so the small flame lit the ruin of her face. "When I came down to hold the Ashen, I gave it my light to keep it fed and sleeping — a little, always a little, so it would not have to take the whole town. And it took me in exchange. Half of me. It let the rest keep hold of a flame so there'd always be a flame to feed it." Her not-quite-there shoulders lifted. "I cannot die while I hold it. I have wanted to for a long, long while. But if I let go, the flame goes out, and the dark that I have held here so long comes up the stair — up into all those little shuttered rooms — and takes them all in one night. So I hold. Do you see? I have held so that they above could keep to their own hearths and never know what I keep down here on their behalf."

Wren thought of the town folding itself away, board over board. A hundred lights that never touched. Kept safe, kept apart, kept ignorant — by this.

"The Wardens," she said. "The tithe. They give you a child every dark-tide—"

"They give the *Ashen* a child." Something like grief crossed Morren's face and did not have the strength to stay. "Not me. Never me. When the flame I hold burns low and the dark wakes and stirs for more, the town above feeds it a lone wick to buy it back to sleep. A bright one, if they can. Cawley wicks burn brightest and hold the dark the longest — which is why the dark loves them best, and why it is always my own line the ledger takes." The flame guttered; she breathed on it, and it steadied, and Wren understood with a lurch that the old woman's whole existence had narrowed to that one small task, keeping that one flame alive, hour upon hour upon age. "Every tithe buys a few years' sleep. And then it wakes hungry again. It never ends, child. The bargain does not end. It only feeds."

"Then I'll end it." Wren's teeth were chattering; she made them stop. "I've come for my brother. Fen. They took him. I can bring him back — I can share my wick, I know how, I'll relight him and I'll take him home and we'll—"

"No." The word was quiet and absolute, and it stopped Wren cold. Morren was looking at her with those ancient eyes and there was pity in them, which was worse than any warning. "You cannot fight it that way. Listen to me, and hear it, for I will not have breath to say it twice. The Ashen is not a beast you can strike. It has no throat to cut, no heart to stop. It is a hunger and a cold, and it is old past your reckoning, and *force is nothing to it.* A lone flame it snuffs. Two lone flames it snuffs harder — I have watched them try, down the years, the ones who came alone to save someone alone. Do you know what a shared wick is, brought down here by itself?" She leaned forward, and the dark leaned with her. "Two hollows instead of one. It welcomes you. It has room for you both."

"Then how?" Wren heard the crack in her own voice and hated it. "There has to be a *how*. You wouldn't have — you didn't hold on for a hundred lifetimes so you could tell me it can't be done."

And there — there — a light came into the old ruined face, faint as the flame in her hands, but real. "No," Morren said softly. "I held on to tell you it *can*." She looked at the small failing flame she cupped, and then out at the vast cold dark that pressed on every side of their little light, and then back at Wren. "The Ashen cannot be broken. But it can be *outshone*. A flame it can snuff, because a flame stands alone, and anything that stands alone can be put out — a wick, a lantern, an old woman on a rock. But light that will not stand alone—" Her voice dropped, and the words came out worn smooth, as if she had turned them over in the dark for centuries waiting for an ear to say them into. "Light that will not stand alone cannot be put out. That is the whole of it. That is the thing your gran and hers and hers before them never learned, or learned and buried, because the buried truth kept the town obedient. The Ashen has never once faced the whole of Nethercote's light joined together — because the Wardens keep every hearth apart. Shame. Silence. *Keep your fire, and hold your own.* They have kept the town small enough to feed on, one lone wick at a time, for longer than either of us can count." Her hands trembled around the flame. "I have been alone down here so long. That is why I am failing. Not because the dark is stronger. Because a light kept alone always, always burns down in the end. Even mine."

Wren stood in the black water and felt the size of it settle on her — not just Fen, not just tonight, but the whole shape of what would have to happen, and how far it was past one girl with a lantern. Bring the town together. Make Nethercote, which had been taught since its founding to burn apart, join its light. It was impossible. It was the only thing that wasn't.

"I don't have the town," she said. "I have a lantern and a button and — and me. Rook's caught. Mabb's taken. There's no one left up there who'd even—"

"Then you have tonight to lose," Morren said, "and a brother to lose it for. One thing at a time, Cawley. First the boy." And the pity in her face sharpened suddenly into something urgent, something almost like fear, and she uncupped one hand from her flame — the small light shrank at once, and the dark pressed nearer, and even that cost her — and she pointed. Past Wren. Deeper. Into the black where the cavern narrowed and dropped and the dark-tide sound came up thickest, where the water ran on into the true heart of the Deepings.

"You hear that?" Morren whispered.

Wren went still. Under the endless shaftwind, under the lap of black water, there was something new — voices, a chant carried down through the rock, *light to light and none alone,* and the small dragging scuff of feet that did not want to walk, coming down and down toward the water's edge.

"They are not waiting for the ceremony above," Morren said, and her failing flame shook as she held out her pointing hand. "They are bringing him to the relighting hollow — to the heart, to the water, to *it.* Now. Tonight. This hour." Her ancient eyes fixed on Wren's, and for the first time all the tiredness in them was burned away by something fierce and old and unbearably kind. "Go, child. Go quickly. Your brother is almost at the edge of the dark — and once he is given down, no light on earth, joined or alone, will bring him back up."
