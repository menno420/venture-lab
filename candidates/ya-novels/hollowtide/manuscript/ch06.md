# Chapter 6 — Those Who Kept Their Dead

Rook found her, in the end, and not the other way round. Wren had gone back to the ropewalk at first dark, half-mad with what she carried, and he'd taken one look at her face over the coiled ropes and said, "You read it, then," and not made her say any more.

"There's people you should meet," he said. "People who won't turn their eyes away from you. But you'll not tell a soul they exist, Wren Cawley, not your mam, not the pigeon-boy, no one — because the Wardens hunt them, and if you give them up you may as well have written the names yourself."

He led her down. Not into the Deepings — she was glad of that, some cowardly part of her had thought for a moment he meant the shaft — but under the town, through a coal-cellar behind the ropewalk and along a low passage that had been a haulage-way when the mine still lived, the walls sweating, the air thick with the smell of tallow and turned earth and unwashed grief. Wren went with one hand on the cold stone and her wick a small stubborn coal in her chest, and thought: *this is how it will feel, going down to Fen. Cold like this, and dark like this, and worse.*

The passage opened into a chamber, and the chamber was full of light.

Not much light. Careful light — a dozen small flames in a dozen jars and lanterns, hooded so none of it would show at any crack to the moor above. But after the black of the haulage-way it was warm as a held hand, and Wren stood in the mouth of it and felt her own eyes sting, and did not know why until she understood: it was the first warm room she'd stood in since the vigil-house, and the vigil-house's warmth had been a lie. This one wasn't. This one was people keeping each other lit in the dark, on purpose, against the law.

There were perhaps twenty of them. Ordinary Nethercote faces — a fishwife she bought from, a man who mended boots, two of the ropewalk lads, an old woman by the fire with a face like a walnut. They looked up at her, and Wren braced for the flinch, the sliding-away of the eyes that every neighbour had given her family since the moths came.

It did not come. They looked at her plainly, the way you look at someone who has the same wound as you.

"Idony Cawley's granddaughter," said the walnut-faced old woman, and it was not a question and not quite a welcome. "Come to keep your dead, or come to count us for the Wardens?"

"Fen's not dead," Wren said. "That's the whole of why I'm here."

Something went through the room at that — not agreement, but recognition. Rook put a hand on her shoulder, briefly, which for Rook was practically an embrace. "This is Old Mabb," he said. "She kept her own son eleven nights past the three, longer than anyone's held one. The Wardens burned her out twice. She keeps going."

"Twelve nights," Old Mabb said. "Before he walked. I keep the count." She patted the low stool beside her. "Sit, Cawley. You've the look of a girl about to do something that'll get her killed, and I'd sooner she did it knowing than not."

Wren sat. Across the chamber, in the warmest corner, a shape she had taken for a heap of blankets moved, and she saw that it was a person — a girl a little older than herself, wrapped to the chin, being fed something warm spoon by spoon by a young man who never took his eyes from her face. The girl's hands, where they showed, were grey-white and too still. Her eyes were open and turned, always, past the man who loved her, past the fire, toward the black passage and the moor beyond it. Every little while a moth — one of the small black ones, an ash-moth — lifted off her collar and drifted toward the dark, and the young man reached up without pausing his spooning and caught it in his fist and crushed it, gently, the way you'd close a door on a draught. He had been doing it a long time. Wren understood, watching, that he would go on doing it until she walked or he did, and that it would not be enough, and that he knew it would not be enough and did it anyway.

She looked away, because to look longer felt like theft. This was what waited for Fen. This grey stillness, this turning toward the dark. This was what she was racing.

"You want to know why the sharing fails," Old Mabb said, watching her watch the girl. She had a way of answering the question under the question. "He's tried it, that boy. Everyone here has tried it once. You press your hand to their heart and you pour your own warmth in, and for a night, two nights, they come back to you — they know your face, they hold your hand, the moths thin out. And then the dark comes for the tithe at the next dark-tide, and it doesn't take one of them. It takes both. The one you were keeping, and the fool who gave their light to keep them." She held up her own hand to the jar-light. Across the palm ran a scar, white and old, the twin of the one on Gran's hand. "I know. My son's gone and half my own light with him. I'm a candle-end. But I'll tell you the thing the Wardens have known for two hundred years and hanged men for saying." She leaned close, and her breath smelled of onions and cold. "It's not the sharing that fails. It's the *alone.*"

"I don't—" Wren started.

"Think, girl." Mabb's eyes were bright and hard in the walnut face. "The Ashen can put out a candle. Easy. It can put out two candles pressed together — that's your sharing, that's why it fails, two flames is still a small thing in a great dark, and it snuffs them together and calls it a good night's work. It's put out lone flames for longer than there's been a town up there to burn them. But hear me now." She took Wren's wrist, the same place Gran had gripped, but soft. "It has *never once* faced the whole town's light at the same time. Not once. Not since Morren went down. Do you know why?"

Wren shook her head, though something was rising in her, cold and enormous, that felt almost like the answer.

"Because the Wardens won't let us." Mabb sat back. "Why d'you think the law is *keep to your own hearth?* Why d'you think a hollowing's a shame, so the neighbours turn away, so no two griefs ever stand in the same room? Why d'you think they've hunted a little warm cellar like this one my whole long life, when all we do is sit with our dying? It's not to spare us the shame, love. It's to keep the light *apart.* Every hearth alone, every family behind its own door, every wick a lone candle the dark can pinch out one at a time forever. The bargain doesn't only need a tithe. It needs us *scattered.* A town that joined its light — all of it, at once, at the mine-mouth — that's the one thing in two hundred years the Ashen has never had to swallow. And I have sat down here in the dark my whole life thinking on it, and I do not believe it could."

The chamber was very quiet. The young man in the corner had stopped spooning and was listening, his fist still closed around a moth. The fishwife had a hand pressed to her own chest. Wren realised they had all heard Mabb say it before — that this was the thing they held onto down here in the warm dark, the heresy they passed hand to hand like a coal — and that not one of them had ever had the power to try it, because they were the shamed and the hunted and the half-hollow, twenty candle-ends in a cellar, and it would take the whole town, and the whole town had been taught since it could walk to keep its fire and hold its own.

*Light to light, and none alone,* Wren thought — the rhyme they'd all been sung to sleep with. *Keep your fire, and hold your own.* And she heard it, for the first time, as the lie it was built to be. Not a comfort. A leash.

"You're saying," she said slowly, "that if Nethercote lit one fire together — everybody, all their wicks in one place—"

"I'm saying nothing," Mabb said, "to a Cawley I met an hour past. I'm an old woman talking to the wall, and you never heard me." But her eyes said the rest, and held Wren's, and would not let go.

Rook crouched beside her. "It's why I brought you," he said, low. "You're the one with a name that opens doors. I'm nobody from the edge of town, Mabb's a wanted woman, these folk can't so much as show their faces in the square. But a Cawley—" He stopped. "A Cawley might make the town do a thing it's afraid of. If there were time."

"How much is there?" Wren asked. "Time. Fen had three nights. That's—" She counted, and her stomach dropped, because she had lost count somewhere in the vigil-house and Gran's front room, lost it the way you lose the bottom stair in the dark. "That's tonight and—"

"No." The young man in the corner spoke for the first time, hoarse, not turning from his girl. "The relighting's not kept every dark-tide-night. It's kept on the dark-tide *proper.* The high one. When the tide's full." He finally looked round at Wren, and his eyes were terrible and kind. "That's tomorrow. Tomorrow at nightfall the tide comes full and the Wardens go down to give the dark its due. Whoever's name is written—" He looked back at the wrapped, grey, turning girl he loved, and did not finish, and did not have to.

"Tomorrow," Wren said. "Fen's third night."

"Fen's third night," Old Mabb agreed, and her old hand tightened once on Wren's wrist and let go. "So whatever mad bright thing you mean to do, Cawley — you've got until the dark comes full. You've got hours. Not nights. Hours."

Above them, faint through the sweating stone, the town bell rang the hour, and Wren counted the strokes, and each one fell on her like a stair going down.
