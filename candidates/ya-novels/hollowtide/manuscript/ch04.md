# Chapter 4 — The Lamplighters' Book

The vigil-house stood at the top of the market square, older than the chapel and colder, a long low building of black stone with lamps burning in every window even by day. No Nethercote child went near it without being sent for. Which was, Wren thought, exactly why no one would look twice at a Cawley walking up to the door as if she had a right to be there.

She had a right. That was the ugly, useful part. She was Idony Cawley's granddaughter, and the Cawley name opened doors in this town the way a key opened a lock — not because people loved you, but because they had learned not to ask.

She waited until the noon bell, when the Wardens took their meal in the back room and left only one lamp-boy at the door. She knew him — Perch, a soft round boy two years younger who kept pigeons and owed her for a winter's worth of not telling his mother where he really got the birds.

"Gran wants the small ledger from the vigil-room," Wren said, and let her voice go flat and bored, the way Gran's went when she expected to be obeyed. "The offerings one. You want to come and find it in the dark yourself, or you want to let me do it and get back to your dinner?"

Perch looked at the warm doorway behind him, and at Wren, and decided his dinner mattered more than his duty. He always would. That was why Gran called him a lamp-boy and not a Warden.

The vigil-room was at the top of a stone stair, and it smelled of tallow and old paper and, faintly, underneath, of wet ash — that smell Wren was learning to hate, the smell that had come off Fen's collar the night the moths took him. She stood in the doorway a moment and made herself breathe it.

Lamps everywhere. A long table. And on a shelf built into the wall, chained to the stone like something in the chapel, a book.

It was enormous. Leather gone the colour of a drowned thing, the spine cracked and re-sewn a dozen times, the pages so many that the block of them had bowed the shelf. Wren lifted the cover and the smell that came up was the deep one, the mine-smell, coal-damp and cold, as if the book had been kept somewhere far underground and only lately brought up into the light.

The first pages were in a hand she didn't know — narrow, slanted, ancient, the ink gone brown as tea. But the words she knew. Everyone in Nethercote knew them, though no one said them like this.

*The dark's takings,* the top of the page said. And under it, columns. A name. A date. A word beside each name that was always the same word.

*Relit.*

She turned pages. The hands changed as the years went by — she could see the town's whole line of Chief Lamplighters in the changing ink, one dying and the next taking up the pen, the letters growing rounder or sharper by the decade. But the shape of the thing never changed. Every dark-tide, one name. Always one. Sometimes two in a bad year. Never none.

*Relit. Relit. Relit.*

And the names. She had thought, walking up the square, that she might find strangers here, a list of the poor and the friendless, the ones nobody would miss. She had been ready to be angry about that.

She was not ready for how many of the names were hers.

Cawley. Cawley. A Ashby, twice. Cawley. Morren, Ivo, Hale, the old moorland families — the ones with the bright wicks, the ones Rook had ringed on his tally at the ropewalk with his bitten-down pencil, saying *why always these, why always the good ones.* Here was his answer, written in a Cawley hand and chained to a wall. The town had not been feeding its worst to the dark.

It had been feeding its best.

Wren's own wick pulsed under her sternum, a small stubborn warmth, and for the first time in her life the feeling of it frightened her — as if it were a coin in her chest that someone had been saving up.

She turned to the last written page. The ink here was fresh enough to still be black.

The hand was Gran's. She would have known it anywhere; she had watched it write shopping-lists and death-notices and the labels on the jars of the good honey. Neat, upright, unhurried. It had written a date — this dark-tide, the one they were living in now — and beside it a name.

*Fen Cawley.*

And the word beside it was not written yet. The space for it was blank, waiting, the way a grave is dug before the body comes.

Wren stood very still.

She had come here half-hoping to find the town innocent. To find that Fen's snuffing had been the dark's own doing, unlucky, unchosen — a thing that happened, terrible and random, to a boy who liked buttons and was afraid of the corridor to the privy. That would have been a grief she could have carried.

But the name was written before the moths came. She was sure of it. The ink was older than three nights; she pressed her thumb to it and it did not smear. Someone had chosen Fen — had set his name down in the book of the dark's takings — while he was still warm, still hers, still complaining that his boots leaked. Chosen him, and then waited for the wick to go out on schedule. As if the snuffing were not the dark reaching up to take, but the town reaching down to give.

Her grandmother had written her brother's name in a ledger and dated it like an appointment.

Down in the square, the noon bell finished its round of strikes, and in the sudden quiet Wren heard, very clearly, boots on the stone stair.

Not soft boots. Not Perch. A slow, heavy, certain tread, one that had climbed these stairs ten thousand times and did not need to hurry.

Warden Coll.

Wren had a heartbeat and a half to decide, and her body decided for her — she let the great cover fall shut, snuffed the nearest lamp with a pinch that burned her fingers so the room would look darker than it was, and went sideways into the shadow behind the standing shelf, wedging herself into the cold gap between it and the wall. Dust and the mine-smell filled her mouth. She pressed her hand flat over her own chest as if she could hold the warmth of her wick still, keep it from shining out and giving her away, the way you'd cup a candle in the wind.

Coll came in. She heard him more than saw him — the creak of leather, the small dry sound of a man breathing through a broken nose. He stopped by the table. He was a big man and he filled the room with waiting.

"Perch says my lady wanted the offerings book." A pause. She could hear him not believing it. "My lady's in her own house with the boy. My lady sent no one."

Silence. The lamp she'd snuffed still smoked; she could smell it, and prayed he'd read it as a wick guttering low and not a pinched-out flame. His boots turned. One step toward the shelf. Two.

Wren stopped breathing. Through the crack she could see a slice of him — the grey Warden's coat, the ring of keys, the hand hanging by his hip, a hand she'd seen rest on Fen's small shoulder two days ago with what she'd taken for kindness. The cold gap seemed to breathe against her back, and she understood suddenly that the whole wall behind her was the outer wall, and that on the other side of the black stone was the moor, and under the moor the Deepings, and that this house had been built here, above all other places, because it was the nearest dry ground to the top of the shaft.

Coll's hand rose. Reached toward the shelf's edge.

Below, in the square, a pigeon-basket clattered and Perch's voice went up in a wail — one of his wretched birds loose in the vigil-house, wings loud on the stair, the boy blundering after it full of apology. Coll's head turned toward the noise, the way any man's would, annoyed and distracted, and he swore under his breath and went out to deal with it, his boots going down fast.

Wren did not thank Perch's pigeons. She did not have the breath. She came out of the gap on legs that had gone to water and knew she had one moment and no more.

She should have gone. Every sensible thing in her said *go, now, down the back way, out.* Instead she pulled the great cover open one last time — not to the fresh page, but back, deeper, into the brown-inked years, into the hand that had written before Gran's, the round upright hand of the Chief Lamplighter before her. She turned the leaves fast, looking for nothing, looking for the shape of the thing, and a name went by under her thumb and her whole body stopped as if she'd stepped off a step that wasn't there.

She turned back one leaf. Read it again. Read it a third time, because the first two times her mind refused to let the letters mean what they meant.

A dark-tide from long ago, the year Wren had been small enough to still believe the story she'd been told. The year the pumps failed and the low workings drowned and they had held an empty funeral because there had been no body to bury, only water. The flood, they always said. *Lost in the flood.* Poor Nell, lost in the flood, and don't ask your Gran about it, child, it breaks her heart.

Here was the name, in Gran's own younger hand, upright and unhurried, dated to that same dark-tide.

*Nell Cawley.*

And beside it, black and dry and long since finished, the one word the book gave to all its takings.

*Relit.*

No flood had taken her aunt. Wren's grandmother had written the name of her own daughter in this book, and led her down into the dark, and come home, and lit a keeping-flame for a girl who was already given away, and let the whole town go on saying *the flood* for sixteen years.

Somewhere below, Perch was still chasing his pigeon. Wren closed the book very softly, as if it could hear her, and went down the back stair into the daylight with her aunt's name burning a hole through everything she thought she'd known.
