# Chapter 13 — Joining the Light

The bell-rope was crusted with frost and it tore the skin of Wren's palms when she pulled it, and she was glad of the hurt, because the hurt was warm.

She had climbed the little stone cote at the head of the market square with Fen huddled at its foot and Rook standing guard over him, and she had taken the vigil-bell's rope in both cold hands, and now she hauled on it as if she meant to pull the whole long dark down out of the sky. The bell was meant to be rung once an hour, softly, a Warden's clock. Wren rang it like an alarm, like a birth, like the end of something. The iron voice went out across the shuttered roofs and rolled off over the moor and came back off the fells, and rang again, and again, until the frozen air itself seemed to shake.

Shutters cracked. Doors opened a hand's width, throwing thin blades of firelight into the dark. Pale faces. All across Nethercote the town looked out at the one thing it had been taught its whole life never to do — a light lit loud, in the open, calling.

"Nethercote!" Wren's voice came out cracked and small and she made it bigger, made it carry, dragged it up out of the cold place in her chest. "Nethercote, bring your fires out! Bring them to the mine-mouth! Every hearth, every candle, every wick — bring it up the hill and bring it *together!*"

For a moment, nothing. A hundred doors, half-open, and behind them a hundred years of *keep your fire and hold your own.*

Then boots on cobbles, and the Wardens came.

They came out of the scattered vigil-fires in the square, four of them, five, the ones who had not gone down the shaft — and at their head a grey-faced woman Wren half-knew, Morren Cawley's namesake bell in her hand, the deputy who kept the hours. She planted herself at the foot of the cote and threw back her hood.

"Take your hand off that rope." Her voice shook, but not with doubt — with fright at the sheer size of the wrongness Wren was doing. "You'll wake it. Girl, you'll wake the whole dark down there. Keep to your own fire — that's the law. That's the *only* law. Light to light, and none alone; keep your fire, and hold your own —"

"You've been saying it wrong for a hundred years," Wren said.

She let go of the rope. The bell rang itself out, slow, into silence, and into that silence she spoke, and she did not have the strength to shout so she made them lean in to hear, and somehow that was stronger.

"*None alone,*" she said. "You thought it meant every hearth keeps to itself. It doesn't. It never did. It means *no light stands alone.* One flame the dark can snuff — I've been down there tonight, I've watched it do it, I've felt it try to do it to me." She held up her hand, and even in the firelight they could see it: the greyness in her, the low guttering of a Cawley wick gone dim. "That's why it takes the bright ones. That's why it took Nell, and Merron Ashby, and every child you told yourselves the dark *chose.* It didn't choose them. *You gave them.* One at a time, alone, because a flame alone is the only kind it can eat." She let that sit. "But it has never once — not in all your hundred years — faced the whole of us at once. Because you made *sure* of it. That's the whole trick. That's the whole bargain. Keep them apart, and feed it one."

"That is Warden business," the grey woman said, but her voice had thinned. "You don't know — "

"My grandmother is down there right now holding the door shut with her own life." Wren's voice broke and she let it. "So is Warden Coll. So is the first of us, Morren, who's held it alone since before your grandmother's grandmother. *Alone.* Every one of them, alone. I am done with alone."

She looked past the Wardens, then, over their heads, to the doors.

"You've all lost someone to the dark," she said. "Every house. And you weren't even let to grieve them out loud — the dark *chose* them, so you looked away, so you kept your fire and held your own and buried the shame with the body. I'm asking you to stop looking away. Bring your fire up the hill. Not to hold your own. To hold *each other.*"

Silence. Cold. The moan of the dark-tide down in the earth, rising.

Then Rook Ashby stepped out into the open where all of them could see him, and he had his sister's name in his mouth. "Merron Ashby," he said, loud, into the square, the first time anyone in Nethercote had said a hollowed child's name in the open in living memory. "She went hollow last winter and I couldn't hold her alone. Nobody would stand with me. That's the only reason she's gone." He turned a slow circle, meeting eyes. "Who else has a name they were never let to say?"

From the dark at the edge of the square, an old voice: "Tam Sowerby." A keeper, bent, one of the bereaved underground, come up out of hiding into the firelight. She lifted a rushlight in a shaking hand. "My Tam. Nine years this tide."

And then another, from a doorway: "Bess Colley." And a man's low voice: "Little Nan." And the freed keepers came up out of the cellars and the ginnels where the Wardens had scattered them, Rook's people, the ones who had always secretly kept their dead, and each of them carried a light and each of them said a name into the open air, and the saying of the names was itself a kind of fire, catching.

"You will *stop,*" the grey Warden said, but her voice was drowning now.

A door opened wide at the low end of the square. Full light spilled out, warm, unshuttered — a whole hearth's worth, carried out into the night in an iron fire-pot with two bare hands wrapped round the hot metal, and behind it a woman's face Wren would have known in any dark ever made.

Alys. Her mother.

"Alys Cawley, get back inside," the Warden said. "Your own mother-in-law is Chief Lamplighter, you know the — "

"My mother-in-law is dying in that hole for what she did to my Nell and my Tom and near did to my son." Alys's voice did not shake at all. She had been afraid for sixteen years and something in her had finally used the fear all up. She walked out into the middle of the square with her hearth-fire in her hands and the heat of it blistering her palms and she did not put it down. She walked to the foot of the cote, to Fen — living, dripping, blinking up at her — and her whole face came apart, and then she looked at Wren on the steps above and she said, so the whole square heard it:

"Tell me where to bring it."

"The mine-mouth," Wren said. Her throat was too tight for more.

Alys turned and started up the hill with her fire, alone, one woman, and for three steps she was alone.

Then she wasn't.

A door. Another door. A candle first, an old man cupping it against the wind, hurrying to fall in behind her. Then a whole family, a lamp and two rushlights and a child carrying an ember in a warming-pan. Then the Ashbys' cellar-full of keepers with their hidden vigil-flames finally, gloriously unhidden. Firelight bloomed door to door down the slope of Nethercote like something spreading, like something *catching,* and the shuttered black town that Wren had crawled up into an hour ago turned, house by house by house, into a river of moving light climbing the hill toward the Deepings' mouth.

The Wardens fell back before it. What could they do? They had one law, and the law was *apart,* and here was the whole town coming *together,* and there is no wall you can build against your neighbours all deciding at once to stand near you. The grey woman with the bell stood in the tide of light and, after a moment, Wren saw her take a rushlight from a passing girl's hand and turn, and climb with the rest, weeping.

Family by family they came, and family by family the flames drew near, and — this was the thing, this was the forbidden and holy thing — they let the flames *touch.* A candle tilted to a lantern. A rushlight to a fire-pot. Hearth to hearth, wick to wick, the little separate lights of a hundred lonely houses leaning in against one another at the black lip of the mine until they were not a hundred lights any more but one light, one great joined blaze roaring up into the dark-tide sky, and Wren stood in the heart of it with her mother's arm around her and her brother's cold hand in hers and felt, for the first time in her whole life in Nethercote, warm from the outside in.

Her own wick was near guttered — she could feel it, a poor grey coal where a bright thing used to burn, the price of Fen written into her chest for good. But it did not matter now, and that was the whole revelation of it, that was the thing the Wardens had spent a hundred years making sure no one ever learned. Her small failing light did not have to hold the dark alone. It only had to lean, like all the others, into the light beside it. A candle carried to a lantern loses nothing. A hearth shared warms two houses. She had been taught that giving light away emptied you. She stood in a fire built of a whole town giving its light away at once, and no one in it was empty, and the dark had never in its long life seen anything like it.

"Say it right," she told them — told the town, all of it, gathered and burning at the door of the dark. "Say it the way it was meant."

And her mother said it first, and then Rook, and then the keepers, and then the whole hillside, ragged and then together, the old rhyme reforged and made true at last:

"Light to light, and none alone — no fire of ours stands on its own."

Wren turned to the mine-mouth and lifted the joined fire high, and let it pour down into the Deepings — all of it, every wick in Nethercote, a hundred years of hoarded lonely light flung at last into the dark all at once.

Far below, in the drowned heart of the world, the Ashen felt it come — and for the first time in memory, the dark drew back.
