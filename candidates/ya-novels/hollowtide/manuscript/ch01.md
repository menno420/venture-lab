# Chapter 1 — Ash-Moths

The button had rolled somewhere out past the last lamp, and Fen would not come home without it.

"It's brass," he said, as if that settled everything, crouched over the heather with his nose nearly in the dirt. "It's got a bird on it. You can't just leave a bird."

"You can leave a bird," Wren said. "Birds leave each other all the time. It's practically what they're famous for."

But she went with him anyway, out past the edge of the lamplight where the moor began, because that was the whole trouble with being sixteen and having a brother who was nine — you were always following him into places you had the sense to stay out of. The last of the day had gone a bruised, coal-smoke grey behind the chimneys of Nethercote. Below them the town sat low and huddled in its bowl of peat and stone, every roof leaking a thread of smoke, the chapel bell squat and silent, the ropewalk a pale scar off to the west. Behind every shuttered window a keeping-flame would already be lit. It was that time of year.

The dark-tide season. The turn into the long dark, when the days shrank down to a coin of light and the old people said the dark below came up to meet the dark above, and you kept your fire close.

"Found it!" Fen surged up with something pinched between two filthy fingers, triumphant. In the grey light Wren couldn't have told brass from a beetle, but he was grinning, and his grin was worth the cold. He dropped the button into the tin he kept his treasures in — buttons, mostly, and pebbles with holes worn through them, and one small grey bone he swore was a wren's, which he thought was hilarious. He rattled the tin at her. "Now we can go."

"Kind of you."

Down in the town, the first lamplighter came out into the square with a long brass pole, and the first lamp bloomed gold. Then the next. Then the next, the light walking itself along the streets the way it did every night of the dark-tide, and thin and far off Wren heard the lamplighter's rhyme carried up on the wind, the one every child in Nethercote learned before they learned their letters.

*Light to light, and none alone; keep your fire, and hold your own.*

Keep your fire and hold your own. Meaning: bank your hearth, bar your door, don't go carrying your flame into the dark or into anyone else's house, because the dark was hungry and a fire left out was a fire lost. Wren had sing-songed it a thousand times without once thinking it meant anything. It was just what you said, the way you said grace, the way you touched two fingers to your breastbone for luck.

She touched them there now, without deciding to. Under the bone, faint as a held breath, the warmth. Her wick. Everyone in Nethercote had one — a second small heart, an ember you couldn't see but could always feel, a low coal of warmth burning under the sternum from the day you were born. It was your light and your life. Gran said the whole town's wicks together were what kept the dark below asleep, all those little fires burning in all those little houses. Wren had never thought hard about that either.

She thought about it now, a little, only because the cold up here was the wrong kind of cold. She'd been out on the moor after dark a hundred times. This was different — not the honest bite of wind off the peat, but a thin, seeping chill that seemed to come up through the soles of her boots from somewhere far below, and that her wick answered with a low, uneasy flutter, the way a candle leans away from a draught you can't feel on your own skin.

"Fen," she said. "Tin's full. Come on, before Mam sends the whole street looking."

Fen had stopped walking.

He was standing very still a few feet ahead of her on the path, his tin dangling forgotten from one hand, his face turned up and out — not toward home, but away, out across the black shoulder of the moor to where the ground fell and folded toward the old mine-workings. The Deepings. The flooded shafts nobody went near.

"Fen." Something in Wren's chest went tight. "Fen, come on, it's freezing."

He didn't answer. And then Wren heard it too, and understood why he'd gone so still.

A sound was coming up out of the ground. Low, so low it was almost only a feeling — a long, dragging moan, like wind pouring down a shaft, like a door opening somewhere very deep and never finishing. The dark-tide sound. She'd heard the old ones speak of it in the shame-lowered voices they kept for such things, but she had never once heard it herself, and now that she did every hair on her arms stood up and her wick gave a small frightened flare, as if it too had heard and did not like it.

"We're going home," she said. "Now. Come here."

She reached for his hand.

And that was when the first moth landed on him.

She saw it settle on his collar — a small thing, black, its wings the dull soft black of ashes gone cold. Then another, on his sleeve. Then they were coming out of the dark all at once, out of the heather and the low sky, black and silent and unhurried, settling on Fen's shoulders and his hair and the backs of his hands, more of them every heartbeat, and Wren knew what they were because everyone in Nethercote knew, though nobody ever said the word aloud where the failing could hear.

Ash-moths. The sign. The moths gathered on you when your wick was going out.

"No," Wren said, stupidly, to the moths, to the dark, to the whole grey moor. "No, not him, he's fine, he's nine —" and she got both hands on him and pulled him against her, brushing at the moths, which did not fly and did not crush, only shifted under her fingers like flakes of soft cold ash. Fen felt too light in her arms. He turned his face up to her and it was calm, dreadfully calm, a small polite puzzlement on it, as if she'd woken him from something warm.

"It's cold," he said. "Wren, it's gone cold."

"You're all right." Her voice cracked straight down the middle. She pressed her palm flat to his chest, over the breastbone, over the place where his wick should be — and felt it. Felt the warmth there under her hand, his small steady coal that she had felt a hundred times when he'd crawled into her bed frightened of the dark. Felt it flicker.

Felt it gutter.

And then, against her palm, quiet as a candle pinched between two wet fingers, felt it go out.

There was a smell on the wind now, threading up under the peat and the coal-smoke — the smell of wet ash, of cold char, of a fire drowned days ago. It came up off the moor from the direction of the Deepings and it filled the whole world.

Fen sagged. Not falling — just settling, going quiet, the way a room goes quiet when someone turns down the lamp. The moths lifted off him all at once, a soft black exhalation into the dark, and were gone, and he stood in the circle of her arms cold and small and looking up at her with that terrible mild curiosity, as if she were the strange one, as if nothing at all had happened.

"Wren," he said, "why are you crying?"

She didn't remember beginning to run. She only knew that she had him by the hand, that his hand was cold, colder than the night could account for, and that she was hauling him down off the moor toward the lamplight and the smoke and the low bright houses with her breath tearing in her throat and the dark-tide sound sinking away behind them into the ground. His tin bounced against his hip. He came without protest. He came the way you'd follow anyone, if you no longer particularly minded where you went.

The door of their house stood at the end of the lane, and the keeping-flame burned orange in the window, and Wren fell against the door and got it open and pulled him into the warmth and the coal-smoke and the ordinary blessed light, and turned to shout for their mother —

And Gran was already there.

She was standing in the middle of the room in her grey shawl, her face turned to the door as though she had been watching it, as though she had known to be watching it. Idony Cawley, Chief Lamplighter, Wren's grandmother, straight-backed and terrible in the firelight. Her lips were moving. Wren knew that look — Gran counting under her breath, the way she always did, numbers passing like beads through her fingers, wick after wick after wick, keeping her tally of the town's small fires. On her open palm, turned up toward the flame, an old white scar shone.

She was looking at Fen. And as Wren watched, her grandmother's face changed — not with grief, not with shock, but with something worse, something that shut. It closed over from the eyes down like a door swinging to on a cold room, quiet and final, latched from the inside.

She had stopped counting.

"Gran," Wren said. "Gran, help him, his wick — it went out, I felt it, you have to —"

"I know, child." Idony's voice was very level. She did not come to Fen. She did not kneel to him or take his cold hands or gather him up. She only looked at him the way you'd look at a sum already worked, an answer you'd known before you asked.

"By the old law," Gran said, "Fen had three nights."

Had. Not has.

The fire cracked in the grate. Outside, far off and low beneath the town, the dark-tide sound went on and on, patient as water, and Fen stood cold in the doorway and did not shiver at all.
