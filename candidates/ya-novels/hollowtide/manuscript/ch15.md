# Chapter 15 — What the Light Cost

Morning came to Nethercote and did not know how to be looked at.

Wren sat on the cold lip of the Deepings' mouth with a blanket somebody had put round her shoulders and watched the town discover, in the flat grey light, what it had spent. There was no cheering. She had half-thought there might be — that people saved from a dark that had hunted them for centuries might make some sound of gladness. But the ones who came up out of that night stood about the moor in ones and twos with their guttered lanterns hanging from their hands, and looked at each other, and looked away, and did not know where to put their eyes now that there was nothing left to hide.

The tally came in slowly, the way bad tallies do.

Gran was gone. Wren had carried Fen up and then gone back down with Rook and two of the freed keepers to bring the bodies out of the dark, because she would not leave her grandmother in the place that had taken so much from her, and they had laid Idony Cawley on the heather with her sharing-scar turned up to the sky. Morren they could not bring; there had been nothing left of the First Lamplighter but a drift of pale ash against a rotted prop, three hundred years old at last, and they had left her where she'd earned her rest.

Old Mabb was gone. She had been taken in the raid two nights back and not seen since; they found her in the vigil-house cellar where the Wardens had held her, and her old heart had simply stopped in the cold and the fear, and no light had come in time. Wren had liked her. She had been the one to say it first — that the Ashen had never faced the whole town at once because the Wardens kept everyone apart. She had planted the answer and not lived to see it grow. Wren made herself go and look at her, and say thank you where no one could hear, because someone should.

A keeper named Sennet had gone into the black water on the long climb, holding the rear so the others could pass, and had not come up. Warden Coll had gone the same way and for the same reason — Coll, who had believed to the end that the bargain saved the town, and who had chosen, when the water rose, to spend himself buying other people time. Wren did not know what to do with grief for a man who had helped write Fen's name in the ledger. She held both things at once. She was learning that you could.

And there were the hollowed.

This was the hardest part, and the town came to it slowly. When the joined light held the Ashen, some of the already-taken did not come back — the ones snuffed too long ago, given down in tithes years and decades gone, absorbed too deep into the dark to be called out of it. Nell did not come back. Merron, Rook's sister, did not come back. The light had not been a resurrection. It had only stopped the dark from taking more, and closed the door on the ones already through it, and that was a grief with no bottom to it, and the families who'd hoped stood at the Deepings' mouth a long time before they let themselves believe it.

A few did come up. Three of the recently-snuffed, the ones still within their three nights, walked up out of the dark on their own feet when the light poured down — dazed, blinking, cold in places that would not warm. A girl called Tamsin who had gone hollow a week past, and did not remember the moor now, and would flinch from the dark all her life. An old man. A boy younger than Fen who would not speak. They came back changed. Cold in patches, quiet where they'd been loud, carrying a chill in the chest that the keepers said might never quite go. But they came back, and their people wept over them, and that was something the town had never once been allowed before.

Fen came back.

Wren watched him all that first morning like she was afraid he'd go out again if she blinked. He sat on the floor of their cottage in a square of thin grey sun with his tin of small things tipped out in front of him — buttons, a bird's skull, three moor-pebbles, the brass button she'd carried down into the dark as a thread to him and pressed back into his palm at the top of the stair. He was sorting them. He always sorted them. Bone with bone, brass with brass, the pebbles by size, muttering the private order of them under his breath the way he'd done since he was four.

He was a little cold. When she took his hand it did not warm hers the way it used to; there was a coolness in him now that the keepers said some of the come-back children kept. But he was ordinary. He argued about breakfast. He complained that his good pebble had a chip. He was nine years old and hers and warm enough, and Wren sat on the floor beside him and sorted buttons and did not cry where he could see, and that was its own kind of vigil.

Her own cost she carried quiet.

She was dimmed. She had known it in the dark and she knew it worse in daylight — the place under her sternum where her Cawley wick had burned so bright, brightest of all the old line, was low now and would stay low. She had spent too much of it into Fen; you did not get that back. There was a scar of cold in her chest where the fire had been, a still cool place, a quiet where there had been a roar. She would not go hollow — the joined light had caught her before the frost took the whole of her — but she would never burn the way she had. She would be cold in the mornings all her life. She would feel the dark-tide season in her bones before anyone else did. It was a small permanent winter, set down in the middle of her, and it was the price of her brother and she would not have unpaid it, but it was a price, and she let herself feel it honestly.

Alys held her that afternoon a long time without words. Her mother had chosen her over the Wardens at the mine-mouth, had brought her own hearth-fire to the joining when the law said keep to your own; but her mother had also spent years turning away from the truth of Nell, and they both knew it, and the holding was an apology going both directions.

"I should have told you," Alys said finally, into Wren's hair. "About your aunt. About what they did. I knew and I let myself not know."

"Everyone did," Wren said. "That was the whole trick of it. Everyone knew and no one was allowed to say." She pulled back and looked at her mother in the grey light. "That's over now. That's the one thing we bought. We're allowed to say it now."

And in the evening, the town said it.

No one organised it exactly. It simply happened, the way the joining had happened, family by family drawn out of habit toward the place they'd spent their whole lives being taught to shun. They came to the Deepings' mouth as the light failed — not the dark-tide, that was broken, but ordinary dusk — and they brought their lanterns, and this time they did not keep to their own scattered fires. They set the lights together in a long line along the lip of the mine, the way they had learned to the night before, and they stood, and for a while no one knew how to begin because no one in Nethercote had grieved out loud in three generations. Shame had eaten the words.

It was Rook who started it. He stood at the mouth of the dark that had taken his sister and he said, into the quiet, in a rough flat voice: "Merron Ashby." Just the name. Then, because it had to be said and he was the one brave enough: "The dark didn't choose her. We gave her. I'm sorry, Merron."

The silence after held a hundred years of held breath.

Then an old woman down the line said her son's name. And a man said his wife's. And it went along the line of lights slow at first and then not slow, name after name after name, all the takings the ledger had hidden and the ones it hadn't bothered to write, spoken aloud into the dark for the first time — not as shame, not as *the dark chose them*, but as grief, plain and owned and shared.

When it came to her, Wren stood with Fen's cold hand in hers and Alys at her shoulder, and she said the two names she had come up out of the dark carrying.

"Nell Cawley," she said. Her aunt, given down by her own mother, lost in a flood that had never happened. "You weren't lost. We gave you, and I'm sorry, and I know your name."

The light along the mine-mouth burned steady.

"Idony Cawley," Wren said, and her voice cracked on it and she let it. Gran. Terrible and loving and wrong for fifty years and, at the very end, in the dark, right. "She gave the last of herself to hold the door. She died free of it. Gran." She breathed. "Thank you."

She said the third name too, because she'd promised. "Morren Cawley. The first of us. She was afraid the whole time and she did it anyway. She held it three hundred years so we didn't have to, and she asked us to say her name, so — Morren."

Along the whole line of joined lights, the town of Nethercote spoke its dead into the dark, out loud, unashamed, the first open grieving in living memory. And the Deepings took the names and stayed quiet, and gave nothing back, and asked for nothing, and that — Wren understood, standing cold and dimmed and whole between her mother and her brother — that was what the light had actually bought. Not the dead returned. Only the right to mourn them in the open, together, and never feed the dark another child in secret again.

It was not enough. It was everything they had. She held her brother's cool hand and let the tears come where the whole town could see, and no one looked away.
