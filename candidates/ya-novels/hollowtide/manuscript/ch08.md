# Chapter 8 — Down the Deepings

The dark-tide came up over Nethercote the way water comes up in a sinking boat — not all at once, but from below, and everywhere, and with no place left dry.

Wren felt it before she heard it, a pressure behind the breastbone, a leaning of the whole night toward the moor. Then the sound arrived, low and endless, wind down a shaft that had no bottom and no wind in it, and one by one the last unshuttered windows of the town went dark. She stood in the black between two peat-stacks and watched Nethercote fold itself away. Board over board. Bar over bar. In each house a single hearth-flame kept alone behind its own door, a hundred little lights that never touched, and the custom she'd run past all her life she now saw whole: this was the shape of the bargain made visible. A town taught to burn apart.

And down the square, from the tall doors of the vigil-house, the Wardens came out in procession.

She pressed flat to the stone and watched them pass at the head of the lane — Warden Coll first with the great lantern, and behind him the others in their dark coats, unhurried, chanting the rhyme under their breath so it came to her in pieces. *Light to light, and none alone; keep your fire, and hold your own.* And in the middle of them, small between two grown men, walking because they led him and not because he wished to go anywhere at all, was Fen.

Even at that distance she knew the wrongness of him. Her brother had never in his life walked so quiet. He'd have been three steps ahead or ten behind, stooping for a bright pebble, a bone, a button. Now he went where the hand on his shoulder sent him, his head turned toward the moor like a compass toward its north, and around him — she had to bite down not to make a sound — around him the air was moving. Ash-moths. A slow black drift of them, thick as the omen ever came, settling on his collar and his hair and lifting and settling again, the visible sign that his wick was all but out.

They were taking him to the mine-mouth to give him down. And if she went after the procession she'd be seen and caught and it would all end in the square.

So she didn't. She went the other way.

Rook had told her once, in the ropewalk cellar, that the old workings had more than one throat. The Wardens used the mine-mouth on the moor, the ceremonial way, wide and stepped. But at the ropewalk end there was an old adit, an air-drift the diggers had cut generations back, half-choked with fallen stone and long forbidden — and it let, if you could get through it, onto the head of the spiral stair. She'd bring no procession and no chant. She'd bring a lantern, a keeping-flame stolen from her own hearth and shielded in a horn case, and she'd bring the one thing that was only Fen's.

She took the brass button out of her pocket and closed her hand round it. He'd lost it on the moor the night his wick snuffed — the night she'd looked away at the wrong moment, the moment she would spend the rest of her life not having back. She'd found it after, in the peat, and kept it. It was warm from her pocket. It was the plainest, most ordinary thing in the world, and it was a thread, and she was going to follow it all the way down and tie it round her brother and haul him back into the light.

The adit was where Rook said. She hauled the rotten boards away and squeezed through the gap into cold that met her like a wall.

It was not the cold of a winter night. Winter cold was clean. This was old and wet and it had a smell in it — wet ash, coal-damp, the reek of a fire long drowned — and it did not just chill her skin. It reached. She'd gone ten steps down the sloping drift, her lantern throwing its small yellow ball ahead, when she understood what it reached for. The warmth under her breastbone, her wick, her bright steady Cawley coal — the cold *leaned* on it, the way the dark-tide leaned on the town, and she felt her own light give back a fraction, a guttering, before she gritted her teeth and held it. *Find your own light first. Feel it clear.* She found it. She held it clear. She went on.

The drift met the stair.

She'd known there'd be a stair. She had not known it would be like this. It spiralled down out of the reach of her lantern, cut straight into the living rock, treads worn to shallow dishes by however many feet had gone down them and never come up, and it turned round a central well of nothing, and out of the nothing came the sound — the dark-tide, close now, the endless shaftwind — and under it, further down, a slow black lapping. Water. There was water below, and it was rising to meet her.

She went down.

The lantern was the whole world. Beyond its edge the dark was not empty; it was full, the way deep water is full, pressing. The cold came up the stair to meet her step for step and it worked at the wick the whole way, patient, and she found she was talking to keep it clear — not aloud, or not much, just naming warm things under her breath the way Gran counted wicks. *The hearth. The keeping-flame. Fen's hands before they went cold. The button. The button. Warm from my pocket.* The horn-shielded flame threw her shadow wheeling round and round the well, huge and then small and then huge, and once she thought the shadow moved when she hadn't, and she did not let herself think about that again.

The ash-moths began about halfway down.

First one, blundering up out of the dark to bat at the lantern-horn. Then a dozen. Then they were a soft black storm around the light, so thick she had to keep a hand cupped over the flame to keep them off it, and their small dry bodies brushed her face and caught in her lashes and she thought, *this is what settled on Fen, this is his omen, this is the air the dark breathes out,* and she went down through them with her jaw set and the button clenched till its edge bit her palm.

The water came sooner than she wanted.

Her boot went in to the ankle before she saw it — the stair simply continued down into black water, level and still and giving back her lantern in a shivering yellow smear. Flooded, from here on. The old mine, drowned. The cold of it climbed her leg and gripped, and her wick shrank from it so hard she gasped and had to stop, one hand flat to her own chest, hauling her light back up from wherever it had tried to flee. She stood on the last dry tread with the black water at her shin and understood that going on meant going *into* it, and that the cold in the water would go for her wick the way nothing yet had.

She thought about Rook, held against a wall, saying *don't you dare stop for me.* She thought about her mother's hands. She thought about Fen walking quiet between two men with the moths in his hair.

She stepped down into the water.

It took her to the waist and it was worse than she'd braced for, a cold that was almost a sound, and her light dropped low and guttered and for one plunging instant she felt the hollow reach right into the middle of her — that emptied, hungry, quiet feeling she'd watched come over her brother — and she nearly went under with the shock of it. But she got her palm to her sternum in the black and she *held*, she poured her own want back into her own wick the way Rook had taught her to pour it into another, and the flame steadied, and the coal in her chest burned up bright again and pushed the cold back a hand's breadth. Not far. But far enough to wade.

She waded. The stair continued under the water, she could feel the drowned treads with her feet, and she went down them into the black holding the lantern high over the surface, following the only thread she had, and the moths thinned as she got below them and the dark-tide sound got into her bones, and there was nothing in the whole flooded world but her small yellow light and the cold and the button in her fist and the going-down.

She lost count of the treads. Time went strange in the dark. She might have been an hour going down; she might have been longer. The water rose to her chest and stayed there, and the ceiling of the working dropped close over her head, dripping, and she bent under it and kept on, and told herself the whole time there would be nothing at the bottom but water and cold and her brother somewhere off through the drowned dark where she would find him.

She was wrong about the nothing.

She came round the last turn of the well into a wider dark, a drowned cavern where her lantern couldn't find the far wall, and she stopped dead in the water because there — far off, low across the black surface, where the deepest part of the Deepings should have held nothing but the sleeping cold — there was a light.

It was small. It was steady. It was the yellow of a held flame, a lantern or a wick burning honest against the dark, and it had no business existing in the drowned heart of a dead mine leagues below the town, and it did not flicker when the dark-tide breathed. It simply burned, far below her and further in, the way a thing burns that has been burning a very long time.

Wren stood to her chest in black water with her own flame shaking in her fist and stared at the light that shouldn't be there.

And out of the dark around it, thin and dry and worn to almost nothing, unmistakably a voice, and it spoke to her across all that black water in a tone of terrible tiredness, as if it had been waiting for her a great deal longer than she had been alive.

"Wren," it said. "Wren Cawley. You came down after all."
