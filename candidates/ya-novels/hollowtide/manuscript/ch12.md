# Chapter 12 — The Long Climb

The stair went up forever, and the water came up faster.

Wren had Fen's arm slung over her shoulders and one hand fisted in the back of his shirt, and she was climbing on legs that no longer felt like hers. He was heavier than a nine-year-old ought to be — heavier the way wet rope was heavy, the way a thing that could not help you hold itself was heavy. Behind them, below them, the Deepings breathed. The cold came up the spiral in a slow tide, black water lapping two steps under her heels and then one, and with it came the sound she had learned to hate — the low, hollow wind-down-a-shaft moan of the dark-tide, and under it a hunger with no throat to make it.

"Keep your feet," she said, to Fen, to herself. "Up. One more. That's it."

Fen's lips moved. No sound. His wick was in him again — she had put it there, some of hers, palm to his sternum, the burn of it still a raw coal in her own chest — but it was a candle in a gale, and she could feel how little of it there was. She could feel, too, the place in her own body where the warmth used to sit steady under the breastbone, and how it sat there now like an ember someone had breathed on wrong. Cold crept out from it in thin threads. Hollowing. She knew the word from the inside now.

Ash-moths came off the water in a soft black storm, blundering into her cheeks, her lashes, her open mouth. She spat and climbed.

"Wren." A shape swung down out of the dark above her — she nearly struck at it before she knew the voice. "Wren, it's me, it's me —"

Rook. Rook Ashby, of all the impossible things, his face slick and streaked, a raw split over one eyebrow crusted dark. He must have run every step of the Deepings on the strength of nothing but stubbornness. He got a look at Fen hanging off her and didn't waste breath on questions. He ducked under Fen's other arm and took the weight, and Wren's whole left side sang with relief.

"They raided the meeting," he said, hauling. "Coll's men. I got out a window. Followed the Wardens down the main shaft and then — I heard the water and I came the low way." He glanced at her, at whatever her face was doing. "You look half-snuffed."

"I shared," she said.

His jaw went tight. He didn't say the thing Gran had said, two hollows instead of one. He just took more of Fen's weight, and they climbed together, matched, and it was faster. It was faster and it was still not fast enough.

The water found her ankle. Ice — not the honest cold of moor-water but a cold that reached, that wanted, that knew there was a low flame in her to put out. She kicked free and drove upward and it came again, another step gained by the tide before they'd gained two.

"It's rising with us," Rook said. "It's herding us."

"Then we climb faster than it herds."

Below, far down the well of the stair, there was light — small, wrong, defiant. Two lights. Wren looked back once and saw them: Gran's lantern and, beyond it, Morren's single failing flame that had burned in the dark since before Nethercote had a name. The two old women stood in the drowned mouth of the relighting hollow with the black rising around their knees, and they were not climbing. They had set themselves like stones in a millrace, and the dark broke against them and slowed — a hand's breadth of slowness, a heartbeat bought and handed up the stair to Wren.

"Gran," Wren shouted down. "Gran, come up, come — "

"Go, girl." The voice came up thin and terrible and almost kind. "You know what you have to bring me. Go and bring it."

Wren went.

They came to the drowned landing where the stair doubled back, and here the old timbers had rotted to sponge and the water stood waist-deep and utterly still, which was worse than moving water, because still water in the Deepings was the Ashen paying attention. Rook went first with Fen half-floated between them, and Wren followed with her lantern lifted over her head, and the flame in it guttered and shrank as if something enormous had leaned close to breathe on it.

Halfway across, Fen slipped under.

He went without a splash — that was the horror of it, no thrash, no cry, just a boy who forgot he was a body sliding down into the black. Wren dropped the lantern's arm and plunged both hands after him and caught his collar, his hair, his cold small wrist, and hauled, and the water did not want to give him back. It held. For one endless second it held him against a pull she could not see, and she thought, with a calm that frightened her, *it will have him or it will have me, and I will not let go,* and then Rook had his other arm and they broke him up out of the water gasping — Fen actually gasping, a live sound, the best sound she had ever heard.

"Wren," he said. Her name. First word in three nights. "It's cold."

"I know, little wick. I know. We're going up to the fire."

She did not tell him there was no fire. Not yet. She would make one. She would make the whole town a fire if she had to knock on every shuttered door on the moor with her bare and hollowing hands.

They reached the upper stair where it turned to hewn rock and old iron rungs, and here they met Warden Coll.

He was coming down. That was the thing Wren would remember — he was coming down, against the tide, with a Warden's lantern in his fist and his hard dutiful face set, and she braced for him to be the last wall between her and the surface. Instead he looked at Fen — living, dripping, saying his sister's name — and something in the man's face broke quietly, the way ice breaks under snow, from underneath.

"The others turned back," Coll said. "When the water rose. They said the bargain was spoiled. They ran." He said it as if it were happening to someone else. "I served the bargain forty years. I told myself it saved us." He looked past her, down the well, at the two small lights holding in the flood. "It saved us the way a leech saves you. Bleed a little every winter and call it health."

"Then help me get him up," Wren said.

"I'll do better." He set his lantern in her free hand, folding her cold fingers around it, and his own hand was warm — he still had his wick, all of it, forty years' worth. "The landing below won't hold the water back much longer. Somebody has to keep the low door shut while your grandmother does what she's going to do. It only wants a body in the gap." He almost smiled. "I've the shoulders for a door."

"Coll — "

"Go up, Cawley." Not unkind. "Ring the bell. Do the thing we spent our lives making sure nobody would ever do." He was already turning, already going down into the cold with his empty hands, toward the still water and the leaning dark and the two old women who would not run. "Somebody ought to."

Wren did not have breath to argue and would not have won. She climbed. Behind and below she heard the water surge and heard a man set his shoulders against it, and she did not look back, because looking back was the sin Nethercote had built its whole cold church on, and she was done with it. She climbed with Fen and Rook and two dead men's lanterns, and she did not look back, and that was how she honored him.

The cold in her chest spread. She fed the climb with her own low wick and felt it dim another shade, and she thought of nothing, on purpose, but the next rung. There was a game to it, a grim arithmetic she could not stop doing — how much light was left to her, how many rungs it would buy, whether there would be any of her ember left at the top or only the cold. Every step she took, the ember answered a little slower, like a fire banked for the night, like a person going quietly to sleep in the snow. She knew that feeling now from the inside too. She named the rungs to keep from listening to it. Iron. Iron. Rotten wood. Iron.

"Nearly," Rook lied, beside her, and she loved him for the lie, and climbed.

Ash-moths thinned. The moan of the dark-tide changed pitch, and then — impossibly, blessedly — there was air that did not smell of wet ash but of peat-smoke and moor-cold and rain. The Deepings' mouth. The old adit, the black stone lip of it, and above it the low iron sky of Nethercote at the turn of the long dark.

Wren came up out of the earth on her hands and knees, dragging her brother into the night, and Rook came up beside her, and for a moment the three of them simply lay in the wet heather and breathed like things newly born, which was near enough to true.

Then Wren lifted her head and looked at the town, and her heart, low and cold as it was, went colder.

Nethercote was out there in the dark-tide — but shut. Every house on the slope stood black-shuttered behind its own lone door, and behind each door, she knew, a single family crouched over a single hearth, keeping its own fire, holding its own, exactly as the rhyme had taught them for a hundred hollowtides. Down in the market square the Wardens' vigil-fires burned — but scattered, one to a corner, deliberate, apart, each ringed by its own small watch, no two flames near enough to touch. A whole town full of light, and every light alone. A hundred candles that had never once been let to become a fire.

Below her feet, through the stone, she felt the Ashen turn its slow attention upward toward all that separate, lonely warmth, the way a hungry thing turns toward a laid table.

"We have to bring it together," she whispered. Her breath smoked. "All of it. Or he doesn't stay. None of them stay."

The vigil-bell hung in its little stone cote at the top of the square, cold and silent, rung only to mark the Wardens' hours. Wren got her feet under her, and Fen's hand in hers, and started down the slope toward it.
