# Chapter 2 — The Keeping-Flame

Morning came grey and grudging, the way all mornings came now, and found Wren on her knees at the hearth building the keeping-flame.

You did it a certain way. Gran had shown her before dawn without softness, her scarred hand guiding Wren's: peat at the base, then coal, then a curl of dry heather at the heart of it, and you lit the heather from the old flame, never a fresh match, so the fire had a memory in it. A keeping-flame was not for warmth, or not only. It was a vigil. It was the family's own fire pledged against a hollowing loved one's cold, and if you kept it burning day and night, close as you dared, it slowed the going. It bought a night, maybe two. It did not turn anything back. Gran had been very clear on that, in the grey before the light, her voice like a hand pressed flat over Wren's mouth: *It does not save him. Nothing saves him. It only makes the leaving slower.*

Wren blew on the heather until it caught, and the flame stood up gold, and she stayed there on her knees longer than she needed to, watching it, because the fire was doing something and she could not.

Fen sat where they had put him, in the good chair pulled close to the grate, wrapped in three blankets and their father's old coat, and he was cold. Cold to look at, even. He had the blankets pulled up but his hands lay outside them, palms turned toward the flame, reaching for it the way a plant reaches at a window. His fingers, when Wren had held them, were like river-stones. He was quieter than she had ever known him, and Fen had never in his life been quiet — Fen narrated. Fen had opinions about everything, birds especially. Now he watched the fire with a soft, drifting attention and said almost nothing, and every little while his head turned, slow and unbothered, toward the window and the moor beyond it, as if someone out there had said his name too low for anyone else to hear.

"Fen." Wren pushed his tin of treasures into his lap. "Show me the bird button. The one we froze half to death for."

He looked down at the tin. For a moment there was nothing, and Wren's heart dropped into her boots. Then his cold fingers moved among the pebbles and buttons, and he found the brass one, and turned it to the light. "It's a wren," he said. "Same as you." A ghost of the old grin crossed his face and was gone. "That's why I couldn't leave it."

She hadn't known that. She'd thought it was just a bird. She had to look hard at the flame for a moment.

Their mother had not come down.

Alys had gone up in the night when Gran told her, and had not come down, and Wren could hear her overhead moving from room to room without purpose, the floorboards telling on her. When she did come, mid-morning, she came in a great false brightness, a scarf tied over her hair, her sleeves pushed up as if for a day of ordinary work.

"Right," Alys said. "Broth. He wants feeding up, that's all, he's chilled through — you shouldn't have had him out on the moor, Wren, you know better —" and she banged the pot onto the hook and busied her hands and did not once look directly at the boy in the chair. She talked over him and around him. She asked him if he wanted bread with it. She told him the Trewins' hens had stopped laying. She kept a wall of small warm words up between herself and the thing in the room, and behind the wall her eyes were wide and dry and terrified, and Wren understood that her mother had decided, sometime in the night, that if she simply did not say it, it would not be so.

"Mam," Wren said quietly. "His wick's out. I felt it go."

"Don't." Alys's voice snapped like a stick. "Don't you say that. He's cold, is all. He'll come right by the fire." She stirred the broth that wasn't ready. "The dark doesn't take a Cawley child in his own kitchen with his mam right here. It doesn't. So we won't be talking that way in this house."

Wren let it be. There was nowhere to go with it. She took the water bucket out to the pump instead, to have somewhere to put her hands.

That was where she learned how it would be.

Mrs. Trewin was at the pump. She had known Wren since Wren was smaller than Fen; she had pressed apples on them at the market and asked after their mother every Sunday. And when she saw Wren coming with the bucket she went still, and then she gathered her shawl and turned her face away — deliberately, carefully away, the way you'd turn from something you'd been raised not to look at — and she left the pump with her own bucket half full rather than stand beside a Cawley whose brother had been chosen by the dark. Down the lane, old Sennen on his step did the same, folding his newspaper and going in and shutting his door. A shutter closed somewhere. Then another.

By noon it was all through Nethercote. Wren felt it like weather. Nobody said a cruel word — that was the worst of it. Nobody said anything. They only looked away, all of them, every door in the town pulling its own fire a little closer and turning its own shoulder, because a hollowing was a shame, and shame was catching, and everyone knew the rule they had all been raised on. *Keep your fire, and hold your own.* The dark chose Fen. That was between the Cawleys and the dark. You did not carry your light into another house's grief; you kept to your own hearth and let the dark take its own, and you were quietly, shamefully glad it had not come for yours.

Wren went back inside and shut the door on all of them and did not trust herself to speak.

She set the bucket down too hard. Fen was still in the chair, still reaching his cold hands at the flame, and she went and knelt by him and chafed his fingers between her palms the way you would a frostbitten thing, trying to rub some of her own warmth into them. His skin took the heat and gave nothing back. Under her hands his fingers stayed the temperature of the pump-water, of stones at the bottom of a stream, and where she pressed her thumb into his palm she thought she could feel, very faintly, the cold trying to climb into her instead — a thin, patient, drawing chill, moving the wrong way. She let go of him. She didn't mean to. Her body did it for her, the way you snatch back from a stove, and she hated herself for it before her hands had even finished the flinching.

"Sorry," she whispered. "Fen, I'm sorry."

He only smiled at her, mild and far away, and turned back to the window.

The Wardens came in the afternoon.

Two of them, in the lamplighters' grey, and Gran at their head, and behind Gran the big silent shape of Warden Coll with his hard, patient face. They filled the small room. They stood over Fen's chair and looked down at him with a kind of professional gentleness that turned Wren's stomach, and Gran laid her scarred hand on the boy's cold hair, and Fen tipped his head toward the touch like a cat, hungry for the warmth of a living hand.

"He's peaceful," Gran said, to Alys, and it was meant as comfort. "You see? There's no pain in it. There never is, once the wick's gone. It's the fighting that hurts them." She smoothed Fen's hair. "Keep the flame lit. Keep him warm. Don't let him wander at night — they want to walk, toward the end, and it frightens the neighbours. And when it's time —" her voice did not change at all — "let the dark take its own. It's the oldest law there is. It's mercy, Alys. Grieve him gentle and let him go."

"Yes," Alys whispered, staring at the floor. "Yes. Thank you."

Coll said nothing. He only watched Fen with those flat, dutiful eyes, and once he looked at the window and the moor beyond it, and Wren had the sudden cold conviction that he was measuring something. Judging the distance. Reading a clock only he could see.

"There's nothing to be done," Gran said again, more softly, and this time she looked at Wren when she said it, straight at her, a warning folded inside the words. "Nothing, child. Do you hear me? Better children than Fen have been let go. Don't make it worse by hoping."

Then they went, the grey coats, out into the failing light, and Gran with them, and the door shut, and the keeping-flame threw its small gold light up the wall, and Fen turned his head to look out at the moor.

Wren stood in the middle of the quiet room with her fists shut so hard her nails bit, and something turned over in her that was not grief.

*Let the dark take its own.* Everyone said it. Gran, the Wardens, Mrs. Trewin turning at the pump, her own mother whispering it into the broth. The whole town lined up and looking away, so practiced at it, so sure — as if this had all happened before, over and over, as if there were a shape to it they had all long since agreed not to look at.

Nothing to be done. Wren didn't believe that. She refused it the way you'd refuse to step off a cliff just because someone told you the ground was down there and soft.

Somebody, somewhere, had once tried to look. She was certain of it. And then she remembered who.

Rook Ashby. The Ashby boy from the edge of town, out by the ropewalk, the one whose sister Merron had gone hollow last winter and walked out onto the moor and never come back. The one everyone said had gone strange after — who'd stood up in the middle of the chapel and asked the Wardens straight out where the hollowed *went*, who counted things, who wrote things down, who asked and asked until people crossed the street to be spared the asking. Everyone said he was bitter. Everyone said he was trouble. Everyone looked away from Rook Ashby too.

Wren looked at her cold, quiet brother turning his face to the dark, and she thought: *good.*

She banked the keeping-flame so it would hold, and told her mother she was going for more coal, and pulled her coat on, and went out to find the boy who wouldn't stop asking questions.
