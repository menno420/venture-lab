# Chapter 16 — The Vigil We Keep

Weeks on, the doors of Nethercote stood open, and Wren had almost stopped finding it strange.

It had been the strangest thing of all, at first — stranger than the joined light, stranger than the quiet where the dark-tide used to moan. All her life the town had been a scatter of shut doors and lone hearths, each family walled in with its own small fire and its own held tongue, because that was the law and the law was the rhyme: *keep your fire, and hold your own.* Now, as the long dark came round again and the nights drew cold, the people of Nethercote pushed their hearths toward one another instead. They propped their doors. They carried chairs into the market square. Where there had been a hundred separate guarded flames there was one long shared warmth, and the town sat in it together and did not go out.

They kept a vigil now, in place of the tithe.

It was a simple thing, once you stopped being afraid of it. Each night of the dark-tide season, a few families took the watch at the Deepings' mouth — not to give the dark anything, only to keep a joined light burning over its sleep, so it never woke hungry and alone-fed again. The weight Morren had carried by herself for three centuries was spread so thin across so many hands that no one felt it as weight at all. It was a night's work, a few times a season, shared out. A watch. Wren had heard an old keeper call it that and the word had stuck: *the watch we keep.* No child was ever chosen for it. That was the whole point of it. That was the thing they had gone down into the dark to buy.

Tonight it was Wren's watch, and Rook's, and she was glad of the company.

They sat together at the mine-mouth with the joined lanterns set in their long line along the lip, the same as the first night, and the dark below lay quiet under the light. Rook had brought his tally-book out of old habit, but there was nothing to tally anymore — no snuffings, no clusters, no schedule hidden in the deaths — and after a while he'd stopped opening it and just held it, the way you hold a thing that used to be your whole reason for getting up. His sister was still gone. That did not mend. But he came to the watch every time it was offered him, and Wren thought she understood: he was keeping, now, the light he'd failed to keep for Merron, and it was late, and it counted anyway.

"They finished the stones," he said, and nodded down the shaft.

They had. It had taken the town three weeks of arguing about it — whether to carve the names at all, whether to leave the dark unmarked and try to forget it, the way they'd forgotten everything for so long. But forgetting was the old way, the Warden way, and they were done with it. So they had set stones at the Deepings' mouth, a long low wall of them, and cut into the stones every name the ledger had hidden. The founders first: Morren Cawley, who went down and could not die. Then the tithed children, all of them, decade on decade, every wick given to the dark in secret — Nell Cawley's name among them, and Merron Ashby's, and Tamsin's little brother who had not come back, cut deep so the moor-weather would take a long time to wear them away. Not hidden. Not shamed. Remembered.

Wren ran her cold fingers over Gran's name where they'd cut it at the end of the line, with the founders rather than the takings, because Idony Cawley had ended as one of the ones who held the door. She did not know yet how she felt about her grandmother, and she thought perhaps she never would land on one feeling, and she'd decided that was allowed. You could love someone and be furious at them and grieve them, all at once, in the cold place where your wick used to burn bright. She did it every day.

Fen was down in the square with Alys and the others, sorting somebody's spare buttons into the town's shared button-tin, entirely happy, entirely ordinary, a little cold in the hands. She could see him from up here — a small dark shape in the long warm light. She checked on him the way you check a step you know is there. He was there. He was warm enough. He was hers.

Then, below in the town, the vigil-bell rang the turn of the night, and Nethercote spoke the rhyme.

They spoke it together now, the whole town at once, the way you'd say grace — the children learning it new, the old ones unlearning what it used to mean. Wren had grown up with the true old words, the Warden words, the ones that had kept everyone apart and fed the dark one child at a time: *Light to light, and none alone; keep your fire, and hold your own.* She would hear those words in her bones till she died.

But that was not what the town said tonight.

The voices came up to her from the square, and joined the voices of the watch at the mine-mouth, and Wren said it with them, cold-chested and dimmed and unafraid:

*"Light to light, and none alone;*
*no fire of ours stands on its own."*

The same rhyme, reforged. *None alone* meaning, at last, what it should always have meant — not keep to your own hearth, but no light stands by itself. No child given down in the dark for the rest to keep warm. No fire, ever again, left to burn alone until the dark could snuff it.

The words went out over the quiet Deepings and down into the sleeping dark and did not come back changed.

Wren sat with the whole of it a moment — the open doors below, the long shared warmth, the stones with the names cut deep, her brother a small warm shape in the light, Rook beside her holding a tally he'd never need to write in again. And she turned her attention, the way she had learned to do carefully, to the cold place under her sternum where her wick had once burned so bright.

It was small now. It would always be small. There was a still winter set in the middle of her where the roar had been, and on cold mornings she felt the dark-tide season before anyone else in the town, and she had made her peace that she always would. That was what her brother had cost, and she had paid it on purpose, and she did not want it back.

But it was warm.

Small, and low, and steady — and joined. She could feel the others now, if she went still and listened for it the way Gran used to count wicks under her breath: her mother's fire, and Fen's cool small one, and Rook's, and the long line of the watch, and every hearth in the open town below, all of them tied into one unbroken light that the dark could not put out because it would not stand alone. Her own small flame was a thread in it. Less than it had been. Joined to a thousand others. And for the first time in her whole life, sitting at the mouth of the dark that had taken her aunt and her grandmother and nearly her brother and a part of her own heart, Wren Cawley was not afraid of the dark-tide coming.

Something pale lifted off the stones at her feet.

She went still. An ash-moth — one, small and black-grey, come up out of the quiet Deepings on the cold air. Once the sight of it had meant a wick was failing, a child was going out, the dark was choosing. Once she'd have gone cold at it to the marrow.

It fluttered up past her face, aimless and slow, no swarm behind it, no cold in its wake. Just a moth, up out of the dark, harmless now. It caught the warm light of the joined lanterns for a moment, a small grey scrap of the old fear made ordinary, and then it drifted up over the mine-mouth and away across the dark moor, and was gone, and took nothing with it.

Wren watched it go. Then she pulled her blanket close over the cold place in her chest, and leaned her shoulder against Rook's, and kept the watch — one small steady warmth among a thousand, in a town that had finally learned to keep its light together — while below them the Deepings slept, and above them the long dark turned toward morning, and nobody, anywhere in Nethercote, sat alone by their fire.
