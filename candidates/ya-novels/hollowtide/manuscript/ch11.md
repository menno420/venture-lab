# Chapter 11 — What Gran Gave

Gran came down the last of the stair the way she did everything, without hurry, as if the risen dark were a draught in a doorway she had lived with all her life. Which, Wren understood, it was.

The moth-storm gave way in front of her lantern and closed again behind it. Warden Coll went to her at once, his voice pitched low and urgent — "Idony, it's begun, the girl shared, there's two of them lit now, you have to finish it before the whole thing wakes" — and Gran walked past him as though he weren't there. She came to the water's edge and looked down at Wren and Fen huddled on the cold stone, and Wren, kneeling with her grey hand cradled against her chest and her brother's fragile new warmth shivering against her side, looked up and did not know, for one long moment, whether the woman above her had come to save them or to give them down.

"You shared," Gran said. Her eyes had gone to Wren's hand — the greyed knuckles, the frost creeping up from the palm. "You foolish, foolish child. Look what you've done to yourself. Look what's coming into you."

"You wrote his name," Wren said. Her voice shook, cold-thinned. "You wrote it before he even went out. You chose him."

"Yes." No excuse in it, no softening. The lantern didn't waver in the old woman's fist. "I chose him because his wick is the brightest in Nethercote save yours, and the brightest hold the dark the longest, and a Cawley given down buys the town more years than any other child. I have known the arithmetic of it since before your mother was born. I have never once been able to make it wrong." She looked out over the black lake, and something moved in her face that Wren had never been let see. "I stood on this stone once before with a lantern in my hand. There was a girl at the water then, too. Cold, and leaning toward it, the way your brother was. She had my chin. She had my temper." A pause you could have fallen into. "Her name was Nell. She was mine. My daughter. And I brought her down that stair myself, and I told her it was to save the town, and I let the dark take her, and I climbed back up alone and told Nethercote that the flood had her."

"Gran—"

"I told myself I was strong," Gran said. "I told myself that was what it was to be strong — to give what you loved so that others kept theirs. To let the child go down into the dark so no one had to see the town starve. I have said the rhyme over forty children in forty years. *Keep your fire, and hold your own.*" She said it like something rotten in her mouth. "And every one of them went down that stair alone. Every single one. Because that is the law we made. Nell went alone. I made sure of it. I thought that was mercy. I thought a clean quick going-alone was kinder than the whole town failing slow." Her voice cracked at last, straight across the middle. "I have been cold in the chest since the day I climbed back up without her. Do you understand me, Wren? I gave her, and it hollowed me, and I have been half in that water for forty years, and it did not save anything. It only bought time. It always only buys time. And then the tide comes again and there is another child at the edge."

The lake was rising as she spoke. The cold was pressing in against the two small flames, Wren's and Fen's, closing the room down around them, and Wren felt her own light stagger under it — felt the leaning start again, the terrible warm invitation of the dark, *come in, it's warm in here, you're so tired* — and she gripped Fen and dug her nails into her own cold palm to stay in the world.

"It's snuffing us," she managed. "Gran. It's going to take both of us and there's nothing—"

Wren thought of the ledger then, the long column of names in the Cawley hand going back and back, each one a child led down this same stair by someone who loved them and had decided that love meant letting go. Nell near the top of it. Fen at the bottom, already written, in this old woman's careful hand. Forty years of arithmetic, and every sum came out the same, and every child paid it alone.

"There is one thing," Gran said.

She came down off the last step and knelt — Idony Cawley, who Wren had never once in her life seen kneel, not in chapel, not at a graveside — knelt on the cold wet stone between her grandchildren and the black water, and she set her lantern down beside them where its flame stood straight and unafraid, and she opened her old scarred hand.

"Coll," she said, without turning. "Take your Wardens up the stair. This is the last relighting. There will not be another after tonight, one way or the other. Go up, and whatever you hear, do not come down." And whatever the enforcer saw in her face, he obeyed it; Wren heard the Wardens' lamps retreating up the spiral, and the dark leaned closer where they had been.

Gran laid her open palm flat against Fen's chest, and her other hand she pressed to Wren's — over the sternum, over the frost, over the failing Cawley ember — and she bowed her white head between them.

"No," Wren said, understanding. "No, Gran, you'll hollow, you'll go, don't—"

"Child," Gran said, almost gently, "I hollowed forty years ago. There is only this much left of me, and I have finally found the one thing worth spending it on." She lifted her head and looked at Wren, and for the first time in Wren's whole life there was no door in her grandmother's face, none, only an old woman who had loved terribly and wrongly and was, at the very end, choosing to love right. "I let one child go down into the dark alone. I will not do it a second time. Not you. Not him. Not while I have a coal left to give."

Then she gave it.

Wren felt it come — not a needle's-width like her own careful sharing, but a flood, an old banked fire opening all at once, the whole guttering remainder of Idony Cawley pouring out through her two hands into the children she had condemned. Warmth roared back into Wren's chest, shocking, painful, her greyed hand flushing pink to the knuckle; beside her Fen gasped and sat up straight as his fragile flame caught and steadied and burned. And it did not stop there. Gran turned her scarred palm toward the water — toward the deep, toward the single ancient failing flame that Wren knew was burning far below in Morren Cawley's keeping hand, the first light, the oldest, the one that had held all of this since the founding and was going out from sheer loneliness — and Gran gave to that too, sent her warmth down into the dark to the woman who had held it alone for centuries.

"Light to light," Gran said, and her voice was fading even as she said it, thinning like Wren's had thinned, "and none alone."

The dark recoiled.

It was only a moment — Wren felt it, the great cold hunger flinching back from the sudden joined warmth of four flames burning as one, Gran's and hers and Fen's and Morren's, threaded together palm to palm to water. The moth-storm faltered. The risen lake sagged. For one breath the Ashen drew back from the edge of the stone like a tide going out, baffled, as if it had reached for two lone candles and closed its hand on something it could not put out.

One breath. And then Wren felt Gran begin to go.

The warmth still poured, but the hand on her chest had gone light, and lighter, the grip loosening, the old woman swaying where she knelt. Gran had spent forty years' worth of banked fire in a handful of heartbeats and there was almost nothing left of her now, and Wren caught her by the shoulders as she listed, and felt through her thin frame how little was in there, how cold she had gone, how far she had leaned already toward the warm dark room she had stood in the doorway of for forty years.

And even so — even with all of it spent — the dark was already coming back.

Wren felt it return through the soles of her feet, the cold gathering itself off the black water, unhurried, patient, a thing that had all of time. Four joined flames had made it flinch. Four had not made it stop. It leaned in again at the edge of the stone and the moths began, once more, to rise. Gran's given warmth was already thinning against it, four small lights against a lake of night, and Wren understood, with a clarity that was almost calm, that this was the wall they had all broken on. Nell alone. Merron alone. Forty children alone. And now four of them together, and it was more, it was so much more — and it was still not enough. It would never be enough, not four, not ten, not a hundred lone flames spent one at a time in the dark.

Old Mabb's voice came back to her out of the bereaved underground, dry and certain: *It has never faced the whole town's light at once, because the Wardens keep everyone apart.*

Not four flames. Not the bright doomed few who found their way down here alone.

All of them. Every hearth in Nethercote, every wick the Wardens had taught to burn behind its own shut door — brought together, joined, carried down into the dark as one light that would not stand alone and so could not be put out.

Wren gathered her brother against her, his small heart beating warm and real under her hand at last, and she got her arm under her fading grandmother, and she turned her face up toward the long black throat of the stair and the town shuttering itself above it, each behind its own lone flame, exactly as it had been taught.

"Fen," she said. "We have to get up. We have to get you home, and then I have to make the whole town come down here — all of it, together — or none of this holds." She felt her own barely-relit wick tremble at the size of it. "Can you climb?"

Fen looked at her with her brother's eyes, and closed his cold small hand around Da's brass button, and nodded.
