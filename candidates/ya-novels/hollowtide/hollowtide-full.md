# HOLLOWTIDE

*A dark-fantasy thriller — young adult (ages 14–17)*

A complete novella (~28,595 words).

# Chapter 1 — Ash-Moths

The button had rolled somewhere out past the last lamp, and Fen would not come home without it.

"It's brass," he said, as if that settled everything, crouched over the heather with his nose nearly in the dirt. "It's got a bird on it. You can't just leave a bird."

"You can leave a bird," Wren said. "Birds leave each other all the time. It's practically what they're famous for."

But she went with him anyway, out past the edge of the lamplight where the moor began, because that was the whole trouble with being sixteen and having a brother who was nine — you were always following him into places you had the sense to stay out of. The last of the day had gone a bruised, coal-smoke grey behind the chimneys of Nethercote. Below them the town sat low and huddled in its bowl of peat and stone, every roof leaking a thread of smoke, the chapel bell squat and silent, the ropewalk a pale scar off to the west. Behind every shuttered window a keeping-flame would already be lit. It was that time of year.

The dark-tide season. The turn into the long dark, when the days shrank down to a coin of light and the old people said the dark below came up to meet the dark above, and you kept your fire close.

"Found it!" Fen surged up with something pinched between two filthy fingers, triumphant. In the grey light Wren couldn't have told brass from a beetle, but he was grinning, and his grin was worth the cold. He dropped the button into the tin he kept his treasures in — buttons, mostly, and pebbles with holes worn through them, and one small grey bone he swore was a wren's, which he thought was hilarious. He rattled the tin at her. "Now we can go."

"Kind of you."

Down in the town, the first lamplighter came out into the square with a long brass pole, and the first lamp bloomed gold. Then the next. Then the next, the light walking itself along the streets the way it did every night of the dark-tide, and thin and far off Wren heard the lamplighter's rhyme carried up on the wind, the one every child in Nethercote learned before they learned their letters.

*Light to light, and none alone; keep your fire, and hold your own.*

Keep your fire and hold your own. Meaning: bank your hearth, bar your door, don't go carrying your flame into the dark or into anyone else's house, because the dark was hungry and a fire left out was a fire lost. Wren had sing-songed it a thousand times without once thinking it meant anything. It was just what you said, the way you said grace, the way you touched two fingers to your breastbone for luck.

She touched them there now, without deciding to. Under the bone, faint as a held breath, the warmth. Her wick. Everyone in Nethercote had one — a second small heart, an ember you couldn't see but could always feel, a low coal of warmth burning under the sternum from the day you were born. It was your light and your life. Gran said the whole town's wicks together were what kept the dark below asleep, all those little fires burning in all those little houses. Wren had never thought hard about that either.

She thought about it now, a little, only because the cold up here was the wrong kind of cold. She'd been out on the moor after dark a hundred times. This was different — not the honest bite of wind off the peat, but a thin, seeping chill that seemed to come up through the soles of her boots from somewhere far below, and that her wick answered with a low, uneasy flutter, the way a candle leans away from a draught you can't feel on your own skin.

"Fen," she said. "Tin's full. Come on, before Mam sends the whole street looking."

Fen had stopped walking.

He was standing very still a few feet ahead of her on the path, his tin dangling forgotten from one hand, his face turned up and out — not toward home, but away, out across the black shoulder of the moor to where the ground fell and folded toward the old mine-workings. The Deepings. The flooded shafts nobody went near.

"Fen." Something in Wren's chest went tight. "Fen, come on, it's freezing."

He didn't answer. And then Wren heard it too, and understood why he'd gone so still.

A sound was coming up out of the ground. Low, so low it was almost only a feeling — a long, dragging moan, like wind pouring down a shaft, like a door opening somewhere very deep and never finishing. The dark-tide sound. She'd heard the old ones speak of it in the shame-lowered voices they kept for such things, but she had never once heard it herself, and now that she did every hair on her arms stood up and her wick gave a small frightened flare, as if it too had heard and did not like it.

"We're going home," she said. "Now. Come here."

She reached for his hand.

And that was when the first moth landed on him.

She saw it settle on his collar — a small thing, black, its wings the dull soft black of ashes gone cold. Then another, on his sleeve. Then they were coming out of the dark all at once, out of the heather and the low sky, black and silent and unhurried, settling on Fen's shoulders and his hair and the backs of his hands, more of them every heartbeat, and Wren knew what they were because everyone in Nethercote knew, though nobody ever said the word aloud where the failing could hear.

Ash-moths. The sign. The moths gathered on you when your wick was going out.

"No," Wren said, stupidly, to the moths, to the dark, to the whole grey moor. "No, not him, he's fine, he's nine —" and she got both hands on him and pulled him against her, brushing at the moths, which did not fly and did not crush, only shifted under her fingers like flakes of soft cold ash. Fen felt too light in her arms. He turned his face up to her and it was calm, dreadfully calm, a small polite puzzlement on it, as if she'd woken him from something warm.

"It's cold," he said. "Wren, it's gone cold."

"You're all right." Her voice cracked straight down the middle. She pressed her palm flat to his chest, over the breastbone, over the place where his wick should be — and felt it. Felt the warmth there under her hand, his small steady coal that she had felt a hundred times when he'd crawled into her bed frightened of the dark. Felt it flicker.

Felt it gutter.

And then, against her palm, quiet as a candle pinched between two wet fingers, felt it go out.

There was a smell on the wind now, threading up under the peat and the coal-smoke — the smell of wet ash, of cold char, of a fire drowned days ago. It came up off the moor from the direction of the Deepings and it filled the whole world.

Fen sagged. Not falling — just settling, going quiet, the way a room goes quiet when someone turns down the lamp. The moths lifted off him all at once, a soft black exhalation into the dark, and were gone, and he stood in the circle of her arms cold and small and looking up at her with that terrible mild curiosity, as if she were the strange one, as if nothing at all had happened.

"Wren," he said, "why are you crying?"

She didn't remember beginning to run. She only knew that she had him by the hand, that his hand was cold, colder than the night could account for, and that she was hauling him down off the moor toward the lamplight and the smoke and the low bright houses with her breath tearing in her throat and the dark-tide sound sinking away behind them into the ground. His tin bounced against his hip. He came without protest. He came the way you'd follow anyone, if you no longer particularly minded where you went.

The door of their house stood at the end of the lane, and the keeping-flame burned orange in the window, and Wren fell against the door and got it open and pulled him into the warmth and the coal-smoke and the ordinary blessed light, and turned to shout for their mother —

And Gran was already there.

She was standing in the middle of the room in her grey shawl, her face turned to the door as though she had been watching it, as though she had known to be watching it. Idony Cawley, Chief Lamplighter, Wren's grandmother, straight-backed and terrible in the firelight. Her lips were moving. Wren knew that look — Gran counting under her breath, the way she always did, numbers passing like beads through her fingers, wick after wick after wick, keeping her tally of the town's small fires. On her open palm, turned up toward the flame, an old white scar shone.

She was looking at Fen. And as Wren watched, her grandmother's face changed — not with grief, not with shock, but with something worse, something that shut. It closed over from the eyes down like a door swinging to on a cold room, quiet and final, latched from the inside.

She had stopped counting.

"Gran," Wren said. "Gran, help him, his wick — it went out, I felt it, you have to —"

"I know, child." Idony's voice was very level. She did not come to Fen. She did not kneel to him or take his cold hands or gather him up. She only looked at him the way you'd look at a sum already worked, an answer you'd known before you asked.

"By the old law," Gran said, "Fen had three nights."

Had. Not has.

The fire cracked in the grate. Outside, far off and low beneath the town, the dark-tide sound went on and on, patient as water, and Fen stood cold in the doorway and did not shiver at all.

# Chapter 2 — The Keeping-Flame

Morning came grey and grudging, the way all mornings came now, and found Wren on her knees at the hearth building the keeping-flame.

You did it a certain way. Gran had shown her before dawn without softness, her scarred hand guiding Wren's: peat at the base, then coal, then a curl of dry heather at the heart of it, and you lit the heather from the old flame, never a fresh match, so the fire had a memory in it. A keeping-flame was not for warmth, or not only. It was a vigil. It was the family's own fire pledged against a hollowing loved one's cold, and if you kept it burning day and night, close as you dared, it slowed the going. It bought a night, maybe two. It did not turn anything back. Gran had been very clear on that, in the grey before the light, her voice like a hand pressed flat over Wren's mouth: *It does not save him. Nothing saves him. It only makes the leaving slower.*

Wren blew on the heather until it caught, and the flame stood up gold, and she stayed there on her knees longer than she needed to, watching it, because the fire was doing something and she could not.

Fen sat where they had put him, in the good chair pulled close to the grate, wrapped in three blankets and their father's old coat, and he was cold. Cold to look at, even. He had the blankets pulled up but his hands lay outside them, palms turned toward the flame, reaching for it the way a plant reaches at a window. His fingers, when Wren had held them, were like river-stones. He was quieter than she had ever known him, and Fen had never in his life been quiet — Fen narrated. Fen had opinions about everything, birds especially. Now he watched the fire with a soft, drifting attention and said almost nothing, and every little while his head turned, slow and unbothered, toward the window and the moor beyond it, as if someone out there had said his name too low for anyone else to hear.

"Fen." Wren pushed his tin of treasures into his lap. "Show me the bird button. The one we froze half to death for."

He looked down at the tin. For a moment there was nothing, and Wren's heart dropped into her boots. Then his cold fingers moved among the pebbles and buttons, and he found the brass one, and turned it to the light. "It's a wren," he said. "Same as you." A ghost of the old grin crossed his face and was gone. "That's why I couldn't leave it."

She hadn't known that. She'd thought it was just a bird. She had to look hard at the flame for a moment.

Their mother had not come down.

Alys had gone up in the night when Gran told her, and had not come down, and Wren could hear her overhead moving from room to room without purpose, the floorboards telling on her. When she did come, mid-morning, she came in a great false brightness, a scarf tied over her hair, her sleeves pushed up as if for a day of ordinary work.

"Right," Alys said. "Broth. He wants feeding up, that's all, he's chilled through — you shouldn't have had him out on the moor, Wren, you know better —" and she banged the pot onto the hook and busied her hands and did not once look directly at the boy in the chair. She talked over him and around him. She asked him if he wanted bread with it. She told him the Trewins' hens had stopped laying. She kept a wall of small warm words up between herself and the thing in the room, and behind the wall her eyes were wide and dry and terrified, and Wren understood that her mother had decided, sometime in the night, that if she simply did not say it, it would not be so.

"Mam," Wren said quietly. "His wick's out. I felt it go."

"Don't." Alys's voice snapped like a stick. "Don't you say that. He's cold, is all. He'll come right by the fire." She stirred the broth that wasn't ready. "The dark doesn't take a Cawley child in his own kitchen with his mam right here. It doesn't. So we won't be talking that way in this house."

Wren let it be. There was nowhere to go with it. She took the water bucket out to the pump instead, to have somewhere to put her hands.

That was where she learned how it would be.

Mrs. Trewin was at the pump. She had known Wren since Wren was smaller than Fen; she had pressed apples on them at the market and asked after their mother every Sunday. And when she saw Wren coming with the bucket she went still, and then she gathered her shawl and turned her face away — deliberately, carefully away, the way you'd turn from something you'd been raised not to look at — and she left the pump with her own bucket half full rather than stand beside a Cawley whose brother had been chosen by the dark. Down the lane, old Sennen on his step did the same, folding his newspaper and going in and shutting his door. A shutter closed somewhere. Then another.

By noon it was all through Nethercote. Wren felt it like weather. Nobody said a cruel word — that was the worst of it. Nobody said anything. They only looked away, all of them, every door in the town pulling its own fire a little closer and turning its own shoulder, because a hollowing was a shame, and shame was catching, and everyone knew the rule they had all been raised on. *Keep your fire, and hold your own.* The dark chose Fen. That was between the Cawleys and the dark. You did not carry your light into another house's grief; you kept to your own hearth and let the dark take its own, and you were quietly, shamefully glad it had not come for yours.

Wren went back inside and shut the door on all of them and did not trust herself to speak.

She set the bucket down too hard. Fen was still in the chair, still reaching his cold hands at the flame, and she went and knelt by him and chafed his fingers between her palms the way you would a frostbitten thing, trying to rub some of her own warmth into them. His skin took the heat and gave nothing back. Under her hands his fingers stayed the temperature of the pump-water, of stones at the bottom of a stream, and where she pressed her thumb into his palm she thought she could feel, very faintly, the cold trying to climb into her instead — a thin, patient, drawing chill, moving the wrong way. She let go of him. She didn't mean to. Her body did it for her, the way you snatch back from a stove, and she hated herself for it before her hands had even finished the flinching.

"Sorry," she whispered. "Fen, I'm sorry."

He only smiled at her, mild and far away, and turned back to the window.

The Wardens came in the afternoon.

Two of them, in the lamplighters' grey, and Gran at their head, and behind Gran the big silent shape of Warden Coll with his hard, patient face. They filled the small room. They stood over Fen's chair and looked down at him with a kind of professional gentleness that turned Wren's stomach, and Gran laid her scarred hand on the boy's cold hair, and Fen tipped his head toward the touch like a cat, hungry for the warmth of a living hand.

"He's peaceful," Gran said, to Alys, and it was meant as comfort. "You see? There's no pain in it. There never is, once the wick's gone. It's the fighting that hurts them." She smoothed Fen's hair. "Keep the flame lit. Keep him warm. Don't let him wander at night — they want to walk, toward the end, and it frightens the neighbours. And when it's time —" her voice did not change at all — "let the dark take its own. It's the oldest law there is. It's mercy, Alys. Grieve him gentle and let him go."

"Yes," Alys whispered, staring at the floor. "Yes. Thank you."

Coll said nothing. He only watched Fen with those flat, dutiful eyes, and once he looked at the window and the moor beyond it, and Wren had the sudden cold conviction that he was measuring something. Judging the distance. Reading a clock only he could see.

"There's nothing to be done," Gran said again, more softly, and this time she looked at Wren when she said it, straight at her, a warning folded inside the words. "Nothing, child. Do you hear me? Better children than Fen have been let go. Don't make it worse by hoping."

Then they went, the grey coats, out into the failing light, and Gran with them, and the door shut, and the keeping-flame threw its small gold light up the wall, and Fen turned his head to look out at the moor.

Wren stood in the middle of the quiet room with her fists shut so hard her nails bit, and something turned over in her that was not grief.

*Let the dark take its own.* Everyone said it. Gran, the Wardens, Mrs. Trewin turning at the pump, her own mother whispering it into the broth. The whole town lined up and looking away, so practiced at it, so sure — as if this had all happened before, over and over, as if there were a shape to it they had all long since agreed not to look at.

Nothing to be done. Wren didn't believe that. She refused it the way you'd refuse to step off a cliff just because someone told you the ground was down there and soft.

Somebody, somewhere, had once tried to look. She was certain of it. And then she remembered who.

Rook Ashby. The Ashby boy from the edge of town, out by the ropewalk, the one whose sister Merron had gone hollow last winter and walked out onto the moor and never come back. The one everyone said had gone strange after — who'd stood up in the middle of the chapel and asked the Wardens straight out where the hollowed *went*, who counted things, who wrote things down, who asked and asked until people crossed the street to be spared the asking. Everyone said he was bitter. Everyone said he was trouble. Everyone looked away from Rook Ashby too.

Wren looked at her cold, quiet brother turning his face to the dark, and she thought: *good.*

She banked the keeping-flame so it would hold, and told her mother she was going for more coal, and pulled her coat on, and went out to find the boy who wouldn't stop asking questions.

# Chapter 3 — The Boy Who Counts

The ropewalk stood at the western edge of Nethercote where the houses gave out and the moor took over — a long low open-sided shed, a hundred yards of it, where in better years men had walked backward twisting hemp into cable for the mine that no longer needed any. Now it was half fallen and wholly abandoned, its roof open to the grey sky in places, the old rope-hooks rusting in their rows, and Wren almost turned back twice on the walk out. It was the sort of place you didn't go. Which was, she supposed, exactly why he'd be there.

He was.

Rook Ashby sat on an upturned crate at the far end of the ropewalk with a lamp beside him and a great ledger-sized book open on his knees, and he had a pencil behind his ear and another in his hand, and he did not look up when she came in out of the wind, though she knew he'd heard her — the whole shed had announced her, board by board.

"If you're here to tell me I've said something to upset your gran," he said, still writing, "get in the queue. And shut the door, you're letting the moor in."

"There isn't a door."

"Then stand where the door would be. It's the principle." He finished his line, set the pencil down, and finally looked up at her, and Wren was startled by his face — younger than the town's stories made him, seventeen at most, but with a grey tiredness around the eyes that didn't belong to seventeen at all. He took her in: the coat thrown on wrong, the redness she hadn't managed to scrub off, the way she was standing. Something in his own face shifted and went careful.

"Cawley," he said. "Wren, isn't it." Not a question. "Whose is it."

She hadn't said a word about it. "How do you—"

"Because nobody comes out here to see me," Rook said, "unless the dark's got its hand on somebody they love, and they've finally worked out that everyone else in that town would rather they suffered quietly. So. Whose is it?"

Wren's throat closed. She got it out anyway. "My brother. Fen. He's nine." And then, because he was the first person all day who had looked straight at it instead of away: "His wick went out last night, on the moor. I felt it go. And everyone's telling me there's nothing to be done and to let the dark take its own, and I don't — I can't —"

"Sit down," Rook said, "before you fall down," and cleared a second crate with his boot, and she sat.

For a moment he only looked at her, that grey weariness in his eyes gone deep and old. "Merron," he said at last. "My sister. Last winter. So I know exactly what your kitchen looks like this morning, and I know what your gran said, word for word, because they say it word for word every time. *No pain in it. It's the fighting that hurts them. Let the dark take its own.*" His mouth twisted. "It's a script, Cawley. They've said it so many times they've smoothed all the thinking out of it."

"So many times." Wren leaned toward the lamp. "That's what I felt. Like it had happened before. Like they all knew the steps."

"They have." Rook turned the ledger around on his knees so she could see it, and Wren looked, and did not at first understand.

It was a tally. Page after page of it, in a cramped careful hand — names, dates, little marks. Down the moor-side of each page ran a column of the dark-tide seasons going back years, and beside them the names of the snuffed, every hollowing Rook Ashby had been able to dig out of the town's careful silence: parish records, grave-plots with no bodies in them, old people's half-memories, the names nobody was supposed to say. *Merron Ashby.* And above her, and below, all the others.

"I started it after Merron," he said, quiet now, the sourness gone out of him. "Because they told me she was just unlucky. The dark took her, no reason, nothing to be done, and everyone looked at their shoes. And I thought — fine. If it's just chance, then it'll fall like chance. Scattered. Random. A wick here, a wick there, no pattern to it." He tapped the page. "It isn't random."

Wren looked where his finger went, and slowly, coldly, she began to see it. The marks weren't scattered. They came in a rhythm. A cluster of them, then a long quiet, then another cluster — spaced out across the years as regular as tide-marks on a wall. As regular as a bell.

"One a season," Rook said. "Sometimes more, never fewer. Every dark-tide, right at the turn of the long dark, when they say the dark below comes up." His voice was very level, the way you keep a voice level over a drop. "That's not the dark choosing where it likes, Wren. That's a schedule. Somebody's keeping a schedule."

The wind came down the length of the ropewalk and stirred the pages, and Wren pulled her coat tighter and could not get warm.

"There's more," Rook said, and hesitated, and she watched him decide whether to hand her something heavy. "There's a — there are things people don't say. I've picked at it for a year. Half of it's old wives and half of it's true and I can't always tell which." He met her eyes. "The Wardens don't just leave the hollowed to wander off and die on the moor. When it's time, they *lead them down.* Into the Deepings. Themselves. There's a word for it — they call it *relighting.* Like they're doing the dark a service. Like they're keeping something lit."

"Down into the Deepings," Wren repeated. The wet-ash smell, the sound coming up out of the ground. Fen's face turning toward the moor. "That's why they don't want them wandering. That's why Coll kept looking at the window."

"You're quick," Rook said, without warmth, only noting it. "Yes. And there's one more thing, and it's the thing I'd give anything not to know." He turned his left hand over. Across the palm, pale and shining, ran an old scar much like the one on Gran's hand — the mark of a fire held too long. "You can fight a snuffing. It's forbidden — it's the most forbidden thing there is, they don't even have the shape of a word for it out loud. Wick-sharing. You press your palm to their breastbone, over where their wick was, and you give them some of your own. You push your own fire into the cold place." He closed the scarred hand. "It relights them. For a while."

Wren was already on her feet. "Then that's it, that's what I do, I've a strong wick, Gran always says—"

"It burns you," Rook said flatly, and something in how he said it put her back down onto the crate. "It burns the giver. That's why it's forbidden — you don't give light for free, you give it out of yourself, and give too much and you go cold in their place. Two hollows instead of one." He was looking at the scar and not at her. "I tried it on Merron. Last winter. I held my hand to her chest till it blistered and I gave her everything I could stand to give, and she warmed for a night. One night. And then the dark just — took it back. Took her, and took what I'd given, and I couldn't do it twice or I'd have gone down with her." His jaw worked. "So don't look at me like I've handed you the answer. I handed you the reason I don't sleep."

The lamp guttered between them. Somewhere far under the moor, so low it was almost only the memory of a sound, the dark-tide moaned in the ground.

"But it *works*," Wren said, low and stubborn. "For a night, it works. That's a night more than nothing to be done."

Rook looked at her a long moment, and something in his tired face gave way — not to hope, exactly. To recognition. As if he'd found, at the world's edge, one other person built the same wrong way as himself.

"There's a thing that's been eating me," he said slowly, "and I couldn't work out why, until you walked in. Look." He drew the ledger back onto his knees and turned to a fresh page where he'd been working when she came — a list, sorted, the names copied out again in their families. "I went back through all of it and I sorted the snuffed by name. By line. Because if it's chance, the names should fall like the town falls — Trewins and Sennens and Wrights, all the ordinary crowd, whoever's unlucky that year." He set his pencil against the column. "They don't. Look who the dark keeps taking."

Wren looked. And the cold that went through her then had nothing to do with the wind off the moor.

The names were not the ordinary crowd. Down the page, over and over, season after season, the same handful of old lines repeated — the founding families, the ones whose people had sunk the first shafts, the ones the town still called old-line under its breath. And more than any of them, again and again and again, thick as ash-moths on a failing child, one name above all the rest.

Cawley. Cawley. Cawley.

Her own name, down the years, given to the dark.

"It's not random," Rook said quietly, watching her face. "And it's not the poor and the weak, which is what you'd expect if the dark were only cruel. It's the old lines. It's the *bright* ones. It's yours, Wren, more than anyone's." He closed the ledger. "So here's the question I've been carrying around for a year with no one to ask it to. Why the Cawleys? Why always the brightest wicks?"

And why, Wren thought, her hand pressed flat and cold against her own steady, treacherous warmth — why Fen?

# Chapter 4 — The Lamplighters' Book

The vigil-house stood at the top of the market square, older than the chapel and colder, a long low building of black stone with lamps burning in every window even by day. No Nethercote child went near it without being sent for. Which was, Wren thought, exactly why no one would look twice at a Cawley walking up to the door as if she had a right to be there.

She had a right. That was the ugly, useful part. She was Idony Cawley's granddaughter, and the Cawley name opened doors in this town the way a key opened a lock — not because people loved you, but because they had learned not to ask.

She waited until the noon bell, when the Wardens took their meal in the back room and left only one lamp-boy at the door. She knew him — Perch, a soft round boy two years younger who kept pigeons and owed her for a winter's worth of not telling his mother where he really got the birds.

"Gran wants the small ledger from the vigil-room," Wren said, and let her voice go flat and bored, the way Gran's went when she expected to be obeyed. "The offerings one. You want to come and find it in the dark yourself, or you want to let me do it and get back to your dinner?"

Perch looked at the warm doorway behind him, and at Wren, and decided his dinner mattered more than his duty. He always would. That was why Gran called him a lamp-boy and not a Warden.

The vigil-room was at the top of a stone stair, and it smelled of tallow and old paper and, faintly, underneath, of wet ash — that smell Wren was learning to hate, the smell that had come off Fen's collar the night the moths took him. She stood in the doorway a moment and made herself breathe it.

Lamps everywhere. A long table. And on a shelf built into the wall, chained to the stone like something in the chapel, a book.

It was enormous. Leather gone the colour of a drowned thing, the spine cracked and re-sewn a dozen times, the pages so many that the block of them had bowed the shelf. Wren lifted the cover and the smell that came up was the deep one, the mine-smell, coal-damp and cold, as if the book had been kept somewhere far underground and only lately brought up into the light.

The first pages were in a hand she didn't know — narrow, slanted, ancient, the ink gone brown as tea. But the words she knew. Everyone in Nethercote knew them, though no one said them like this.

*The dark's takings,* the top of the page said. And under it, columns. A name. A date. A word beside each name that was always the same word.

*Relit.*

She turned pages. The hands changed as the years went by — she could see the town's whole line of Chief Lamplighters in the changing ink, one dying and the next taking up the pen, the letters growing rounder or sharper by the decade. But the shape of the thing never changed. Every dark-tide, one name. Always one. Sometimes two in a bad year. Never none.

*Relit. Relit. Relit.*

And the names. She had thought, walking up the square, that she might find strangers here, a list of the poor and the friendless, the ones nobody would miss. She had been ready to be angry about that.

She was not ready for how many of the names were hers.

Cawley. Cawley. A Ashby, twice. Cawley. Morren, Ivo, Hale, the old moorland families — the ones with the bright wicks, the ones Rook had ringed on his tally at the ropewalk with his bitten-down pencil, saying *why always these, why always the good ones.* Here was his answer, written in a Cawley hand and chained to a wall. The town had not been feeding its worst to the dark.

It had been feeding its best.

Wren's own wick pulsed under her sternum, a small stubborn warmth, and for the first time in her life the feeling of it frightened her — as if it were a coin in her chest that someone had been saving up.

She turned to the last written page. The ink here was fresh enough to still be black.

The hand was Gran's. She would have known it anywhere; she had watched it write shopping-lists and death-notices and the labels on the jars of the good honey. Neat, upright, unhurried. It had written a date — this dark-tide, the one they were living in now — and beside it a name.

*Fen Cawley.*

And the word beside it was not written yet. The space for it was blank, waiting, the way a grave is dug before the body comes.

Wren stood very still.

She had come here half-hoping to find the town innocent. To find that Fen's snuffing had been the dark's own doing, unlucky, unchosen — a thing that happened, terrible and random, to a boy who liked buttons and was afraid of the corridor to the privy. That would have been a grief she could have carried.

But the name was written before the moths came. She was sure of it. The ink was older than three nights; she pressed her thumb to it and it did not smear. Someone had chosen Fen — had set his name down in the book of the dark's takings — while he was still warm, still hers, still complaining that his boots leaked. Chosen him, and then waited for the wick to go out on schedule. As if the snuffing were not the dark reaching up to take, but the town reaching down to give.

Her grandmother had written her brother's name in a ledger and dated it like an appointment.

Down in the square, the noon bell finished its round of strikes, and in the sudden quiet Wren heard, very clearly, boots on the stone stair.

Not soft boots. Not Perch. A slow, heavy, certain tread, one that had climbed these stairs ten thousand times and did not need to hurry.

Warden Coll.

Wren had a heartbeat and a half to decide, and her body decided for her — she let the great cover fall shut, snuffed the nearest lamp with a pinch that burned her fingers so the room would look darker than it was, and went sideways into the shadow behind the standing shelf, wedging herself into the cold gap between it and the wall. Dust and the mine-smell filled her mouth. She pressed her hand flat over her own chest as if she could hold the warmth of her wick still, keep it from shining out and giving her away, the way you'd cup a candle in the wind.

Coll came in. She heard him more than saw him — the creak of leather, the small dry sound of a man breathing through a broken nose. He stopped by the table. He was a big man and he filled the room with waiting.

"Perch says my lady wanted the offerings book." A pause. She could hear him not believing it. "My lady's in her own house with the boy. My lady sent no one."

Silence. The lamp she'd snuffed still smoked; she could smell it, and prayed he'd read it as a wick guttering low and not a pinched-out flame. His boots turned. One step toward the shelf. Two.

Wren stopped breathing. Through the crack she could see a slice of him — the grey Warden's coat, the ring of keys, the hand hanging by his hip, a hand she'd seen rest on Fen's small shoulder two days ago with what she'd taken for kindness. The cold gap seemed to breathe against her back, and she understood suddenly that the whole wall behind her was the outer wall, and that on the other side of the black stone was the moor, and under the moor the Deepings, and that this house had been built here, above all other places, because it was the nearest dry ground to the top of the shaft.

Coll's hand rose. Reached toward the shelf's edge.

Below, in the square, a pigeon-basket clattered and Perch's voice went up in a wail — one of his wretched birds loose in the vigil-house, wings loud on the stair, the boy blundering after it full of apology. Coll's head turned toward the noise, the way any man's would, annoyed and distracted, and he swore under his breath and went out to deal with it, his boots going down fast.

Wren did not thank Perch's pigeons. She did not have the breath. She came out of the gap on legs that had gone to water and knew she had one moment and no more.

She should have gone. Every sensible thing in her said *go, now, down the back way, out.* Instead she pulled the great cover open one last time — not to the fresh page, but back, deeper, into the brown-inked years, into the hand that had written before Gran's, the round upright hand of the Chief Lamplighter before her. She turned the leaves fast, looking for nothing, looking for the shape of the thing, and a name went by under her thumb and her whole body stopped as if she'd stepped off a step that wasn't there.

She turned back one leaf. Read it again. Read it a third time, because the first two times her mind refused to let the letters mean what they meant.

A dark-tide from long ago, the year Wren had been small enough to still believe the story she'd been told. The year the pumps failed and the low workings drowned and they had held an empty funeral because there had been no body to bury, only water. The flood, they always said. *Lost in the flood.* Poor Nell, lost in the flood, and don't ask your Gran about it, child, it breaks her heart.

Here was the name, in Gran's own younger hand, upright and unhurried, dated to that same dark-tide.

*Nell Cawley.*

And beside it, black and dry and long since finished, the one word the book gave to all its takings.

*Relit.*

No flood had taken her aunt. Wren's grandmother had written the name of her own daughter in this book, and led her down into the dark, and come home, and lit a keeping-flame for a girl who was already given away, and let the whole town go on saying *the flood* for sixteen years.

Somewhere below, Perch was still chasing his pigeon. Wren closed the book very softly, as if it could hear her, and went down the back stair into the daylight with her aunt's name burning a hole through everything she thought she'd known.

# Chapter 5 — Gran's Vigil

Gran kept her own house at the end of Cawley Row, the oldest house in the oldest street, and she kept it the way she kept everything — warm, spotless, and closed. Wren stood in the lane a long while before she went in, watching the smoke go up straight from the single chimney into the still grey evening, and made herself remember that she had loved this house once, before she knew what its warmth was paying for.

She did not knock. Cawleys did not knock at Cawley doors.

Gran was at the hearth, where she always was at this hour, feeding the keeping-flame she'd lit for Fen with careful small pieces of coal, her lips moving. Counting. She always counted — Wren had thought all her life it was prayer, and perhaps it was, of a kind. *One, two, three,* under the breath, on and on, the way another old woman might tell beads. Wren knew now that she was counting wicks. The town's wicks. Checking them like a shepherd checks a flock at dusk, to see how many were still lit and how many the dark had taken in the night.

"You've been up the vigil-house," Gran said, without turning. "Coll came to tell me a Cawley girl used my name to get in and left a lamp smoking." She set another coal on the fire. "I told him he was mistaken. I don't like being made a liar in my own front room, Wren."

"I read the book."

The counting stopped.

For a moment the only sound was the keeping-flame, hissing where the coal was damp, and from the next room the small terrible quiet that was Fen — not sleeping, Wren knew, but lying with his cold hands under the blankets and his face turned toward the window, toward the moor, the way he turned more with every hour that passed.

Gran stood. She was a small woman, smaller every year, but she had never in Wren's life looked small, and she did not now. She turned, and her face was the face Wren had seen at the door on the first night — a face closing like a door — except that this time, behind it, something was in pain.

"Then you read more than a child should have to," Gran said. "Sit down."

"I'll stand."

"You'll sit, because I am going to tell you the truth, and it is a long thing to hold up under, and I'd rather not watch you fall down in it." She lowered herself back into her chair by the fire and looked into the flame as if the words were written there. "You think I'm a monster. Good. Hold onto that a while; it'll keep you warm. But I'll have you be a monster who understands what she is."

Wren sat, because her legs wanted her to and because she wanted, God help her, to hear it.

"Under this town," Gran said, "there is a thing older than the town. Older than the mine. The miners only ever went down to it, they never made it. We call it the Ashen, when we call it anything, which is seldom, because naming a thing invites it to answer." She turned the poker in the coals. "It is cold and it is dark and it is hungry, and what it hungers for is us. Not our bodies. Our light. The little warmth you feel here—" she pressed two fingers to her own breastbone, and Wren saw, in the firelight, the old white scar across her palm, a scar Wren had asked about as a child and been told was a burn from the bread-oven. "Your wick. Every dark-tide it wakes, and it stirs up out of the Deepings, and it means to put out every wick in Nethercote at once and swallow the whole town cold. It has done it before, to other towns. There are moors out past ours with stone houses and no people in them and no story of where the people went. I have walked in them. That is what happens to a town that will not pay."

"So you pay it," Wren said. "With children."

"With one." Gran's voice did not rise. That was the worst of it — she did not raise her voice at all. "One wick, given down at the dark-tide, and the thing feeds, and it sleeps, and the other thousand wicks in this town stay lit through the winter. One child, so that all the rest may keep their brothers and their mothers and their own small lives. I did not make that bargain, Wren. I inherited it, the way you'll inherit my chair if you live long enough and haven't the sense to run. I only keep it."

"And you chose Fen." Wren's hands were fists in her lap. "You wrote his name before the moths ever came. He was still warm. He was still *complaining about his boots,* and you'd already—"

"I chose him because he would hold the dark longest." Now something moved under the closed face, and it was grief, old and worn smooth as the scar on her hand. "The Ashen doesn't take just any flame, girl. A guttering wick, a mean little life half out already — it snuffs those for spite, in the between-years, and they do it no good. What it wants, what buys the years of sleep, is a *bright* one. The brightest we have. And the brightest we have have always been Cawleys." She looked at Wren then, full on. "You feel it in yourself. Don't tell me you don't. You've felt it burn in you your whole life and thought it was temper. It isn't temper. It's the same fire Fen has. It's the reason your name will be in that book one day if there's a book left to hold it, and mine's in it already, further back than you looked."

"I looked far enough." Wren's voice cracked and she hated it. "I found Nell."

The name went into the room like a stone into a well. Wren watched it fall through her grandmother, all the way down, and heard it land.

"Yes," Gran said, at last, very low. "You found my Nell."

"You said the flood. Everyone says the flood. Mam still lights a candle for her at the flood's remembering, every year, and you *stand next to her and let her.*"

"I led my daughter down the Deepings sixteen years ago," Gran said, and each word came out separately, laid down like coals, "and I gave her to the dark with my own two hands, and I came back up, and I have counted this town's wicks every night since so that no other mother would have to. Don't you *dare* tell me what I have paid." Her voice never rose, but her hand had gone to the scar, closed over it. "I have paid more than anyone living. I gave the brightest thing I ever made. And I have kept this whole town lit on the strength of it for sixteen years, and not one of them knows to thank me, and I do not want their thanks. I want it to be *over,* and it is never over. It only comes round again."

The keeping-flame hissed. In the other room, Fen said something, thin and querulous — *cold,* Wren thought he said, *I'm so cold* — and neither of them went to him, because there was no warmth in that house that would answer him, and both of them knew it.

"It started with one of us," Gran said, quieter now, spent. "Morren Cawley. The First Lamplighter. When the dark first rose and began to take the town wick by wick, she went down into the Deepings alone to hold it back, and she found she could not die while she held it, and she has never come up. We are her blood. That is why it is always Cawleys in the book. We lit the first vigil and we have carried it ever since, because we are the ones the dark wants most and the ones best able to stand against it a little while. It is not a punishment, Wren. It is closer to a—" she searched, and did not find a kind word, and gave up. "It is what we are for."

Wren stood up. She could not sit under it any longer.

"There's a way to bring him back," she said. "I know there is. You press your hand to their chest and you give them some of your own, and it relights the wick. I know it can be done."

Gran went so still that for a moment Wren thought she'd stopped breathing.

"Who told you that word," Gran whispered. "Wick-sharing. Who put that in your mouth."

"Does it matter? It's real. You *know* it's real, look at your own hand—"

"I know it better than anyone alive." Gran rose, and crossed the room, and took Wren's wrist in a grip that was all bone and terribly strong, and she was not angry now; she was afraid, and that was so much worse. "Listen to me, because I will only say it the once and I am saying it to save your life. It does not save them. Do you hear me? A shared flame, given alone, does not save the one you give it to. The Ashen is not fooled by it. It only snuffs the two of you a little harder, together, instead of the one. You give your light away in the dark and the dark takes both. You do not bring your brother back. You go down beside him." Her grip tightened. "You make two hollows instead of one. That is *all* wick-sharing has ever made. Two hollows where there was one. Swear to me you'll not try it."

Wren looked at her grandmother's frightened face, and at the scar on the hand that held her, and did not swear.

"I've the vigil to keep," she said instead, and pulled her wrist free, and went out into the dark of Cawley Row with the plan whole and burning in her chest, exactly where her wick was, and just as impossible to put down.

# Chapter 6 — Those Who Kept Their Dead

Rook found her, in the end, and not the other way round. Wren had gone back to the ropewalk at first dark, half-mad with what she carried, and he'd taken one look at her face over the coiled ropes and said, "You read it, then," and not made her say any more.

"There's people you should meet," he said. "People who won't turn their eyes away from you. But you'll not tell a soul they exist, Wren Cawley, not your mam, not the pigeon-boy, no one — because the Wardens hunt them, and if you give them up you may as well have written the names yourself."

He led her down. Not into the Deepings — she was glad of that, some cowardly part of her had thought for a moment he meant the shaft — but under the town, through a coal-cellar behind the ropewalk and along a low passage that had been a haulage-way when the mine still lived, the walls sweating, the air thick with the smell of tallow and turned earth and unwashed grief. Wren went with one hand on the cold stone and her wick a small stubborn coal in her chest, and thought: *this is how it will feel, going down to Fen. Cold like this, and dark like this, and worse.*

The passage opened into a chamber, and the chamber was full of light.

Not much light. Careful light — a dozen small flames in a dozen jars and lanterns, hooded so none of it would show at any crack to the moor above. But after the black of the haulage-way it was warm as a held hand, and Wren stood in the mouth of it and felt her own eyes sting, and did not know why until she understood: it was the first warm room she'd stood in since the vigil-house, and the vigil-house's warmth had been a lie. This one wasn't. This one was people keeping each other lit in the dark, on purpose, against the law.

There were perhaps twenty of them. Ordinary Nethercote faces — a fishwife she bought from, a man who mended boots, two of the ropewalk lads, an old woman by the fire with a face like a walnut. They looked up at her, and Wren braced for the flinch, the sliding-away of the eyes that every neighbour had given her family since the moths came.

It did not come. They looked at her plainly, the way you look at someone who has the same wound as you.

"Idony Cawley's granddaughter," said the walnut-faced old woman, and it was not a question and not quite a welcome. "Come to keep your dead, or come to count us for the Wardens?"

"Fen's not dead," Wren said. "That's the whole of why I'm here."

Something went through the room at that — not agreement, but recognition. Rook put a hand on her shoulder, briefly, which for Rook was practically an embrace. "This is Old Mabb," he said. "She kept her own son eleven nights past the three, longer than anyone's held one. The Wardens burned her out twice. She keeps going."

"Twelve nights," Old Mabb said. "Before he walked. I keep the count." She patted the low stool beside her. "Sit, Cawley. You've the look of a girl about to do something that'll get her killed, and I'd sooner she did it knowing than not."

Wren sat. Across the chamber, in the warmest corner, a shape she had taken for a heap of blankets moved, and she saw that it was a person — a girl a little older than herself, wrapped to the chin, being fed something warm spoon by spoon by a young man who never took his eyes from her face. The girl's hands, where they showed, were grey-white and too still. Her eyes were open and turned, always, past the man who loved her, past the fire, toward the black passage and the moor beyond it. Every little while a moth — one of the small black ones, an ash-moth — lifted off her collar and drifted toward the dark, and the young man reached up without pausing his spooning and caught it in his fist and crushed it, gently, the way you'd close a door on a draught. He had been doing it a long time. Wren understood, watching, that he would go on doing it until she walked or he did, and that it would not be enough, and that he knew it would not be enough and did it anyway.

She looked away, because to look longer felt like theft. This was what waited for Fen. This grey stillness, this turning toward the dark. This was what she was racing.

"You want to know why the sharing fails," Old Mabb said, watching her watch the girl. She had a way of answering the question under the question. "He's tried it, that boy. Everyone here has tried it once. You press your hand to their heart and you pour your own warmth in, and for a night, two nights, they come back to you — they know your face, they hold your hand, the moths thin out. And then the dark comes for the tithe at the next dark-tide, and it doesn't take one of them. It takes both. The one you were keeping, and the fool who gave their light to keep them." She held up her own hand to the jar-light. Across the palm ran a scar, white and old, the twin of the one on Gran's hand. "I know. My son's gone and half my own light with him. I'm a candle-end. But I'll tell you the thing the Wardens have known for two hundred years and hanged men for saying." She leaned close, and her breath smelled of onions and cold. "It's not the sharing that fails. It's the *alone.*"

"I don't—" Wren started.

"Think, girl." Mabb's eyes were bright and hard in the walnut face. "The Ashen can put out a candle. Easy. It can put out two candles pressed together — that's your sharing, that's why it fails, two flames is still a small thing in a great dark, and it snuffs them together and calls it a good night's work. It's put out lone flames for longer than there's been a town up there to burn them. But hear me now." She took Wren's wrist, the same place Gran had gripped, but soft. "It has *never once* faced the whole town's light at the same time. Not once. Not since Morren went down. Do you know why?"

Wren shook her head, though something was rising in her, cold and enormous, that felt almost like the answer.

"Because the Wardens won't let us." Mabb sat back. "Why d'you think the law is *keep to your own hearth?* Why d'you think a hollowing's a shame, so the neighbours turn away, so no two griefs ever stand in the same room? Why d'you think they've hunted a little warm cellar like this one my whole long life, when all we do is sit with our dying? It's not to spare us the shame, love. It's to keep the light *apart.* Every hearth alone, every family behind its own door, every wick a lone candle the dark can pinch out one at a time forever. The bargain doesn't only need a tithe. It needs us *scattered.* A town that joined its light — all of it, at once, at the mine-mouth — that's the one thing in two hundred years the Ashen has never had to swallow. And I have sat down here in the dark my whole life thinking on it, and I do not believe it could."

The chamber was very quiet. The young man in the corner had stopped spooning and was listening, his fist still closed around a moth. The fishwife had a hand pressed to her own chest. Wren realised they had all heard Mabb say it before — that this was the thing they held onto down here in the warm dark, the heresy they passed hand to hand like a coal — and that not one of them had ever had the power to try it, because they were the shamed and the hunted and the half-hollow, twenty candle-ends in a cellar, and it would take the whole town, and the whole town had been taught since it could walk to keep its fire and hold its own.

*Light to light, and none alone,* Wren thought — the rhyme they'd all been sung to sleep with. *Keep your fire, and hold your own.* And she heard it, for the first time, as the lie it was built to be. Not a comfort. A leash.

"You're saying," she said slowly, "that if Nethercote lit one fire together — everybody, all their wicks in one place—"

"I'm saying nothing," Mabb said, "to a Cawley I met an hour past. I'm an old woman talking to the wall, and you never heard me." But her eyes said the rest, and held Wren's, and would not let go.

Rook crouched beside her. "It's why I brought you," he said, low. "You're the one with a name that opens doors. I'm nobody from the edge of town, Mabb's a wanted woman, these folk can't so much as show their faces in the square. But a Cawley—" He stopped. "A Cawley might make the town do a thing it's afraid of. If there were time."

"How much is there?" Wren asked. "Time. Fen had three nights. That's—" She counted, and her stomach dropped, because she had lost count somewhere in the vigil-house and Gran's front room, lost it the way you lose the bottom stair in the dark. "That's tonight and—"

"No." The young man in the corner spoke for the first time, hoarse, not turning from his girl. "The relighting's not kept every dark-tide-night. It's kept on the dark-tide *proper.* The high one. When the tide's full." He finally looked round at Wren, and his eyes were terrible and kind. "That's tomorrow. Tomorrow at nightfall the tide comes full and the Wardens go down to give the dark its due. Whoever's name is written—" He looked back at the wrapped, grey, turning girl he loved, and did not finish, and did not have to.

"Tomorrow," Wren said. "Fen's third night."

"Fen's third night," Old Mabb agreed, and her old hand tightened once on Wren's wrist and let go. "So whatever mad bright thing you mean to do, Cawley — you've got until the dark comes full. You've got hours. Not nights. Hours."

Above them, faint through the sweating stone, the town bell rang the hour, and Wren counted the strokes, and each one fell on her like a stair going down.

# Chapter 7 — The Betrayal

The plan fit in the space between two heartbeats, which was about all the room they had left.

Wren crouched in the low cellar under the ropewalk, a stub of tallow burning in a saucer between her and Rook Ashby, and she laid it out to him the way she'd laid it out to herself a hundred times since dawn — flat, in order, so the fear couldn't get a hand under any part of it. "The dark-tide rises tonight. They relight the bargain at the mine. So I go down into the Deepings before they finish, I reach Fen, and I bring him back."

"Bring him back," Rook repeated. He said it without mockery, which was somehow worse. His tally-book lay open on his knee, the columns of the snuffed marching down the page in his cramped hand. "You've seen a deep-hollow child, Wren. You've seen your own brother these three days. There's no *back* to walk them to. Not with words."

"Not with words." She held out her hand, palm up, the way she'd watched Old Mabb hold hers. "With this. You said it could be done. Palm to sternum. You share the wick."

Something crossed his face — Merron, she knew, the sister he'd failed, though he never let the name past his teeth. For a moment she thought he'd refuse her outright. Then he set the book aside and took her hand in both of his, and his hands were rough and cold as an outsider's always were, this far from a family hearth.

"All right," he said. "Then learn it properly, because it doesn't forgive mistakes. You put your palm here." He pressed her open hand flat to her own breastbone, under the throat-hollow, and beneath her fingers she felt it — the small steady warmth every Nethercote child carried, her wick, her second heart, burning bright the way Cawley wicks were said to burn. "You find your own light first. You have to feel it clear, or you can't move it. Then you press your hand to theirs, same place, and you *want* it across. You pour. And here's the part they don't tell you." His grip tightened. "It burns the giver. Not like a candle. Like the inside of you going out one coal at a time. The more you give, the deeper the cold comes into your own chest. Give too much—"

"And I go hollow instead." She'd heard it from Gran, sneering. *Two hollows instead of one.* "I know the price."

"You know the word for the price." He let her go. "Not the same thing." He looked at her a long moment in the tallow-light, this prickly boy who'd been to the edge of it and come back with nothing but a dead sister and a book full of names, and then he nodded once, as if she'd passed something. "I'll be down there with you. Whatever it's worth. I couldn't do it for Merron alone. Maybe two of us—"

The cellar door banged.

Not knocked. Banged — the flat of a hand, then boots on the stair, and Wren was up with the saucer in her fist before she'd thought about it, the flame guttering. But it was only Bett, one of the keeper-women from Mabb's underground, and Bett's face was wrong, grey and wet-eyed, and she got out three words before her voice broke.

"They've taken Fen."

The floor tilted. "Taken—"

"To the vigil-house. Coll and two Wardens, an hour past. *For his protection*, they said, so no one interferes with the—" Bett's mouth worked. "With the tide. Your gran signed it. They've moved him behind the Wardens' doors, Wren, where none of us can reach him till they carry him down themselves."

The vigil-house. Wren saw it: the tall shuttered building on the square where she'd stolen the ledger, where Fen's name already stood written in Gran's hand under a column of the dead. They'd put him inside the very place the bargain was kept. Of course they had. She should have known they'd move him the moment the third night came.

"Then we go tonight regardless," she said. "We don't go through the vigil-house. We go down the Deepings and reach him at the water before they finish. Rook, the old adit at the ropewalk end — you said it still opens to the workings—"

"Wren." Bett hadn't finished. That was the thing about bad news; there was always more underneath, like water under a rotten floor. "That's not why I came. The meeting — Mabb's meeting — someone told."

Rook went very still. "Told who."

"The Wardens." Bett pressed her knuckles to her mouth. "One of us. I don't — I won't say who, it doesn't matter now, they were frightened, they've a hollowing girl of their own and the Wardens promised — promised she'd be *spared* the tide if the network was given up. So they gave it up. All of it. The cellar off Chapel Row, the vigil we've been keeping there—" Her voice dropped to nothing. "They came in an hour ago. Coll and six of them. They took Mabb."

"Mabb," Wren said stupidly.

"And they've got—" Bett looked at Rook, and couldn't finish, and didn't have to.

Because there were boots on the stair again, more of them this time, unhurried, the tread of men who knew the door only opened one way. Rook moved fast — shoved his tally-book down the back of his shirt, caught Wren's sleeve and hauled her toward the coal-hatch at the far wall, the one that let onto the ropewalk alley. "The hatch. Go. *Go*—"

She got her fingers under the wood. The night air came through cold and smelling of tar. Behind her the cellar door burst inward and lantern-light flooded the low room, and a Warden's voice said, calm as chapel, "Ashby. Stay where you are."

She turned. She couldn't not. Rook had put himself square between her and the light, his back to her, his shoulders set, buying her the width of the doorway with his own body. "Run," he said, not looking round. "You know the way. Run, Wren, and don't you dare stop for me."

Then a Warden had him by the collar and slammed him into the wall hard enough that the tallow-saucer jumped, and another was reaching past him toward the hatch, toward her, and there was nothing to do with the reaching hand but be gone from in front of it. She went through the coal-hatch on her belly into the dark of the alley, and the last she saw of Rook Ashby was the side of his face pressed to the stones and his eyes finding hers through the men's legs, and his mouth making the word again — *run* — before the hatch-lid she kicked shut behind her took the light away.

She ran.

She ran the ropewalk fences and the peat-stacks and the black gaps between houses where no lamp showed, and the whole town seemed to be pulling its shutters as she passed, board over board, a thousand small refusals to see. By the time she fetched up gasping in the lee of her own doorstep, the fear had caught her fully and her hands wouldn't hold still. Rook. Mabb. The network she'd only just found, folded up in an hour like a hand of cards. And Fen behind the Wardens' doors.

The door opened before she touched it. Alys stood in it — her mother, apron still on, a keeping-flame guttering low on the table behind her, and Wren saw at once that she knew. Someone had run to her too.

"You'll come in," Alys said, "and you'll stop this."

"Mam—"

"You'll *stop*." Alys pulled her over the threshold and held her by both arms, and her mother's face was not angry, which Wren could have fought; it was broken open, which she could not. "I've had one child chosen. I will not stand in that door and watch them carry you down after him. Do you hear me? I won't."

"They've taken him to the vigil-house. Tonight they give him to the dark. And I can reach him — Mam, I can, there's a way—"

"There is no way." Alys's voice cracked straight down the middle. "Do you think you're the first? Do you think I didn't—" She stopped. Her hands were shaking on Wren's arms. "When Nell was chosen. Your aunt. I was younger than you are now and I loved her more than my own breath, and I had a *way*, I had a plan, I had it all worked out same as you've got that look on you now. And Mother — your gran — she sat me down and she said, *if you go after her, I lose two daughters instead of one, and the dark takes you both, and Nethercote burns cold besides.* And I believed her. God forgive me, I let them lead Nell down and I stayed by my own hearth and I told myself it was love that kept me there." The keeping-flame threw her face into hollows. "It wasn't love. It was fear wearing love's coat. And I have carried Nell every day of my life since, and I am *begging* you — don't hand me another one to carry. Stay. Please. Just stay."

For a long moment Wren couldn't speak, because her mother had just handed her the shape of the whole thing — the thing Gran did to daughters, the thing the Wardens did to the whole town — *stay by your own hearth, keep to your own fire, and let the dark take its own.* Isolation dressed as safety. Every shut shutter she'd run past. The bargain wasn't only kept at the mine. It was kept in this doorway, in her mother's shaking hands, in three generations of Cawley women told that the loving thing was to stand still.

She put her arms around her mother and held her, and felt how thin she'd got, and hated all of it.

"I'm not Nell," she said into Alys's shoulder. "And you're not Gran. You didn't choose him, Mam. She did." She stepped back. Outside, faint and low and rising, she could hear it beginning — that sound like wind down a shaft, the dark-tide coming up under the town. The wick under her own breastbone burned bright and steady, brighter than her mother's, brighter than anyone's, and for once she was glad of the terrible Cawley brightness, because tonight she was going to need every coal of it. "I love you. Bar the door after me and keep your flame."

"Wren—"

But she was already turning for the moor, and the mine, and the black stair down. Rook was caught. Mabb was taken. There was no network left and no army and no clever plan — only her, and a stub of light in her chest, and hours.

She went alone.

# Chapter 8 — Down the Deepings

The dark-tide came up over Nethercote the way water comes up in a sinking boat — not all at once, but from below, and everywhere, and with no place left dry.

Wren felt it before she heard it, a pressure behind the breastbone, a leaning of the whole night toward the moor. Then the sound arrived, low and endless, wind down a shaft that had no bottom and no wind in it, and one by one the last unshuttered windows of the town went dark. She stood in the black between two peat-stacks and watched Nethercote fold itself away. Board over board. Bar over bar. In each house a single hearth-flame kept alone behind its own door, a hundred little lights that never touched, and the custom she'd run past all her life she now saw whole: this was the shape of the bargain made visible. A town taught to burn apart.

And down the square, from the tall doors of the vigil-house, the Wardens came out in procession.

She pressed flat to the stone and watched them pass at the head of the lane — Warden Coll first with the great lantern, and behind him the others in their dark coats, unhurried, chanting the rhyme under their breath so it came to her in pieces. *Light to light, and none alone; keep your fire, and hold your own.* And in the middle of them, small between two grown men, walking because they led him and not because he wished to go anywhere at all, was Fen.

Even at that distance she knew the wrongness of him. Her brother had never in his life walked so quiet. He'd have been three steps ahead or ten behind, stooping for a bright pebble, a bone, a button. Now he went where the hand on his shoulder sent him, his head turned toward the moor like a compass toward its north, and around him — she had to bite down not to make a sound — around him the air was moving. Ash-moths. A slow black drift of them, thick as the omen ever came, settling on his collar and his hair and lifting and settling again, the visible sign that his wick was all but out.

They were taking him to the mine-mouth to give him down. And if she went after the procession she'd be seen and caught and it would all end in the square.

So she didn't. She went the other way.

Rook had told her once, in the ropewalk cellar, that the old workings had more than one throat. The Wardens used the mine-mouth on the moor, the ceremonial way, wide and stepped. But at the ropewalk end there was an old adit, an air-drift the diggers had cut generations back, half-choked with fallen stone and long forbidden — and it let, if you could get through it, onto the head of the spiral stair. She'd bring no procession and no chant. She'd bring a lantern, a keeping-flame stolen from her own hearth and shielded in a horn case, and she'd bring the one thing that was only Fen's.

She took the brass button out of her pocket and closed her hand round it. He'd lost it on the moor the night his wick snuffed — the night she'd looked away at the wrong moment, the moment she would spend the rest of her life not having back. She'd found it after, in the peat, and kept it. It was warm from her pocket. It was the plainest, most ordinary thing in the world, and it was a thread, and she was going to follow it all the way down and tie it round her brother and haul him back into the light.

The adit was where Rook said. She hauled the rotten boards away and squeezed through the gap into cold that met her like a wall.

It was not the cold of a winter night. Winter cold was clean. This was old and wet and it had a smell in it — wet ash, coal-damp, the reek of a fire long drowned — and it did not just chill her skin. It reached. She'd gone ten steps down the sloping drift, her lantern throwing its small yellow ball ahead, when she understood what it reached for. The warmth under her breastbone, her wick, her bright steady Cawley coal — the cold *leaned* on it, the way the dark-tide leaned on the town, and she felt her own light give back a fraction, a guttering, before she gritted her teeth and held it. *Find your own light first. Feel it clear.* She found it. She held it clear. She went on.

The drift met the stair.

She'd known there'd be a stair. She had not known it would be like this. It spiralled down out of the reach of her lantern, cut straight into the living rock, treads worn to shallow dishes by however many feet had gone down them and never come up, and it turned round a central well of nothing, and out of the nothing came the sound — the dark-tide, close now, the endless shaftwind — and under it, further down, a slow black lapping. Water. There was water below, and it was rising to meet her.

She went down.

The lantern was the whole world. Beyond its edge the dark was not empty; it was full, the way deep water is full, pressing. The cold came up the stair to meet her step for step and it worked at the wick the whole way, patient, and she found she was talking to keep it clear — not aloud, or not much, just naming warm things under her breath the way Gran counted wicks. *The hearth. The keeping-flame. Fen's hands before they went cold. The button. The button. Warm from my pocket.* The horn-shielded flame threw her shadow wheeling round and round the well, huge and then small and then huge, and once she thought the shadow moved when she hadn't, and she did not let herself think about that again.

The ash-moths began about halfway down.

First one, blundering up out of the dark to bat at the lantern-horn. Then a dozen. Then they were a soft black storm around the light, so thick she had to keep a hand cupped over the flame to keep them off it, and their small dry bodies brushed her face and caught in her lashes and she thought, *this is what settled on Fen, this is his omen, this is the air the dark breathes out,* and she went down through them with her jaw set and the button clenched till its edge bit her palm.

The water came sooner than she wanted.

Her boot went in to the ankle before she saw it — the stair simply continued down into black water, level and still and giving back her lantern in a shivering yellow smear. Flooded, from here on. The old mine, drowned. The cold of it climbed her leg and gripped, and her wick shrank from it so hard she gasped and had to stop, one hand flat to her own chest, hauling her light back up from wherever it had tried to flee. She stood on the last dry tread with the black water at her shin and understood that going on meant going *into* it, and that the cold in the water would go for her wick the way nothing yet had.

She thought about Rook, held against a wall, saying *don't you dare stop for me.* She thought about her mother's hands. She thought about Fen walking quiet between two men with the moths in his hair.

She stepped down into the water.

It took her to the waist and it was worse than she'd braced for, a cold that was almost a sound, and her light dropped low and guttered and for one plunging instant she felt the hollow reach right into the middle of her — that emptied, hungry, quiet feeling she'd watched come over her brother — and she nearly went under with the shock of it. But she got her palm to her sternum in the black and she *held*, she poured her own want back into her own wick the way Rook had taught her to pour it into another, and the flame steadied, and the coal in her chest burned up bright again and pushed the cold back a hand's breadth. Not far. But far enough to wade.

She waded. The stair continued under the water, she could feel the drowned treads with her feet, and she went down them into the black holding the lantern high over the surface, following the only thread she had, and the moths thinned as she got below them and the dark-tide sound got into her bones, and there was nothing in the whole flooded world but her small yellow light and the cold and the button in her fist and the going-down.

She lost count of the treads. Time went strange in the dark. She might have been an hour going down; she might have been longer. The water rose to her chest and stayed there, and the ceiling of the working dropped close over her head, dripping, and she bent under it and kept on, and told herself the whole time there would be nothing at the bottom but water and cold and her brother somewhere off through the drowned dark where she would find him.

She was wrong about the nothing.

She came round the last turn of the well into a wider dark, a drowned cavern where her lantern couldn't find the far wall, and she stopped dead in the water because there — far off, low across the black surface, where the deepest part of the Deepings should have held nothing but the sleeping cold — there was a light.

It was small. It was steady. It was the yellow of a held flame, a lantern or a wick burning honest against the dark, and it had no business existing in the drowned heart of a dead mine leagues below the town, and it did not flicker when the dark-tide breathed. It simply burned, far below her and further in, the way a thing burns that has been burning a very long time.

Wren stood to her chest in black water with her own flame shaking in her fist and stared at the light that shouldn't be there.

And out of the dark around it, thin and dry and worn to almost nothing, unmistakably a voice, and it spoke to her across all that black water in a tone of terrible tiredness, as if it had been waiting for her a great deal longer than she had been alive.

"Wren," it said. "Wren Cawley. You came down after all."

# Chapter 9 — Morren

The light did not come to her. She had to go to it.

Wren waded across the drowned cavern with her lantern held high and her heart going hard, and the small steady flame ahead grew as she came on until she could see what held it: a figure, seated, on a shelf of black rock that rose just clear of the water at the cavern's heart. An island the size of a hearthstone. And on it, cross-legged, still as the stone itself, an old woman cupping a single flame in her two hands.

Except *old* was not the word, and *woman* was only half of it.

Wren stopped a few feet off, standing to her waist in the black water, and made herself look. The figure was made of two things that should not have been in one body. Part of her was a person — a face, ancient beyond any face in Nethercote, the skin drawn thin as wet paper over the bones, a fall of hair gone past white to the grey of old ash. And part of her was the dark itself. Where the flame's small light didn't reach her she seemed to go on into the cold, to have no edge, to be of a piece with the drowned black around them, so that Wren could not have said where the old woman ended and the Deepings began. Ash-moths sat on her shoulders and did not stir. And between her cupped hands burned the flame — small, honest, and failing, guttering low and steadying and guttering again, the way a wick burns when there is almost nothing left to feed it.

"You know my name," Wren said. Her voice came out steadier than she felt. "Say yours."

Something moved across the ancient face that might once have been a smile. "It's a long time since anyone came down far enough to be owed it." The voice was dry as moth-wings, and it came as much from the dark as from the mouth. "I am Morren Cawley. I lit the first vigil in Nethercote, when there was a Nethercote to light. I came down this stair to hold the dark so the town above could keep its warmth." The flame in her hands dipped, and she cupped it closer, tender, a mother with a cold child. "That was — I have lost the count of it. A great many dark-tides ago."

Wren's own light shook in her fist. *Morren Cawley.* She'd heard the name in Gran's terrible voice, in the ledger's first pages — the First Lamplighter, the founding, the reason the Cawleys carried the bargain. She'd thought it a story to frighten children into their own hearths. And here it sat on a rock in the drowned dark, holding a flame that had not gone out in a hundred lifetimes, and it knew her by her face.

"You're — but you'd be dead," Wren said. "Centuries dead."

"Yes." Morren said it without weight, the way you'd agree it was cold. "I would. That is the trouble." She turned her cupped hands a little so the small flame lit the ruin of her face. "When I came down to hold the Ashen, I gave it my light to keep it fed and sleeping — a little, always a little, so it would not have to take the whole town. And it took me in exchange. Half of me. It let the rest keep hold of a flame so there'd always be a flame to feed it." Her not-quite-there shoulders lifted. "I cannot die while I hold it. I have wanted to for a long, long while. But if I let go, the flame goes out, and the dark that I have held here so long comes up the stair — up into all those little shuttered rooms — and takes them all in one night. So I hold. Do you see? I have held so that they above could keep to their own hearths and never know what I keep down here on their behalf."

Wren thought of the town folding itself away, board over board. A hundred lights that never touched. Kept safe, kept apart, kept ignorant — by this.

"The Wardens," she said. "The tithe. They give you a child every dark-tide—"

"They give the *Ashen* a child." Something like grief crossed Morren's face and did not have the strength to stay. "Not me. Never me. When the flame I hold burns low and the dark wakes and stirs for more, the town above feeds it a lone wick to buy it back to sleep. A bright one, if they can. Cawley wicks burn brightest and hold the dark the longest — which is why the dark loves them best, and why it is always my own line the ledger takes." The flame guttered; she breathed on it, and it steadied, and Wren understood with a lurch that the old woman's whole existence had narrowed to that one small task, keeping that one flame alive, hour upon hour upon age. "Every tithe buys a few years' sleep. And then it wakes hungry again. It never ends, child. The bargain does not end. It only feeds."

"Then I'll end it." Wren's teeth were chattering; she made them stop. "I've come for my brother. Fen. They took him. I can bring him back — I can share my wick, I know how, I'll relight him and I'll take him home and we'll—"

"No." The word was quiet and absolute, and it stopped Wren cold. Morren was looking at her with those ancient eyes and there was pity in them, which was worse than any warning. "You cannot fight it that way. Listen to me, and hear it, for I will not have breath to say it twice. The Ashen is not a beast you can strike. It has no throat to cut, no heart to stop. It is a hunger and a cold, and it is old past your reckoning, and *force is nothing to it.* A lone flame it snuffs. Two lone flames it snuffs harder — I have watched them try, down the years, the ones who came alone to save someone alone. Do you know what a shared wick is, brought down here by itself?" She leaned forward, and the dark leaned with her. "Two hollows instead of one. It welcomes you. It has room for you both."

"Then how?" Wren heard the crack in her own voice and hated it. "There has to be a *how*. You wouldn't have — you didn't hold on for a hundred lifetimes so you could tell me it can't be done."

And there — there — a light came into the old ruined face, faint as the flame in her hands, but real. "No," Morren said softly. "I held on to tell you it *can*." She looked at the small failing flame she cupped, and then out at the vast cold dark that pressed on every side of their little light, and then back at Wren. "The Ashen cannot be broken. But it can be *outshone*. A flame it can snuff, because a flame stands alone, and anything that stands alone can be put out — a wick, a lantern, an old woman on a rock. But light that will not stand alone—" Her voice dropped, and the words came out worn smooth, as if she had turned them over in the dark for centuries waiting for an ear to say them into. "Light that will not stand alone cannot be put out. That is the whole of it. That is the thing your gran and hers and hers before them never learned, or learned and buried, because the buried truth kept the town obedient. The Ashen has never once faced the whole of Nethercote's light joined together — because the Wardens keep every hearth apart. Shame. Silence. *Keep your fire, and hold your own.* They have kept the town small enough to feed on, one lone wick at a time, for longer than either of us can count." Her hands trembled around the flame. "I have been alone down here so long. That is why I am failing. Not because the dark is stronger. Because a light kept alone always, always burns down in the end. Even mine."

Wren stood in the black water and felt the size of it settle on her — not just Fen, not just tonight, but the whole shape of what would have to happen, and how far it was past one girl with a lantern. Bring the town together. Make Nethercote, which had been taught since its founding to burn apart, join its light. It was impossible. It was the only thing that wasn't.

"I don't have the town," she said. "I have a lantern and a button and — and me. Rook's caught. Mabb's taken. There's no one left up there who'd even—"

"Then you have tonight to lose," Morren said, "and a brother to lose it for. One thing at a time, Cawley. First the boy." And the pity in her face sharpened suddenly into something urgent, something almost like fear, and she uncupped one hand from her flame — the small light shrank at once, and the dark pressed nearer, and even that cost her — and she pointed. Past Wren. Deeper. Into the black where the cavern narrowed and dropped and the dark-tide sound came up thickest, where the water ran on into the true heart of the Deepings.

"You hear that?" Morren whispered.

Wren went still. Under the endless shaftwind, under the lap of black water, there was something new — voices, a chant carried down through the rock, *light to light and none alone,* and the small dragging scuff of feet that did not want to walk, coming down and down toward the water's edge.

"They are not waiting for the ceremony above," Morren said, and her failing flame shook as she held out her pointing hand. "They are bringing him to the relighting hollow — to the heart, to the water, to *it.* Now. Tonight. This hour." Her ancient eyes fixed on Wren's, and for the first time all the tiredness in them was burned away by something fierce and old and unbearably kind. "Go, child. Go quickly. Your brother is almost at the edge of the dark — and once he is given down, no light on earth, joined or alone, will bring him back up."

# Chapter 10 — The Relighting Hollow

The stair gave out onto the heart of the Deepings, and Wren stopped at the edge of it because there was nowhere left to go but down.

The cavern was vast in a way that hurt to look at — a drowned bell of stone with the roof lost somewhere in the dark above, and beneath it a lake of black water that did not move and did not shine. It swallowed the light of her lantern whole. Where the flame should have laid a gold road across the surface there was only a hole in the world, and the cold came off it in slow breaths that smelled of wet ash and coal-damp, the smell she would have known in her sleep now, the smell of the thing that had taken her brother.

There were lights at the water's edge. Warden lamps, four of them, ranged in a careful line along a lip of stone that ran down to meet the lake. And between the lamps and the black water stood a small figure, and Wren's whole body understood who it was before her mind would let her say it.

"Fen," she said.

Her voice went out across the water and came back to her thinned, as if the cavern had taken the warmth out of it too.

Warden Coll turned. His hard face in the lamplight was not cruel — that was the worst of it, she would think later; it was only tired, and certain, a man doing a terrible thing because he had decided long ago that it was the kind thing. "You shouldn't be here, Cawley girl," he said. "Go back up. You don't want to carry this in your eyes."

She didn't answer him. She was already going down the stone lip, half sliding, the lantern swinging, the brass button clenched so hard in her other fist that its edges had bitten a shape into her palm. Coll moved to stop her and one of the other Wardens caught his sleeve — some argument passed between them that she didn't hear, some old flinch in men who had done this before and knew who the girl was and whose blood — and in the gap of it she got past them and dropped to her knees in front of her brother.

Up close he was worse than she had feared. Fen had always been a warm, restless, sticky-fingered creature, forever crouched over some treasure, buttons and bird-bones and the clean round pebbles he loved. The boy in front of her now was still and grey. His lips had gone the colour of the water. His eyes were open and turned, without any particular interest, toward the black lake, the way she had seen the hollowed look toward the moor — that dull leaning of the whole self toward the dark, as if the dark were a warm room and he was cold in the doorway. Ash-moths sat on his shoulders and in his hair, folding and unfolding their small black wings, unhurried, at home.

"Fen. It's me. It's Wren." She took his face in her hands and his skin was so cold it frightened her more than anything the Wardens could have said. "Look at me. Look at me, not the water."

His eyes came round to her slowly. There was nothing behind them she could reach. "Cold," he said, in a small voice that was almost his and almost not. "It wants me to come in. It's warm in there."

"It isn't warm. It's the coldest thing there is. It's lying to you." She pressed the brass button into his open hand and folded his stiff fingers around it. "Look. Look what I brought. You lost it on the moor, remember? You cried about it. You made me go back out past dark to look for it and I said you were a menace and I went anyway. It's the ugly brass one off Da's old coat. It was in your pocket the whole time, you daft thing — I found it after."

Fen looked down at the button in his hand. For a moment nothing. The moths stirred. Then his thumb moved, once, the smallest thing, tracing the raised anchor stamped in the brass — the way he'd worn it smooth doing exactly that, a hundred evenings by the hearth while she pretended to be annoyed at the fidgeting.

"Da's coat," he said.

"Da's coat," she said, and her throat closed. "You've got the whole collection at home. The good pebbles. The jay's feather. The little grey bone you swore was a dragon's. I kept them for you. I kept the box on the sill where you left it. You have to come home and count them, Fen, because I don't know the order and you'll be furious."

Something flickered in his face — a boy's exasperation, the ghost of it, *you'd get the order wrong, you always do* — and for one breath his eyes were only her brother's eyes, huge and scared and nine years old, looking up at her out of a cold he couldn't climb out of alone.

"Wren," he whispered. "I can't feel it anymore. The warm bit. Under here." His free hand pressed flat against his own chest, over the place where his wick should have been burning. "It went out and I can't find it."

"I know," she said. "I know, love. I'm going to help you find it."

And she knew what she was going to do, had known since the ropewalk, since Rook had turned her hand over and pressed his thumb to the centre of her palm and told her the shape of it and told her, grimly, not to. *Palm to the sternum. Let it want to go. It burns like nothing you've felt, and it takes and takes, and you have to stop before it takes too much — but you won't want to stop, that's the trap of it, so decide now how much you'll give.*

She hadn't decided. She realised that only now, kneeling on the cold stone. She had never once decided how much. The answer had been *all of it* the whole time and she simply hadn't let herself hear it.

"Cawley girl." Coll's voice, close behind her now, low and almost pleading. "It doesn't work. One flame to one flame, down here, in its own house — it can't be done. You'll only give it two of you to swallow instead of one. Idony knows it. She's watched it fail. Don't make me carry that up as well."

"Then don't watch," Wren said, and laid her palm flat against her brother's chest.

She reached down inside herself for the warmth under her own sternum — the Cawley ember, the bright one, the one Gran counted and the Ashen wanted — and she opened her hand around it and let it want to go.

It went like fire drawn through the eye of a needle.

She had thought she understood pain. She had skinned her knees and burned her fingers and once broken her wrist falling off the chapel wall. This was none of those. This was her own light leaving her by the width of a palm, and every part of it screamed to stay. The burn started in the centre of her hand and ran up the bones of her arm and into her chest and it was as if someone were pouring the heat of her straight out of a wound. She heard herself make a sound she didn't recognise. The lantern tipped and guttered on the stone. The ash-moths rose off Fen in a startled cloud.

*Decide how much,* Rook had said.

She gave until she felt her own edges going thin. She gave past the point where the cold began to answer in her — a small creeping frost starting where her wick had been full, a hollow draught opening in her chest, the first true breath of the thing she had watched come for her brother now turning, mildly, curiously, toward her. She felt herself begin to lean. Toward the water. Toward the warm-cold room. She understood, in a distant appalled way, that this was how it started, that this was the doorway, that she was standing in it now.

And under her hand — Fen's heart caught.

It was the smallest thing. A stir of warmth against her palm, a coal breathed back to orange, a wick guttering up out of nothing. Colour came into his lips. He gasped like a swimmer breaking surface, and his cold hand closed hard around her wrist, and his eyes — his eyes were his, all the way down, terrified and alive.

"Wren — you're cold — Wren, you've gone *cold* —"

"It's all right," she lied. Her voice had frost in it. Her hand, when she took it back from his chest, was grey to the knuckle. "It worked. See? You've got it back. You've got your warm bit."

Two small flames, then, at the edge of the black water. Hers guttering low and his newly lit and fragile, and both of them alone, and both of them burning in the one place in all the world where the dark was most awake.

The lake answered.

It did not rise like water. It rose like night — a swelling of the cold and the dark and the old bottomless hunger, lifting off the surface of the lake in a wave that had no crest, only a leaning weight that put out the nearest Warden lamp as easily as breath. The ash-moths came with it, not a cloud now but a storm, a howling black snow of them pouring up out of the water and around the two small lights, battering at Wren's face, catching in her mouth, and behind them the cold reached in — she felt it reach, felt it find the two lone flames the way a hand finds two candles in a dark room, and close.

Fen screamed. She threw herself over him, both arms, her body between him and the water, as if a body could be a wall, as if anything of hers still had warmth enough to matter.

*Two hollows instead of one,* she thought, wildly, and it was Gran's voice saying it.

And then there was light above them.

It came down the black cavern from the mouth of the stair — a single lantern, held high, its flame steady in a fist that did not shake. It threw Wren's own huddled shadow long across the water. The moth-storm broke around it and streamed.

Wren lifted her face out of her brother's hair and looked up.

Gran stood on the stair with the lantern raised over her head, her white hair loose, her old sharing-scar bright on the hand that held the light — Idony Cawley, Chief Lamplighter, who had written Fen's name in the book before his wick ever guttered, who had led her own daughter down into this dark and never spoken of it since.

She was looking at the two small failing flames of her grandchildren, and at the risen dark that had come to eat them, and her closed door of a face was, for the first time Wren had ever seen, open all the way to the grief behind it.

# Chapter 11 — What Gran Gave

Gran came down the last of the stair the way she did everything, without hurry, as if the risen dark were a draught in a doorway she had lived with all her life. Which, Wren understood, it was.

The moth-storm gave way in front of her lantern and closed again behind it. Warden Coll went to her at once, his voice pitched low and urgent — "Idony, it's begun, the girl shared, there's two of them lit now, you have to finish it before the whole thing wakes" — and Gran walked past him as though he weren't there. She came to the water's edge and looked down at Wren and Fen huddled on the cold stone, and Wren, kneeling with her grey hand cradled against her chest and her brother's fragile new warmth shivering against her side, looked up and did not know, for one long moment, whether the woman above her had come to save them or to give them down.

"You shared," Gran said. Her eyes had gone to Wren's hand — the greyed knuckles, the frost creeping up from the palm. "You foolish, foolish child. Look what you've done to yourself. Look what's coming into you."

"You wrote his name," Wren said. Her voice shook, cold-thinned. "You wrote it before he even went out. You chose him."

"Yes." No excuse in it, no softening. The lantern didn't waver in the old woman's fist. "I chose him because his wick is the brightest in Nethercote save yours, and the brightest hold the dark the longest, and a Cawley given down buys the town more years than any other child. I have known the arithmetic of it since before your mother was born. I have never once been able to make it wrong." She looked out over the black lake, and something moved in her face that Wren had never been let see. "I stood on this stone once before with a lantern in my hand. There was a girl at the water then, too. Cold, and leaning toward it, the way your brother was. She had my chin. She had my temper." A pause you could have fallen into. "Her name was Nell. She was mine. My daughter. And I brought her down that stair myself, and I told her it was to save the town, and I let the dark take her, and I climbed back up alone and told Nethercote that the flood had her."

"Gran—"

"I told myself I was strong," Gran said. "I told myself that was what it was to be strong — to give what you loved so that others kept theirs. To let the child go down into the dark so no one had to see the town starve. I have said the rhyme over forty children in forty years. *Keep your fire, and hold your own.*" She said it like something rotten in her mouth. "And every one of them went down that stair alone. Every single one. Because that is the law we made. Nell went alone. I made sure of it. I thought that was mercy. I thought a clean quick going-alone was kinder than the whole town failing slow." Her voice cracked at last, straight across the middle. "I have been cold in the chest since the day I climbed back up without her. Do you understand me, Wren? I gave her, and it hollowed me, and I have been half in that water for forty years, and it did not save anything. It only bought time. It always only buys time. And then the tide comes again and there is another child at the edge."

The lake was rising as she spoke. The cold was pressing in against the two small flames, Wren's and Fen's, closing the room down around them, and Wren felt her own light stagger under it — felt the leaning start again, the terrible warm invitation of the dark, *come in, it's warm in here, you're so tired* — and she gripped Fen and dug her nails into her own cold palm to stay in the world.

"It's snuffing us," she managed. "Gran. It's going to take both of us and there's nothing—"

Wren thought of the ledger then, the long column of names in the Cawley hand going back and back, each one a child led down this same stair by someone who loved them and had decided that love meant letting go. Nell near the top of it. Fen at the bottom, already written, in this old woman's careful hand. Forty years of arithmetic, and every sum came out the same, and every child paid it alone.

"There is one thing," Gran said.

She came down off the last step and knelt — Idony Cawley, who Wren had never once in her life seen kneel, not in chapel, not at a graveside — knelt on the cold wet stone between her grandchildren and the black water, and she set her lantern down beside them where its flame stood straight and unafraid, and she opened her old scarred hand.

"Coll," she said, without turning. "Take your Wardens up the stair. This is the last relighting. There will not be another after tonight, one way or the other. Go up, and whatever you hear, do not come down." And whatever the enforcer saw in her face, he obeyed it; Wren heard the Wardens' lamps retreating up the spiral, and the dark leaned closer where they had been.

Gran laid her open palm flat against Fen's chest, and her other hand she pressed to Wren's — over the sternum, over the frost, over the failing Cawley ember — and she bowed her white head between them.

"No," Wren said, understanding. "No, Gran, you'll hollow, you'll go, don't—"

"Child," Gran said, almost gently, "I hollowed forty years ago. There is only this much left of me, and I have finally found the one thing worth spending it on." She lifted her head and looked at Wren, and for the first time in Wren's whole life there was no door in her grandmother's face, none, only an old woman who had loved terribly and wrongly and was, at the very end, choosing to love right. "I let one child go down into the dark alone. I will not do it a second time. Not you. Not him. Not while I have a coal left to give."

Then she gave it.

Wren felt it come — not a needle's-width like her own careful sharing, but a flood, an old banked fire opening all at once, the whole guttering remainder of Idony Cawley pouring out through her two hands into the children she had condemned. Warmth roared back into Wren's chest, shocking, painful, her greyed hand flushing pink to the knuckle; beside her Fen gasped and sat up straight as his fragile flame caught and steadied and burned. And it did not stop there. Gran turned her scarred palm toward the water — toward the deep, toward the single ancient failing flame that Wren knew was burning far below in Morren Cawley's keeping hand, the first light, the oldest, the one that had held all of this since the founding and was going out from sheer loneliness — and Gran gave to that too, sent her warmth down into the dark to the woman who had held it alone for centuries.

"Light to light," Gran said, and her voice was fading even as she said it, thinning like Wren's had thinned, "and none alone."

The dark recoiled.

It was only a moment — Wren felt it, the great cold hunger flinching back from the sudden joined warmth of four flames burning as one, Gran's and hers and Fen's and Morren's, threaded together palm to palm to water. The moth-storm faltered. The risen lake sagged. For one breath the Ashen drew back from the edge of the stone like a tide going out, baffled, as if it had reached for two lone candles and closed its hand on something it could not put out.

One breath. And then Wren felt Gran begin to go.

The warmth still poured, but the hand on her chest had gone light, and lighter, the grip loosening, the old woman swaying where she knelt. Gran had spent forty years' worth of banked fire in a handful of heartbeats and there was almost nothing left of her now, and Wren caught her by the shoulders as she listed, and felt through her thin frame how little was in there, how cold she had gone, how far she had leaned already toward the warm dark room she had stood in the doorway of for forty years.

And even so — even with all of it spent — the dark was already coming back.

Wren felt it return through the soles of her feet, the cold gathering itself off the black water, unhurried, patient, a thing that had all of time. Four joined flames had made it flinch. Four had not made it stop. It leaned in again at the edge of the stone and the moths began, once more, to rise. Gran's given warmth was already thinning against it, four small lights against a lake of night, and Wren understood, with a clarity that was almost calm, that this was the wall they had all broken on. Nell alone. Merron alone. Forty children alone. And now four of them together, and it was more, it was so much more — and it was still not enough. It would never be enough, not four, not ten, not a hundred lone flames spent one at a time in the dark.

Old Mabb's voice came back to her out of the bereaved underground, dry and certain: *It has never faced the whole town's light at once, because the Wardens keep everyone apart.*

Not four flames. Not the bright doomed few who found their way down here alone.

All of them. Every hearth in Nethercote, every wick the Wardens had taught to burn behind its own shut door — brought together, joined, carried down into the dark as one light that would not stand alone and so could not be put out.

Wren gathered her brother against her, his small heart beating warm and real under her hand at last, and she got her arm under her fading grandmother, and she turned her face up toward the long black throat of the stair and the town shuttering itself above it, each behind its own lone flame, exactly as it had been taught.

"Fen," she said. "We have to get up. We have to get you home, and then I have to make the whole town come down here — all of it, together — or none of this holds." She felt her own barely-relit wick tremble at the size of it. "Can you climb?"

Fen looked at her with her brother's eyes, and closed his cold small hand around Da's brass button, and nodded.

# Chapter 12 — The Long Climb

The stair went up forever, and the water came up faster.

Wren had Fen's arm slung over her shoulders and one hand fisted in the back of his shirt, and she was climbing on legs that no longer felt like hers. He was heavier than a nine-year-old ought to be — heavier the way wet rope was heavy, the way a thing that could not help you hold itself was heavy. Behind them, below them, the Deepings breathed. The cold came up the spiral in a slow tide, black water lapping two steps under her heels and then one, and with it came the sound she had learned to hate — the low, hollow wind-down-a-shaft moan of the dark-tide, and under it a hunger with no throat to make it.

"Keep your feet," she said, to Fen, to herself. "Up. One more. That's it."

Fen's lips moved. No sound. His wick was in him again — she had put it there, some of hers, palm to his sternum, the burn of it still a raw coal in her own chest — but it was a candle in a gale, and she could feel how little of it there was. She could feel, too, the place in her own body where the warmth used to sit steady under the breastbone, and how it sat there now like an ember someone had breathed on wrong. Cold crept out from it in thin threads. Hollowing. She knew the word from the inside now.

Ash-moths came off the water in a soft black storm, blundering into her cheeks, her lashes, her open mouth. She spat and climbed.

"Wren." A shape swung down out of the dark above her — she nearly struck at it before she knew the voice. "Wren, it's me, it's me —"

Rook. Rook Ashby, of all the impossible things, his face slick and streaked, a raw split over one eyebrow crusted dark. He must have run every step of the Deepings on the strength of nothing but stubbornness. He got a look at Fen hanging off her and didn't waste breath on questions. He ducked under Fen's other arm and took the weight, and Wren's whole left side sang with relief.

"They raided the meeting," he said, hauling. "Coll's men. I got out a window. Followed the Wardens down the main shaft and then — I heard the water and I came the low way." He glanced at her, at whatever her face was doing. "You look half-snuffed."

"I shared," she said.

His jaw went tight. He didn't say the thing Gran had said, two hollows instead of one. He just took more of Fen's weight, and they climbed together, matched, and it was faster. It was faster and it was still not fast enough.

The water found her ankle. Ice — not the honest cold of moor-water but a cold that reached, that wanted, that knew there was a low flame in her to put out. She kicked free and drove upward and it came again, another step gained by the tide before they'd gained two.

"It's rising with us," Rook said. "It's herding us."

"Then we climb faster than it herds."

Below, far down the well of the stair, there was light — small, wrong, defiant. Two lights. Wren looked back once and saw them: Gran's lantern and, beyond it, Morren's single failing flame that had burned in the dark since before Nethercote had a name. The two old women stood in the drowned mouth of the relighting hollow with the black rising around their knees, and they were not climbing. They had set themselves like stones in a millrace, and the dark broke against them and slowed — a hand's breadth of slowness, a heartbeat bought and handed up the stair to Wren.

"Gran," Wren shouted down. "Gran, come up, come — "

"Go, girl." The voice came up thin and terrible and almost kind. "You know what you have to bring me. Go and bring it."

Wren went.

They came to the drowned landing where the stair doubled back, and here the old timbers had rotted to sponge and the water stood waist-deep and utterly still, which was worse than moving water, because still water in the Deepings was the Ashen paying attention. Rook went first with Fen half-floated between them, and Wren followed with her lantern lifted over her head, and the flame in it guttered and shrank as if something enormous had leaned close to breathe on it.

Halfway across, Fen slipped under.

He went without a splash — that was the horror of it, no thrash, no cry, just a boy who forgot he was a body sliding down into the black. Wren dropped the lantern's arm and plunged both hands after him and caught his collar, his hair, his cold small wrist, and hauled, and the water did not want to give him back. It held. For one endless second it held him against a pull she could not see, and she thought, with a calm that frightened her, *it will have him or it will have me, and I will not let go,* and then Rook had his other arm and they broke him up out of the water gasping — Fen actually gasping, a live sound, the best sound she had ever heard.

"Wren," he said. Her name. First word in three nights. "It's cold."

"I know, little wick. I know. We're going up to the fire."

She did not tell him there was no fire. Not yet. She would make one. She would make the whole town a fire if she had to knock on every shuttered door on the moor with her bare and hollowing hands.

They reached the upper stair where it turned to hewn rock and old iron rungs, and here they met Warden Coll.

He was coming down. That was the thing Wren would remember — he was coming down, against the tide, with a Warden's lantern in his fist and his hard dutiful face set, and she braced for him to be the last wall between her and the surface. Instead he looked at Fen — living, dripping, saying his sister's name — and something in the man's face broke quietly, the way ice breaks under snow, from underneath.

"The others turned back," Coll said. "When the water rose. They said the bargain was spoiled. They ran." He said it as if it were happening to someone else. "I served the bargain forty years. I told myself it saved us." He looked past her, down the well, at the two small lights holding in the flood. "It saved us the way a leech saves you. Bleed a little every winter and call it health."

"Then help me get him up," Wren said.

"I'll do better." He set his lantern in her free hand, folding her cold fingers around it, and his own hand was warm — he still had his wick, all of it, forty years' worth. "The landing below won't hold the water back much longer. Somebody has to keep the low door shut while your grandmother does what she's going to do. It only wants a body in the gap." He almost smiled. "I've the shoulders for a door."

"Coll — "

"Go up, Cawley." Not unkind. "Ring the bell. Do the thing we spent our lives making sure nobody would ever do." He was already turning, already going down into the cold with his empty hands, toward the still water and the leaning dark and the two old women who would not run. "Somebody ought to."

Wren did not have breath to argue and would not have won. She climbed. Behind and below she heard the water surge and heard a man set his shoulders against it, and she did not look back, because looking back was the sin Nethercote had built its whole cold church on, and she was done with it. She climbed with Fen and Rook and two dead men's lanterns, and she did not look back, and that was how she honored him.

The cold in her chest spread. She fed the climb with her own low wick and felt it dim another shade, and she thought of nothing, on purpose, but the next rung. There was a game to it, a grim arithmetic she could not stop doing — how much light was left to her, how many rungs it would buy, whether there would be any of her ember left at the top or only the cold. Every step she took, the ember answered a little slower, like a fire banked for the night, like a person going quietly to sleep in the snow. She knew that feeling now from the inside too. She named the rungs to keep from listening to it. Iron. Iron. Rotten wood. Iron.

"Nearly," Rook lied, beside her, and she loved him for the lie, and climbed.

Ash-moths thinned. The moan of the dark-tide changed pitch, and then — impossibly, blessedly — there was air that did not smell of wet ash but of peat-smoke and moor-cold and rain. The Deepings' mouth. The old adit, the black stone lip of it, and above it the low iron sky of Nethercote at the turn of the long dark.

Wren came up out of the earth on her hands and knees, dragging her brother into the night, and Rook came up beside her, and for a moment the three of them simply lay in the wet heather and breathed like things newly born, which was near enough to true.

Then Wren lifted her head and looked at the town, and her heart, low and cold as it was, went colder.

Nethercote was out there in the dark-tide — but shut. Every house on the slope stood black-shuttered behind its own lone door, and behind each door, she knew, a single family crouched over a single hearth, keeping its own fire, holding its own, exactly as the rhyme had taught them for a hundred hollowtides. Down in the market square the Wardens' vigil-fires burned — but scattered, one to a corner, deliberate, apart, each ringed by its own small watch, no two flames near enough to touch. A whole town full of light, and every light alone. A hundred candles that had never once been let to become a fire.

Below her feet, through the stone, she felt the Ashen turn its slow attention upward toward all that separate, lonely warmth, the way a hungry thing turns toward a laid table.

"We have to bring it together," she whispered. Her breath smoked. "All of it. Or he doesn't stay. None of them stay."

The vigil-bell hung in its little stone cote at the top of the square, cold and silent, rung only to mark the Wardens' hours. Wren got her feet under her, and Fen's hand in hers, and started down the slope toward it.


# Chapter 13 — Joining the Light

The bell-rope was crusted with frost and it tore the skin of Wren's palms when she pulled it, and she was glad of the hurt, because the hurt was warm.

She had climbed the little stone cote at the head of the market square with Fen huddled at its foot and Rook standing guard over him, and she had taken the vigil-bell's rope in both cold hands, and now she hauled on it as if she meant to pull the whole long dark down out of the sky. The bell was meant to be rung once an hour, softly, a Warden's clock. Wren rang it like an alarm, like a birth, like the end of something. The iron voice went out across the shuttered roofs and rolled off over the moor and came back off the fells, and rang again, and again, until the frozen air itself seemed to shake.

Shutters cracked. Doors opened a hand's width, throwing thin blades of firelight into the dark. Pale faces. All across Nethercote the town looked out at the one thing it had been taught its whole life never to do — a light lit loud, in the open, calling.

"Nethercote!" Wren's voice came out cracked and small and she made it bigger, made it carry, dragged it up out of the cold place in her chest. "Nethercote, bring your fires out! Bring them to the mine-mouth! Every hearth, every candle, every wick — bring it up the hill and bring it *together!*"

For a moment, nothing. A hundred doors, half-open, and behind them a hundred years of *keep your fire and hold your own.*

Then boots on cobbles, and the Wardens came.

They came out of the scattered vigil-fires in the square, four of them, five, the ones who had not gone down the shaft — and at their head a grey-faced woman Wren half-knew, Morren Cawley's namesake bell in her hand, the deputy who kept the hours. She planted herself at the foot of the cote and threw back her hood.

"Take your hand off that rope." Her voice shook, but not with doubt — with fright at the sheer size of the wrongness Wren was doing. "You'll wake it. Girl, you'll wake the whole dark down there. Keep to your own fire — that's the law. That's the *only* law. Light to light, and none alone; keep your fire, and hold your own —"

"You've been saying it wrong for a hundred years," Wren said.

She let go of the rope. The bell rang itself out, slow, into silence, and into that silence she spoke, and she did not have the strength to shout so she made them lean in to hear, and somehow that was stronger.

"*None alone,*" she said. "You thought it meant every hearth keeps to itself. It doesn't. It never did. It means *no light stands alone.* One flame the dark can snuff — I've been down there tonight, I've watched it do it, I've felt it try to do it to me." She held up her hand, and even in the firelight they could see it: the greyness in her, the low guttering of a Cawley wick gone dim. "That's why it takes the bright ones. That's why it took Nell, and Merron Ashby, and every child you told yourselves the dark *chose.* It didn't choose them. *You gave them.* One at a time, alone, because a flame alone is the only kind it can eat." She let that sit. "But it has never once — not in all your hundred years — faced the whole of us at once. Because you made *sure* of it. That's the whole trick. That's the whole bargain. Keep them apart, and feed it one."

"That is Warden business," the grey woman said, but her voice had thinned. "You don't know — "

"My grandmother is down there right now holding the door shut with her own life." Wren's voice broke and she let it. "So is Warden Coll. So is the first of us, Morren, who's held it alone since before your grandmother's grandmother. *Alone.* Every one of them, alone. I am done with alone."

She looked past the Wardens, then, over their heads, to the doors.

"You've all lost someone to the dark," she said. "Every house. And you weren't even let to grieve them out loud — the dark *chose* them, so you looked away, so you kept your fire and held your own and buried the shame with the body. I'm asking you to stop looking away. Bring your fire up the hill. Not to hold your own. To hold *each other.*"

Silence. Cold. The moan of the dark-tide down in the earth, rising.

Then Rook Ashby stepped out into the open where all of them could see him, and he had his sister's name in his mouth. "Merron Ashby," he said, loud, into the square, the first time anyone in Nethercote had said a hollowed child's name in the open in living memory. "She went hollow last winter and I couldn't hold her alone. Nobody would stand with me. That's the only reason she's gone." He turned a slow circle, meeting eyes. "Who else has a name they were never let to say?"

From the dark at the edge of the square, an old voice: "Tam Sowerby." A keeper, bent, one of the bereaved underground, come up out of hiding into the firelight. She lifted a rushlight in a shaking hand. "My Tam. Nine years this tide."

And then another, from a doorway: "Bess Colley." And a man's low voice: "Little Nan." And the freed keepers came up out of the cellars and the ginnels where the Wardens had scattered them, Rook's people, the ones who had always secretly kept their dead, and each of them carried a light and each of them said a name into the open air, and the saying of the names was itself a kind of fire, catching.

"You will *stop,*" the grey Warden said, but her voice was drowning now.

A door opened wide at the low end of the square. Full light spilled out, warm, unshuttered — a whole hearth's worth, carried out into the night in an iron fire-pot with two bare hands wrapped round the hot metal, and behind it a woman's face Wren would have known in any dark ever made.

Alys. Her mother.

"Alys Cawley, get back inside," the Warden said. "Your own mother-in-law is Chief Lamplighter, you know the — "

"My mother-in-law is dying in that hole for what she did to my Nell and my Tom and near did to my son." Alys's voice did not shake at all. She had been afraid for sixteen years and something in her had finally used the fear all up. She walked out into the middle of the square with her hearth-fire in her hands and the heat of it blistering her palms and she did not put it down. She walked to the foot of the cote, to Fen — living, dripping, blinking up at her — and her whole face came apart, and then she looked at Wren on the steps above and she said, so the whole square heard it:

"Tell me where to bring it."

"The mine-mouth," Wren said. Her throat was too tight for more.

Alys turned and started up the hill with her fire, alone, one woman, and for three steps she was alone.

Then she wasn't.

A door. Another door. A candle first, an old man cupping it against the wind, hurrying to fall in behind her. Then a whole family, a lamp and two rushlights and a child carrying an ember in a warming-pan. Then the Ashbys' cellar-full of keepers with their hidden vigil-flames finally, gloriously unhidden. Firelight bloomed door to door down the slope of Nethercote like something spreading, like something *catching,* and the shuttered black town that Wren had crawled up into an hour ago turned, house by house by house, into a river of moving light climbing the hill toward the Deepings' mouth.

The Wardens fell back before it. What could they do? They had one law, and the law was *apart,* and here was the whole town coming *together,* and there is no wall you can build against your neighbours all deciding at once to stand near you. The grey woman with the bell stood in the tide of light and, after a moment, Wren saw her take a rushlight from a passing girl's hand and turn, and climb with the rest, weeping.

Family by family they came, and family by family the flames drew near, and — this was the thing, this was the forbidden and holy thing — they let the flames *touch.* A candle tilted to a lantern. A rushlight to a fire-pot. Hearth to hearth, wick to wick, the little separate lights of a hundred lonely houses leaning in against one another at the black lip of the mine until they were not a hundred lights any more but one light, one great joined blaze roaring up into the dark-tide sky, and Wren stood in the heart of it with her mother's arm around her and her brother's cold hand in hers and felt, for the first time in her whole life in Nethercote, warm from the outside in.

Her own wick was near guttered — she could feel it, a poor grey coal where a bright thing used to burn, the price of Fen written into her chest for good. But it did not matter now, and that was the whole revelation of it, that was the thing the Wardens had spent a hundred years making sure no one ever learned. Her small failing light did not have to hold the dark alone. It only had to lean, like all the others, into the light beside it. A candle carried to a lantern loses nothing. A hearth shared warms two houses. She had been taught that giving light away emptied you. She stood in a fire built of a whole town giving its light away at once, and no one in it was empty, and the dark had never in its long life seen anything like it.

"Say it right," she told them — told the town, all of it, gathered and burning at the door of the dark. "Say it the way it was meant."

And her mother said it first, and then Rook, and then the keepers, and then the whole hillside, ragged and then together, the old rhyme reforged and made true at last:

"Light to light, and none alone — no fire of ours stands on its own."

Wren turned to the mine-mouth and lifted the joined fire high, and let it pour down into the Deepings — all of it, every wick in Nethercote, a hundred years of hoarded lonely light flung at last into the dark all at once.

Far below, in the drowned heart of the world, the Ashen felt it come — and for the first time in memory, the dark drew back.


# Chapter 14 — Hollowtide

The light came down the Deepings like water finding a crack.

Wren felt it before she saw it — a warmth at the back of her neck where there had been only the wet-ash cold, a thread of gold laid down the black throat of the mine. Then it reached the drowned cavern and the dark had nowhere left to hide. Every lantern the town of Nethercote owned, every hearth-flame carried in a cupped hand, every wick still burning under every sternum on the moor above — all of it poured together down the spiral stair and spilled across the black water in a single unbroken sheet.

She had never seen the Deepings lit. In sixteen years no one had. The cavern was vast and low and old, ribbed with rotted timber, and the joined light showed her all of it at once: the water, oil-still and endless; the drowned pit-props standing like the ribs of some huge dead thing; and at the water's edge, where the stone shelved down into nothing, the place where the dark lived.

The Ashen recoiled.

There was no other word for it. The cold that had been rising around her — the hunger she had felt reaching up the shaft for hours, patient as tide — flinched back from the light like a hand from a stove. The ash-moths that had storm-swarmed the cavern scattered, blundering, blind. And the low sound of the dark-tide, that wind-down-a-shaft moan that had underlain everything since Fen's wick went out, faltered. Broke. Stuttered like a snuffed candle trying to catch.

"It cannot put it out," said Morren.

The First Lamplighter stood at the edge of the shelf where she had stood for three hundred years, her single held flame small and blue and failing in her cupped, ancient hands. She was more shadow than woman now, half-eaten by the thing she guarded, her face the grey of old ash. But she was looking up at the light coming down, and something Wren had not seen in her — not in the long descent, not in all her weary talk of release — moved across that ruined face. Wonder.

"A lone flame it snuffs," Morren said. "It has always snuffed the lone flame. That is the whole of the bargain, girl — keep them apart, feed it one at a time, and it sleeps." Her voice cracked. "But light that will not stand alone —"

"— cannot be put out," Wren finished. Her own chest ached where her wick had guttered low, where she had spent it into Fen and gone cold in the giving. She was hollowing; she knew that now, knew it in the frost creeping out along her collarbones, in the way her hands would not properly warm. But she stood in the joined light of her whole town and she did not go out. None of them went out. The light held because it held together.

Fen stirred against her side. He was warm again — not the deep animal warmth he'd had before the moor, but warm, back from wherever the cold had taken him, his small face turned up to the falling gold. "Wren," he said. "It's raining light."

"Yes," she said. "It is."

Below them the Ashen gathered itself and struck.

It came not as a shape but as a subtraction — a wave of pure cold rolling up off the black water, reaching for the nearest flames to snuff them one by one as it had always done. It found Morren's failing blue candle first. It closed over it.

And could not take it. The little flame did not stand alone anymore. It was tied by a hundred feet of unbroken light to every hearth in Nethercote, and when the dark tried to snuff it the whole joined sheet flared white and threw the cold back down. The water hissed. The moan of the dark-tide rose to a shriek and then — Wren felt this in her teeth, in the marrow of her — it began to fold. To sink. The thing that had fed on her town's children for longer than the town had been named was being pushed, at last, back down into the deep where it had been made.

But it would not sink while the bargain still held it up.

"Morren." Wren dropped to her knees at the old woman's side. The cold of the shelf came up through her skin. "You said you couldn't die while you held it. You said the tithe keeps it — feeds it — that it never ends."

"Never ends," Morren agreed. Her blue flame trembled between her palms. "I went down to hold the dark, and I could not let go, because there was no one to take the weight from me. One woman cannot set down what a whole town made." She smiled, and it was terrible, all ash and years. "The Cawleys held it after me because I could not. Your Nell. Your Fen. Each of them, one at a time, because that was the only way I knew to feed the thing I could not put down."

"Then set it down now." Wren gripped the woman's cold wrist. Above them the joined light poured and poured. "The town's here. All of it. Every wick at once. Give it to them — spread it — nobody carries it alone. A watch instead of a — instead of a tithe."

Something in Morren went still. Three hundred years of held breath.

"Distributed," she said softly. "Light to light. And none of them alone." She looked up the shaft one more time, toward the surface, toward the thousand small warmths joined into one. "I have wanted so long to be tired properly. To be let."

"Then be let," Wren whispered. "You held it long enough. Give it up."

Morren Cawley opened her hands.

The blue flame she had guarded for three centuries lifted off her cupped palms — small, so small, the last coal of the first vigil ever kept in Nethercote — and rose into the falling gold and was taken up into it. Not snuffed. Joined. Wren watched the weight of it spread out along the joined light, thinning as it went, splitting a thousand ways so that no single wick above ever felt more than a feather of it — the burden of holding the dark divided so fine among so many that it stopped being a burden at all. It became a vigil. A thing a town could keep between them and hardly notice the keeping.

And Morren, unburdened, at last began to go out.

She did not fall. She sat down against the pit-prop she had leaned on for lifetimes, and the ash-grey of her face eased, and the terrible ancient tension in her went slack. She looked, for one moment, like a young woman who had walked a long way and was finally allowed to stop.

"Thank you," she said to Wren. Her voice was almost gone. "You came down. No one ever came down for me. They only ever came to give me someone." Her eyes found the falling light. "Tell them my name was Morren. Tell them I was afraid the whole time and I did it anyway."

"I'll tell them," Wren said. Her throat had closed. "I promise."

"Light to light," said Morren Cawley, and let go, and was gone — not snuffed, not taken, but spent, freely, into the joined light of the town she had founded and could finally trust to stand without her.

The Ashen screamed and sank.

Without the bargain to prop it, without a lone flame to feed on, with the whole of Nethercote's light standing joined and unputoutable above it, the dark had nothing left to grip. Wren felt it draining down through the black water, back into the old deep places, sated on the flame Morren had given it and unable to reach for more. Not dead. She understood that even then, kneeling on the cold shelf with her brother's hand in hers — you did not kill a thing that old, a hunger that had been in the rock before the rock was a mine. But held. Pushed down. Sleeping under a watch that would not let it wake hungry again.

"Wren." Gran's voice, behind her, thin as paper.

She had forgotten Gran. She turned.

Idony Cawley knelt on the stone a few feet off, her lantern dark beside her, and Wren saw at once that her grandmother was going the way Morren had gone. Gran had shared her own wick — into Fen, into Wren, into Morren's failing flame at the last, *light to light, and none alone* — and there had been so little of it left after a lifetime of the bargain hollowing her out from the inside. She had spent the rest of herself here, in the dark, holding the rear so the town's light could reach them. She was grey now too. Frost stood in the deep lines of her face.

"Gran." Wren went to her. "The light's here. It held. You don't have to —"

"I know it held." Gran's ruined mouth curved. Her hand came up, the old sharing-scar white across her palm, and touched Wren's cheek, and her fingers were so cold Wren nearly flinched away and made herself not. "I counted them coming down the shaft. Every wick. I have been counting wicks my whole life, girl, and tonight they were all lit. All of them." Her eyes were wet and clear. "I gave my Nell to this dark and told myself it saved the rest. I was wrong the way only the frightened are wrong, for a very long time." A breath, shallow. "Take your brother up. Go and stand in the light I never let anyone stand in."

"Come with us."

"There's not enough of me to carry up," Gran said gently. "I spent it right. First time in fifty years." She looked past Wren to Fen, small and warm and blinking in the falling gold, and something in her face broke open and healed at once. "Look at him. Warm." She said it like a benediction. "That's paid, then. That's paid."

Wren held her grandmother's cold hand while the last of Idony Cawley's wick went quiet — not screaming, not taken by the dark, but spent, given, gone out clean into the joined light like Morren before her. When it was done Gran's face was easy. She had died free of the thing she'd served. It was more than she'd let anyone she loved have, and Wren thought she had known that, at the end, and chosen it on purpose.

Then the light began to change, and Wren looked up, and it was dawn.

She had not known how long the night had run. But the falling gold thinned and paled and mixed, far up the shaft, with a grey that was not lantern-light but morning — real morning, the first she'd have believed she would see. The dark-tide sound was gone. The black water lay flat and quiet and only water now. The ash-moths were dead in drifts across the shelf, harmless, and the cold that had lived in the Deepings her whole life had drawn down and down and lay still under a watch a whole town would keep from now on.

Fen leaned his warm weight against her arm. "Can we go up now?" he asked. "I'm hungry."

A sound came out of Wren that was half a sob and half a laugh, and she pressed her cold lips to the top of his warm head and held him, and below them the Deepings kept its new quiet, and above them — she could feel it, a thousand small warmths joined into one steady light at the mine's mouth — her whole town was standing together in the dawn, for the first time in living memory, and none of them alone.

She left Gran and Morren where they lay, spent and free, and carried her brother up toward the light.


# Chapter 15 — What the Light Cost

Morning came to Nethercote and did not know how to be looked at.

Wren sat on the cold lip of the Deepings' mouth with a blanket somebody had put round her shoulders and watched the town discover, in the flat grey light, what it had spent. There was no cheering. She had half-thought there might be — that people saved from a dark that had hunted them for centuries might make some sound of gladness. But the ones who came up out of that night stood about the moor in ones and twos with their guttered lanterns hanging from their hands, and looked at each other, and looked away, and did not know where to put their eyes now that there was nothing left to hide.

The tally came in slowly, the way bad tallies do.

Gran was gone. Wren had carried Fen up and then gone back down with Rook and two of the freed keepers to bring the bodies out of the dark, because she would not leave her grandmother in the place that had taken so much from her, and they had laid Idony Cawley on the heather with her sharing-scar turned up to the sky. Morren they could not bring; there had been nothing left of the First Lamplighter but a drift of pale ash against a rotted prop, three hundred years old at last, and they had left her where she'd earned her rest.

Old Mabb was gone. She had been taken in the raid two nights back and not seen since; they found her in the vigil-house cellar where the Wardens had held her, and her old heart had simply stopped in the cold and the fear, and no light had come in time. Wren had liked her. She had been the one to say it first — that the Ashen had never faced the whole town at once because the Wardens kept everyone apart. She had planted the answer and not lived to see it grow. Wren made herself go and look at her, and say thank you where no one could hear, because someone should.

A keeper named Sennet had gone into the black water on the long climb, holding the rear so the others could pass, and had not come up. Warden Coll had gone the same way and for the same reason — Coll, who had believed to the end that the bargain saved the town, and who had chosen, when the water rose, to spend himself buying other people time. Wren did not know what to do with grief for a man who had helped write Fen's name in the ledger. She held both things at once. She was learning that you could.

And there were the hollowed.

This was the hardest part, and the town came to it slowly. When the joined light held the Ashen, some of the already-taken did not come back — the ones snuffed too long ago, given down in tithes years and decades gone, absorbed too deep into the dark to be called out of it. Nell did not come back. Merron, Rook's sister, did not come back. The light had not been a resurrection. It had only stopped the dark from taking more, and closed the door on the ones already through it, and that was a grief with no bottom to it, and the families who'd hoped stood at the Deepings' mouth a long time before they let themselves believe it.

A few did come up. Three of the recently-snuffed, the ones still within their three nights, walked up out of the dark on their own feet when the light poured down — dazed, blinking, cold in places that would not warm. A girl called Tamsin who had gone hollow a week past, and did not remember the moor now, and would flinch from the dark all her life. An old man. A boy younger than Fen who would not speak. They came back changed. Cold in patches, quiet where they'd been loud, carrying a chill in the chest that the keepers said might never quite go. But they came back, and their people wept over them, and that was something the town had never once been allowed before.

Fen came back.

Wren watched him all that first morning like she was afraid he'd go out again if she blinked. He sat on the floor of their cottage in a square of thin grey sun with his tin of small things tipped out in front of him — buttons, a bird's skull, three moor-pebbles, the brass button she'd carried down into the dark as a thread to him and pressed back into his palm at the top of the stair. He was sorting them. He always sorted them. Bone with bone, brass with brass, the pebbles by size, muttering the private order of them under his breath the way he'd done since he was four.

He was a little cold. When she took his hand it did not warm hers the way it used to; there was a coolness in him now that the keepers said some of the come-back children kept. But he was ordinary. He argued about breakfast. He complained that his good pebble had a chip. He was nine years old and hers and warm enough, and Wren sat on the floor beside him and sorted buttons and did not cry where he could see, and that was its own kind of vigil.

Her own cost she carried quiet.

She was dimmed. She had known it in the dark and she knew it worse in daylight — the place under her sternum where her Cawley wick had burned so bright, brightest of all the old line, was low now and would stay low. She had spent too much of it into Fen; you did not get that back. There was a scar of cold in her chest where the fire had been, a still cool place, a quiet where there had been a roar. She would not go hollow — the joined light had caught her before the frost took the whole of her — but she would never burn the way she had. She would be cold in the mornings all her life. She would feel the dark-tide season in her bones before anyone else did. It was a small permanent winter, set down in the middle of her, and it was the price of her brother and she would not have unpaid it, but it was a price, and she let herself feel it honestly.

Alys held her that afternoon a long time without words. Her mother had chosen her over the Wardens at the mine-mouth, had brought her own hearth-fire to the joining when the law said keep to your own; but her mother had also spent years turning away from the truth of Nell, and they both knew it, and the holding was an apology going both directions.

"I should have told you," Alys said finally, into Wren's hair. "About your aunt. About what they did. I knew and I let myself not know."

"Everyone did," Wren said. "That was the whole trick of it. Everyone knew and no one was allowed to say." She pulled back and looked at her mother in the grey light. "That's over now. That's the one thing we bought. We're allowed to say it now."

And in the evening, the town said it.

No one organised it exactly. It simply happened, the way the joining had happened, family by family drawn out of habit toward the place they'd spent their whole lives being taught to shun. They came to the Deepings' mouth as the light failed — not the dark-tide, that was broken, but ordinary dusk — and they brought their lanterns, and this time they did not keep to their own scattered fires. They set the lights together in a long line along the lip of the mine, the way they had learned to the night before, and they stood, and for a while no one knew how to begin because no one in Nethercote had grieved out loud in three generations. Shame had eaten the words.

It was Rook who started it. He stood at the mouth of the dark that had taken his sister and he said, into the quiet, in a rough flat voice: "Merron Ashby." Just the name. Then, because it had to be said and he was the one brave enough: "The dark didn't choose her. We gave her. I'm sorry, Merron."

The silence after held a hundred years of held breath.

Then an old woman down the line said her son's name. And a man said his wife's. And it went along the line of lights slow at first and then not slow, name after name after name, all the takings the ledger had hidden and the ones it hadn't bothered to write, spoken aloud into the dark for the first time — not as shame, not as *the dark chose them*, but as grief, plain and owned and shared.

When it came to her, Wren stood with Fen's cold hand in hers and Alys at her shoulder, and she said the two names she had come up out of the dark carrying.

"Nell Cawley," she said. Her aunt, given down by her own mother, lost in a flood that had never happened. "You weren't lost. We gave you, and I'm sorry, and I know your name."

The light along the mine-mouth burned steady.

"Idony Cawley," Wren said, and her voice cracked on it and she let it. Gran. Terrible and loving and wrong for fifty years and, at the very end, in the dark, right. "She gave the last of herself to hold the door. She died free of it. Gran." She breathed. "Thank you."

She said the third name too, because she'd promised. "Morren Cawley. The first of us. She was afraid the whole time and she did it anyway. She held it three hundred years so we didn't have to, and she asked us to say her name, so — Morren."

Along the whole line of joined lights, the town of Nethercote spoke its dead into the dark, out loud, unashamed, the first open grieving in living memory. And the Deepings took the names and stayed quiet, and gave nothing back, and asked for nothing, and that — Wren understood, standing cold and dimmed and whole between her mother and her brother — that was what the light had actually bought. Not the dead returned. Only the right to mourn them in the open, together, and never feed the dark another child in secret again.

It was not enough. It was everything they had. She held her brother's cool hand and let the tears come where the whole town could see, and no one looked away.


# Chapter 16 — The Vigil We Keep

Weeks on, the doors of Nethercote stood open, and Wren had almost stopped finding it strange.

It had been the strangest thing of all, at first — stranger than the joined light, stranger than the quiet where the dark-tide used to moan. All her life the town had been a scatter of shut doors and lone hearths, each family walled in with its own small fire and its own held tongue, because that was the law and the law was the rhyme: *keep your fire, and hold your own.* Now, as the long dark came round again and the nights drew cold, the people of Nethercote pushed their hearths toward one another instead. They propped their doors. They carried chairs into the market square. Where there had been a hundred separate guarded flames there was one long shared warmth, and the town sat in it together and did not go out.

They kept a vigil now, in place of the tithe.

It was a simple thing, once you stopped being afraid of it. Each night of the dark-tide season, a few families took the watch at the Deepings' mouth — not to give the dark anything, only to keep a joined light burning over its sleep, so it never woke hungry and alone-fed again. The weight Morren had carried by herself for three centuries was spread so thin across so many hands that no one felt it as weight at all. It was a night's work, a few times a season, shared out. A watch. Wren had heard an old keeper call it that and the word had stuck: *the watch we keep.* No child was ever chosen for it. That was the whole point of it. That was the thing they had gone down into the dark to buy.

Tonight it was Wren's watch, and Rook's, and she was glad of the company.

They sat together at the mine-mouth with the joined lanterns set in their long line along the lip, the same as the first night, and the dark below lay quiet under the light. Rook had brought his tally-book out of old habit, but there was nothing to tally anymore — no snuffings, no clusters, no schedule hidden in the deaths — and after a while he'd stopped opening it and just held it, the way you hold a thing that used to be your whole reason for getting up. His sister was still gone. That did not mend. But he came to the watch every time it was offered him, and Wren thought she understood: he was keeping, now, the light he'd failed to keep for Merron, and it was late, and it counted anyway.

"They finished the stones," he said, and nodded down the shaft.

They had. It had taken the town three weeks of arguing about it — whether to carve the names at all, whether to leave the dark unmarked and try to forget it, the way they'd forgotten everything for so long. But forgetting was the old way, the Warden way, and they were done with it. So they had set stones at the Deepings' mouth, a long low wall of them, and cut into the stones every name the ledger had hidden. The founders first: Morren Cawley, who went down and could not die. Then the tithed children, all of them, decade on decade, every wick given to the dark in secret — Nell Cawley's name among them, and Merron Ashby's, and Tamsin's little brother who had not come back, cut deep so the moor-weather would take a long time to wear them away. Not hidden. Not shamed. Remembered.

Wren ran her cold fingers over Gran's name where they'd cut it at the end of the line, with the founders rather than the takings, because Idony Cawley had ended as one of the ones who held the door. She did not know yet how she felt about her grandmother, and she thought perhaps she never would land on one feeling, and she'd decided that was allowed. You could love someone and be furious at them and grieve them, all at once, in the cold place where your wick used to burn bright. She did it every day.

Fen was down in the square with Alys and the others, sorting somebody's spare buttons into the town's shared button-tin, entirely happy, entirely ordinary, a little cold in the hands. She could see him from up here — a small dark shape in the long warm light. She checked on him the way you check a step you know is there. He was there. He was warm enough. He was hers.

Then, below in the town, the vigil-bell rang the turn of the night, and Nethercote spoke the rhyme.

They spoke it together now, the whole town at once, the way you'd say grace — the children learning it new, the old ones unlearning what it used to mean. Wren had grown up with the true old words, the Warden words, the ones that had kept everyone apart and fed the dark one child at a time: *Light to light, and none alone; keep your fire, and hold your own.* She would hear those words in her bones till she died.

But that was not what the town said tonight.

The voices came up to her from the square, and joined the voices of the watch at the mine-mouth, and Wren said it with them, cold-chested and dimmed and unafraid:

*"Light to light, and none alone;*
*no fire of ours stands on its own."*

The same rhyme, reforged. *None alone* meaning, at last, what it should always have meant — not keep to your own hearth, but no light stands by itself. No child given down in the dark for the rest to keep warm. No fire, ever again, left to burn alone until the dark could snuff it.

The words went out over the quiet Deepings and down into the sleeping dark and did not come back changed.

Wren sat with the whole of it a moment — the open doors below, the long shared warmth, the stones with the names cut deep, her brother a small warm shape in the light, Rook beside her holding a tally he'd never need to write in again. And she turned her attention, the way she had learned to do carefully, to the cold place under her sternum where her wick had once burned so bright.

It was small now. It would always be small. There was a still winter set in the middle of her where the roar had been, and on cold mornings she felt the dark-tide season before anyone else in the town, and she had made her peace that she always would. That was what her brother had cost, and she had paid it on purpose, and she did not want it back.

But it was warm.

Small, and low, and steady — and joined. She could feel the others now, if she went still and listened for it the way Gran used to count wicks under her breath: her mother's fire, and Fen's cool small one, and Rook's, and the long line of the watch, and every hearth in the open town below, all of them tied into one unbroken light that the dark could not put out because it would not stand alone. Her own small flame was a thread in it. Less than it had been. Joined to a thousand others. And for the first time in her whole life, sitting at the mouth of the dark that had taken her aunt and her grandmother and nearly her brother and a part of her own heart, Wren Cawley was not afraid of the dark-tide coming.

Something pale lifted off the stones at her feet.

She went still. An ash-moth — one, small and black-grey, come up out of the quiet Deepings on the cold air. Once the sight of it had meant a wick was failing, a child was going out, the dark was choosing. Once she'd have gone cold at it to the marrow.

It fluttered up past her face, aimless and slow, no swarm behind it, no cold in its wake. Just a moth, up out of the dark, harmless now. It caught the warm light of the joined lanterns for a moment, a small grey scrap of the old fear made ordinary, and then it drifted up over the mine-mouth and away across the dark moor, and was gone, and took nothing with it.

Wren watched it go. Then she pulled her blanket close over the cold place in her chest, and leaned her shoulder against Rook's, and kept the watch — one small steady warmth among a thousand, in a town that had finally learned to keep its light together — while below them the Deepings slept, and above them the long dark turned toward morning, and nobody, anywhere in Nethercote, sat alone by their fire.
