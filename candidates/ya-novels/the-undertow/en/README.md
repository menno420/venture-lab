# The Undertow

**Title:** The Undertow (standalone novella)
**Genre:** Dystopian science fiction
**Age band:** 13–17 (upper YA)
**Word count:** 27,781 words (17 files: epigraph + 15 chapters + epilogue)
**POV / tense:** First person, present tense (Nari)

## Pitch

In the drowned dome-city of Aequor, the air is manufactured, metered, and never enough. A throat implant called a reed counts out every citizen's breaths like coins, and the poorest — the Undertow — kneel in the deep galleries and breathe in unison to help power the very machines that ration them. On the day she turns sixteen, Narine Solenne expects Calibration to lift her out of the Shallows; instead the Authority tiers her *down*, to the edge of suffocation, and she finally understands why: the test doesn't measure how bright you are. It measures how quietly you'll go along. Then a smuggler slips Nari a stolen data-sliver proving her sister Tess never shipped out to the surface at all — she was throttled, erased, and hidden somewhere below the city that swears she's a hero. To find Tess, Nari will have to descend past the last honest map, learn the secret the Concord kills to keep — that the poisoned sky is quietly healing — and decide whether to save one sister in the dark, or hand the whole city its first free breath, out loud, where everyone can hear it.

## Content note

*The Undertow* is written for readers roughly 13–17 (upper YA). It carries the tension you'd expect of dystopian fiction: an authoritarian state, a coming-of-age rite rigged to keep people compliant, class cruelty, grief and a missing-sister mystery, and scenes of peril and pursuit. Asphyxiation is used as a tool of control ("throttling" and "shallowing"), rendered through disorientation and dread rather than graphic detail; there is one off-page character loss and non-graphic injury during the climax. A light, restrained romantic thread stays at a look and a hand — nothing more. No sexual content, no graphic gore, no strong profanity. Themes: who gets to breathe is who gets to be heard; the cost of complicity; the difference between surviving and living.
