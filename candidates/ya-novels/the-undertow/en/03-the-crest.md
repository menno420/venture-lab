## Chapter 3 — The Crest

I courier because a body has to eat air.

That's the joke down here — you don't earn a wage, you earn breaths, and a courier earns them by carrying what can't go on the public lines: a sealed packet, a message someone paid to keep off record, a small legal something between a low house and a high one. You run it up through the tiers and they credit your reed at the far end, and if you're fast and quiet and forgettable, they keep using you. I'm all three. I've been running the up-lines since I was thirteen. It's how I know that the higher you climb in Aequor, the easier it gets to breathe, until near the top it stops costing anything at all.

Today the packet goes to the Crest. I've only been up this high twice. The day before my Calibration, I don't have any business standing in the gold — but the run pays four breaths, and after Salt and the token and Papa's gray shaking hands, four breaths and a reason to be somewhere that isn't home is worth more than the scrip.

You feel the change before you see it. You go up through the mid-tiers where the reeds glow green and the air loses its metal, up a lift that actually works, and then the pressure shifts in your chest like a hand unclenching, and you take a breath and it just — keeps coming. There's more where that came from. Your reed can go quiet a while. Mine doesn't know how; the little blue figure under my jaw keeps ticking my count out of habit, forty, thirty-nine, and up here that's obscene, up here counting your breath is like counting your own blinks.

The Crest is gardens.

Real ones. Not the filter-moss they grow in the galleries — actual plants, green and wet and wasteful, banked in tiers along the high concourse under lamps that mimic a sky none of these people have ever needed to see, because they make their own. Oxygen-gardens. Living lungs kept for the pleasure of the few, breathing out the surplus that the rest of us line up in the dark to make with our own bodies. People stroll here. They stroll. Gold light at every throat, warm and steady, no flicker in it anywhere, and they laugh with their whole chests and never once put a hand to their reed to check.

I hate it. I want it so badly I could cry. Both, at the same time, all the way up the concourse — that's the Crest. It shows you the thing you'll never have and dares you to be a good enough person not to want it.

The delivery address is an Authority annex off the high garden, and that's where it goes wrong. There's a checkpoint I didn't know about — new, or new to me, a scanner-gate with a bored officer, and my thin flickering blue lights it up like an alarm the second I step into the beam. An Undertow reed on the Crest, day before Calibration, no standing pass. The officer straightens off the wall. His hand moves toward the throttle-pad every checkpoint carries, the little slate that can reach into your reed and turn you down to nothing where you stand.

"You're a long way off your tier," he says.

The gray wants to come. Fear does that even faster than low scrip — my heart eats breath and my edges soften and some ancient docile thing in me wants to lower my eyes and apologize and be small. I fight it. I count. I hold out the packet's manifest with a hand I keep steady on purpose.

"Courier, sir. Sealed delivery to the annex. It's on the manifest."

"Manifest's not a pass." His thumb finds the pad.

"She's with the intern intake."

The voice comes from behind me, easy and certain, the voice of someone who has never in his life wondered whether he was allowed to speak. A boy about my age steps out of the annex — Crest through and through, gold at his throat, clothes that have never been patched, a face that's open in a way faces don't get to be down where I'm from. Open, and right now, aimed at the officer with a calm authority that isn't quite his yet but fits him like a coat he'll grow into.

"The delivery's for me," he says. "Vale. I'm expecting it." He glances at the manifest in my hand, then back at the officer, and adds, mild as anything, "My father does prefer his interns' packets arrive unthrottled."

The officer's thumb comes off the pad. "Vale. Of course. Didn't clock her as yours." He waves me through like I was never worth stopping, and the beam lets me go, and I walk out of it on legs that want to shake and don't, because I won't give this gold place that.

The boy — Vale — falls into step beside me under the garden lamps. Up close he's all earnestness, the kind of person who's decided the world is basically fair and hasn't yet met anything to change his mind.

"Sorry about him. The gates went up last month, half the couriers don't know yet." He says it like it's an inconvenience, like the little pad that could've put me on the floor is a scheduling error. "Orrin, by the way. You can put the manifest down, nobody's going to eat you."

"Nari." I hand him the packet because it's his, apparently. "You didn't have to do that."

"He was going to throttle you for standing in the wrong light. Of course I had to." He frowns, honestly puzzled, like the phrase have to could point anywhere else. "That's — you're a courier, you're working, that's exactly the kind of thing Calibration's supposed to reward. Initiative. Nerve." He smiles at me, and it's a good smile, unguarded, and I feel the pull of it and I resent the pull. A Calibrator's son. An Authority intern. My father's gray shaking hands, and this boy's father is one of the ones who make hands shake. "You're near sixteen? You've got that look. The pre-Calibration look."

"Tomorrow," I say, and hearing myself say it out loud drops the floor out of my stomach. Not two days. Not soon. Tomorrow. The run, the climb, the whole long day — it's the eve now. I stand tomorrow.

"Then you should be excited." He means it. That's the terrible thing about him, I can already tell — he means all of it. "It's the fairest day of your life, honestly. I know how it sounds from down —" he catches himself, recolors it — "from outside the Authority. But I've seen the intake. It's not somebody deciding your worth on a whim. It reads you. Your aptitude, the real shape of your mind. People from every tier come out higher than they went in. It happens all the time."

"Does it." I keep my voice flat. "Because where I'm from, mostly people come out about where they started. Funny how the fairest test in the city keeps arriving at the same answer."

Something flickers behind his open face — not offense. Discomfort. The look of someone who's heard a note he can't quite place and doesn't want to hear again. "That's not — look, it isn't only about aptitude," he says, and he says it to reassure me, he genuinely thinks he's making it better. "That's the part people misunderstand. You can be brilliant and still not place high, and it's not a flaw in the test, it's the test working. Temperament matters more than people think. How you handle pressure. Whether you're — steady. Whether you'll hold together under strain instead of making things worse for everyone around you." He nods, pleased with the fairness of it. "It's not just can you think. It's can you be trusted with what thinking gets you. That's mercy, really, when you look at it right. Better to know where a person's steady than to hand them more than they can carry."

I stop walking.

Whether you'll hold together. Whether you can be trusted. Steady. I think of my instructors and their careful voices — Nari tests high — always with that little pause after, like the words might cost them. I think of Tess, who was the sharpest person I ever knew and never once held her tongue when something was wrong, Tess who tested so high they took her straight up and out. I think of a reed-ping fourteen months old coming up out of a level that isn't on any map, below the below, and a silver seal on my wall that my own father can't look at.

Temperament matters more than people think.

They don't measure how well you think. They measure how quietly you'll agree.

I don't say it. I'm not stupid, and this boy standing here in the gold, grinning, would not even understand the shape of what I'd be saying — he'd try to fix it, gently, and mean it. But something cold and clear has opened in my chest, clearer than any breath the Crest ever gave me, and I feel the seed of it settle in and take root and I know it's not coming out.

"I should go," I say. "Delivery's made."

"Right. Yeah." Orrin steps back, awkward now, sensing he's lost something and not knowing what. He reaches out and taps the pad at his belt — not the throttle, a credit — and my reed warms half a degree as the four breaths land, and then two more on top of them. "For the trouble at the gate," he says. "And — Nari. Good luck tomorrow. You'll do better than you think. I mean that." He hesitates, and the open face does something younger, almost shy. "If it ever goes sideways up here again — the annex knows my name. Ask for Vale."

The annex knows my name. Ask for Vale.

I take the long way down, out of the gold, back toward the green and then the deepening blue, and the air thickens and warms and starts to taste of metal again the lower I go, and my reed picks its count back up, forty, thirty-nine, thirty-eight, all the way home to the hum in the floor. I've got a copied token against my chest and a thread now into the Authority itself, a Calibrator's son who'd help me and never know why. And I've got the thing Orrin handed me without meaning to, the thing worth more than six breaths of Crest scrip.

They don't tier you for your mind. They tier you for your silence.

And tomorrow, they read mine.
