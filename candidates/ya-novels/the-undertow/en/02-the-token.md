## Chapter 2 — The Token

Juno's nook is a dead pocket of gallery behind the coolant runs, a space the maps forgot, which is exactly why she works there. You get in through a panel that used to be a maintenance hatch and now is a door if you know it's a door. Inside, it's warm and close and blue-lit and smells of hot metal and the sweet burnt tang of scorched reed-wire. Every surface is tools. Every tool is stolen. It's the safest place I know, which tells you something about the rest of the places I know.

I've had the token against my chest for a whole day. I couldn't sleep. I lay on my mat and felt it not-glowing through my shirt and counted the Choir instead of counting sheep, in and hold and out, and thought about silver seals and dated pings until the gray came for me and I let it, just to stop.

Now I put the sliver in Juno's palm and watch her face change.

"Where did Salt even get this," she says, turning it in the light. She's not grinning anymore. "You know what this is. This is Authority-grade. This is a reed-ping log, this is the stuff their scanners spit out tracking implants around the city. You don't hold this. Holding this is a throttling. Holding this is somebody knocking your panel in the night and you're just — gone, and there's a nice letter about a transfer." She looks at me. "You want me to read it."

"I want you to tell me it's junk."

"I want that too." She's already reaching for the cradle — a scavenged reader she built out of three broken ones, wired to a screen the size of my hand. "If it's real, we don't say what it says out loud in here, we don't say it out loud anywhere, and after I'm done you let me wipe it and we throw the shell in the coolant and we never had it. Deal?"

"Deal."

"Say it like you mean it, Nari."

"Deal." I don't mean it. She knows I don't. She seats the sliver anyway, because she's Juno, and my not-meaning-it is the exact reason she won't let me do this alone.

The reader wakes. The screen crawls with corrupted junk — most of a stolen sliver is garbage, dead sectors, cross-talk. Juno's fingers move like she's calming an animal. She strips layers, coaxes, mutters at it. The blue light off the screen puts hollows under her eyes.

This is the thing about Juno that the tier will never read, because the tier only reads whether you'll behave: she is the best hands in the low city, and she's spent them all her life turning the Authority's own numbers against the Authority. A reed is supposed to be honest. It tells you your count and it tells them your color and it can't be argued with. Juno argues with it. She's talked reeds into showing a few more breaths than a body's owed, talked a checkpoint into reading a Shallow throat as a mid-tier green for exactly as long as it takes to walk past. Every one of those tricks is a Reassigning if the wrong officer ever catches it. She does them anyway, quiet, careful, for whoever needs a breath they can't buy. I've never told her out loud that she's braver than anyone with a gold reed will ever have to be. She'd only make a joke of it.

"Okay," she says. "Okay. It's a ping log, like I said. Hundreds of IDs, thousands, it's a raw sweep, whoever pulled this just grabbed a whole region's worth of reed-traffic and dumped it. That's why it reads as junk. It's not for any one person, it's —" She stops. "There."

She doesn't read it out loud. She turns the screen so I can.

A reed-ID. A long string, and I don't know most of the city's IDs but I know one, I've known it my whole life, it's stitched into the front of every form our family ever filed: SOLENNE, T. — and the tier-flag beside it, the color-code, still reads the low Undertow blue. Not gold. Not the tier of a surface-corps hero. Blue. Ours.

Beside it, a timestamp.

I do the arithmetic the way I do everything, fast and cold, because arithmetic is the one thing they can't fog out of me. Tess stood for Calibration three years ago. Shipped to the surface — so the letter said, so the wall says — within the month. This ping is dated fourteen months back. Fourteen months. That's — I count it twice to be sure I'm not fooling myself — that's a year and a half after my sister left the city. Her reed, breathing, pinging, alive, a year and a half after she was supposed to be gone.

And the origin.

"That location code," Juno says quietly. "I've pulled a lot of gallery maps. I know every level that has a name. That's not one of them." She magnifies it, and it's just a designator, a depth-string, a number with too many digits after it. "It's below. Below the deep galleries. Below the Choir." She sits back. Her reed flickers blue at her throat, same as mine, same as the code on the screen. "There's not supposed to be a below."

The nook is very quiet. Somewhere through the wall the coolant sighs. Under my feet the floor hums, in and hold and out, and I think: there's a below, and my sister's breath came out of it, fourteen months ago, and everyone who loves her thinks she's up in the cold green air giving back the sky.

"It could still be junk," Juno says, but she says it the way you offer someone a blanket, not because it'll fix anything. "IDs corrupt. Timestamps corrupt. A stray sweep, a mislabel —"

"Two things corrupting into my sister's exact ID at a depth that isn't on any map." My voice doesn't sound like mine. "You believe that?"

She doesn't answer. That's the answer.

I make her copy it. She fights me — the deal, Nari, we wipe it — but she copies it onto a clean shell for me anyway and wipes the original and drops the dead sliver into the coolant, where it hisses once and is gone, because that part of the deal I let her keep. I put the copy against my chest. It's a throttling in my pocket now. It's worth it. Fourteen months. Below the galleries.

"Nari." Juno catches my sleeve at the door, careful, no wasted breath. "Whatever this is — you stand for Calibration in two days. You test high. You are two days from up and out, from a window, from never counting again. Whatever this is, it can wait two days. Please tell me it can wait two days."

"It's waited three years," I say. "It can wait two days."

Neither of us believes me.

---

Papa is home when I get back, which is wrong for the hour, and he's holding a cup he isn't drinking, which is right for every hour. The commendation glows faint on the wall, catching the reed-light — silver seal, silver lie, my sister's name in letters that were proud once.

I sit across from him. I don't have a plan. I have a token against my chest and two days on a clock and a father who has read every one of Tess's letters four times, and I open my mouth and what comes out is small and stupid and true.

"Papa. When Tess writes — does she ever say what the sky looks like? Up there. Does she ever describe it?"

He goes still.

Not the settling this time. Still like the machine when a filter jams — a wrongness you feel before you understand it. His hands close on the cup. "Why would you ask me that."

"I just — I stand in two days. I want to know what I'm testing toward. If I tier up, if there's a chance of the corps, I want to know it's —" I make myself hold his eyes. "Is it green up there, Papa? Like she used to say? Cold and green and too much of it?"

"Where did that come from." His voice has dropped somewhere I've never heard it go. Low and flat and afraid. "Where is this coming from, Nari."

"It's not coming from anywhere. It's a question about my own sister."

"Don't." He sets the cup down and his hand is not steady and he sees me see it and he puts the hand in his lap. "Don't go asking about Tess. Not now. Not — not to anyone, do you hear me. Not the week of your Calibration, not the week after, not — you keep your head down and you test and you take the tier they give you and you thank them for it." He's not looking at the wall. He's making a point of not looking at the wall, which is its own kind of looking. "Your sister is where she is. Some things you don't dig up. Some things, if you love this family, you leave in the ground."

"Papa —"

"I'm tired, Nari." He stands. His face has gone the gray of low scrip, and he's not low on scrip, we both know he's not. "I'm going to lie down. You should sleep too. Big days coming." And he goes to the back and pulls the curtain, and I sit alone at the table with a token burning against my chest, and I understand three things at once.

He knows something.

He's terrified of it.

And in three years of that silver seal on the wall, he has never, not once, told me what the sky looks like — because he can't, because he doesn't know, because nobody in this family has ever seen it.

The floor hums under me, in and hold and out, and somewhere below the below my sister's breath went into that log fourteen months ago, and I stop pretending, even to myself, that I'm going to leave it in the ground.

Two days to Calibration.

I'm going to find her.
