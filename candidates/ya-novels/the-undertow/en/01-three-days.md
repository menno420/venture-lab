## Chapter 1 — Three Days

I wake counting, the way you do down here.

The number sits in the dark before I even open my eyes, a soft blue figure hanging under my jaw where the reed reads out. I don't need light to see it. I turn my head on the mat and there it is, floating against the ceiling in the reflection off the water pipe: forty-one. Forty-one breaths of scrip, my whole day, and it isn't yet light in the galleries above us.

Forty-one. Enough, if I'm careful. If I don't run, don't laugh too hard, don't let Juno make me laugh too hard.

The floor hums. It always hums, but at this hour the hum comes up clean through the mat and into the bones of my back, low and even, a sound with no top to it. The Choir. Somewhere below the deep galleries the Shallow workers are already down there breathing in their long slow rows, in and hold and out, in and hold and out, and the machine drinks it and the machine gives the city air, and I lie on my mat feeling it in my teeth. You stop hearing it the way you stop hearing your own heart. Then, some mornings, you hear it, and it's the loneliest sound in Aequor.

The reed under my jaw glows a thin blue. When I tilt my chin it flickers — a bad connection, or a bad tier, and mine is both. Shallow blue. Undertow blue. The color that says: this one breathes what we let her.

Three days.

In three days I stand for Calibration, and this thin flickering blue becomes something else. That's the whole point of being sixteen down here. You are one color your whole childhood and then the Authority reads you — your mind, your aptitude, the shape of you — and gives you the color you've earned. Green, maybe. Green is mid-tier, green is a room with a window that shows the inside of another room, green is not counting to forty-one. I'm a strong student. Everyone says so, my instructors in that careful way they have, like the words might cost them. Nari tests high. Nari could tier up.

Could tier up and out. Could pull Papa up behind me, the way Tess was going to.

I sit up slow, so the gray doesn't come. When you move fast on low scrip the edges of the world go soft and gray and your thoughts get agreeable, and agreeable is the thing I fight hardest. I breathe shallow, from the top of my chest, and I count, and I stand.

Papa is already at the table, which means he barely slept. He's a scrubber-tech, thirty years in the galleries keeping the lungs of the city from failing, and it's carved him down to something narrow and gentle and always tired. He has a cup of the gray tea in both hands and he isn't drinking it. He's just holding it for the warmth, and looking at the wall.

I know what's on the wall. I don't look, because if I look he'll see me look.

It's Tess's commendation. A real frame, salvaged, the only real frame we own, and behind the glass the seal of the Surface Reclamation Corps stamped in proud silver: SOLENNE, TESSARA. FOR SERVICE TO THE RECLAMATION OF THE OUTER WORLD. Three years it's hung there. Three years since my sister stood for her own Calibration and tested so high they took her straight up and out, straight to the surface corps, the proudest posting there is — reclaiming the world beyond the dome, giving the sky back its blue. She writes when she can. The letters come rarely and short and they don't sound like her, and Papa reads each one four times and puts it in the box under the frame and doesn't talk about her for a week after.

I used to trace that silver seal with my finger and think: soon. Soon it's me on a wall.

I don't remember our mother. She went shallow when I was small — that's the word they use down here, gentler than the truth, a person who ran out of air one breath at a time until there wasn't a next one. Papa doesn't say her name. Tess used to, at night, low so Papa wouldn't hear, telling me the color of a woman I can't picture. After Tess left, the names just stopped, both of them, and the flat where I grew up got very quiet, and the only voice left in it that ever pushed back was mine. So I learned to carry them for us. The pushing-back. Somebody has to keep the edge in a family, or the gray takes all of you at once.

"You're up early," Papa says, not turning.

"Big day coming." I fill a cup, don't drink it either. "Three of them, actually."

His shoulders do a thing. Not a flinch. A settling, like a man bracing a load. "You'll do well," he says. "You test high."

"I test high," I agree, and I hate how much I want him to promise me the rest of it, and I hate more that he won't. He just holds his tea and looks at his oldest daughter's face behind the glass, and I go to the market before the silence can finish forming.

The scrubber-market runs along Shallow-2's cross-gallery, under pipes that sweat warm and metallic. This is where the low city trades in the things the low city needs — patched filter-mesh, salvage, contraband scrip if you know the face to ask, and if you know a better face, worse things than scrip. The air here tastes the way all our air tastes. Thin. Warm. A little of metal, a little of the inside of a mouth. I've breathed it my whole life and I only know it's wrong because Tess told me once that real air, surface air, is cold and green and makes you dizzy with how much of it there is. I was little. I thought she was making it up.

"There she is." Juno drops in beside me out of nowhere, which is Juno's whole method of arrival. "The scholar. The pride of the tier. Say something clever, I've had a slow morning."

"Forty-one," I tell her.

She winces in sympathy. "That's a crime. That's a genuine crime, they've got you on nothing." She's got her jacket lumpy with tools — Juno is a reed-tinker, which is the polite word, and the impolite word is what she'll be Reassigned for if the Authority ever catches her elbow-deep in someone's throat implant, quietly teaching it to lie about its numbers. Her own reed glows the same low blue as mine. She's never once let it decide what she's worth. "Come by after, I'll trick you up a few breaths for the big day. Can't have you fainting in front of the Calibrators. Bad for the family name." She grins. "The name's already bad. Worse, then."

"You're going to get yourself hollowed out."

"I'm going to get us both up and out, is what I'm going to do. You test high, I tinker high. Between us we're practically Crest." She bumps my shoulder, careful, the way we're all careful, no wasted breath. "Three days, Nari. Then you never count again."

That's when I feel the hand on my arm.

Salt. I know him by the grip before I turn — dry and hard as a cable, an old scrubber-tech gone smuggler, older than Papa and twice as leathered, a man who moves scrip and tokens through the low city and knows which pipe to be behind when the patrols come. I've bought illicit breaths from him before. He's never once looked at me the way he's looking at me now.

He looks scared.

"Girl." His voice is nothing, a scrape under the market noise. "Solenne's girl. Tess's sister." Not a question. He's checking. His eyes go over my face like he's matching it to another one.

"What," I say.

He presses something into my palm and folds my fingers over it before I can see. Small. Flat. Warm from his hand. A sliver, the kind of data-token the Authority uses and the low city forges — a scrap of contraband memory you're throttled just for holding.

"I move a lot of these," Salt says, low and fast, close to my ear. "Skimmed logs, reed-pings, junk data, I don't read most of it, I don't want to read most of it. But your sister's ID came up. Tess Solenne. It pinged." He swallows. "Off a sub-level. Inside the dome, girl. Below the galleries."

"That's — no. Tess is surface corps. Three years." My mouth's gone dry as the air.

"The ping's dated." His hand tightens on mine, once, hard. "Months after she shipped out. Months. Your sister's reed was breathing inside Aequor long after she was supposed to be up top reclaiming the sky." He lets go like the token's burned him through both our hands. "I don't know what it means. I don't want to. But somebody ought to, and it ought to be blood." He's already stepping back into the crowd, already just another bent old man among the pipes. "Your sister never left the city," he says. "Don't tell anyone I told you."

And he's gone.

Juno is staring at me. "Nari. What did he give you."

I open my hand. The sliver sits there dark and dead, no light in it, nothing to see, everything to know. My reed flickers thin blue under my jaw, forty-one and dropping, and the floor hums up through my feet — the Choir, in and hold and out, the whole low city breathing on a leash.

Below the galleries. Months after.

"Nothing," I say, and close my fist, and my heart is going too fast, and I can feel the scrip bleeding out of me one panicked breath at a time. "It's nothing. It's junk data. Slivers glitch all the time, you know that, IDs cross, it's nothing."

"Sure," Juno says, watching my face. "Sure it is."

I put the token in my inside pocket, against my chest, where I can feel it not-glowing. Three days to Calibration. Three days to the color I've earned, to green, to a window, to a wall with my own silver seal on it.

Three years my sister's face has hung behind that glass, and I never once wondered if the silver was a lie.

I go home the long way, breathing shallow, counting, so Papa won't see anything in my face at all.
