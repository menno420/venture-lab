# The Undertow — If Expanded to a Full Novel

The novella is complete as it stands. This one-page note sketches how it would grow into a full-length YA novel (~75–95k words), or open a series, if the owner wants to take it further.

## Where the room is
- **Widen the middle.** The investigation (chapters 2–9) moves fast. A full novel would add a second courier-run subplot deepening the Crest/Undertow contrast; a Shallow-2 friend-group whose members each embody a different response to being written off (compliance, denial, quiet sabotage); and one *failed* early descent that raises the cost before the successful one.
- **Deepen the antagonist.** Give Vane one or two scenes (through Orrin) that show the arithmetic from the inside — a Calibrator who is not wrong that the air is scarce, only wrong about who gets to decide. A full novel earns his final crack rather than asserting it.
- **The Choir as a world.** More time in the galleries: the culture the Breath-labor build in whispers, the rhythm-songs, the ones who have been down long enough to forget their own names. This is the book's most original image and rewards expansion.

## Series potential (Book 1 of 2–3)
- **Book 1 (this story):** the truth is out; one free breath; Vane cracked, not toppled. Ends on the first sanctioned surface delegation.
- **Book 2 — the surface.** The delegation reaches the thinning coast. The Concord's power was built on scarcity, so a habitable outside detonates it politically: who leaves, who is *allowed* to, and the faction that would rather rule a dying dome than share a living world. A recovering Tess becomes a leader; Nari the reluctant symbol.
- **Book 3 — rebuild.** The messy work of a city learning to breathe without a meter — and the temptation to raise a new hierarchy on the far side of the old one.

## Shelf / comparables
Sits with upper-YA dystopian sci-fi — Scott Westerfeld's *Uglies*, M. T. Anderson's *Feed*, Neal Shusterman's *Scythe*: one strong social mechanic, a coming-of-age rite weaponized by the state, and a protagonist who breaks the system by refusing its terms.
