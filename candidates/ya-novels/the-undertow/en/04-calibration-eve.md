## Chapter 4 — Calibration Eve

The night before they measure you, the whole Undertow goes down to the galleries to breathe.

They call it the Vigil. Papa calls it that, anyway, in the careful voice he uses for things he doesn't want questioned. On my reed the number reads 31 — thirty-one breaths of scrip until the meter turns over at dawn — and the light around the implant is the same dim blue it has always been, the blue of the lowest levels, the blue I have spent sixteen years believing I will trade tomorrow for green. Maybe gold. A strong student tiers up and out. Everyone has told me so since I could count.

So why does my throat feel like a fist.

Juno finds me on the descent stair, three flights down into the warm metal dark, and falls into step without asking. She is carrying her tinker-satchel, which she is not supposed to carry to a Vigil, which is exactly why she has carried it.

"You look like a girl about to be measured," she says.

"I am a girl about to be measured."

"Then look grateful. It's a civic honor." She says it flat, the way you'd read a warning label, and I almost laugh, and the almost-laugh loosens the fist a little. This is what Juno is for. She turns everything one degree sideways until you can look at it.

The stair opens out and the galleries swallow us.

I have been to the edge of them before — everyone in the Undertow has, on scrip-days, to hear the counts read. I have never been all the way in on a Vigil, not since I was small enough to hold Tess's hand and not remember it. I remember it now. The scale of the place hits first, a cavern of ribbed dark so tall the ceiling is only a suggestion, and running down the center of it, rank on rank, the workers.

The Choir.

That is the word for it, though no one sings. Hundreds of them, thousands maybe, kneeling in ordered rows on the gallery floor, and above each row a metering-rail glowing thin blue, and every worker breathing in time to the rail. In. Hold. Out. Not one of them off the beat. The rails pulse and the breath answers, a great slow tide of lungs, and the sound it makes is the sound I have felt through the floor of our unit my entire life without knowing its face. The low hum. The one that comes up through the soles of your feet while you eat, while you sleep. It was never the machines.

It was them.

Their breath goes into intake grilles that run the length of each rank, and the grilles feed the scrubbers, the great bio-lungs that make the air for the city, and I stand at the rail and do the arithmetic against my will. Their rationed breath runs the machine that rations their breath. In. Hold. Out. They are breathing to make the air that they are allotted to breathe, and the allotment is less than what they spend, always less, that is the whole design, and no one in all these ranks looks up.

"Nari." Juno's hand on my sleeve. "Your face."

I fix my face.

A Choir-warden walks the aisle between ranks, a mid-tier in green, tapping a slow baton against her palm to hold the beat, and where a worker lags she stops and waits, patient, terrible, until the lagging lungs find the rhythm again. Nobody is chained here. That is the part I can't hold. Nobody is chained. The reed does it. Fall off the beat and your allotment thins and the shallowing fog rolls in, that gray-edged softness Papa comes home wearing, and the fog wants you compliant, wants you back on the beat, and so you go back on the beat. The cruelty is that they built it so the body does the guard's work for them.

Horror, I think, should be loud. This is the quietest room in Aequor.

We stand at the rail a long time. Long enough that I start counting the breaths with them without deciding to, in, hold, out, my own chest falling into their tide, and I have to make myself stop. Tomorrow, I think. Tomorrow I go up and out and I never stand at this rail again. The thought used to taste like sugar. Tonight it tastes like the air down here — thin, warm, metal on the tongue.

I look for Papa.

I find him in the fourth rank from the rail, and for a moment I don't know him, because the man I'm looking at is old. Papa is not old. Papa is forty-one. But the man kneeling in the fourth rank has a scrubber-tech's gray coveralls and a scrubber-tech's stooped shoulders and a face gone slack in the beat, in, hold, out, and it takes me the length of three breaths to be sure, and being sure is worse than not knowing. He doesn't see me. His eyes are on the rail-light and his lungs are on the rail-light and wherever the rest of him is, it isn't here.

"He's on Vigil duty," Juno says, quiet. "Techs get pulled. It's normal."

"I know it's normal." That's the thing about all of it. It is all completely normal.

Juno pulls me back from the rail, into the shadow of a support rib where the wardens don't walk, and swings the satchel around, and I understand that she has brought me here for a reason that is not the Vigil.

"I got into something," she says. "Last night. After you went up to the Crest." Her fingers are already moving, unspooling a hair-thin lead, coaxing a cracked reader-slate to life with its glow hooded under her jacket so it won't carry. "You know how I told you the token's ping came off an Authority relay? I've been climbing the relay. Sideways. Into the intern-tier records, the stuff the new Authority kids handle before they're trusted with anything real." She glances up. "Your Crest boy's tier, actually."

"He's not my Crest boy."

"Uh huh." She turns the slate so I can see. "I pulled a file, Nari. I pulled her file."

And there it is. The slate's glow, blue on Juno's chin, and in the glow a record header, and the name in the header is SOLENNE, TESSARA and my breath just — stops. Not shallowed. Stopped. On purpose, mine, because I don't trust it to come out steady.

The file is a ruin. Juno only got fragments, she says, the rest walled behind clearances she can't touch, but the fragments are enough to knock the floor out. There's Tess's Calibration date, three years and two months ago. There's a tier stamp — she calibrated to green, mid-tier, which we always knew, which is why the Surface Corps posting made everyone so proud, a green girl chosen to reclaim the outside. And there, under the tier stamp, the disposition line, the one that's supposed to say where a citizen went.

TRANSFERRED — SURFACE RECLAMATION CORPS.

The words on the wall of our unit. The words on the commendation in its frame, the certificate Papa dusts and never looks at. The proud lie, printed here in Authority gray exactly as it's printed at home, and for one sick second I think the token was wrong, Salt was wrong, she left after all, she's up there under the Char in a rebreather planting something green.

Then Juno drags her finger down and the file scrolls and under the transfer line there's another line, buried, a system flag she says the interns aren't even meant to see, tagged in a different hand, a different gray.

R-CLASS.

That's all. Two letters, a hyphen, a word. No key. No note. A flag stapled to my sister's name under a transfer that the ping-log already proved was a lie, because you cannot be on the surface and ping a sub-level inside Aequor fourteen months after you shipped out.

"R-Class," I say. My mouth is dry. "What is R-Class."

Juno shakes her head slowly, and Juno always knows, Juno has an answer for every tier and code and clearance in this city, and the fact that she doesn't have this one is the coldest thing that's happened tonight. "I don't know," she says. "It's not in any glossary I've got. It's not a tier. It's not a posting." She kills the slate's glow. In the dark her voice is very small. "But it's on her, and it's not on anyone else's file I pulled. Whatever it is, they wrote it on your sister and then they wrote 'surface corps' on top of it and hung a certificate on your wall."

Below us the Choir breathes. In. Hold. Out. The tide of it comes up through the rib we're leaning on, up through my feet, into my teeth.

"Put it away," I say. "Someone'll see the glow."

She stows the slate. We stand in the dark a while longer, not talking, and out past the rib Papa kneels in the fourth rank breathing to power the machine that keeps him breathing, and somewhere under all of this, fourteen months ago, sixteen months, some when, my sister's reed pinged a level that isn't on any map. And now I have a word for what they did to her. I just don't have its meaning.

R-Class.

I turn it over and over. It doesn't get lighter.

"You should go home," Juno says finally. "You calibrate at second bell. You should sleep."

"I'm not going to sleep."

"No," she agrees. "But you should try. Whatever they read tomorrow, Nari — go in rested. Go in sharp." She squeezes my arm, hard, the way she does when she means a thing she can't say straight. "Go in you."

I climb the descent stair backward in my head all the way up, counting flights instead of breaths, because if I count breaths I fall into the tide and I have felt tonight what the tide does. At the top I look back once. From up here the galleries are just a well of thin blue light, rank on rank, pulsing, and you cannot tell from here that the light is made of people.

Twenty-eight breaths on my reed. R-Class in my chest like a swallowed coin.

Tomorrow they measure me. Tomorrow I go up and out.

I almost believe it.
