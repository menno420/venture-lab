## Chapter 6 — Shallowing

Shallow-2 is not a place. It's a weather.

Three days in it and I understand what they took. Not the air, exactly. The air is bad — my reed reads a number now that isn't a number a person lives on, it reads it in that thin flickering blue and I feel the meter's mean little hand on my lungs from the moment I wake — but the air isn't the theft. The theft is what the air does.

The fog. It comes in with the low count and it sits behind my eyes. Gray at the edges. A softness in the will. I reach for a thought and it slides. I reach for anger and get a warm nothing. This is the thing. This is what it's for. Not to kill you. To sand you down. To make the shape of you smaller until you fit the shape they measured.

I catch myself agreeing with things.

That's the worst of it. Papa says something careful and complicit at the table and I open my mouth to fight it and the fog rolls up and the fight just — goes. Sinks. Why fight. Easier not to. And I hear the easier-not-to in my own head in a voice that is nearly mine and I want to be sick, because I know that voice, I've seen it on Papa for years, I saw it on the whole Choir kneeling in rank. This is how they do it. Not chains. This.

Count, I tell myself. Count something they can't fog.

I count the reed. 20 breaths. 18. I count Tess's name. I count the two letters. R. Class. I hold the edges of things. It's like holding water. But I hold.

Shallow-2 comes with a posting. They don't ask. Second day, a rail-slip arrives on my reed — a rank, a shift, a gallery number. The Choir. Of course the Choir. Where else do you put a girl whose whole worth is the breath in her. I don't go yet. There's a grace, a few days, before they scan for you. But it's there now, a weight with my name on it, waiting at the bottom of every stair. Down there is a kneeling-place and a metering-rail and my father four ranks over. In. Hold. Out. That's the life they measured me into. That's the arithmetic.

Papa can't look at me. He looked at the board too, on the screen at home, and something in him just folded. He set my meal in front of me the first night and put his hand flat on the table by mine, not touching, and said, "It's a good tier for staying safe. You keep your head down and the air comes steady." And I heard, under it, the thing he wasn't saying, the thing he's been not-saying for three years. Stay small. Stay quiet. Don't go looking. He said it to someone once before and she's not here.

I didn't fight him. The fog took the fight. And later I hated myself for the peace of that.

Juno finds me on the fourth day, on the low stair, and she takes one look and her face does a thing I've never seen it do. Juno turns everything one degree sideways. She can't turn this. She just looks at me and I watch her decide something.

"Not like this," she says. "You don't get to go out like this. Come on."

She takes me to the nook. Her tinker-nook, the crawl-space under the vent-run where she works, and she pulls a jar from behind a panel, and in the jar is a slim token, black-market, warm-looking somehow, and I know what it is before she says.

"A boost," she says. "Bootleg scrip. It writes false credit to your reed — tells the meter you're owed air you're not. It'll clear the fog. Couple days, maybe three." She turns it in her fingers, doesn't hand it over yet. "Nari. This is the kind of thing they throttle people for. Not thin you. Cut you. If they scan your reed and find forged credit —"

"I know."

"You don't. You've been sharp your whole life. You've never been dumb enough to need to know." She's angry, which is how Juno does scared. "People who buy these disappear. It's not a slap. It's a — it's the thing that happens to people who don't come back."

The word sits between us. Disappear. And I think of a disposition line that says surface corps over a flag that says R-Class, and I think, that word already happened to my family once, and no one said it out loud then either.

"Give it to me," I say.

She gives it to me.

I press it to the reed and it takes a second, a cold second, and then — air. Real allotment, or the lie of it, flooding the meter, the number jumping up past anything Shallow-2 should ever see, and the fog pulls back like a tide going out. The gray at the edges of the room retreats. My thoughts come back sharp and whole and mine, all at once, and I stand there in the nook breathing full for the first time in four days and I almost cry, not from relief. From fury. Because now I can feel exactly how much they'd taken. You can't measure the fog while you're in it. You can only measure it when it lets go, and it lets go and I feel the size of the theft and it is enormous, it is my whole self held under, and they do this to the Choir every day, every day, they do this to Papa, they did this to Tess until she went soft in some sub-level and stopped surfacing.

The anger doesn't slide anymore. I hold it easy now. It's clean and it's cold and it has edges I could cut with.

"There she is," Juno says quietly, watching my face. "There you are."

I breathe. Whole sentences of breath. And I say the thing that's been forming under the fog the whole four days, the thing I couldn't hold long enough to finish until now.

"She's down there, Juno. The token pinged a sub-level. The file says R-Class over a lie. They didn't send her to the surface. They sent her down." I look at the flickering blue that the forged credit has painted false and steady. "I'm going to find the level. And I'm going to go down and get her."

Juno doesn't tell me it's impossible. That's the thing about Juno. She just goes very still and then nods, once, like she's been waiting four days for me to say it so she could start solving it. "The reed betrays you below," she says, already somewhere else, already working. "Deeper you go, the more the rails read you. We can't just walk you in. I'd need to spoof your ID, dampen the reed, get a map that doesn't exist." She looks up. "We'd need someone with access. Real access. Authority access."

And into the silence after that, my reader-slate chimes.

A message, routed strange, bounced through three relays to reach a Shallow-2 reed that shouldn't be worth reaching. No name on it. But I know the tier of the words before I know the sender, the careful Crest phrasing, the way a good boy writes when he's frightened and won't say so.

I saw. I was in the hall. I saw what they did to your board.

Then, under it, after a gap, like he deleted it three times and sent it anyway:

That isn't how it's supposed to work. Aptitude first. You had the highest aptitude in the cohort. I saw the score. I don't understand what happened and I can't stop thinking about it. — O

Orrin. Watching from inside the white, from the Authority tier, the Calibrator's son, the intern who told me on the Crest that temperament matters more than people think and didn't hear how it sounded. He heard it now. He watched the machine take the best student in the room and drop her to the bottom band and something in him cracked, and he doesn't have the word for the crack yet, but I do. Doubt. The seed of it. The exact seed I've been carrying since the Crest, sprouting now on the other side, in the one person in Aequor who can get me down.

I read it twice. Juno reads it over my shoulder and lets out a low breath.

"Well," she says. "There's your access."

"He doesn't know anything yet." I turn the slate over. "He thinks it's a mistake. He thinks the system's kind and something broke."

"So let him keep thinking it," Juno says, hard, "and use him."

But that's not it. I look at the message, at the three-times-deleted honesty of it, and I don't want to use him, I want him to see, I want to hand him the thing I saw on the white floor and watch it land, because a boy who can't stop thinking about it is a boy who's already halfway to the truth and doesn't know it. And a boy like that, if he turns all the way — he'd bring the whole inside of this thing out into the light with him.

I don't answer yet. Not yet. Sharp, Juno taught me. Go in sharp.

Instead I sit in the nook with my best friend and the forged breath in my throat and I make myself say the plan out loud so it's real, so the fog can't take it back when the boost wears off, which it will, in a couple of days, and then I'll be down in the weather again fighting to hold the edges.

"Find the level," I say. "Get down. Get Tess." I press my thumb over the reed, over the flickering blue, over the lie of steady air. "And find out what R-Class means before it means me."

Juno starts pulling panels, laying out tools, muttering about ID-spoofs and rail-dampers, alive with it. Below us, faint, through the floor of the nook, through the soles of my feet, the Choir breathes. In. Hold. Out. The hum I grew up thinking was machines.

I know its face now. I know it's people. I know one of them is mine.

I hold the anger. It has edges. I go down to meet it.
