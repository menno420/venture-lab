## Chapter 12 — The Char Is Falling

The amber light sweeps and I do the maddest thing I have ever done. I turn my back on it.

"Ask the ones who can't tell," Tess said. *They kept it for me.* I look at the last rank, the oldest, the ones gone to stone, and I understand her the way you understand a thing only when there's no time left to understand it: the safest hiding place in Aequor is inside a person the Concord has already decided is nothing. Who searches the pockets of the dead choir? Who frisks furniture?

"Juno. The old ranks. Reeds — check their reeds, the casings, anything that opens."

She doesn't argue. That's what I love about Juno; when it counts she moves first and doubts later. She's down the row with her sliver out, popping reed-casings with a tinker's tenderness, whispering *sorry, sorry, sorry* to each blank face she lifts. Salt plants himself at the mouth of our rank, a wall of old man between us and the light. Orrin —

"Orrin. The counter bought your audit once. Buy us one more minute."

He looks at the amber light coming. He looks at me. Something finishes happening in his face, some long fall that started the day he watched my Calibration from inside the booth and has been falling ever since, and lands. He is not a Crest boy anymore. He is not an intern. He is a traitor to everything that made him, and he has decided it, all the way down.

"A minute," he says. "Maybe less." He pulls something from inside his jacket — a slim Authority key-token, gold-cased, the real thing, no forgery. "Nari. Whatever you find down here, it's worth nothing if we can't put it where the city can see it. This is my father's broadcast clearance. Every relay, every screen, the Calibration feed itself. I lifted it three days ago and I've been telling myself I took it by accident." His mouth twists. "I didn't take it by accident."

I stare at the little gold key. The whole city, in his palm.

"That ends you," I say. "If they know you have it —"

"They already know what I am. They just haven't caught up to it yet." He closes my hand around the token, over the finder-sliver already there, and for half a second his cold shaking fingers stay wrapped around mine. "Keep it. You're the one they'll believe."

Then he's gone, walking at the amber light, chin up, intern's voice ringing off the wet stone — "Rank audit, hold your position, who authorized a sweep on this —" buying us the minute, spending the last of who he used to be to do it.

"Got it," Juno breathes. "Nari. *Got it.*"

She comes back with two tokens cupped in her hands like water she's afraid of spilling. Old casings, scavenged-looking, pulled from the reeds of two figures in the deepest rank who never so much as flinched as she opened their throats. One token is scored with a wave-line — a sensor's mark. The Char readings. Ten years of a thinning sky, logged and hidden inside the exact people the world was built to forget. The other is scratched with a small crude spiral. Juno turns it and squints and goes pale.

"This one's a gallery schematic," she whispers. "The whole engine. How the Choir feeds the scrubbers. The throttle-cycles. The R-Class intake logs — names, Nari, dates, where they came from and what they were tiered down for." She swallows. "This is the machine confessing. This is all of it."

Proof. Real proof, Tess said. In my hands now, both halves — the good news they killed her for, and the crime of killing her for it.

An alarm goes off.

It is not a bell. Down here nothing is loud, because loud costs air. It is worse than a bell: the hum falters. The Choir's endless in-hold-out stutters, catches, and every reed in the gallery — ten thousand dying blue coals — flushes at once to a hard warning red, and the ranks, the standing dead, all of them, turn their fogged faces toward us on a single beat. Not seeing. Steered. The engine has noticed a wound in itself and is looking at the wound with ten thousand borrowed eyes.

"They cut the masking," Juno says, checking her sliver, and her voice cracks. "Our reeds are lit. They see us. They see *exactly* us."

My own reed flares red-blue at my throat and the throttle hits and the air goes to nothing — gray slams in from every side, the fog, the undertow, the pull to just *stop*, to stand in the rank and breathe on the beat and let it all be simple. I go down to one knee. So does Orrin, far off, mid-sentence. So does Juno, gasping.

"Up," Salt roars, and it is the loudest thing I have heard in the deep, an old man spending years of breath in one word. "Up the east stair — *go* — I've got the counters —"

I can't. There's no air. There's no —

*One more.* Tess's cold fingers, ghost-light on my throat. *You always have one more.*

I find one. I always have one more. I get my sister's arm over my shoulders — she comes off the beat with a small lost sound, dead weight and cold, but she comes — and I haul us both upright, and Juno takes Tess's other side, and we go, we drag her toward the east stair through ranks that turn to watch us with empty red eyes.

Salt does not come with us.

He goes the other way. Toward the counters pounding down the central aisle, toward the amber lights, toward the choke-point where the ducts and the throttle-lines all run through one old junction that a scrubber-tech of forty years would know better than the men who built it. I look back once. I shouldn't. He's at the junction with both hands buried in the machine's throat, doing something to the throttle-lines — and up and down the gallery, in ragged patches, red reeds flicker back toward blue, throttles releasing, air coming back into a hundred starved chests at once so that a hundred bodies stagger and *breathe*, really breathe, and the counters are on him, and he does not stop, and he is grinning, I swear he is grinning, the way you grin when you've finally found the one thing you were saving all that breath for.

"Salt —"

"Go, girl." He doesn't turn around. "Tell it loud. Tell it so loud they can't ever make it quiet again."

The gallery swallows him. I don't see the end of it. I am not allowed to see the end of it, and I understand that this is a mercy, his and the dark's together — that some things you carry out are lighter for not being seen. My reed is climbing back to that thin flickering blue, air trickling in, because whatever he did to the lines is holding, because he spent himself to buy every last one of us the breath to run. I don't know if he lives. I will never quite know. I choose, in the years after, to keep him grinning at that junction, hands in the machine, uncounted and unbought at last.

We climb.

The east stair is endless and my reed is a coal and Tess weighs nothing and everything, and Juno counts for both of us — "one, two, one, two" — and Orrin catches us at the third landing, bloodied at the temple where a counter caught him, the gold broadcast token gone into some inner pocket, fully ours now, fully fallen, no way back to the Crest for him ever again. He gets Tess's weight off me before I drop.

The stair turns and turns. Twice we hear boots above us and press Tess flat between our bodies against the wet stone and wait, counting, while amber light slides across the landing overhead and moves on. Once a duct-grille breathes hot used air across us and Tess's head comes up and her lips start to move toward the beat, reaching for the rhythm they trained into her bones, and I hold her face to my collarbone and count louder — one, two, one, two — until her breathing snags free of it again. She is heavier at every landing. She is lighter than she has any right to be. My reed is a coal and then, slowly, a flicker, air trickling back as Salt's work on the throttle-lines holds and holds.

We come up out of the deep the way you break the surface of black water — too fast, ears roaring, lungs tearing at air that is only a little less thin than the air below but tastes, God, tastes almost sweet after that. Into a service passage. Into the lowest edge of the Undertow I know, the thin blue reed-light of home flickering along the low ceiling. Behind us the alarm still stutters through the stone, and somewhere down there the ranks are singing again, or falling silent, and Salt is one or the other.

We stop, the four of us, in the dark, holding each other up. Tess between us, breathing off the beat now, ragged and hers. In my fist: two tokens — the thinning sky, the confessing machine. In Orrin's jacket: the whole city's screens. Around us: a hunt, waking. My reed is red-flagged now; they'll read it anywhere I go. They know my face. They know all our faces.

"They'll be coming," Orrin says quietly. "Every Calibrator in Aequor. By morning my father will know it was me."

Juno wipes her mouth, leaves a streak of grime, and lets out a laugh that is half a sob. "Then let's give them something to come *for.*"

I look down at my sister's face in the thin blue light of my dying reed. Her eyes are shut. Her chest rises on a rhythm that is finally, barely, her own.

We have her. We have the proof. We have the codes.

We have lost Salt to get them, and the whole weight of the Concord is turning over in its sleep to find us, and the surface is thinning over the drowned coast above us while a city breathes on the beat below.

"Then let's give them something," I say, and we go up, into the Undertow, into what's left before the light.
