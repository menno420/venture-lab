## Chapter 7 — R-Class

The message comes in on Juno's stolen frequency, a week after Calibration, while I am sitting on the floor of our unit counting my breaths down from twenty.

Twenty. That is what Shallow-2 buys you in a day. I have learned to make it last by moving slow, by not climbing, by keeping my sentences short even inside my own head. The reed at my throat glows a thin, flickering blue, like a candle somebody keeps almost blowing out. When I stand too fast the gray comes in at the edges and the fog behind it, soft and patient, whispering that it would be easier to lie down. To agree. To sing.

I do not lie down.

Juno's contraband patch buzzes against my collarbone. A single word scrolls across the film she stitched under my skin last week, faint green letters only I can see.

*ROOF. NOW. — O.*

Orrin.

I go up. It costs me. By the time I reach the maintenance roof over Tier Nine my count is at sixteen and my knees are singing a song of their own. The roof is not really a roof — nothing in Aequor opens to the sky, the sky would kill us, the Char would eat our lungs from the inside — it is a service deck under a false ceiling, pipes and condensation and the great warm breath of the scrubbers pushing up from below. But it is high, for us. The reed-light of the whole Undertow spreads out beneath the grating, thousands of thin blue points in the dark, a drowned city of stars.

Orrin is waiting by the vent housing. Crest boy. Gold reed-light at his throat, soft as butter, and I hate for one ugly second how much air that color means. He is not standing like the Orrin I met on my courier run — the earnest one, the one who defended the system with his whole clean face. He is standing like someone who has been sick.

"You shouldn't have come up," he says. "Your allotment—"

"You called me up."

"I know." He drags a hand through his hair. "I know. I'm sorry. I couldn't send it. I had to say it out loud, to a person, or I was going to—" He stops. Starts over. "I went into my father's files."

I wait. Waiting is cheap. It costs no air.

"You're not supposed to be able to," he says. "I'm an intern. I file things. But I have his old credentials from when he trained me on the archive, and he never—people don't think interns look. People don't think their own sons look." A short, sick laugh. "I looked because of you. Because I watched them tier you down and I've watched a hundred Calibrations and I've never once—" He shakes his head. "You tested at the top, Nari. I saw the raw scores before they sealed them. Top of your cohort. Aptitude, retention, problem-set, all of it. And they put you in Shallow-2."

"I know what they did." My voice comes out flat. The fog likes flat. "Tell me the thing you came to tell me."

He pulls a data-sliver from his sleeve — Authority issue, matte gray, nothing like Salt's grubby smuggled token. "Your sister's file has a flag on it. R-Class. You told Juno, Juno told me, and I—I found what it means." He turns the sliver in his fingers like it burns. "R-Class isn't a posting. It's not the Surface Corps. The Surface Corps is—" His throat works. "There is almost no Surface Corps, Nari. Nobody goes up. You can't go up. The Char would—everybody knows the Char would."

"Then where do the vanished go," I say. Not a question. I already feel the answer coming up through the soles of my feet, in the hum. In the Choir's low patient hum that never, ever stops.

"Down," Orrin says. "R-Class means Reassigned."

The word lands and I understand it all at once, the way you understand you've cut yourself a full second before it hurts.

"They take the ones who won't be quiet," he says, faster now, like if he stops he'll never finish. "Not criminals. Dissenters. People who ask the wrong questions in the wrong rooms. They don't jail them, that would leave a record, a body, a family asking. They *throttle* them. All the way down — down past shallowing, down to where the fog isn't a fog anymore, it's a fog you live in, it dims you until you don't argue, you don't remember, you just—breathe. In time. And they put them in the deep galleries, below the ones the maps admit to, and they make them Breath-labor. They sing in the Choir that runs the scrubbers." His voice cracks clean in half. "The people we disappear power the machine we disappear them with. And we tell their families they went to the surface to be heroes. We give the families a *commendation* to hang on the wall."

The commendation. Tess's commendation. Surface Reclamation Corps, embossed and proud, over our table, the thing Papa dusts and won't talk about. The proud lie. I have eaten dinner under my sister's proud lie for three years.

I don't remember standing up. The gray comes in hard and the roof tilts and Orrin catches my arm — his hand is warm, steady, a Crest boy's unhurried hand — and for a moment I let it hold me, which I will think about later and be angry about.

"Breathe," he says. "Nari. Count."

I count. Twelve. Eleven. The fog wants me to sit down and be grateful he told me. The fog wants me grateful for everything, that's its whole job.

"She's alive," I say.

Orrin goes still.

"That's what you're telling me." The words come out of the gray slow and terrible and I hold onto each one. "Tess didn't go up and burn. Salt's token pinged her reed fourteen months ago from a sub-level. If they don't kill the R-Class — if they *use* them — then she's down there. Right now. Under our feet." My hand finds the grating, the warm rising breath of the machine, the hum. "She's been under my feet the whole time. Singing."

"Nari—"

"She could be alive."

"She could be." He says it carefully, like a thing that might break. "I don't know how—I don't know what fourteen months of throttling does to a person, I don't—" He stops himself from saying it. *I don't know if there's enough of her left.* I hear it anyway. I file it somewhere I won't look at yet.

"You know what your father does," I say.

The color goes out of his face. Gold reed, white skin. "Don't."

"He's the Prime Calibrator. He signs the flags. R-Class on my sister's file — that's his hand, or a hand he trained." I am not being kind and I can't afford to be. "You've spent your whole life eating his air, Orrin. Breathing free because he decided my sister shouldn't."

"I didn't know." His eyes are wet and furious, at me, at himself, at the sliver in his hand. "You think I knew? You think I'd—" He presses the heels of his hands to his eyes. When he takes them away something has changed in his face, some soft last thing has gone out of it. "My whole life he told me it was mercy. Rationed mercy. He'd sit me on his knee and tell me a city that can't breathe for all has to teach its children how much air they've earned. Like it was a lullaby. And I *believed* it, because he was gentle when he said it, because he tucked me in with clean lungs while somewhere under the floor a girl my age was—" He can't finish. He doesn't have to.

The hum comes up through both of us. I have felt it my whole life — through the floor of our unit, through the soles of my shoes at the scrubber-market, through the bones of my feet when I lie awake counting breaths. The low patient hum of the Choir, the metered breathing of the Undertow, the sound I was taught to call the heartbeat of the city. I used to think it was comforting. A city breathing for you while you sleep. Now I hear it for what it is: thousands of throttled mouths, on the count, driving the machine that throttles them. And somewhere in that sound, one voice I would know anywhere, breathing exactly as much as she's allowed and not one breath more.

"I'll help you," he says.

"You can't take it back if you say that."

"I know." He holds out the gray sliver. "Everything I found. The gallery schematics — the real ones, the deep levels. The reassignment logs, what I could pull before the credentials timed out. It's not enough to go on yet. But it's a start." His jaw sets. "There's a way down, Nari. There has to be, because they get *them* down there somehow, and things that go down can come up."

I take the sliver. It's cold. Everything Authority is cold.

Below us the reed-lights of the Undertow flicker in their thousands, thin and blue and metered, and now I can't look at them the same. Every one of those lights is a person breathing exactly as much as Vane decided they'd earned. And somewhere below the lowest of them, below the galleries the maps admit, in a dark that isn't on any map, my sister is breathing in time with the machine that's erasing her.

"They sent her down for asking a question," I say.

"For something," Orrin says. "The file doesn't say what. Just R-Class. Just—too loud."

Too loud. Tess, who used to sing me to sleep off-key on purpose to make me laugh, who told me the world was bigger than the dome even when saying so could tier you down. Too loud.

I close my hand around the sliver until the edges bite.

"Then I'm going to go get her," I say. "Down past the last light. Wherever they put the people who wouldn't be quiet."

The fog comes for me, soft, reasonable, offering rest. *Sit down. Be grateful he told you. Let it be enough to know.*

I count my breaths and I do not sit down.

Nine. Eight.

I have air enough to get back home. And now I know exactly where I'm spending the rest of it.
