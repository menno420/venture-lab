## Chapter 8 — The Deep Line

Juno spreads Orrin's schematic across the floor of her tinker-nook and we look at Aequor the way you'd look at a body — from the inside, at all the parts that keep it alive and all the parts it hides.

"So this is the lie," she says, tapping the public map. Neat, clean, official. It shows the city like a stacked cake: the Crest at the top with its gold light and its oxygen-gardens, the mid-tiers under it in green, the Undertow at the bottom in blue. And under the Undertow, the galleries — where the scrubbers live, the great bio-machines, the lungs of the city. On the public map the galleries are the floor of the world. The map ends there. Nothing below. A clean black line, and under the line, printed small: *STRUCTURAL — NO ACCESS.*

"And this," Juno says, laying Orrin's gray sliver's image over it, "is the truth."

Under the clean black line there is more. A lot more. Levels the public map pretends are solid rock, drawn now in the Authority's own hand — deep galleries, spiraling down, ring under ring under ring, each one darker than the last on the schematic like the mapmaker was running out of ink or nerve. At the very bottom, unlabeled, a wide black chamber where all the airflow arrows converge. Everything the scrubbers breathe comes up from there. Everything the city exhales goes down to it.

"The deep line," Juno says, and traces the black boundary with one grease-dark finger. "Above it, you're a citizen. Below it, you don't exist." She looks at me. "And your reed knows exactly which side you're on."

That's the part that's been keeping me up, on the nights I have air enough to lie awake. The reed. This thing in my throat that meters me and colors me and tells the Concord where I am every second of every day. You cannot walk into a place that doesn't exist wearing a device that announces you.

"Talk me through it," I say.

Juno's whole face changes when you ask her about tech — the funny goes quiet and something fierce comes up under it. She was always going to be tiered down, my Juno. She's too good at seeing how things really work.

"Three problems," she says, holding up three fingers, each one blunt-nailed and burn-scarred. "One: your reed pings its ID to the gallery relays constantly. Cross the deep line and an unregistered ID lights up like a flare — no Breath-labor tag, no work assignment, just a Shallow-2 girl where no Shallow-2 girl should be. They'd throttle you before you got a level down." She folds the finger. "Two: even if I mask your ID, your *allotment* still comes from up here. The deeper you go, the farther your air has to reach, and the relays that feed you get thinner and older and easier for them to just—" she snaps, "—cut. Below the last known gallery there might be no signal to feed you at all. You'd be breathing whatever's in the tank I can strap to you and nothing more."

"How much is that?"

"With what I can steal? A few hours. Maybe." She won't lie to me, Juno. It's one of the things I love in her and one of the things that terrifies me. "Three." The last finger. "The Choir. The deep galleries run on it — the workers breathing in metered unison, in rhythm, feeding the scrubbers. If you're down there and you're not singing on the count, you stand out. Your chest moves wrong. They *watch* for that. A body breathing free in a room full of metered breath is the loudest thing in the world."

I look at the black chamber at the bottom of the map. The place all the arrows point.

"Can you get me past all three?"

Juno is quiet for a moment. Then she pulls a cracked case from under her bench and opens it, and inside, nested in foam, is a mess of her handiwork — a spoofed reed-collar to fake a Breath-labor ID, a slim tank the size of my forearm, a little counter that will breathe with me, ticking the Choir's rhythm against my wrist so my chest learns to lie. Weeks of work. She's been building this since before I asked.

"You knew I'd go," I say.

"I've known you since we were seven." She closes the case gently. "You went the second Salt handed you that token. The rest of us are just catching up."

—

Papa finds out because I get careless with the fog.

That's the cruelty of shallowing nobody warns you about — it doesn't just thin your body, it thins your *guard*. I come home with three hours' air still ringing in my ears from Juno's counter and I leave the gray sliver on the table while I splash water on my face, and when I come back Papa is standing over it, and the schematic is glowing up out of it into the dim room. The deep galleries. The black line. The chamber at the bottom.

He doesn't shout. That's the worst of it. My father, who is careful about everything, careful the way small animals are careful, careful because he learned that loud people vanish — he just sits down at the table very slowly, like his legs have stopped agreeing to hold him, and he stares at the map of the place under the city, and his face falls apart.

"Where did you get this," he says.

"Papa—"

"Where did you get this, Narine."

"Does it matter? You already know what it is." I'm too tired and too breathless to be gentle and I hate myself for it even as it happens. "You work the scrubbers. You've worked them my whole life. You've stood over that black line every single day." My voice is shaking now. "Tell me you didn't know."

He puts his face in his hands.

And my father, who I have watched dust Tess's commendation every week for three years, who taught me to keep my sentences short and my questions shorter, who I thought was just broken and small and afraid — my father starts to cry, and it is the most frightening thing I have ever seen.

"They came to me," he says into his hands. "After they took her. A Calibrator. Not Vane — one of his. He sat where you're sitting. He was very kind." He laughs, a terrible wet sound. "They're always kind when they're doing the worst thing. He told me Tess had been reassigned. He used that word. Reassigned. And he told me she was safe, and that if I was a good father, a *quiet* father, she'd stay safe, and so would you. He said—" Papa's hands come down and his eyes are red and ruined and looking at something that isn't in the room. "He said, *You have one daughter left, Elias. Think about her air.*"

The room is very quiet. Under the floor, the hum.

"You knew she didn't go to the surface," I say.

"I knew." Barely a whisper.

"You knew she was *down there.*" I point at the black chamber like I could push my finger through the floor to it. "Under us. All this time. And you dusted her lie and you told me to keep my head down and you let me *grieve* her like she was gone when she was—"

"I was protecting you!" It bursts out of him and then folds instantly back into something smaller, more shamed. "I was protecting you. I told myself that every day. Every day, Nari. I said it so many times I almost—" He stops. "It stopped being true a long time ago and I kept saying it because the truth was that I was a coward, and a coward with one daughter left will tell himself anything." He looks at me and I have never seen a person so far down. "The silence ate her, wherever she is. And it's been eating me. Every breath I've taken in this room for three years I've stolen from the one I let them take."

I should feel vindicated. I don't. I feel like the floor of the world has cracked and shown me the deep galleries under my own father.

"Then help me get her back," I say.

"No." Instant. He's on his feet. "No. You've seen the map, so now you'll do what you always do, you'll go, and they'll take *you* too, and I'll have a wall with two commendations and no daughters at all." He grips my shoulders, and his hands are a scrubber-tech's hands, strong and scarred, and they're trembling. "Nari. Please. I am begging you. Let me have kept one of you safe. Let that be the thing I did. Stop."

For a moment I almost do.

The fog helps him make his case — *stop, be safe, be grateful you still breathe at all, let it be enough.* The fog and my father want the exact same thing and that is how I know I can't give it to them.

"I can't, Papa," I say, and gently, gently, I take his shaking hands off my shoulders. "You kept me safe by keeping me quiet. And I've been quiet my whole life, and it didn't save Tess, and it won't save me." I fold his hands closed around nothing. "I'd rather go loud."

He sits back down. He doesn't say yes. But he doesn't take the sliver, doesn't wipe the map, doesn't call it in — he just sits there under the weight of three years, looking at the door I'm going to walk out of, and something moves behind his eyes that isn't quite a decision yet.

Not help. Not yet.

But the door in him that's been shut for three years is standing open a crack, and light is coming through.

"When," he says finally. Hoarse. Not *if.*

"Soon," I say. "As soon as Juno's ready."

I gather up the sliver and the map of the place under the city, and I go to learn how to breathe like a lie.
