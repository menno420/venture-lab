## Chapter 9 — Vane

The summons comes on Authority letterhead, which means it isn't a summons, it's a fact that hasn't happened yet.

*Narine Solenne. Shallow-2. You are requested to attend the Prime Calibrator. Tier Nine, the Assessor's Terrace, at second bell.* No reason. They don't give reasons. The reed at my throat warms for a half-second when I read it — a little pulse, *we can find you, we can feel you, we know where you are* — and then goes back to its thin flickering blue.

Juno wants to mask me and run. "He knows," she says. "Nari, if he's calling you up by name, he *knows.*"

"Then running proves it." My hands are steady, which surprises me. "If I don't go, they take me tonight and I never see the deep line. If I go and he doesn't know everything, I walk out and we move up the descent." I check the spoof-collar, hidden under my real one. "Either way I learn what he has."

"And if he does know everything?"

"Then at least one of us should know what the last conversation looked like." I mean it as a joke. It comes out true. Juno doesn't laugh.

—

The Assessor's Terrace is the highest place I have ever stood. It is a garden. That is the thing that undoes me before Vane says a single word — that this high in the city, they've spent the air on *plants*, real ones, green and wet and breathing, oxygen just pouring off the leaves into a room where three people sit where a hundred could. My reed reads the richness and flickers, confused, hungry. For the first time in a week my head clears. The fog lifts. Up here I could think for the rest of my life.

That's the trap and I know it's the trap and it works anyway.

Calibrator Vane stands at the rail with his back to me, looking down through a viewport at the drowned coast beyond the dome — gray water, gray haze, the Char lying over the dead world like a lid. Gold reed-light at his throat, so bright and steady it barely reads as a color, more like a small sun he swallowed and kept.

"Narine," he says, without turning. "Do you know why they put the gardens at the top?"

"So the people at the top can breathe."

"So the people who make the decisions can breathe *clearly.*" He turns. He is not what I expected. I expected a monster and I get a tired, elegant man with kind eyes and Orrin's exact jaw, and the kindness is the worst part, because it's real, I can see that it's real, and that means everything he's about to do he's going to do *gently.* "Fear is a poor engine for governance. A frightened man rations badly. So we lift the ones who decide above the fear, and give them air enough to be humane." He gestures me to a seat, to the green air, to the clarity. "Sit. Breathe. You've been shallow a week. I'd rather you thought clearly for this. It's the least I can offer someone as capable as you."

I sit. The air fills me and it is glorious and I hate how glorious it is.

"You've seen your scores," he says. "Orrin will have shown you, he's soft that way. Top of your cohort. So you already know what you came here to accuse me of. Say it. I find it's better spoken."

"You tier down the ones who'll fight you." My voice is clearer than it's been in days; the good air lends it edges. "Calibration doesn't measure what we can do. It measures whether we'll stay quiet about it. You take the strong and the loud and you throttle them small."

"Yes," Vane says.

Just that. No flinch. It lands harder than any denial could.

"Now let me tell you why," he says, and sits across from me, and folds his hands, and here it comes — not a monster's rant, a teacher's lesson, patient and terrible. "The air is scarce, Narine. Not as a policy. As a *fact.* The scrubbers make what they make and no more, and every year there are more lungs and the same machines, and the Char is what it is, and the sea is where it is, and no speech, no anger, no clever girl from the Undertow changes the arithmetic of how many breaths exist to be divided." He leans in. "So the only question — the *only* question — is how we divide them. And here is what I have learned in thirty years of dividing air. When you share it equally among the frightened, the frightened panic, and the panicked hoard, and the hoarding kills more than any rationing ever could. I have seen it. Before the Concord there were open cities on this coast that tried to breathe freely, all at once, everyone equal, and do you know what they are now?" He nods at the viewport, the gray water. "They are *under* it. Every soul. They breathed themselves to death being fair."

"So you sedate people. You erase them."

"I *dim* the few who would otherwise inflame the many." His voice never rises. That's what makes it monstrous — not cruelty, calm. "One girl at the top of her cohort, loud and gifted and certain, breathing full and telling the Undertow that they've been robbed — do you know what she costs, in air? Not her own breaths. The panic. The unrest. The crackdowns. The rationing we tighten on everyone to survive the disorder she creates. She costs a *thousand* breaths for every one she takes. So yes. I put her small. I put the loud ones down where the loud does no harm and their breath still turns the machine that keeps the quiet ones alive." He spreads his hands, and for one moment I see that he is not lying and not gloating — he *believes* it, all the way down, this is a man who tucks his son in at night and sleeps soundly. "A sedated many is a cruelty. A suffocated all is an extinction. I choose the cruelty. Every day, with clear eyes, I choose it." He says it like the last line of a prayer. "This is not cruelty. This is arithmetic."

And here is the thing I will never tell Juno, or Orrin, or anyone:

For three breaths of that beautiful stolen garden air, I believe him.

Because he isn't wrong that the air is scarce. He isn't wrong that people panic. I have watched the fog make my own mother's neighbors turn on each other over a day's scrip. I have felt, this very week, how much easier it is to comply, how the body *wants* to be small and safe and fed. He has taken the true and the terrible and braided them so tight I can't find the seam, and part of me — the shallow, tired, frightened part the throttle built — wants to lay my head down in this green air and call it wisdom.

Then I think of Tess singing me to sleep off-key, on purpose, to make me laugh.

And I find the seam.

"You keep saying *the arithmetic,*" I say slowly. "Like it's weather. Like it fell out of the sky. But you built the machine that makes the numbers this small. You decide how many scrubbers. You decide who gets the gardens." I look around the terrace, the plants, the hundred people's air spent on three. "You've got enough oxygen up here to breathe on the *decorations.* Don't tell me it's arithmetic. It's arithmetic *you're doing on purpose,* and you keep the sum small because a small sum is the only thing that makes you necessary."

Something moves in his face. Not anger. Interest. Like I'm a problem that turned out harder than the file said.

"You see," he says softly, "*that* is why you're at the top of your cohort. And *that* is exactly why you can't be allowed to breathe freely." He sits back. "So. Let me be as honest as you've been. I know you have the deep schematics. I know Orrin has been in my files — no, don't, I'm not going to touch him, he's my son and this is a phase and phases end. I know you're looking for your sister." He lets that sit. "Tess is alive, Narine. In a manner of speaking."

My whole body goes to ice in the warm green air.

"She's comfortable," he says, and it is the cruelest word anyone has ever said to me. "She's quiet. She doesn't suffer, wherever the throttle takes them, past the point of suffering. And here is what I'm offering you, once, clearly, in air good enough to think in." He leans forward. "Stop. Give me the sliver, forget the deep line, and I will move you up. Tier Six. Green light, real rooms, no more counting. Your father breathes easier. And your sister stays exactly as she is — untouched, undisturbed, *left alone.* You can't wake her, Narine. That's not on the table; there's no waking them. But you can stop poking the place she's buried before you get her buried deeper, and yourself beside her." He spreads his hands, gentle, final. "You can breathe. You can live. You can have a life. All you have to do is stop trying to have the truth."

And it is real. That's what he's counted on and he's counted right. It is a real offer and a real life and I can *feel* the green air in my chest telling me to take it, telling me I've done enough, telling me that knowing where she is could be enough, that some fights you lose by winning them.

I look at him for a long time.

"Okay," I say.

His eyes narrow, just slightly.

"I'm tired," I say, and I let my shoulders go, and I let the fog I've been fighting all week finally show on my face, because the one thing they trained into me my whole life is how to look small and quiet and beaten, and I have never needed the skill more. "I've been shallow a week and I can't think and I'm scared, and I don't want to end up wherever she is." My voice cracks and the crack is real even if the surrender is not. "Tier Six. And you leave her alone. And you leave Juno alone." A breath. "I'll bring you the sliver tomorrow."

Vane studies me. He is brilliant and I am sixteen and shallow and I do not, for one second, believe I've fooled him completely — but I've given him the shape of what he wanted, and men who believe they're merciful *want* to believe the girl chose mercy. He wants my surrender to be true almost as much as I want it to look true.

"Tomorrow," he agrees. He stands. "You're a sensible girl, Narine. Your sister would be proud you chose to breathe." He turns back to the rail, to the gray coast, done with me. "Second bell. Don't be late. And Narine—" He doesn't look around. "Enjoy the air on the way down. It's the last of it you'll waste on a garden for a while."

I walk out of the green room and back toward the thin blue, and with every step the fog comes back into my head and I let it, I welcome it, because a girl walking down into the fog is a girl who's been beaten, and that's exactly what he needs to see on the cameras.

Tomorrow I won't bring him the sliver.

Tomorrow I go down.
