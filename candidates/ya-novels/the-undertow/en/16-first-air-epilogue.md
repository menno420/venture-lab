## Epilogue — First Air

Months later, on the morning the delegation is meant to go up, the whole Undertow dims its reeds at once.

You wouldn't see it if you didn't know to look — and the Authority still doesn't, quite, or has decided it's cheaper not to. Just a soft blue tide moving through the deep levels at dawn, ten thousand reeds falling together to the same low, flickering blue, held for one breath, then rising. It isn't sanctioned. It isn't forbidden either, because no one has found the words to forbid it, and I have come to love that most of all — that the thing they can't stop is the smallest thing there is, a whole tier of people breathing at each other in the dark to say *we remember.*

The delegation is sanctioned, though. That's the part I still can't quite hold in my head. Six people, chosen and argued over and grudgingly stamped by a cracked Concord that could not, in the end, keep pretending the Char readings didn't exist once half a million people had seen them scroll across their own walls. Six people in sealed suits, going up through the old surface locks that haven't opened in three generations, to stand on the drowned coast and test the thinning sky. To take a reading no one is allowed to bury this time. Maybe, if the numbers are kind, to open a visor for one careful breath of outside air.

The first real breath of the surface in longer than anyone alive can remember.

Tess and I are at the high viewport when it's time, the one on the maintenance line Papa used to service, where you can see past the dome-skin to the outside. Her hand is in mine. Her reed reads a steady mid-blue now, most days — not free, not gold, but steady, and steady is a country I once thought she'd never live in again. She still breathes four-and-four when she's tired. This morning she isn't tired. This morning she's breathing however she wants, ragged and uneven and gloriously wrong, and I've never heard a better sound.

Outside, the coast is gray and drowned and enormous, the old streets under the old water, the bones of the world before the Rising. And above it, where all my life there has been only the low sick bruise of the Char, there is a change.

You have to have grown up under it to see it. A thinning. A place, low over the horizon where the sun is coming up, where the haze has worn to something you could almost call translucent — and through it, faint, impossible, the color I have only ever seen in the Crest's stolen gardens and never once in the sky.

Green. A green edge on the world.

"There," Tess breathes. "There it is. I told them. I told them and they put me in the dark, and there it is anyway."

Far below and to the east, a lock cycles. I can't see the six of them step out onto the drowned coast, but I feel the city hold still around it — feel, through the soles of my feet, the Choir in the deep galleries falter and stop its metered rhythm to listen, ten thousand held breaths, the whole Undertow leaning up toward the surface at once.

I think about Salt, who is a name we say softly and don't finish. I think about the names on the token and the names that weren't. I think about a green reed I didn't take and a blue one I'll wear the rest of my life. I think, mostly, about a girl three days from her Calibration who checked her scrip number in the thin blue light and thought the whole world was the size of the count.

My reed reads its low flickering blue. It will never read gold. I've made my peace with that. The machine did not stop; the sky is not yet ours; there is so much still to finish that some mornings the size of it sits on my chest like water.

But out there on the drowned coast, right now, six people are lifting their visors to a thinning sky, and any second one of them is going to draw breath — the first un-metered, un-earned, un-rationed breath the surface has been given back in generations — and it will not glow a color, and no one will have told them how much of it they deserve, and no one will be able to take it away.

Tess squeezes my hand.

We stand at the viewport, my sister and I, and we watch the Char thin over the drowned coast, and we breathe — badly, freely, ours — and for the first time in my whole counted life I do not count.
