## Chapter 14 — Everyone Breathes

The Calibration broadcast opens the way it has opened all my life: the low hum swelling under the whole city as the scrubbers take the strain, the wall-screens brightening in every corridor from the Crest down to the last flickering room of the Shallows, and Vane's voice, calm as a closed door, telling the children of Aequor that today they will learn how much air they have earned.

I am standing in the wings of the Authority hall in a courier's gray, and my heart is going so hard I'm sure the reed can hear it.

Orrin got me in. That's the part that could have killed us at the first checkpoint and didn't — his face, his name, his father's name, the intern's badge he hasn't turned in, walking me past two Calibrators like I'm a runner carrying his notes. I feel their eyes slide off me. I feel the fog-edge of my own shallowing pull at the corners of the room, that gray softness that wants me to be quiet, be small, be good, and I breathe wrong on purpose, ragged, and keep my edge.

In my ear, Juno's voice, thin over the stolen channel. "We're in the spine. I have the screens. I have the Choir feed. Orrin's got the throttle handshake queued to your dad." A crackle. "Nari. Once I flip the screens to you, there's no flipping back quiet. You ready to be the loudest girl in Aequor?"

Below us, three sub-levels down, in the deep dark, ten thousand people are breathing four-and-four to keep the lights on up here.

"Ready," I say.

On the stage, in the gold, Vane lifts a hand, and the first name of the first sixteen-year-old scrolls up the great screen, and a girl in mid-green steps into the light to be measured.

"Now," I say.

Juno flips the city.

Every wall-screen in Aequor — the Crest gardens, the mid-tier corridors, the Shallow rooms, the deep galleries where they've bolted screens so the Choir can watch what it can never have — every one of them cuts from Vane's gold stage to a gray girl in a courier's coat with a thin flickering blue reed at her throat, and that girl is me, and for one endless second I forget every word.

Then I think of Tess folding her hand around the proof, and I find them.

"My name is Narine Solenne," I say, and the sound of my own voice going out to a whole city nearly stops me, but I don't stop. "I'm sixteen. Three days ago I was told by this city that I had earned the lowest air it makes. I want to tell you what I've learned since, because in about one minute they are going to try very hard to make me quiet, and I need you to hear it first."

On the stage, Vane has gone still. Not panicked — still, the way deep water is still. His head turns, unhurried, toward the wings, and his eyes find me across the hall, and I feel the weight of his attention like a hand on my chest.

"There is a classification you were never told about," I say fast, faster, Juno feeding the R-Class token up beside my face so the names scroll where the whole city can read them. "R-Class. Reassigned. The people who don't come home from Calibration, the ones you're told transferred to the Surface Corps — they didn't go up. They went *down*. They're in the deep galleries right now, throttled to nothing, breathing in rows to run the machines that ration your air. My sister is one of them. Tess Solenne. Read the names. They're your names. They're your neighbors. Nobody went to the surface."

I hear it start — a sound that isn't in the room, that's in the whole city at once, ten thousand rooms drawing one breath of shock through ten thousand reeds.

Vane is walking toward me now. Two Calibrators peel off the stage behind him.

"They tell you the air is scarce," I say, and I get Juno to throw up the Char readings, the ten years of them, the falling line, "and it *was*, it was true once, that's the cruelest part, it was true. But look. *Look.* These are the Char readings they don't broadcast. The haze is thinning. Ten years it's been thinning. There's a green edge on the world. The surface is coming back — and if the surface comes back, the scarcity ends, and if the scarcity ends —"

"— then no one needs us to decide who breathes," Vane finishes.

He's close now, close enough that his voice comes to me twice, once through the air and once, a half-beat behind, through the whole city on the open channel he's just stepped into. He is very calm. He is the most frightening thing I have ever stood next to.

"Narine." Almost gentle. "You have the numbers. You've clearly worked so hard. So do the arithmetic with me, in front of everyone, since you've opened the door." He turns, unhurried, to include the city in it, this man who has never once had to raise his voice. "The haze thins by a fraction each year. Suppose I told them today. Suppose I opened the doors. There is not yet enough clean sky for a city of half a million to walk out into — not yet, not for years. So there is a window. In that window, hope is the most expensive thing there is. Hope makes people climb. People who climb, breathe hard. A city that all breathes hard, all at once, on air that is *almost* but not *yet* enough —" his hands open, empty, reasonable "— suffocates. All of it. Everyone. I keep them alive *through the window*, child. Sedated, and small, and alive, until the sky is truly ready. That is not cruelty. That is arithmetic."

And here is the thing I have dreaded ever since he first said those words to me across a table and I had no answer: it's a good argument. It's built like the scrubbers are built, closed and cruel and running. For one shallowing second the gray fog reaches all the way up and I have no words, and half a million people are listening to me have no words.

Then I say the truest thing I know.

"You don't get to breathe for us," I say. "That's it. That's the whole answer. You might even be right about the window — I don't know, I'm sixteen, I can't do half a million people's math. But you did it *for* us, in the dark, and you built a machine out of my sister's lungs so we'd never get to argue. Maybe the sky isn't ready. *We* get to decide what we risk to find out. Not you." I look straight into the gold light, into every room in the city. "So let me show you what you took."

"Papa," I say. "Now."

Three sub-levels down, in a room over Trunk Nine that he has serviced for twenty years, my quiet father puts both hands on the throttle-master and opens every reed in the Undertow at once.

And the whole city breathes.

I feel it before I understand it — a change in the air of the hall, a pressure, a sweetness — and then I feel it in my own throat as my thin flickering blue floods to full and keeps going, and the gray fog at the edges of the room just lifts, peels back, gone, and my head fills with a cold clean dizzy light, and three levels down ten thousand people who have counted every breath since the day they were born feel the count fall silent, feel the throttle open, feel their reeds go gold-bright and stay, and they stop singing four-and-four because they don't have to, because for the first time in any of their lives the air is simply *there*, more than there, an ocean of it, and I hear it come up through the floor and through the spine and through the open channel — not a sound at first, just an enormous held breath finally let go, a whole underclass inhaling all the way to the bottom of lungs that have never once been full, and then the sound, oh, the sound, ten thousand people laughing and weeping and gulping the sweet impossible air at the same time, a noise like a tide coming in, like a city surfacing, like the drowned coming up into the light after generations underwater, and it rolls up through Aequor from the deepest gallery to the Crest gardens and there is not one reed in the whole broadcast that is not, in this impossible minute, running free.

For a few minutes, everyone breathes.

I stand in it. I let my own lungs fill and fill and fill and I think, *so this is what they have up here every single day,* and I think, *Tess, are you feeling this, wherever you are, is it reaching you,* and I hope the Char token is warm in her open hand.

Vane's face doesn't change. But his hand moves.

"Enough," he says, very quietly, to someone I can't see, and he flicks his wrist, the small gesture I have watched in nightmares.

He throttles me. Hard.

The air goes out of the room. Out of my throat. Gray — gray slams up all at once, not the soft fog, the hard edge, the black-rimmed narrowing, and my knees go, and the gold light forks and doubles. Down. I'm down on the boards. Count. Nothing to count. He's cut me to almost nothing and the whole city is watching him do it, watching him do the exact thing I said he does, and part of me, the last clear part, thinks: *good. Let them see.*

Somewhere far away Juno's voice cracks in my ear — "Nari — Nari he's tracing the spine, he's in, I can't hold both, I'm not — " and then a sound I don't like, a short sharp sound and then no Juno, and much later I'll learn what the surge did to her hands and it isn't graphic and it isn't fatal but it is real and it is a cost and she paid it holding the channel open one more minute so my broken voice could keep going out.

And it does. Broken. Fragmented. Present.

"See," I get out, from the floor, into the light. "This. Him. Right now. On me. This is. What. He does."

Across the hall Orrin steps between his father and the throttle-panel.

I can barely turn my head. But I see it. The Crest boy in the Authority gray, planting himself in front of the man whose whole cold gospel raised him, and I hear him — not loud, Orrin's never loud — say, "No. Not her too. I read the files, Father. I read all of them. You can throttle the whole hall. You cannot make me not have read them." And Vane looks at his son the way you'd look at a machine you built that has started, impossibly, to argue back, and in that one flicker — that one hairline flicker of a father's face — I see the crack open. Not the fall. The crack.

It's enough. Orrin gets his hand on the panel. My reed jumps — not to gold, he can't undo what Vane's locked, but off the floor, up to a flickering, staggering blue, enough to think, enough to breathe, ragged, in, out, wrong, alive.

The screens are still full of the falling Char line. The names are still scrolling. The city is still coming down off the deepest breath it has ever taken, and Juno was right, there's no flipping it back quiet, because half a million people just felt in their own chests the difference between what they've been given and what was always there.

Papa closes the throttles before the Authority can trace the room — small again, gone again, safe, our secret valve unfound. The reeds fall back to their colors. The gold, the green, the dim and flickering blue.

But it happened.

It happened, and they felt it, and up and down the whole drowned city, in ten thousand rooms, people are standing very still with their hands on their own throats, remembering — for the rest of their lives now — exactly what it felt like to breathe all the way to the bottom.

I lie on the boards in the gold light and pull the sweet-thin manufactured air into my aching lungs, one breath, wrong, mine, and I count it, and it is not enough, and it is a beginning.
