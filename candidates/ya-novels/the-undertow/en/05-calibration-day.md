## Chapter 5 — Calibration Day

They calibrate you in the light.

That's the first surprise. My whole life the Authority has been a thing that happens in the dark of the galleries, at the edge of the counts, in the gray coveralls that come home slack-eyed. But the Calibration Hall is high in the mid-tiers where the air is a shade less thin, and it is bright — a wide white drum of a room with a domed ceiling and a broadcast-eye at its center, and the eye is watching, because all of Aequor is watching. Every screen in the city, gallery to Crest, carries Calibration Day. It is the one time the tiers all look at the same thing.

We come in by cohort, thirty of us, sixteen years old to the day or near it, in the plain gray shifts they give you at the door so no reed-color shows. That's the mercy they're proud of: for one morning no one can see your tier. We stand equal on the white floor and above us the disposition-board glows blank, thirty names, no tiers yet, and my name is up there — SOLENNE, N. — and my hands are steady. I made them steady on the stair last night. I am going in sharp. I am going in me.

A voice fills the drum, warm, recorded, the Creed I have heard every Calibration of my life. A city that cannot breathe for all must teach its children how much air they have earned. The cohort mouths it. I don't. Small defiance, invisible, mine.

Then the Calibrators come in, six of them in Authority white, and at their center a tall man, older, unhurried, and the room changes temperature. I don't need anyone to tell me who he is. You can feel the deference bend toward him like light around a weight. Prime Calibrator. His whites are cut a little finer; his reed glows a gold so pale it's nearly white. He walks the front of the cohort once, slowly, reading our faces the way the Choir-warden read the ranks, and when his eyes reach me they stop. Just a beat. A held beat. Then move on.

I file it away. I don't know yet to be afraid of it.

The aptitude tasks come first, and the aptitude tasks are mine.

They give us slates and problems, and the problems open under my hands like they were made for me. Airflow logic — balance a scrubber-load across failing intakes, keep the most people breathing with the least scrip. I solve it two ways and pick the better one. A pattern-cascade that half the cohort stalls on; I see the break in it in seconds. A memory-run, a reasoning-run, a spatial map of levels I've never walked that I hold whole in my head because holding whole things in my head is the one thing I have always been able to do. The broadcast-eye drifts to my station and lingers. Somewhere in the Undertow, Papa is watching a screen. Somewhere Juno is watching, chewing her thumb.

I am good at this. I have always been good at this. I let myself believe, for the span of the tasks, that good is what they're measuring.

A green-tier proctor collects my final slate and I catch her small double-take at the score, and she doesn't quite smile at me but almost, and it's the almost that undoes me later. She thought it too. She thought I was going up.

"Temperament read," calls a Calibrator, and the cohort stills.

This is the part no one can study for. They line us up and go down the row and at each of us they do a thing to the reed. A slim Authority wand touches the implant at your throat and the reed answers — reads you, they say, reads your steadiness, your fit. The Creed calls it temperament. Aptitude and temperament, merit and fit, that's the frame, that's what the whole city believes it's watching. I have believed it my whole life without once asking what fit means, or who decides it, or why the word for a good child and the word for a quiet one are allowed to be the same word.

They come to me.

The wand touches my throat and the reed goes hot, and something reaches down the implant into the meter of me, and I feel it the way you feel a stranger's hand close over your wrist — every instinct I have pulls back. I can't help it. It isn't a decision. The thing reaching in wants me to hold still and open and I have spent my whole life in a city that thins your air until you hold still and open, and my sister is buried in a sub-level with two letters stapled to her name, and no. No. Everything in me says no, and the reed reads the no, because the no is the whole point, the no is what the wand is for.

The Calibrator holding the wand doesn't react. But down the row, at the center, the Prime Calibrator turns his head. Just his head. And looks at me. And this time the beat isn't held. This time it's a note taken.

I stand very straight. Let him take his note. I answered every problem right.

They send us to the wall to wait while the board resolves, and it resolves top-down, the way it always does, the proud way, gold names first so the Crest kids can be cheered by their families. One boy up to gold — an audible sob from a good tier, joy. Names roll into green, mid-tier, most of the cohort, the broad safe middle, and I wait for mine among them because green is a floor for me, green is the least of it, I have already outrun green in my head, I am waiting to see how far past it they'll send me.

Green fills. My name isn't in green.

The board keeps rolling. Down out of green into the low mid-tiers, and my chest starts to do something I don't have a word for, and still my name doesn't fall, and now there are only the bottom bands left, the Undertow bands, the blue I wore in the door and thought I was leaving forever, and the cohort has gone quiet around me because they can count too, they can see there's one strong student's name that hasn't landed and only bad places left for it to land.

SOLENNE, N.

It doesn't stop at blue.

It goes past blue. Down to the last band, the thinnest one, the flickering one, the tier that isn't even a place to live so much as a place to be used.

SHALLOW-2.

The drum is silent. Thirty gray shifts and not one sound. Somewhere a screen in the Undertow is showing my father this. Somewhere Juno has stopped chewing her thumb.

I don't understand it. That's the first thing, before the fear, before anything — a pure clean bafflement, because it doesn't compute, it violates the arithmetic they taught me, best scores tier up, that is the rule, that is the mercy, aptitude plus temperament, merit and — 

And temperament.

And the wand at my throat, and the no it read, and the Prime Calibrator turning just his head.

Oh, I think. The floor of the whole world tilts and slides and I finally, finally see the shape of it, standing on the white in a gray shift with my name at the bottom of the board. It was never aptitude. It was never merit. They measured how far I'd bend and I wouldn't, so they put me where the bent go. I aced every problem. That's not why I'm here. I'm here because I said no with my body when the wand asked, and they heard it, and this is the answer. Tier down the capable. Breathless is docile. A strong girl who won't kneel is more dangerous than a weak one who will, so you take her air until she kneels anyway.

I understand it all in the space of one breath, and then they take the breath.

The reed at my throat goes cold and then dim, the blue I've worn my whole life guttering down to a thin flickering blue, the Shallow-2 blue, the Choir blue, and with it the meter turns, and the number that was forty-something this morning drops like a stone, and the first throttle rolls into me.

It comes as fog. Not pain — that would be easier to fight. A softness. A gray creeping in at the edges of the bright white room, the domed ceiling going furred and distant, and a warmth in my head that wants, that actively wants, to lie down and let go and stop making the fist of a face I'm making. My thoughts thin out. Reach for the anger and it slides. Reach for Tess and — Tess — the name goes soft in my hand. This is the fog Papa comes home wearing. This is what the Choir breathes in. They are not just taking my air. They are taking my edge, thinning it down until the girl who said no can't remember why she'd want to.

No. Hold it. Count. Count the way Juno taught me, count something they can't fog. Thirty names. Six Calibrators. One domed room. Hold the edges. Hold —

The Prime Calibrator crosses the white floor to me.

Up close he is not a monster. That's the worst of it. He has a tired, intelligent face, an almost-kind face, and he stops in front of me while the fog laps at my knees and he looks at me with real attention, cold and complete, the way you'd look at a reading that surprised you. He knows my name without the board. I can see that he knows it.

"Solenne," he says. Quiet, under the broadcast. "The tessellation problem. You solved it two ways." A small pause. "Your sister solved it two ways as well."

The fog is in my mouth. I can't answer. He didn't ask a question.

"It is a hard thing," he says, "to be measured true. Most are grateful, in time." He studies the flickering blue at my throat, and something moves behind his eyes that might, in another man, be regret. "You will find the air teaches better than I can."

Then he steps back, and the board holds my name at the bottom, and the broadcast-eye swings away to some gold family's joy on the far side of the drum, and it is over. I am done. I am measured. Sixteen years old and calibrated Shallow-2, the lowest working tier, the Choir tier, and the Prime Calibrator of the Calibration Authority knew my sister's name and the way she thought, and told me so, on purpose, so that I would carry it out of this room.

Calibrator Vane. Someone in the cohort whispers it, awed, afraid. Calibrator Vane spoke to her.

They walk us out through fog and flickering blue, and I make my legs work, and I make my spine stay up, because the one thing I have left is that they can thin the air but they cannot yet make me kneel on the way out the door. I hold that. It's small. I hold it.

They measured me true, he said.

They measured me exactly. That's not the same thing. And now I know it, and there is no giving it back, and somewhere below all this white a sub-level holds a girl who solved the same problem two ways and got the same word stapled to her name.

R-Class.

I carry it down the stair into the thin blue, breathing shallow, breathing counted, awake.
