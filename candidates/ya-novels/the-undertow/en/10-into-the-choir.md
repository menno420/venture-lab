## Chapter 10 — Into the Choir

The stair down to the deep galleries has no light of its own. We bring our own, or we bring none.

Salt goes first, because Salt has been down before and come back, which is more than anyone I know can say. He moves the way old scrubber-techs move, all economy, no waste, one hand always on the wall. Behind him Juno. Behind Juno, me. Behind me, Orrin, and I can hear the wrongness of him even in the dark — a Crest boy's breathing, too easy, too full, a boy who has never in his life counted a single one.

"Reeds," Salt says, low, when the stair bottoms out.

I press two fingers to my throat. My reed is warm under the skin, its thin flickering blue the only color in the world. Shallow-2. The lowest working light there is, and down here it might as well be a lantern held over my head, a hand raised, saying *here, here, take her.*

Juno reaches back. "Hold still."

Her tinker's sliver clicks against the reed's casing. For one bad second the blue guts out entirely and my throat goes cold and the fog rushes up behind my eyes — no meter, no allotment, nothing feeding me — and then the light comes back dimmer, steadier, a false quiet blue that says *asleep, docile, nothing to see.* She does Orrin's next. His gold dies down to that same drowned color. He touches it like it's a wound.

"It won't fool a Calibrator up close," Juno whispers. "It'll fool the counters. That's most of them down here. Counters."

"How long?" I ask.

"Long as the charge holds." She won't look at me. "Don't ask me how long the charge holds."

We go on.

The hum starts in the soles of my feet.

I have felt the Choir my whole life. It lives under the floor of the Undertow the way blood lives under skin — a low, patient thrum you stop hearing because it never stops. Up there it is a rumor. Down here it is the whole air. It comes up through my boots and into the long bones of my legs and settles behind my breastbone, and it is not a machine sound. That is the thing no one tells you. It is a *sung* sound. It rises and falls. It breathes.

The passage opens.

I have tried, since, to find words for the first gallery, and the words keep sliding off, the way your eyes slide off the sun. Picture a cavern the size of a drowned cathedral. Picture the dark not as absence but as a substance, thick, warm, wet at the edges. And in the dark, ranks. Rows and rows and rows of them, receding until the rows are only a suggestion, a texture, a field.

People.

They stand in shallow stalls, each in their own, close as reeds in a bed, and each of them wears a reed gone the dimmest blue I have ever seen — not lit so much as smoldering, an ember under ash. Their chests move. That is what you notice, after the light: the movement, all of it together. In. Hold. Out. In. Hold. Out. A tide of chests, ten thousand chests, breathing on a beat none of them chose, and every out-breath is drawn away — I can see it now, the fine intake grilles above each stall, the soft suck of the ducts — drawn down and away to feed the great lungs of the scrubbers, which make the air, which meters the air, which allots them exactly enough to keep breathing on the beat.

A closed loop. Their rationed breath runs the machine that rations their breath.

This is the Choir. Not a story Papa told to frighten me into gratitude. Not the low rumor that lived under my whole childhood, the thing you learned to stop hearing. This.

All my life I sang a smaller version of it. Every Undertow child does. You breathe on the ration schedule because the alternative is running out before the bell, and the schedule becomes your pulse, and one day you notice you've been keeping the city's time in your own chest without ever being asked. What's below me now is only that, taken to the bottom of itself — the schedule become the whole person, the pulse become the machine. I could have ended here. Any of us could. On a bright morning a Calibrator decides how deep your song goes, and this is the deepest.

"Don't stop," Salt breathes. "Whatever you do down here. Don't stop walking and don't start staring. Staring's a shape. They see shapes."

I make myself walk. I make my face a counter's face, bored, dim. But my eyes go along the near rank anyway, because I cannot help it, because somewhere in this — in *this* — is my sister.

They don't look up. That is the worst of it. We pass close enough to touch and not one head turns. Their eyes are open and their eyes are empty, fogged the gray-white of a shallowing that never lifts, and I understand in my stomach what I only understood in my head before: this is what full throttling does, held for years. It doesn't kill you. It hollows you and leaves you standing, breathing on the beat, useful. Alive is such a small word. They are alive.

A man in the second rank has Papa's build. My step catches. Salt's hand closes on my arm without seeming to move at all.

"Not him," he says. "Not him. Come on."

We come to a junction where a counter stands — an actual officer, not sung-down, a mid-tier in green with a tally-slate, walking the head of the ranks. Salt's whole body changes. He slouches, drops his chin, becomes furniture. Juno takes my sleeve and turns me toward a stall as if reading its number. Orrin —

Orrin walks straight at the counter.

My heart stops. But he has the walk, God help him, he was raised on the walk — the Crest ease, the intern's clipped nod, the assumption of belonging that no forged token can buy. "Rank audit," he says, flat, not unkind. "Authority. Carry on your line."

The counter's eyes flick over the drowned blue of Orrin's reed. Over the rest of us, luggage. Over the slate in Orrin's hand that is not a slate at all but Juno's sliver held slate-wise. A long, thin second, and the hum rising and falling under it, in, hold, out.

"Late for an audit," the counter says.

"Take it up with the schedule," Orrin says, and yawns.

The counter grunts and turns back to his line, and we are through, and I do not breathe until we are two ranks deep in the dark again and even then I only breathe because the reed makes me.

"You," Juno mouths at Orrin, and it is almost a smile, and it is almost fear. He is gray. His hand, when it brushes mine in the passing, is cold and it is shaking, and I close my fingers around two of his for exactly one step. Just to say: *I saw that. I know what that cost you.* Then I let go, because down here a shape is a shape.

We move deeper.

The token Salt gave me a lifetime ago — the one that started all of this, the data-sliver that pinged Tess's reed-ID from a sub-level that isn't on any map — is warm in my fist. Juno has tuned it to sing when it nears the ID it once caught. A finder's ache. It has been silent through three galleries. In the fourth it stirs.

Just a tremor. A single dry click against my palm, like an insect turning in its sleep.

I stop. This time Salt lets me.

"Which way," he says.

I turn slow, the token cupped to my chest, letting it feel. The click quickens when I face the low ranks along the eastern wall, where the stalls are older, the light nearly out, the workers so still they might be part of the stone. It quickens, and quickens, and becomes a small frantic patter, a heartbeat that isn't mine, and my own heart answers it so hard I taste it.

"There," I whisper. "She's there."

Juno grabs my wrist before I can bolt. "Nari. *Nari.* Look at me. When you find her — she won't. You understand? She won't know you. Don't you dare fall apart in the open. Whatever's left of her, we carry it out on our own feet, quiet, or we don't carry it out at all."

I know she's right. I hate that she's right. I nod.

The eastern ranks are the oldest air I have ever tasted — thin past thin, metallic, warm as breath already breathed a thousand times. My reed drags at it. Gray creeps in at the sides of everything. I count. Four steps. Six. The counting steadies me the way it always has, the way Tess taught me when we were small and the air went short in the night — *just count them, Nari, one at a time, you always have one more* — and the memory of her voice pulls me forward faster than the token does.

Stall by stall. Face by dim, sunken face. None of them look up. In. Hold. Out. The whole field breathing me toward her.

The token screams silent in my hand, one long pull, and I stop in front of a stall in the last rank, against the wet stone, where a figure stands with her head bowed and her chest rising on the beat like all the others, thin as a reed herself, her reed the barest coal of blue.

I know the line of her shoulders. Three years and all this dark and I would know it anywhere. I knew it before I could walk.

"Tess," I say. Not loud. It costs everything not to be loud.

She breathes in. She holds. She breathes out. On the beat. She does not look up.

Behind me the Choir sings on, ten thousand chests, in, hold, out, feeding the lungs that starve them, and I reach out with a hand I can barely feel and touch my sister's arm for the first time in three years.
