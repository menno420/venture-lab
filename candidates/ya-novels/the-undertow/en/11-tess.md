## Chapter 11 — Tess

Her skin is cold. That is the first wrong thing. Tess was always warm — a furnace, Papa said, the only one of us who never counted her breaths at night because her own heat kept the cold off. Now her arm under my hand is cool as the stone she stands against, and I feel the beat moving through her, the Choir's beat, her breath borrowed and given back on a rhythm that isn't hers.

"Tess. It's me. It's Nari."

Nothing. In. Hold. Out.

I come around in front of her, into the space between her bowed head and the intake grille, and I take her face in both my hands and lift it, and I make myself look.

I have been telling myself, all the way down, that she would be Tess. Thinner, maybe. Tired. But Tess — the quick eyes, the crooked grin she wore even when the ration bell rang, the sister who taught me to count and to lie and to want more than the light we were given. I built that Tess out of three years of missing her and I carried her down here like a lamp.

The face in my hands is a stranger wearing my sister's bones.

Her eyes are open. They are the fogged gray-white of the ranks, the shallowing that never lifts, and they are aimed at nothing, at a middle distance no one else can see. Her cheeks have fallen in around the shape of her skull. Her lips move a little with each metered breath, the ghost of counting, a habit worn down to reflex. There is nothing behind the glass. The light is on and the house is empty and I am holding a face that does not know it is being held.

"No," I hear myself say. "No, no, no. Tess. Look at me. It's Nari. It's your Nari."

Juno is at my shoulder. "Quiet," she breathes, but even she can hear it isn't working, even she is starting to shake. "Nari, we may have to just — we may have to carry her. Right now. Before —"

"Not yet." I press my forehead to my sister's. Her breath washes over my mouth, thin and warm and used up. On the beat. "Tess. Remember. One at a time. You always have one more. You told me that. *You told me that.*"

Her breathing does not change. But something at the back of her eyes does. A flicker. Gray water when a stone drops through — those rings that spread and die. Her cracked lips shape a sound with no air behind it.

I stop breathing myself so I can hear it.

"...one more," she says.

It is the smallest voice in the world. It is her voice.

I know it the way I know the hum under the floor, the way I know my own name. All my life it read to me in the dark when the ration bell rang and the cold came up through the thin walls of our level. All my life it told me the number on the reed didn't have to be the size of me. Three years I have been building a Tess out of memory and here, at last, is the real one, a single true thread of her surfacing through all that gray water, and it is almost worse than the emptiness, because now I know exactly how much they took.

"Yes," I sob, and swallow the sob because a shape is a shape, because Juno's hand is a vise on my arm reminding me, and I get it down to a shaking whisper. "Yes. One more. Stay with me. Count with me. One."

"...one," she says. Her eyes find mine. For one impossible second they *focus*, the gray thinning, and behind it is a person, is my sister, surfacing the way a swimmer surfaces from very deep — too slow, lungs screaming, breaking water for just long enough to gasp. "Nari," she says, wondering, like a word in a language she used to know. "You're small. You were small."

"I grew," I tell her. "You missed it. You missed everything. I'm getting you out."

The focus wavers. She's sinking again; I can see it, the undertow taking her, the beat pulling her back down. She fights it. I watch my sister fight the machine for the use of her own mouth, and it is the bravest thing I have ever seen anyone do.

"Don't," she says. "Nari. Don't take me. Take —" Her hand comes up, so slow, and closes on the front of my jacket, and there is nothing in the grip, no strength, just intention. "Take the — they buried it. They buried it *with us.* Down here. Where no one — who would believe the dead."

"Take what, Tess. What did they bury."

Her eyes roll toward the ceiling of the gallery, toward the black stone, toward the drowned coast that is somewhere far above all of us and above that the sky.

"The Char," she says. "The Char is falling."

I think, at first, it is the fog talking. A dying woman's fever. But her hand tightens — actual strength now, borrowed from somewhere, spent all at once — and her voice comes clearer than it has any right to, dragged up from wherever they couldn't reach.

"Listen. Listen to me, I don't get to say it twice." A breath, off the beat now, ragged, hers. "I was on the readings. Surface sensors. Three years I logged the haze. It's *thinning*, Nari. Ten years it's been thinning. The Char's receding. The coast — the outside — it's coming back. A person could stand up there soon. Could breathe. Real air. Green air. Not this." She drags in the thin warm nothing of the gallery and her face twists. "Not *this.*"

The hum goes on under us. In. Hold. Out. Ten thousand chests.

"That can't be why they took you," I whisper. But I already know. I feel the shape of it closing over me like water. "People go missing for less. For nothing. Not for good news —"

"That's *why.*" Her grip shakes. "Think. Think it through, you were always the one who could. If the surface comes back — if there's air up there, free air, for the taking — then what's the dome worth? What's the *scrip* worth? What's a reed worth, or a tier, or a Calibrator, or the whole — the whole arithmetic —" she says the word like it burns her, and I know then that she has met Vane too, that they all meet Vane — "if the thing they're rationing stops being scarce? Scarcity is the whole of it, Nari. Scarcity is the throne. I found the door out of the cage and I tried to show people the door and so they —" Her free hand rises and touches her own reed, the dying coal at her throat. "So they turned me down to this. Not a prison. You can break a prisoner out. They made me *furniture.* They made me the machine. Who breaks the dead out of the choir?"

I do. I am going to. But I can't say it, because my throat has closed, because the enormity of it is standing on my chest.

I think of the ranks we walked through to reach her. Ten thousand chests on the beat. How many of them saw a door and tried to point at it? How many of them just breathed too deep, or asked one question too many, or were only clever in a year the Concord needed them dull? It doesn't take a crime. It never took a crime. It takes being the kind of person who might, someday, notice too much — and the reed reads that in you at sixteen, on a bright civic morning, while the city watches and calls it merit.

They didn't hide her because she rebelled.

They hid her because she was *right*, and being right is the one thing the Concord cannot survive. Every kid tiered down, every breath metered, every name on every commendation on every wall of every family who told themselves their girl went proudly to reclaim the surface — the surface that is reclaiming itself, quietly, without any of us, while they keep us counting in the dark so we'll be grateful for the air they take.

"Papa," I say. Stupid, small. "Papa hung your commendation. On the wall. Surface Corps. He was so —"

Something breaks across Tess's ruined face. Grief, or a memory of it. "He knew," she says. "Some of it. Enough. He hung it so you'd have a proud lie to grow up under instead of a frightened truth." Her eyes swim. "Don't hate him. It's a heavy thing, a lie you hang on a wall. It gets heavier every year."

"I don't hate him." I don't. I find, saying it, that it's true.

She's fading. I can feel her going under, the beat reclaiming her, her breath sliding back onto its rhythm one degree at a time. Her hand loosens on my jacket. Panic takes me.

"Tess. Stay. I'm getting you out, I mean it, tonight, we have a way, but you have to stay —"

"The tokens," she says, very far away now, very deep. "Two of them. I hid them. The readings, and — and what they do to us, the engine, all of it, proof, real proof, I hid it where they'd never — where they already —" Her eyes drift to the last rank, the oldest ranks, the ones so still they've gone to stone. "Ask the ones who can't tell. They kept it for me. They kept it for you."

"I don't understand —"

But Juno makes a sound behind me, sharp, and I turn, and far down the gallery a light has changed. Not blue. Not green. A hard amber sweep, moving along the head of the ranks — a Calibrator's light, or a counter with a live slate, catching on a shape that shouldn't be here, four shapes, three of them warm.

Salt is already moving toward us out of the dark, fast and low. "Time," he says. "It's time. Whatever you've got, we take it, and we go *now.*"

I turn back to my sister. Her eyes are already half gone to gray. But she is looking at me, still, from very far under the water, and she lifts her hand off my jacket and lays two cold fingers against my throat, over my reed, the way she used to when I was small and the night went thin.

"One more," she whispers. "You always have one more."

Then the beat takes her, and her chest rises, and holds, and falls, on the rhythm none of us chose, and the amber light swings toward us across the ranks.
