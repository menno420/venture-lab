## Chapter 13 — The Choice

We come up out of the deep like drowned things, and the first honest thing about the morning is that no one is chasing us in this exact minute.

That's all I let myself count. Not the hours until they find us. Not Salt's face the last time I saw it, tilted up toward the throttling like a man listening to a bell. This minute. This crawlspace above a dead scrubber-line on the far edge of the Undertow, where Juno once ran contraband and swears the Authority's map still shows a wall.

My reed sits at eleven breaths and doesn't climb.

Tess is asleep against the curved pipe, or the thing she does that looks like sleep — eyes half-open, chest going in the metered rhythm they beat into her down there, four counts in, hold, four counts out, the Choir's rhythm, even now, even up here where no one is making her sing. Every time I see it my throat closes. I put two fingers on her wrist and breathe wrong on purpose, ragged, human, and after a while her breathing snags on mine and forgets the count for a few beats. It's the only gift I have. I give it to her over and over.

Juno has the two tokens laid out on a scrap of felt like she's selling them. Char readings on one. On the other, the Choir engine and the R-Class rolls with all the names, the real ones, the not-transferred, the went-down-not-up. And beside them, smaller and somehow heavier, the gold key-token Orrin brought us: the Authority broadcast codes.

"So," Juno says. Her voice is doing the light thing it does when she's scared. "We're rich. We have everything. We have the whole rotten heart of the city on three chips and no lungs to run from it with." She looks at me. "What do we do with everything, Nari?"

Orrin is by the hatch where the cold comes through, arms wrapped around his knees, still in the gray Authority underlayer he wore down into the dark. Crest boy who found out what his city eats. He hasn't slept. When he came up out of the galleries behind us he was shaking so hard the codes almost went into the runoff channel. He held on. I keep having to remind myself he held on.

Papa says the practical thing, because that is the only kind of thing he has let himself say for three years.

"There's a margin line past the eastern locks," he says. Quiet, careful, a scrubber-tech's voice, a man who has spent his life keeping machines from screaming. "Old maintenance warrens. No reeds required if you know how to spoof the count — I do, I've done it, for parts runs. We could take Tess there. Live thin. Live small. They'd stop looking, in time. People do stop looking." He doesn't meet my eyes. "I could keep the four of you breathing. I've kept us breathing this long."

And the terrible thing is I want it.

I want it so much my eyes sting. I am sixteen and I am tired in a way I didn't know a body could be, and there is a version of the next hour where we go east and Tess gets to be a person again somewhere the Choir can't reach, where I never stand in front of Vane again, where I get to be a sister and not a — whatever I'd have to become. Somewhere I stop counting. I let myself stand inside that room for one breath. Nine.

Then Tess's eyes come open all the way.

It happens like that with her, the surfacing — the dim goes out of her and someone's home, just for a moment, and you have to be looking or you miss it. She's looking at me. Her mouth works. The words come out of the count, out of the four-and-four, cracked.

"How many," she says.

"How many what?" I whisper.

"Down there." Her hand lifts an inch off the pipe and falls. "How many are still — singing. Right now. While we — talk about east."

I don't answer. I don't have to. She watched me not answer once before, three years ago, when I was thirteen and asked her why she looked so frightened at her own Calibration, and she smiled and lied and said *nothing, Nari, go to sleep*, and then she vanished into a commendation on the wall. She's not lying now. She doesn't have enough breath to lie.

"I found it," she says, and her voice is going, I can hear the count coming back to take her under again, "I found out the sky is *falling*, the Char is *thinning*, there's a — a green edge on the world, and they put me in the dark so no one would — so no one would *stand up* and —" The dim rolls back over her like water over a stone. Four in. Hold. Her eyes fog. But she gets one more out, and it isn't for me, it's for all of it: "Don't take it east. Don't take it small."

The crawlspace is very quiet. Below us, faint, felt in the soles of my feet more than heard, the hum. The Choir, singing the dawn shift into the scrubbers. Their rationed breath running the machine that rations their breath. It has been under every floor of my whole life and I have never once been able to make it stop.

Tomorrow it stops for the whole city at midmorning — the next cohort's turn, a fresh set of sixteens called up on the cycle — and every reed turns to the wall-screens, and Vane stands up in gold light to tell them exactly how much air they have earned.

The Calibration broadcast.

Every reed. Every screen. Every kid in the Undertow standing where I stood.

I look at the two tokens and the gold key-token and I feel the choice close around me, not like a door — like a breath I've been holding my whole life and can finally, finally let go.

"We don't run," I say.

Juno lets out something between a laugh and a sob. "Oh, I hoped you'd say that. I also hoped you'd say the other thing. I contain multitudes." She's already pulling the codes toward her, already turning them in the light, and I can see her mind go into the sockets and relays of it. "Okay. Okay. If — *if* — we don't run. The broadcast rides the same trunk as the scrubber governor. Same spine. Whoever cut this system this deep on purpose was showing off." A hard grin at Orrin. "Your people are so proud."

"They're not my people," Orrin says. Then, lower: "They're my father."

"Yeah." Juno's grin softens by a degree. "I know."

I turn to Papa.

He is looking at Tess. He is looking at his older daughter breathing in the rhythm they gave her, and something in his face that has been packed down under fear for three years is cracking along its whole length, and it hurts to watch, and I make myself watch.

"You knew," I say. Not cruel. Just true. "You knew she went down, not up. You hung her commendation on the wall and you dusted it."

"To keep you," he says. His voice breaks on it. "To keep *you*." And that was the arithmetic he did, the small terrible arithmetic every parent in the Undertow does, trade one child's truth for the other child's breath, and I understand it now in a way I couldn't have three days ago, and I hate that I understand it. "Nari. If you do this, they will throttle you in front of the whole city. They will not be gentle. They can take you down to nothing on a wristflick and I have watched it and I —"

"I know what it feels like," I say. "I've been shallowing since Calibration Day. I know exactly what it feels like."

That lands on him harder than anything I could have shouted. His mouth closes. He looks at me a long time — at the thin flickering blue of my reed, at whatever I've become in the deep — and I watch him choose. I watch my quiet, broken, careful father decide to be dangerous.

"The governor room over Trunk Nine," he says finally, and his voice has changed, steadied, a tech laying out a repair. "That's where the throttle-master sits. If you want to open the reeds — all of them, the whole Undertow at once — that's the valve. It's mine. I've serviced that room for twenty years. They'll never look twice at me walking in." He almost smiles. It's terrible and it's beautiful. "I can get us to the throttles. I can make the whole Shallows breathe. Once. For a few minutes. Long enough for the city to feel it."

"Long enough to feel it," I repeat.

"After that," he says honestly, "I don't know. No one has ever done this. There's no plan for after, because there's never been a before."

"Then we make the before," I say.

We work it out in the cold with our heads together over the felt, five of us, one of us barely here. Juno and Orrin ride the codes into the spine — the Choir, the air-system, the wall-screens. Papa takes the throttle room, Trunk Nine, ready to open every reed the instant I say. And me — I take the broadcast. I stand up where Vane stands up. I take the gold light and I tell them everything: the R-Class names, the cover, the deep, and the thing under all of it, the thing worth more than my breath and Tess's and Salt's together — that the sky is falling, that the Char is thinning, that there is a green edge on the world and they have been kept in the dark so no one would stand up.

And while I'm talking, we let the whole Undertow breathe.

"For every kid who's going to stand where I stood tomorrow," I say. "That's who it's for. Not for us. We're already marked. For the ones who don't know yet how much they're about to lose."

Nobody argues. Even the hum under the floor seems to hold still.

Tess surfaces one last time before we move — swims up out of the count, finds my face, and her cracked mouth makes a shape I have to lean in to catch.

"Make it loud," she breathes. "I tried quiet. Quiet is how they win."

Then she's under again, counting, four and four, and I fold her hand closed around the Char token so that whatever happens tomorrow, the proof goes up with a Solenne holding it.

"Loud," I promise the whole shallow dark. "Tomorrow. Loud enough for the surface to hear."

My reed reads eleven and holds. It's enough. It's going to have to be enough.
