# Tummel and the Squeak in the Dark

- **Title:** Tummel and the Squeak in the Dark
- **Language:** English (master — voice source of truth)
- **Age band:** read-aloud picture book, ages 3–6
- **Word count:** 536 (manuscript body, excluding spread/illustration notes)
- **Pitch:** When one small unhappy squeak keeps the whole sleeping windmill from resting, a thumb-high sprite named Tummel climbs higher than he ever has to mend it — with nothing but a dewdrop lantern and a single drop of oil.

> Series: *Tummel of the Old Windmill* — cozy-wonder, episodic. This is the flagship stand-alone story (Episode 1). Every spread carries an illustration note in italics; the seat generates no art.

---

[Spread 1]
*Illustration: the great windmill at dusk on the canal's edge, one small window lit gold.*

When the last of the daylight had gone soft and grey over the water, and the miller had climbed the stairs with a yawn, the whole great windmill sighed and settled, like an old dog by a fire.

That was when Tummel woke up.

[Spread 2]
*Illustration: extreme scale — Tummel in his walnut-shell bed, vast dark gears looming overhead like a gentle mountain range.*

Tummel was no bigger than the miller's thumb. He wriggled out of his walnut-shell bed and tugged his little coat close. It was sewn from the corner of a flour-sack, and it still smelled of warm bread.

Then he listened.

[Spread 3]
*Illustration: Tummel with one hand cupped to his ear; the dark mill full of soft sounds drawn as gentle swirls.*

*Tick. Creak. Tock.* The mill was talking in its sleep.

Tummel knew every one of those sounds the way you know the voices in your own house. That soft groan was only the big oak beam, getting comfortable. That patter was the rain, finding the roof.

[Spread 4]
*Illustration: a thin, jagged little sound-line coming down from high in the dark rafters.*

But *that* one — that thin, unhappy little *eeee-eeee*, high up in the dark — that was a squeak that ought not to be there.

"Oh dear," whispered Tummel, who worried about most things, and mended them anyway.

[Spread 5]
*Illustration: Tummel lifting his dewdrop lantern, a single bloom of gold in the dark; a tiny oil-can at his belt.*

He lit his lantern, no bigger than a dewdrop, and its little gold light bloomed in the dark. He took down his oil-can, no bigger than a thimble.

"Coming," Tummel told the squeak, softly, the way you would comfort a child. "I'm coming."

[Spread 6]
*Illustration: Tummel far below, edging along a rope as wide as a road, grain-sacks like hills around him.*

Up he went. Past the sleeping sacks of grain, each one a hill. Along a rope as wide as a road. The squeak cried out again, thinner and higher, and Tummel climbed on.

"I'm coming," he said.

[Spread 7]
*Illustration: Tummel balanced on the rim of an enormous moonlit cog-wheel, one tooth like a stair.*

He came to the great cog-wheel, taller than a church door, and climbed it tooth by tooth, each tooth a stair. At the very top the wind pressed cold against his cheeks, and far, far below, the floor was only a soft brown dark.

Tummel did not look down. He looked up.

[Spread 8]
*Illustration: two round-eyed loft mice peeking from behind a beam.*

From behind a beam, two loft mice blinked at him — greedy Snuffle and timid Fluff.

"Too high," squeaked Fluff. "Come down, Tummel."

"I can't," said Tummel. "Someone up there needs me." And though his knees were shaking, he went on.

[Spread 9]
*Illustration: huge soft dust-grey owl on a rafter, half-lidded eyes, Tummel tiny beneath.*

High in the rafters sat Old Oehoe, the barn-owl, soft and dust-grey and slow.

"Listen with your whole self, small one," said Oehoe, "and the mill will tell you where it hurts."

So Tummel closed his eyes. And he listened with his whole self.

[Spread 10]
*Illustration: Tummel's lantern-light falling on one small dry joint where two beams meet; a hair-thin gap.*

And there it was — one small joint, high in the dark, where two old beams leaned together. Dry as a dead leaf. Every time the mill breathed, they rubbed, and cried that thin unhappy *eeee*.

"There you are," Tummel whispered. "You only wanted a little help."

[Spread 11]
*Illustration: one gold drop of oil hanging from the can's tiny spout, about to fall into the joint.*

He tipped his thimble-can. One drop of oil, round and gold as honey, swelled at the spout — and fell.

The joint drank it up. It stretched. It softened.

And the squeak went quiet.

[Spread 12]
*Illustration: the whole mill in deep restful dark, one moonlit window, Tummel small and glad on the beam.*

The mill let out a long, slow breath and settled deeper into sleep. *Tick. Creak. Tock.* — all the right sounds now, and not one wrong one.

Tummel sat a while in the warm dark, glad. Then he climbed down to his walnut-shell bed, blew out his dewdrop lantern, and pulled his flour-sack coat up to his chin.

"Sleep now," he told the mill.

And, tucked in the great creaking dark, they both did.

*The end.*
