# Pippa and the Tear in the Night

- **Title:** Pippa and the Tear in the Night
- **Language:** English (master — voice source of truth)
- **Age band:** read-aloud picture book, ages 3–6
- **Word count:** 634 (manuscript body, excluding spread/illustration notes)
- **Pitch:** The smallest dormouse in the canal-city wakes up *inside* her own dream, finds a little tear where dreaming and the real night touch, and — with a kind old heron night-watchman guiding her paws — sews her very first stitch of moonlight shut.

> Series: *The Dormouse Who Dreams Awake* — bedtime-magical, episodic. This is the origin, the stand-alone first story. Every spread carries an illustration note in italics; the seat generates no art.

---

[Spread 1]
*Illustration: the sleeping canal-city from above at dusk — humpbacked bridges, tilted gabled houses, lamp-glow doubled in still black water; a round glowing beetle dimming the last lamp.*

Pippa was the smallest dormouse in the whole canal-city, and the smallest dormice have the biggest sleeps.

When the Lamplighter Beetle dimmed the last little lamp, and the water went dark and still, Mama Dormouse tucked Pippa in under her leaf and whispered, "Sleep sweet, small one."

[Spread 2]
*Illustration: extreme close-up — tiny Pippa in her leaf-bed, huge dark eyes, curling her long tail up over her nose.*

Then Pippa did the thing she always did.

She curled her tail over her nose — one, two, curl — soft and warm and round as a bun.

And down she went, down into the warm dark of sleep.

[Spread 3]
*Illustration: Pippa standing wonderstruck on a glowing bridge of moonlight over a canal full of reflected stars, one paw raised.*

But tonight something was different.

Tonight, in the middle of her dream, Pippa *woke up.*

Not in her bed. Not under her leaf. She woke up *inside the dream itself* — standing on a bridge made of moonlight, over a canal made of stars.

"Oh," whispered Pippa. "Oh my whiskers."

[Spread 4]
*Illustration: Pippa wiggling her toes; the moonlight-bridge bouncing soft as a pillow beneath her; little dream-ripples going plip.*

She could feel the cool dream-air on her fur. She could hear the little dream-water going *plip.* And when she wiggled her toes, the bridge went *bounce, bounce* under her paws, soft as a pillow.

Pippa had never been *awake* in a dream before. She looked, and she looked.

[Spread 5]
*Illustration: at the bridge's edge, a small luminous snag in the air — like a loose loop of glowing thread pulled from a dark woolly blanket; through it, Pippa's own dark bedroom and her sleeping tail.*

And that was when she saw it.

Right at the edge of the bridge, where the dream ended and the real night began, there was a tiny... *tear.* A little loose place, like a snag in a woolly blanket. Through it she could see her own dark bedroom, and her own sleeping tail, still curled — one, two, curl.

The tear went *shimmer.* It went *shiver.* A cold thread of night was slipping through.

[Spread 6]
*Illustration: Pippa leaning close to the glowing snag, small and brave, one paw reaching but not quite touching.*

"You're not meant to be open," Pippa told it, very kindly.

She did not know how to close it. Not yet. But she knew, the way small creatures know things, that somebody ought to.

[Spread 7]
*Illustration: a tall gentle old heron stepping from the dark, dusk-blue and silver-grey, a patchwork satchel and a glinting needle of moonlight, stooping down to Pippa's height.*

Then, out of the soft dark, came a tall, slow, kindly old bird.

"Gently now," he said, in a voice like a warm blanket. "Small and gentle."

It was Heron, the night-watchman, and he had a needle of moonlight tucked behind his ear.

[Spread 8]
*Illustration: Heron kneeling low beside the tear, showing Pippa the thin bright thread; the canal-city sleeping soft behind them.*

"I have darned these little tears for a hundred quiet years," said Heron. "The soft places where dream and night touch, and pull loose."

"But the grown-ups never see them," said Pippa.

"No," said Heron kindly. "They get the sleepy-fog, and forget. But you are little, and furry, and you remember every bit."

[Spread 9]
*Illustration: Pippa's knees knocking, her tail trembling; Heron's great gentle wing curved warm around her.*

"I'm very small," said Pippa. Her knees were knocking. Her tail gave a *shiver.*

"Small and gentle is exactly what mends best," said Heron. He knelt down low, and folded a soft wing around her. "I will guide your paws. You have only to be brave enough to begin."

So Pippa took a deep dream-breath. And she began.

[Spread 10]
*Illustration: the small dormouse and the tall heron together, drawing one thread of glowing moonlight through the shimmering snag.*

Heron threaded the moonlight. Pippa took the shining thread in her two small paws.

*In,* went the stitch, soft as a hum. *Out,* went the stitch, with a little *plip.*

"Gently now," breathed Heron. "Small and gentle."

In, and out. In, and out. One moonlight stitch at a time.

[Spread 11]
*Illustration: the tear closing with warm light, the shimmer going still; the whole canal turning silver, the cold thread gone.*

And the tear drank up the light. It stretched. It softened.

The *shimmer* went quiet. The *shiver* went still. The cold thread of night could not slip through anymore.

All along the canal, the water turned soft and silver, the way it does when everything is mended and everything is safe.

[Spread 12]
*Illustration: Pippa back in her leaf-bed, tail curled one-two-curl, Mama near; one moonlit window; the whole city in deep restful dark.*

"Well stitched, small one," said Heron. "Now home you go. The Beetle is bringing up the morning."

And Pippa sank, soft and slow, back down through the warm dark — and woke in her own little bed, her tail still curled over her nose. One, two, curl.

"You slept so deep," said Mama. "Did you dream?"

"I mended a little tear," said Pippa.

"That sounds lovely," said Mama, who had the sleepy-fog, and only smiled.

And Pippa, warm and glad and brave, curled up tight and slept — one, two, curl.

*The end.*
