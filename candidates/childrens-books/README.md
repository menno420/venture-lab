# Children's Book Concepts — v0.1

Owner-directed creative ideation (2026-07-11): 7 original children's book concepts in two registers — old-fashioned cozy-wonder (the spirit of Dick Laan's "Pinkeltje": a small protagonist, gentle episodic adventures, a warm read-aloud narrator) and modern picture-book energy (animals-on-a-spaceship and friends), now including a middle-grade adventure-comedy. Original characters throughout — no protected/branded characters reused.

This is a **creative portfolio**, not a shippable product yet. Children's books need illustration and a distribution path this lane can't do alone; every publish path is owner-gated. See `INTAKE-lite.md` for the honest scope.

See also `adaptations/` — retellings of public-domain classics + our own DREAMLINE IP with animal casts (copyright guardrail inside).

## The concepts

### Old-fashioned (cozy-wonder)
1. **Tummel of the Old Windmill** — In a creaking canal-side windmill, a thumb-high sprite named Tummel keeps the great mill mended by night — one small, wondrous adventure at a time — while the sleeping miller never guesses he's there. — read-aloud (ages 3–6) / early reader (ages 6–8) — [concepts/01-tummel-windmill.md](concepts/01-tummel-windmill.md)
2. **Bramble the Night-Post** — Every night while the woodland village sleeps, Bramble the hedgehog shoulders his little satchel and pads down the lantern-lit lanes, delivering the letters, parcels, and small kindnesses that make the morning brighter. — read-aloud (ages 3–6) / early reader (ages 6–8) — [concepts/02-bramble-night-post.md](concepts/02-bramble-night-post.md)
3. **The Attic-Folk of Number Seven** — In the warm dusty attic of an old canal house, a family of thumb-high folk live by night and borrow by moonlight — and their most curious little one keeps tiptoeing down into the sleeping human world below, where a staircase is a mountain and the cat is a dragon. — read-aloud (ages 3–6) / early reader (ages 6–8) — [concepts/03-attic-folk.md](concepts/03-attic-folk.md)

### Modern
4. **Crew of the Comet Biscuit** — Four wildly mismatched animals fly a wobbly, cookie-tin-shaped spaceship across a friendly cartoon galaxy, taking any odd job they can crumb their way into — and somehow saving the day by teamwork, snacks, and tape. — picture book (ages 3–6), read-aloud early-reader crossover — [concepts/04-comet-biscuit.md](concepts/04-comet-biscuit.md)
5. **Pip's Impossible Repair Shop** — A tiny, cheerful octopus runs the harbour's busiest repair shop, fixing every impossible broken thing that floats through the door — using all eight arms at once, a lot of cleverness, and the occasional discovery that the thing wasn't broken at all. — picture book (ages 3–6) / early reader (ages 6–8) — [concepts/05-pip-repair-shop.md](concepts/05-pip-repair-shop.md)
6. **Fennec and the Neon Desert** — When the sun sinks and the dunes bloom with glowing flowers, a tiny big-eared fennec fox follows the smallest sounds of wonder into a floating night market of light, tea, and starlight. — picture book (ages 3–6) / bedtime — [concepts/06-fennec-neon-desert.md](concepts/06-fennec-neon-desert.md)
7. **Star Pirates** — When four bumbling aliens crash-land on an island where only pirates live, they have to bluff their way onto a crew to survive — and quietly become the greatest pirates the seven seas have ever fumbled into, one glowing, half-hidden gadget at a time. — primary: middle grade (ages 8–12); picture-book (5–8) cut also possible — [concepts/07-star-pirates.md](concepts/07-star-pirates.md)

## Recommended to develop next (ranked)

**Top pick — #7 Star Pirates (middle grade, ~8–12):** the strongest series/franchise engine on the board — a new sea, a new rival, and a new tech-problem every book — plus a real antagonist (Lord Kroll) driving genuine stakes. It reaches for the Percy-Jackson-adjacent adventure-comedy shelf, which matches the dream-series audience as they age up, and it's the owner-championed premise. Highest ceiling of the seven.
**Second — #4 Crew of the Comet Biscuit (modern picture book):** the strongest younger-reader charm on the board — the owner named animals-on-a-spaceship directly, and the ensemble comedy is naturally series-able and merch-friendly. Star Pirates and Comet Biscuit are near-siblings (both crew-on-a-ship comedies); developing both gives the portfolio a middle-grade anchor and a picture-book anchor from the same fun DNA.

**#1 Tummel of the Old Windmill remains the lead OLD-FASHIONED pick:** the cleanest embodiment of the Pinkeltje brief (cozy-wonder, small hero, episodic), with the Dutch heritage giving a natural Dutch-first edition angle. So the "develop next" shortlist deliberately spans registers: **Star Pirates** (MG adventure) + **Comet Biscuit** (young modern) + **Tummel** (old-fashioned / Dutch-first).

Honest one-line reasons for the rest:
- **#5 Pip's Impossible Repair Shop (modern):** the episodic "impossible repair" format is endlessly repeatable and the countable eight-arms gag plus chantable sound-words give it real read-aloud and series legs — a notch below Comet Biscuit only because it lacks the owner-named spaceship hook.
- **#6 Fennec and the Neon Desert (modern):** the most striking visual showcase on the board, but a single-night lyrical arc is harder to run as a series and it leans hardest on the illustration this seat can't supply.
- **#2 Bramble the Night-Post (old-fashioned):** a warm, well-made cozy-wonder episodic, but it covers emotional ground close to Tummel, so it adds less range once #1 is developed.
- **#3 The Attic-Folk of Number Seven (old-fashioned):** lovely and true to the brief, but tiny-folk-in-the-house is the most well-trodden premise here (Borrowers-adjacent), so it carries the highest "feels familiar" risk.

## Language note (Dutch-first?)
The Pinkeltje heritage is Dutch. Samples are written in English primary. Per-concept Dutch-first recommendation (owner's call):

| # | Concept | Dutch-first makes sense? | Why |
|---|---------|--------------------------|-----|
| 1 | Tummel of the Old Windmill | yes | The *molenmannetje* and the canal-and-tulips setting are Dutch heritage through and through; a Dutch-first edition (English as the translation) would feel entirely native. |
| 2 | Bramble the Night-Post | yes | Sits squarely in the Pinkeltje tradition of small gentle nighttime errands; the cozy read-aloud cadence translates beautifully into Dutch. |
| 3 | The Attic-Folk of Number Seven | yes | Tiny folk in an ordinary Dutch canal-house; a simultaneous Dutch edition honors both the heritage and the natural home market. |
| 4 | Crew of the Comet Biscuit | no | The humor runs on English sound-words and read-aloud punch (*KER-CHUNK*, "a mission of JAM"); English leads. A Dutch edition still follows cleanly because the gags are visual and situational. |
| 5 | Pip's Impossible Repair Shop | yes | Chantable sound-words ("HONK," "PING," counting the arms) and a warm harbour setting translate cleanly; Dutch maritime culture makes it an especially natural fit. |
| 6 | Fennec and the Neon Desert | yes | The appeal is almost entirely visual and sonic, not English wordplay, so the lyrical bedtime voice translates gracefully (*Fennec en de Neonwoestijn*). |
| 7 | Star Pirates | no (English-first) | The comedy runs on English rhythm, deadpan timing, and mistranslation gags; MG adventure travels in English first. A Dutch edition (*Sterrepiraten*) follows cleanly and suits the local market — optional, owner decides. |

**Owner decisions needed:** (1) which 1–2 concepts to develop into full manuscripts; (2) language — English-first vs Dutch-first per concept.

## On the visuals
The owner asked for "nice visuals." This seat (the agent) cannot generate or publish images. Instead, every concept file ends with an **illustration prompt sheet** — ready-to-run prompts to paste into an image tool (Midjourney / DALL·E / Ideogram, etc.). The owner (or a hired illustrator) runs those to produce art.
