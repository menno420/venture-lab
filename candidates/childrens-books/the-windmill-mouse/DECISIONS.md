## Premise fidelity (packet → manuscript)

Packet pitch delivered beat for beat: a mouse in the smallest room of a working windmill who believes she keeps the whole mill turning — oiling one tiny gear (Spread 3: "the littlest gear, the one hiding deep between the big ones"), sweeping one wooden tooth (same spread) — until the night of the big storm (Spreads 7–10), when the miller finds out she's been right all along (Spread 11: "So it was you. All along, it was you."). Register held: little-helper competence, quiet pride in small work; the §6 blurb's imagery (oil can, matchstick broom, "a job nobody knows she does", the squeaking gear in the storm, "the smallest helper of all") appears verbatim or near-verbatim in the text.

**⚑ Collision differentiator honored (packet §2, Moderate verdict):** zero "old Amsterdam" phrasing anywhere in any language — no Amsterdam, no landlord-and-mouse comedy, no song-adjacent rhyme scheme; prose read-aloud with a work-refrain instead. The "mouse in clogs" cliché also stays out (no clogs in text or art notes). The register is quiet-pride-in-small-work, disjoint from the 1965 novelty song as the packet requires.

**Kids-cluster keyword rule honored:** no "bedtime" phrasing (Lantern Door/Pippa own the night shelf). The storm happens at night because storms do, but the register is a rescue mission, not a going-to-sleep arc, and the closing spread is golden-hour daytime.

## Age band
Ages 3–6 per the packet. Short declarative sentences, heavy onomatopoeia (drip/whoosh/clack/eeek), a chantable two-line refrain repeated at Spreads 3, 9, and 13 so the youngest listeners can join in, zero threat beyond a squeaky gear — the storm is loud but nobody is ever in danger of harm.

## Character-name decisions
- Heroine — EN **Millie**, NL **Mies**, DE **Minna**. The packet names her Mies and flags the EN drift risk ("Mies" → Mice/Miss autocorrect/read-aloud drift, packet §1) with "keep or anglicize at manuscript time". Decision: anglicize for EN only — **Millie** keeps the m-sound and adds a mill pun an English parent will catch; **Mies** stays in the NL edition where it is a genuinely common, warm Dutch name (honoring the packet's Dutch grounding); **Minna** is the German parallel — old-fashioned-warm, current again, and alliterative with Mühle/Maus.
- The miller stays unnamed ("the miller" / "de molenaar" / "der Müller") in all three languages — he is a role seen from mouse height, and naming him would spend words the refrain needs.
- Setting: "the old windmill on the dike" / "de oude molen op de dijk" / "die alte Windmühle am Deich" — no town name at all, deliberately: the packet's Netherlands flavor lives in dike-and-mill scenery (and reads equally naturally as North-German Deich country in DE), with zero place-name that could tip toward the song's geography.

## Language approach
EN is the master and the voice source of truth; NL and DE are native re-tellings, re-authored for rhythm and idiom rather than translated literally.
- **Refrain:** EN "A drop for the gear, a sweep for the teeth — and round goes the mill: whoosh, clack, whoosh!" · NL "Een drupje voor het wieltje, een veegje voor de tanden — en rond gaat de molen: zoef, klak, zoef!" (double diminutives drupje/veegje/wieltje carry the Dutch coziness) · DE "Ein Tropfen fürs Rädchen, ein Fegen für die Zähne — und rund läuft die Mühle: wusch, klack, wusch!" ("läuft" because a German mill runs, it doesn't go).
- **Onomatopoeia localized, not copied:** mill-song EN whoosh-clack / NL zoef-klak / DE wusch-klack; squeak EN eeek / NL piep / DE quiek; oil EN drip / NL drup / DE tropf; sweep EN swish / NL zwiep / DE wisch. Each is the sound a native child already makes for that thing.
- **NL:** read-aloud Dutch with the language's diminutive warmth (oliekannetje, bezempje, kamertje, raampje, kruimeltje); emphatic accents for read-aloud stress ("zíj het doet", "Bíjna al het werk", "nóg sneller", "bíjna"); idiom over calque ("Loon moet er zijn", "fier op de dijk", "aanbulderen").
- **DE:** native picture-book cadence — correct verb-final rhythm in the storm sentences, "gebraust kommen", "kerzengerade", "Lohn muss sein"; the sign OBERSTE HELFERIN / NL OPPERHULPJE / EN CHIEF HELPER localized as a title a child would be proud of, not a literal translation.

## Concept gaps filled
- The packet gives premise and register but no plot mechanics: chose the three-part sound arc (the mill's "round-and-round song" as baseline → the wrong sound → the song restored mid-storm) so the crisis is audible to a listening child before it is explained.
- Gave Millie a spoken refrain that doubles as her work method, established in peacetime (Spread 3) and reused under pressure (Spread 9) — the same predict-and-chant device the catalog's Painted Stones refrain uses.
- Chose the recognition beats (round window, CHIEF HELPER sign, cheese as "fair wages") so the miller's validation is concrete and re-readable, not just verbal.
- Final spread hands the secret to the reader ("But now you know better") — the competence-pride register's payoff for ages 3–6.
- Every spread carries exactly one italic illustration note per the convention; the seat generates no art.
