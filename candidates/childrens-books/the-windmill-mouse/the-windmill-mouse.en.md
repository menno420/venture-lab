# The Windmill Mouse

- **Title:** The Windmill Mouse
- **Language:** English (master — voice source of truth)
- **Age band:** read-aloud picture book, ages 3–6
- **Word count:** 581 (manuscript body, excluding spread/illustration notes)
- **Pitch:** High in the smallest room of a working windmill lives Millie, a mouse with an oil can and a matchstick broom — and on the night of the big storm, the miller finds out just how much turning depends on the smallest helper of all.

> Series: *Little Helper Stories* — quiet-pride-in-small-work, stand-alone. Every spread carries an illustration note in italics; the seat generates no art.

---

[Spread 1]
*Illustration: A working windmill on a green dike under a wide sky, sails turning; tiny villagers below with flour sacks; warm daylight, grain dust drifting in sunbeams.*
Everyone in the village knows what turns the old windmill on the dike: the wind, of course. The wind pushes the sails, the sails turn the wheels, and the wheels grind the grain into soft white flour. That's what everyone knows. But everyone doesn't know everything.

[Spread 2]
*Illustration: Cutaway of the mill's top floor — behind the biggest gear wheel, a snug matchbox-sized room; Millie the mouse with a tiny oil can and a broom the size of a matchstick.*
High inside the mill, in the smallest room of all, behind the biggest wheel of all, lives Millie — a mouse with an oil can, a broom the size of a matchstick, and a job nobody knows she does.

[Spread 3]
*Illustration: Close-up of the littlest wooden gear tucked between two giant ones; Millie tipping one shining drop of oil onto it, then sweeping flour dust from a wooden tooth.*
Every morning, Millie oils the littlest gear, the one hiding deep between the big ones. Drip, drip. Every evening, she sweeps the flour dust from its wooden teeth. Swish, swish. "A drop for the gear, a sweep for the teeth," she hums, "and round goes the mill — whoosh, clack, whoosh!"

[Spread 4]
*Illustration: Split scene — the miller downstairs whistling as he pats the wall, flour on his apron; above him, unseen, Millie giggling behind a beam.*
Down below, the miller works and whistles. "Good old wind," he says, patting the wall. "You do all the work." Millie giggles into her whiskers. "Almost all," she squeaks. But the miller has never heard her. Millers don't listen for mice.

[Spread 5]
*Illustration: Millie with one ear against a beam, eyes closed and happy; around her, soft sound-words curling through the machinery like ribbons.*
Millie knows every sound the mill can make. The sleepy creak of the ladder. The rumble of the stones. And best of all, the round-and-round song of the wheels: whoosh, clack, whoosh, clack. As long as the mill sings that song, all is well.

[Spread 6]
*Illustration: The sky turning gray through a little round window; sails spinning fast; Millie's ears standing straight up, whiskers alert.*
One gray afternoon, the wind grew wild. The sails spun faster, and faster still. And in between the whoosh and the clack, Millie heard a tiny new sound. Eeek. Eeek. The littlest gear was squeaking.

[Spread 7]
*Illustration: Night storm — rain lashing the mill on the dike, sails whirling dark against lightning-lit clouds; inside, beams trembling, Millie small but determined.*
That night the storm came roaring over the dike. Rain drummed on the roof. The whole mill trembled, and the wheels turned faster than they had ever turned before. Eeek! EEEK! The squeak grew louder. A dry gear is a tired gear — and a tired gear can stop everything.

[Spread 8]
*Illustration: Millie climbing the shaking ladder with the oil can on her back, flour dust whirling around her whiskers; big machinery looming above.*
Millie grabbed her oil can and climbed. Up the shaking ladder. Along the trembling beam. Flour dust whirled around her whiskers, and the wind howled at the walls. "Hold on, little gear," she called. "Your helper is coming!"

[Spread 9]
*Illustration: Close-up drama — Millie gripping a spoke with one paw, tipping the oil can with the other; three shining drops falling toward the hot little gear.*
There it was — the littlest gear, hot and dry, crying eeek, eeek, eeek. Millie held tight with one paw and tipped her can with the other. One drop. Two drops. Three. "A drop for the gear, a sweep for the teeth..."

[Spread 10]
*Illustration: The gear turning smooth and shiny again; the storm still wild outside the window, but inside everything golden and steady.*
The squeak grew small. The squeak grew smaller. The squeak was gone. And the mill sang its round-and-round song right through the storm: whoosh, clack, whoosh, clack.

[Spread 11]
*Illustration: Lantern light spilling up through the hatch — the miller's astonished face level with the floor; before him, the shining gear, the tiny broom, the tiny oil can, and Millie taking a small proud bow.*
Then — lantern light! The miller had climbed up to find the strange noise. He saw the shining gear. He saw the swept wooden teeth. He saw a matchstick broom, a tiny oil can, and one small mouse taking a bow. "Well," said the miller softly. "So it was you. All along, it was you."

[Spread 12]
*Illustration: Morning after the storm, washed blue sky; the miller fitting a tiny round window into Millie's wall; over her doorway a small sign: CHIEF HELPER; a crumb of cheese at the foot of the ladder.*
In the morning the storm was gone, and the mill stood proud on the dike, still turning. The miller made Millie a little round window, so she could watch the sails go by, and over her door he hung a sign: CHIEF HELPER. And every evening he leaves a crumb of cheese at the bottom of the ladder. Fair wages, he says.

[Spread 13]
*Illustration: Wide final view — the windmill turning on the dike at golden hour; in the tiny round window, a tiny light and a tiny silhouette with a broom; villagers walking home with flour sacks below.*
So if you ever pass the old windmill on the dike, ask anyone what keeps it turning. "The wind, of course!" they'll say. And they're almost right. But now you know better: a drop for the gear, a sweep for the teeth — and the smallest helper of all, keeping the big mill turning. Whoosh, clack, whoosh.

*The end.*
