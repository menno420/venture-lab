# Concept — The Attic-Folk of Number Seven

- **Track:** Old-Fashioned (cozy-wonder)
- **Logline:** In the warm dusty attic of an old canal house, a family of thumb-high folk live by night and borrow by moonlight — and their most curious little one keeps tiptoeing down into the sleeping human world below, where a staircase is a mountain and the cat is a dragon.
- **Age band:** read-aloud for ages 3–6; early reader for ages 6–8 (short episodic chapters, one bedtime each)
- **Tone + narrator voice:** A warm, unhurried grandmother-by-the-fire voice that leans in close and lets you in on a secret. Gentle, twinkling, never frightening — the sort of story where nothing worse than a spilled thimble of milk can happen. The narrator loves small things and names them lovingly: a button, a crumb, a moonbeam on the floorboards.
- **Story shape:** episodic chapters — each chapter is one small nighttime expedition down into the great house, so any single one stands alone as a bedtime story while the seasons and small friendships build gently across the book.

## Sample opening

When the last lamp in the street went dark and the canal lay black and shining as a spill of ink, the attic of Number Seven began, very quietly, to wake.

First the dust settled gold in the last of the moon. Then, under the third rafter, behind the trunk that smelled of old winters, a little door no bigger than a matchbox creaked open — and out came the Bramble family, one after another, small as your thumb and twice as tidy.

Papa Bramble stretched. Mama Bramble counted heads, the way she always did. And Nib — who was the youngest, and the most curious, and never once in his short life had counted anything — was already looking down.

Down past the toe of the rafters. Down where the attic floor gave way to the top of the great dark staircase, which went down, and down, and *down*, into the sleeping house where the giants lived.

"Nib," said Mama, without even turning round. "Not tonight."

But oh, thought Nib, wriggling his toes in his acorn-cap slippers. The staircase was a mountain, and he had never climbed a mountain. The kitchen was a canyon, and somewhere in it, everyone said, there was a whole *lake* of milk in a blue bowl. And beyond it slept the dragon — grey, and soft, and terrible — who purred.

Nib waited until Mama was busy oiling the little door. He waited until Papa was winding the cricket-clock. And then, quiet as a falling feather, he slipped over the edge of the world and started down the mountain, into the dark and wondrous house.

## Recurring cast

- **Nib Bramble** — the youngest attic-child, thumb-high, endlessly curious, brave in the way only the very small can be. Our lead; every expedition is his idea, and every idea gets him into gentle trouble.
- **Mama Bramble** — practical, warm, forever counting heads; keeps the attic snug and the borrowing honest ("only what's dropped, only what's spare").
- **Papa Bramble** — a maker and mender; can turn a spool, a thimble, and a length of thread into anything. Winds the cricket-clock each night.
- **Old Aunt Tuppence** — the eldest attic-folk, who remembers when the human family were different faces; tells Nib the old rules and the old maps of the house.
- **Whisk** — the house cat, grey and enormous, the "dragon." Not wicked, only sleepy and vast; the great obstacle of every journey, and — slowly, over the book — a secret gentle ally.
- **Grandmother Applebee** — the human grandmother who lives at Number Seven with her small granddaughter. She half-believes; she leaves out a saucer of crumbs "for the mice," and never quite catches the folk, though once or twice she almost, *almost* does.
- **Small Bee** — Grandmother's granddaughter, a human child Nib's own age (though a hundred times his size), who dreams the attic-folk and can never tell if they're real.
- **Tock** — a friendly house-mouse who knows every crack and drainpipe; Nib's guide and getaway when the dragon stirs.

## Example episodes / scenes

1. **Down the Mountain** — Nib makes his first solo journey down the great staircase to prove he can, discovering that each stair is a cliff and each moonbeam a rope-bridge of light. He reaches the bottom just as the dragon opens one enormous golden eye — and learns that a cat, half-asleep, is more curious than cruel.
2. **The Lake of Milk** — The Brambles need a thimbleful of milk for Aunt Tuppence's tea, so Nib and Tock mount an expedition to the blue bowl in the kitchen canyon. The trouble isn't the milk; it's getting home across the vast cold floor before Grandmother Applebee shuffles down in her slippers for a midnight biscuit.
3. **The Button That Fell From the Sky** — A single pearl button drops through the attic floorboards from Grandmother's sewing basket below, and the whole attic wants it. Nib's quest to fetch it back turns into the tale of how the folk and the mouse learned to share, and how one lost button became the attic's new front-door knob.
4. **The Night the Dragon Cried** — Whisk the cat gets a thorn in her great grey paw and mews so mournfully the whole house can't sleep. Nib is the only one small and brave enough to climb up and pull it free — and from that night on, the dragon lets the little folk pass.
5. **Small Bee's Dream** — The human child leaves a tiny paper house and a note ("for whoever is really up there") on the attic stair. The folk must decide whether to answer. They do — with a single thank-you stitched in thread too small to read by day — and Small Bee wakes believing, just a little, forever.

## Visuals

- **Illustration style direction:** warm watercolor with soft pencil linework, glowing candle- and moonlight, deep old-house textures — grain of floorboards, dust motes in shafts of light, the fuzz of a woolen sock, the shine of a brass keyhole. Cozy and hand-made, like a beloved old book found in a real attic.
- **Palette:** attic-dust gold, moon-silver, teacup-blue, cocoa-brown rafters, candle-amber, mouse-grey, faded-rose thread, and the deep ink-black of the canal at night.
- **Character design notes (in words):** The attic-folk stand about a thumb high, rounded and soft, dressed in scraps of the human world made new — acorn-cap slippers, a bottle-cap hat, a coat cut from a mitten, buttons worn as belt-buckles. Nib has a tuft of hair like dandelion fluff, big round eyes, and cheeks perpetually smudged with adventure-dust. Scale is always readable: a folk figure beside a spool of thread that towers like a barrel, a sewing needle carried like a lance, a teacup they could bathe in, a staircase that fills the whole page while the tiny climber is a speck of warmth near the bottom.
- **Key illustration moments:**
  1. Nib alone at the top of the moonlit staircase, one acorn slipper over the edge, the great dark house yawning down below him — the whole page is the descent, and he is very small and very brave.
  2. The kitchen canyon at midnight: the blue milk-bowl looming like a lake, Nib and Tock the mouse rowing a walnut-shell across its rim, the sleeping grey dragon a soft mountain range in the background.
  3. Whisk the cat's enormous golden eye filling half the page, and reflected tiny in its shine: Nib, standing his ground with a thimble held up like a shield — the moment fear turns into friendship.

## Language note

English primary.
