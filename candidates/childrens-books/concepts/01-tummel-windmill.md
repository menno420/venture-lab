# Concept — Tummel of the Old Windmill

- **Track:** Old-Fashioned (cozy-wonder)
- **Logline:** In a creaking canal-side windmill, a thumb-high sprite named Tummel keeps the great mill mended by night — one small, wondrous adventure at a time — while the sleeping miller never guesses he's there.
- **Age band:** read-aloud (ages 3–6) / early reader (ages 6–8)
- **Tone + narrator voice:** A hushed, kindly bedtime storyteller who leans close and finds the marvelous folded inside the ordinary — a dust-mote is a snowfall, a bread-crumb a banquet. Gentle, unhurried, a little playful, never frightening; the sort of voice that tucks a listener in.
- **Story shape:** episodic chapters — each night brings one small, self-contained trouble to mend, so a listener can drift off after any single chapter and return the next evening to a fresh, cozy adventure.

## Sample opening

When the last of the daylight had gone soft and grey over the water, and the miller had climbed the stairs yawning, and the whole great windmill sighed and settled like an old dog by a fire — that was when Tummel woke up.

He wriggled out of his walnut-shell bed, tugged his little coat close (it was sewn from the corner of a flour-sack, and still smelled faintly of warm bread), and listened.

*Tick. Creak. Tock.* The mill was talking in its sleep.

Tummel was no bigger than the miller's thumb, but he knew every one of those creaks the way you know the voices in your own house. That soft groan was only the big oak beam getting comfortable. That patter was rain finding the roof. But *that* one — that thin, unhappy little *eeee-eeee* from high up in the dark — that was a squeak that ought not to be there.

"Oh dear," whispered Tummel, who worried about most things and mended them anyway.

He lit his lantern, no bigger than a dewdrop, and its tiny gold light bloomed in the dark. Up he went — past the sleeping sacks of grain, over a rope as wide as a road, along the rim of a cog-wheel taller than a church door. Far below, the miller's little girl had left one shoe by the stair, and it looked to Tummel like a boat run aground.

Somewhere above, the squeak cried again.

"Coming," Tummel told it, softly, the way you'd comfort a child. "I'm coming."

## Recurring cast

- **Tummel** — a thumb-high windmill-sprite (a *molenmannetje*) in a flour-sack coat; kind, curious, a worrier who turns out braver than he thinks.
- **The Miller and his family** — glimpsed always from below: warm boots, a spread apron, a lullaby drifting down the stair; giants who love the mill and never quite see the one who keeps it running.
- **Grietje** — the miller's small daughter, who half-believes in Tummel and leaves out crumbs "for the mill," never guessing they're eaten.
- **Snuffel & Pluis** — two grain-loft mice, one greedy and one timid; Tummel shoos them, scolds them, and secretly shares his supper.
- **Old Oehoe** — a wise, slow-blinking barn-owl in the rafters who speaks in riddles, watches the whole valley, and gives Tummel a nudge of courage when he needs it.
- **Kwik** — a green canal frog who ferries news (and the occasional passenger) along the waterline and knows every ripple by name.

## Example episodes / scenes

1. **The Squeak in the Dark** — A single new squeak keeps the mill from resting, and Tummel must climb higher than he ever has to find one dry little joint that only wants a drop of oil. A story about listening, and about small kindnesses being the ones that matter.
2. **The Night the Sail Stuck** — A gale is coming and one great sail has jammed fast; too big a job for one small fellow, until Tummel enlists two quarrelling mice and one sleepy owl to pull together. Teamwork, and the courage to ask for help.
3. **A Crumb for the Mill** — Tummel discovers Grietje leaving crumbs "for the mill" and, one snowy night, leaves her something back — a single tulip petal on her windowsill — sparking a wordless friendship neither can quite explain.

## Visuals

- **Illustration style direction:** Warm watercolor washes over soft graphite linework; an old-storybook, lamplit feel, with generous cozy dark and pools of candle-gold — the register of mid-century Dutch and Golden-Age picture books.
- **Palette:** flour-white, canal-green, lantern-gold, walnut-brown, dusk-blue, tulip-red, and old-oak amber.
- **Character design notes (in words):** Tummel is thumb-high, round-cheeked and a little rumpled, with a mossy tuft of hair, bright dark eyes, and a patched flour-sack coat belted with twine; his lantern is a hollowed dewdrop on a hair-thin wire. Scale is everything — show him dwarfed by a single wheat grain the size of a loaf, a cog-tooth like a stair, a spilled thimble that could be his bathtub. Oehoe the owl is huge, soft, and dust-grey; the mice are the only creatures near Tummel's own size; the humans appear only as boots, hands, and hems from a mouse's-eye-view.
- **Key illustration moments:**
  1. Tummel in his walnut-shell bed, tiny lantern unlit beside him, the vast dark gears of the sleeping mill looming above like a gentle mountain range.
  2. Tummel balanced on the rim of an enormous moonlit cog-wheel, his dewdrop lantern a single spark of gold in the towering dark.
  3. A snowy windowsill at dawn: one red tulip petal laid beside a scatter of crumbs, small footprints leading away, Grietje's wondering face just visible through frosted glass.
