# Concept — Pip's Impossible Repair Shop

- **Track:** Modern
- **Logline:** A tiny, cheerful octopus runs the harbour's busiest repair shop, fixing every impossible broken thing that floats through the door — using all eight arms at once, a lot of cleverness, and the occasional discovery that the thing wasn't broken at all.
- **Age band:** Picture book (3–6), with easy crossover to early reader (6–8) thanks to the short repeatable episodes.
- **Tone + narrator voice:** Warm, quick, and funny, with a wink to the grown-up reading aloud. The narrator is a friendly ringmaster of chaos — short punchy sentences for the gags, a soft landing for the heart. Sound-words and counting ("arm one… arm two… arm SEVEN!") invite kids to chant along.
- **Story shape:** Episodic — each book (or spread-set) is a new customer with a new impossible problem, which gives you an endlessly reusable, satisfying "shop opens → problem arrives → eight-arm scramble → clever twist → happy customer → shop closes" format that early readers love and that scales into a series.

## Sample opening

The sun came up over the harbour, and down at the very, very bottom — past the wobbly jellyfish and the grumpy old anchor — a little light flickered on.

*PING!*

Pip the octopus flipped the sign from **CLOSED** to **OPEN FOR IMPOSSIBLE THINGS**, straightened her tiny brass goggles, and put the kettle on with arm number four.

She had just one sip of seaweed tea before —

*HONK.*

The door burst open. In scuttled Bartholomew Crab, red in the face, holding one enormous claw as far from his ears as he could.

*HONK. HONK-HONK.*

"Pip!" he wailed. "My claw won't stop *honking!* I honked at breakfast. I honked in the bath. I honked at my own *grandmother!*"

*HONK.*

"Grandmothers do not like being honked at," Bartholomew added, sadly.

Pip put down her tea.

She cracked all eight arms like a piano player.

Arm one grabbed a magnifying glass. Arm two grabbed a tiny hammer. Arm three grabbed a bigger tiny hammer. Arm four found a screwdriver, arm five found *seven* screwdrivers, and arm six simply patted Bartholomew on the shell, because sometimes a customer needs that first.

"Don't worry," said Pip, peering right up inside the honking claw. "I fix impossible things. It's the *possible* ones I find boring."

*HONK,* agreed the claw.

"Now hold *very* still," said Pip. "This is going to get loud."

## Recurring cast

- **Pip** — a small, round, sunshine-yellow octopus with brass goggles pushed up on her head; runs the shop alone but never feels alone with eight arms to keep her company. Endlessly curious, quietly kind, secretly proud of her tools.
- **Sluggo** — the shop's snail apprentice. Astonishingly slow (he is still "on his way over" from last Tuesday) but astonishingly wise; arrives at exactly the right moment with exactly the right idea, usually a single perfect sentence. Leaves a trail of glitter, not slime — he insists it's "workshop sparkle."
- **Bartholomew Crab** — anxious, big-hearted neighbour whose things break constantly (claws, doorbells, his own hat). A recurring customer and Pip's best worrier.
- **Marina the Seahorse** — runs the harbour merry-go-round; brings Pip her most tangled, twirliest problems.
- **The Barnacle Twins** — two tiny customers who never break anything but come in every single day "just to watch," narrating Pip's repairs like excitable sports commentators.
- **Old Anchor** — the shop's grumpy front-step landmark who pretends not to care and secretly loves everyone.

## Example episodes / scenes

1. **The Crab Who Wouldn't Stop Honking** — Bartholomew's claw honks at everything and everyone. Pip takes it apart, arm by arm, only to discover a tiny startled shrimp living inside who honks whenever he's frightened. The claw isn't broken — it just needed a braver, quieter home for its tiny tenant.
2. **The Jellyfish With the Broken Glow** — Gloria Jellyfish's beautiful light keeps flickering out, and she's terrified of the dark harbour. Pip tests bulbs, wires, and glow-jelly with all eight arms — until Sluggo points out that Gloria's light only flickers when she's *nervous.* Not a repair at all: a friend to float beside her.
3. **The Whale With the Hiccups** — A whale so big he barely fits in the harbour arrives mid-*HIC*, launching Pip's whole shop into the air with every hiccup. Pip builds a ridiculous eight-armed contraption of funnels and springs, fails gloriously, then fixes it with the oldest trick in the sea: a very, very big surprise.

## Visuals

- **Illustration style direction:** Bold flat color with thick, confident outlines — modern cartoon energy, think saturated storybook-meets-animation. Every spread crammed with lovingly drawn gadgets: gears, funnels, coiled springs, jars of spare glows, labelled drawers. Motion lines, sound-word lettering baked into the art, and lots of tiny background jokes for repeat readings.
- **Palette:** Coral-pink and warm sand for the harbour floor, deep-sea teal and midnight-blue for the water, sunshine-yellow Pip, brass-gold and copper for every gadget and tool, pops of glow-green and jelly-purple for magical bits.
- **Character design notes (in words):** Pip is small and rounded — head like a friendly lightbulb — sunny yellow with a dusting of freckle-spots, brass goggles on her forehead, and eight expressive arms that curl, point, wave, and *work.* The eight-arms gag is the visual engine of the whole book: in any big scene each arm is doing a completely different job (one hammering, one holding tea, one high-fiving a customer, one scratching her head), and readers can count and track them. The shop interior is a glorious clutter of a barnacle-encrusted shipwreck cabin: pegboards of tools, glowing jars, a wall of tiny labelled drawers ("SPARE HONKS," "EXTRA SPARKLE," "LOST BUTTONS"), and a hand-cranked kettle.
- **Key illustration moments:**
  1. Pip mid-repair on the honking claw: all eight arms fanned out in glorious chaos — magnifying glass, two tiny hammers, a fistful of screwdrivers, a reassuring pat, a sip of tea — while a startled shrimp peeks out and *HONK* letters bounce across the spread.
  2. The whole shop launched into the air by a whale hiccup: Pip and her gadgets and jars suspended mid-tumble, arms grabbing everything at once, the Barnacle Twins cheering from a floating drawer.
  3. A quiet, glowing spread: Pip and Gloria Jellyfish floating in the dark harbour, Gloria's light steady and soft now, Sluggo trailing his "workshop sparkle" — the emotional pause after all the noise.

## Language note

English primary. Dutch-first version: yes — the episodic structure, chantable sound-words ("HONK," "PING," counting the arms), and universally warm harbour setting translate cleanly, and a Dutch maritime-and-harbour culture makes this an especially natural fit for a Dutch-first edition with no jokes lost in translation.
