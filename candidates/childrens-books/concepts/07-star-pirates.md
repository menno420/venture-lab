# Concept — Star Pirates

- **Track:** Modern (original) — new concept #7
- **Logline:** When four bumbling aliens crash-land on an island where only pirates live, they have to bluff their way onto a crew to survive — and quietly become the greatest pirates the seven seas have ever fumbled into, one glowing, half-hidden gadget at a time.
- **Age band:** **primary: middle grade (~8–12)** — a Percy-Jackson-adjacent adventure-comedy shelf with a proper laugh-out-loud voice and a dream-series engine. A **picture-book (5–8) cut is also possible** and is labeled clearly in the tone section below; the two are different builds of the same premise, not the same book squeezed.
- **Tone + narrator voice:** Fast, funny, and swashbuckling — a fish-out-of-water buddy comedy at sea, told by a narrator with one boot on a treasure chest and a straight face for the aliens' most catastrophic manners. Big set-piece adventure, cannon-smoke and salt-spray, but the engine is *character comedy*: over-polite aliens colliding with a pirate code they take far too literally, and grizzled pirates short-circuiting at anything that glows. Peril is real enough to grip a nine-year-old and always solved by cleverness, never cruelty. Big heart under the laughs — this is secretly a story about a crew of lost misfits finding a home, which is why the *stay-or-leave* question lands so hard.
  - **Picture-book (5–8) cut:** Same crash, same island, one clean arc — the aliens land, the pirates squint, the aliens sneak-fix the ship, they win one big race, and everyone hugs. Broad visual gags (the translator-blob mistranslating a menu, the big alien accidentally lifting the whole ship), fewer named characters, no rival-lord subplot, sound-words baked into the art. All the sweetness, a tenth of the machinery.
- **Story shape:** Single contained arc for Book 1 (crash → bluff aboard → earn the crew's trust → face the rival lord → choose to stay) sitting on top of an **episodic series engine** — new sea, new rival, new tech-problem each book. Constant crew, constant ship, an ocean-sized joke-and-adventure machine.

## Sample opening

The ship came down over Gallows Cove doing roughly nine hundred impossible things at once, most of them on fire.

"Captain," said Bolt, the engineer, watching the ocean rush up at them with the calm of a creature who had filed the appropriate paperwork. "We are about to become part of the local sea floor."

"We are NOT!" Captain Fizzwick gripped the console with all six of his trembling fingers and read, very fast, from a laminated card. "'In the event of catastrophic descent, remain courteous, remain calm, and thank your crew for their service.' Thank you all for your service. I have loved being your captain. Please brace for—"

The *Wanderling* hit the water, skipped twice like a flat stone, sheared off both wings, and slid to a smoking stop on the beach of an island that was, in every direction, absolutely stuffed with pirates.

There was a very long silence.

Then Squish, the translator-blob, oozed hopefully up onto the dashboard, blinked its single enormous eye at the crowd of cutlasses gathering outside, and translated the pirates' astonished muttering as: *"Welcome, honored guests. Please, help yourselves to our finest jam."*

"Oh, lovely," said Fizzwick, straightening his sash. "Jam."

"That is not what they said," said Bolt.

"They have jam, Bolt. Standards are being met." Fizzwick popped the hatch, tripped over the hatch, apologized to the hatch, and stepped out onto the sand with both hands raised and a smile stretched so wide it wrapped most of the way around his head. Forty pirates took one step back at once.

Nobody had told the aliens that the correct number of teeth to show a pirate is *zero.*

## Recurring cast

- **Alien crew:**
  - **Captain Fizzwick** — jittery, catastrophically over-polite, runs the crew off a laminated card of proper conduct that is useless in every single situation; means well with his whole enormous heart, panics beautifully, secretly braver than he thinks.
  - **Bolt** — the deadpan engineer, four arms and zero facial expressions, bonds instantly and wordlessly with the pirate ship's carpenter over a shared love of a well-joined plank; can turn a broken oar into a wave-skipping drive and will not explain how.
  - **Squish** — a palm-sized translator-blob with one giant eye and boundless confidence, mistranslates everything into something either far too polite or completely unhinged, is somehow *also* the reason the crew keeps making friends by accident.
  - **Gorm** — a mountainous, gentle alien who can lift a mainmast with one hand and cries at sunsets; terrified of crabs; the emotional anchor of the whole crew.
- **Pirate crew:**
  - **Captain Roana "Ironjaw" Vex** — the gruff-but-good pirate captain who takes the aliens in mostly to spite everyone who said she shouldn't; hides a soft heart under a great deal of scowling and one very theatrical eyebrow.
  - **Mr. Pumble** — the perpetually bewildered quartermaster, keeps the ledgers, keeps losing his mind, has an entire column in the logbook now just labeled "GLOWING THINGS (unexplained)."
  - **Old Tessa the Carpenter** — never speaks more than four words, becomes Bolt's silent best friend by page thirty; the two of them communicate entirely in nods and improved rigging.
  - **The crew of the *Bright Revenge*** — a warm, ridiculous ensemble (a superstitious lookout, a cook whose stew is a documented hazard, a parrot with strong opinions).
- **Antagonist:** **Lord Barnabus Kroll** — a rival pirate lord in a coat with too many buttons, who has heard rumors of a ship that skims the waves and glows in the dark, and wants that magic for himself — bag it, cage the aliens, bolt their tech to his own black fleet.

## Example scenes / set-pieces

1. **The first ship-upgrade race** — Desperate to prove they're worth their bunks, the aliens sneak below decks overnight and quietly work alien tech into the old wooden hull — a smoothed, humming keel, rigging that catches wind that isn't there. Next morning the *Bright Revenge* wins the Cove's harbor race by a humiliating distance, leaving Ironjaw's rivals in her spray, and the crew — who have no idea *why* they suddenly fly — decide these weird glowing newcomers can stay forever.
2. **The rival pirate lord** — Word of a wave-skipping ship reaches Lord Kroll, who covets the tech the way lesser men covet gold. He shadows the *Bright Revenge*, tries to buy the aliens, then tries to steal them, forcing the crew into a running sea-chase where the aliens must defend their friends with everything clever and *nothing* lethal.
3. **Repair-the-ship-vs-stay dilemma** — Deep in the story the aliens finally scavenge the last part they need: the *Wanderling* could fly again. They could go home. And standing on the beach beside the ship they crashed in, looking back at a pirate crew that became a family, they have to decide whether "home" is a place you come from or the one you built out of borrowed planks and stolen jam.

## Visuals

- **Illustration style direction:** Painterly graphic-novel adventure — the romance of weathered wooden galleons crossed with soft, bioluminescent alien tech. The whole visual hook is *contrast and integration*: brass-and-coral retrofits screwed into salt-grey oak, rigging that glows faintly cyan where the aliens have re-threaded it, a lantern that is clearly not a lantern. Rich, cinematic, sea-spray energy with expressive cartooned faces so the comedy always reads. Think handsome adventure cover you'd want on a shelf, not flat gag-art.
- **Palette:** sea-teal and storm-grey for the ocean, sunset-gold and warm rum-amber for the pirate world, weathered oak-brown and rope-tan for the ship, and — threaded through it all — alien bioluminescent cyan and hot magenta for every piece of hidden tech, so your eye instantly finds what the aliens have touched.
- **Character design notes (in words):** The aliens read as *soft* against the pirates' hard edges — Fizzwick tall, nervous, over-dressed in a captain's sash he clearly studied a picture of; Bolt low, square, four-armed, unbothered; Squish a glossy translucent teal droplet with one huge eye; Gorm a gentle boulder with sad kind eyes. The pirates are all leather, scars, and swagger — Ironjaw lean and sharp with a silver-capped jaw and that legendary eyebrow, Pumble round and flustered and inkstained, Tessa wiry and silent with a pencil behind one ear. The retrofitted galleon is the real star: a proper weathered three-master with coral-and-brass panels grafted to the hull, cyan-glowing rope, a bubble-shield emitter disguised as a figurehead, and a keel that hums with light just below the waterline.
- **Key illustration moments:**
  1. **The crash on the pirate shore** — night on Gallows Cove: the smoking alien ship half-buried in the sand, its wings sheared off, glowing cyan wreckage-light spilling across a beach crowded with astonished, cutlass-holding pirate silhouettes, four small aliens frozen in the hatchway with their hands raised.
  2. **The retrofitted galleon skimming the waves** — the show-stopper: the *Bright Revenge* at full speed *skipping* across the swell, hull barely touching water, rigging aglow with alien cyan, sunset-gold sky behind, spray flying, the pirate crew whooping in the shrouds while Bolt calmly tightens a glowing bolt.
  3. **The showdown with the rival lord** — Lord Kroll's black fleet closing in through fog while the *Bright Revenge* fires a great blooming net-cannon and raises a shimmering bubble-shield, cannonballs bouncing off harmlessly, aliens and pirates fighting side by side with gadgets instead of blades.

## Series hook

The engine is built to run forever: each book is a **new sea, a new rival, and a new tech-problem** the aliens have to solve with cleverness and scavenged parts, while the *Wanderling* slowly, book by book, gets closer to spaceworthy — a long emotional throughline under the standalone adventures. Possible future-book hooks: **Book 2 — the Frozen Straits,** where the crew must retrofit the ship to survive a sea that freezes solid and a rival who sails an *icebreaker* fortress; **Book 3 — the Storm That Never Ends,** hunting a legendary map inside a permanent hurricane, where the aliens' weather-tech is the only way through; **Book 4 — the return of the *Wanderling*,** when a second alien ship finally comes looking for the lost crew, and the family they built has to decide, together, what home means now.

## Language note

English primary. Dutch-first version: no — the comedy runs on English rhythm, deadpan timing, and mistranslation gags (Squish's cheerfully wrong subtitles are the whole running joke), which land sharpest in English first; but the adventure is broad and visual, so a Dutch edition (*Sterrepiraten*) follows cleanly and suits the local market — owner decides, English leads.
