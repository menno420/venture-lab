# Concept — Bramble the Night-Post

- **Track:** Old-Fashioned (cozy-wonder)
- **Logline:** Every night while the woodland village sleeps, Bramble the hedgehog shoulders his little satchel and pads down the lantern-lit lanes, delivering the letters, parcels, and small kindnesses that make the morning brighter.
- **Age band:** read-aloud (ages 3–6) and early reader (ages 6–8), with short chapters made for bedtime
- **Tone + narrator voice:** Warm, unhurried, and confiding — the voice of a grandparent tucking a blanket around your shoulders. It notices small things (the click of a latch, the hush of falling snow) and treats each tiny errand as quietly important. Gentle humor, never a fright — the dark is a friendly, sleepy place.
- **Story shape:** episodic chapters — each night is one complete round with one small, human-hearted problem that a single well-timed delivery sets right, so any chapter can be a whole bedtime on its own.

## Sample opening

When the last window in Mossgate Village went dark, that was the signal Bramble had been waiting for.

He set his little round cap upon his head, tugged the strap of his satchel snug across his prickles, and stepped out into the cool of the evening. Above the rooftops the moon had come up soft and silver, the way it does when it means to keep you company. Somewhere far off, an owl said one polite word to the night, and the night said nothing back.

"Good," whispered Bramble, for silence was how he knew everyone was safely tucked in. "Now we may begin."

Down the lane he went, snuffle-snuffle, his small feet making no more noise than a moth. The cobbles still held the day's warmth, and the air smelled of dew and chimney-smoke and the badger baker's bread cooling on a sill. In his satchel — he checked, the way he always checked — were seven letters, one get-well card, a parcel of acorns tied with string, and an invitation to a birthday nobody knew about yet.

At the first blue door he stopped. He slid a letter beneath it, ever so gently, so the sleeper on the other side would find it like a little gift in the morning and never guess who had crept by in the dark.

Then on he went, past the sleeping shops, past the pond that held the moon in its lap, doing the quiet work that no one saw — and that made all the difference.

## Recurring cast

- **Bramble** — the Night-Post: a small, tidy hedgehog with a round cap and a well-worn leather satchel. Dutiful and soft-hearted, shy by daylight but quietly brave in the dark; he knows every doorway in the village and every worry behind it.
- **Old Fettle** — the lamplighter beetle: a stout, whiskery beetle who tends the lane-lanterns, walking just ahead of Bramble each night so the postman always has a pool of light to step into. Full of slow, comfortable wisdom.
- **Mrs. Bripp** — the badger baker: broad, floury, and up before anyone, her warm ovens the first light of the coming day. She leaves a saucer of crumbs on her sill for Bramble, and always seems to know when a letter is a sad one.
- **Pip** — a mouse child forever waiting for a letter from a faraway cousin, small nose pressed to the window long after bedtime. Bramble's most faithful, most impatient customer.
- **Wisp** — an owl who rides the night wind: aloof but kind, she carries the far-away letters that come from beyond the wood, dropping them into Bramble's satchel on the wing.
- **The Fenn Family** — a burrow-home of sleepy rabbits whose doorbell Bramble must never, ever ring (there are seven babies), so he has learned to deliver softer than a snowflake.

## Example episodes / scenes

1. **The Letter That Wouldn't Wait** — A get-well card for old Mr. Toad arrives torn and soaked from the rain, its words all run together. Bramble stays out past his usual hour, mending it by lantern-light with a dab of Mrs. Bripp's flour-paste, so the poorly toad still wakes to a card that says *we are thinking of you*.
2. **The Birthday Nobody Knew** — Bramble discovers an invitation addressed to *Everyone in Mossgate* for a party at dawn, but the sender forgot to sign it. On his round he quietly gathers one small clue from each doorway until he can tuck the right name back onto the card — and slip it under the birthday-mouse's own door last of all.
3. **The Night the Lamps Went Out** — A storm snuffs every lantern in the village and Old Fettle can't relight them in the wind. Bramble must finish his round in true darkness, guided only by Wisp calling softly from above and by his own good memory of every latch and step — and finds the dark is not so frightening when a friend is overhead.

## Visuals

- **Illustration style direction:** warm, soft-edged watercolor in the classic bedtime-storybook tradition; gentle nighttime scenes lit from within by glowing lantern-light, so every picture has one warm golden source against the cool blue dark. Cozy, rounded, unhurried — nothing sharp or scary.
- **Palette:** moon-blue and deep indigo for the sky and shadows; lantern-amber and candle-gold for the pools of light; mossy green and bark-brown for the village; soft cream for letters and paper; a dusty rose for warmth in cheeks and doorways.
- **Character design notes (in words):** Bramble is small and round, his prickles rendered as soft grey-brown watercolor tufts (never spiky-looking). He wears a snug knitted cap the color of a ripe conker and a scuffed tan leather satchel worn crossways, its buckle a polished acorn-cap. His face is gentle, with kind dark eyes and a perpetually snuffling little nose. Old Fettle is a rotund beetle in a lamplighter's apron carrying a long brass wick-pole. Mrs. Bripp the badger is broad and aproned and dusted with flour. Pip the mouse is tiny, in a nightcap, always at a lit window. Wisp the owl is pale and dignified, wings spread against the stars.
- **Key illustration moments:**
  1. Bramble setting out from his own little door, cap on and satchel buckled, silhouetted against the last lit window of the village while the moon rises silver above the rooftops.
  2. Bramble crouched at a blue door in a warm circle of lantern-light, sliding a cream-colored letter beneath it with the greatest care, the rest of the lane cool and blue around him.
  3. The storm night: Bramble small in the dark lane with every lamp gone out, looking up at pale Wisp the owl gliding overhead against the stars, her call the only light he needs.
