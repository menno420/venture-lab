# painted-stones — manuscript decisions

Concept source: fresh original (book wave 2), owner delegation 2026-07-12, decide-and-flag mode — decisions below are mine to make; the owner vetoes later. Not derived from any existing book or trademarked property.

**Owner-gated note:** Everything in this folder is a manuscript deliverable only (text in EN/NL/DE). Anything beyond the words — illustration/art, layout, print, ISBN, pricing, distribution — is deliberately NOT done here and awaits owner direction (see "Not done here" below).

## Age band
Chosen: read-aloud picture book, ages 4–8. The vocabulary, sentence length, and gentle mystery structure suit a lap-read or early-reader. Stakes are entirely warm — no crime, threat, loss, or scary reveal — so the youngest end of the band is safe, while the detective method and clue-chaining give the older end something to follow and predict.

## Length & structure
13 spreads (within the requested 12–15). Body word counts: EN 556, NL 570, DE 552 — all inside the 550–700 target, kept close together so a co-edition lays out on the same page grid.
Arc: three "gift" mornings (boat, cat, star) establish the pattern → a heading formalizes the case → four physical clues (blue paint smudge, paint-speckled ribbon, chalk arrows, wet footprint) form a followable trail → the boathouse reveal → warm resolution → a mirror-image final spread where the two children now leave a stone together. Each clue-spread reuses the detective refrain so young listeners can chant along and predict it.

## Which story
Cozy mystery built from the premise seed, refined and kept original:
- Detective with a well-loved notebook; a repeatable method line (the refrain) spoken at each clue.
- The mystery is a string of smooth painted stones left one each morning.
- Warm reveal: the stones are gifts from a shy new kid too nervous to say hello. Ending = a new friendship, sealed by painting a stone together and then "paying it forward" to another newcomer.
- Original touches added: the red-raincoat motif — the shy boy has secretly painted a tiny red-raincoat stone, proof he'd been noticing HER too, which lets the detective "solve" that the case was mutual. The refrain transforms on the last page from "find the answer" to "find a friend."

## Character-name decisions
Names localized per language for native read-aloud feel:
- Detective — EN **Wren**, NL **Fenna**, DE **Frieda**. All short, warm, easy for a child to say; Fenna and Frieda are genuinely common, current given names in NL/DE respectively.
- Shy new kid — EN **Milo**, NL **Bram**, DE **Jonas**. Friendly, unpretentious boys' names native to each language.
- Town — EN **Saltwick** (invented English harbor-town name), NL **Havenlo** (haven + -lo place suffix, sounds like a real Dutch town), DE **Kleinhafen** (plain, storybook-plausible). All fictional.

## Language approach
EN is the master and the voice source of truth; NL and DE are native re-tellings, re-authored for rhythm and idiom rather than translated literally.
- **NL (strongest, owner is Dutch):** flowing, natural read-aloud Dutch. Diminutives (steentje, bootje, katje, nestje, regenjasje) carry the cozy warmth Dutch does so well. Idiom over calque: "de moeite waard," "op precies dezelfde plek," "de één na de ander," "stil als een poes," "op een kier." Emphatic accents kept for read-aloud stress ("háár ook," "twéé kinderen"). Refrain: "Notitieboek open. Volg de aanwijzing, vind het antwoord." → final "Volg de aanwijzing... vind een vriend."
- **DE:** native picture-book cadence, correct nuances ("bemalt," "Spurensuche," "der Rede wert," "auf einem Spalt offen / einen Spalt offen"), natural inversion and comma style. Refrain: "Notizbuch auf. Folge der Spur, finde die Antwort." → final "Folge der Spur... finde einen Freund." "Spur" (trail/clue) doubles neatly as both the detective's "clue" and a literal "trail," which strengthens the refrain in German.
- The onomatopoeia "pit-pat" for the heartbeat was kept identical across all three — it reads and sounds the same aloud in each language and gives co-editions a shared beat.
