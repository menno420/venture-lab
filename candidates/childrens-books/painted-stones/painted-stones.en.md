# The Painted Stones

- **Title:** The Painted Stones
- **Language:** English (master — voice source of truth)
- **Age band:** read-aloud picture book, ages 4–8
- **Word count:** 556 (manuscript body, excluding spread/illustration notes)
- **Pitch:** A small-town girl detective follows a trail of tiny painted stones and cracks the warmest case of her life — a new friend hiding just around the corner.

> Series: *The Little Notebook Mysteries* — cozy mystery, stand-alone. Every spread carries an illustration note in italics; the seat generates no art.

---

[Spread 1]
*Illustration: A misty harbor town at dawn — bobbing boats, tilting rooftops, a girl in a red raincoat holding a well-worn notebook, gulls perched on a railing.*
In the little harbor town of Saltwick, where the boats bobbed and the gulls gossiped, lived a girl named Wren. Wren had two important things: a red raincoat and a well-loved notebook. She wrote down everything worth noticing. And Wren noticed everything. Nothing in Saltwick was too small for Wren — not a loose button, not a hidden nest, not a single crumb out of place.

[Spread 2]
*Illustration: Close-up of Wren's hand lifting a smooth grey stone off the wet cobblestones; a tiny blue boat painted on it.*
One misty morning, on the cobblestone path by the water, Wren found a smooth grey stone. Someone had painted a tiny blue boat on it, small as her thumbnail.
"Curious," said Wren. She opened her notebook. "Notebook out. Follow the clue, find the answer." She slipped the stone in her pocket, warm as a secret.

[Spread 3]
*Illustration: The same spot the next morning; Wren crouched over a second stone with a little orange cat on it, pencil tapping her chin.*
The next morning, another stone waited in the very same spot. This one wore a little orange cat, whiskers and all.
Two stones now. Wren tapped her pencil. Two stones was not an accident. Two stones was a mystery.

[Spread 4]
*Illustration: Wren pressing a star-painted stone to her cheek; her open notebook shows a bold new heading.*
On the third morning — a stone with a golden star. Wren pressed it to her cheek. The paint was still cool and damp.
"Fresh," she whispered. "The painter is close." She wrote at the top of a clean page: THE CASE OF THE PAINTED STONES.

[Spread 5]
*Illustration: A weathered harbor fence with a bright blue paint smudge; Wren sketching it, tongue between her teeth.*
Wren went hunting for clues. By the harbor fence she found a smudge of blue paint, bright against the weathered wood.
"Notebook out. Follow the clue, find the answer." Blue — like the little boat. She sketched the smudge and marched on.

[Spread 6]
*Illustration: Outside a cozy bakery, steam curling from the window; Wren holding up a paint-speckled yellow ribbon dripping from a puddle.*
Outside the bakery, where the air smelled of warm cinnamon, a yellow ribbon lay dropped in a puddle.
Wren picked it up. It was speckled — speckled with tiny dots of paint. "Aha," she breathed. Her heart went pit-pat, the way it did when a mystery leaned close.

[Spread 7]
*Illustration: Chalk arrows on the cobblestones leading along the quay; Wren tiptoeing past fishing nets and rowboats.*
Then she saw them: chalk arrows on the cobblestones, one after another, pointing along the quay.
"Someone wants to be followed," said Wren, "but only just." She followed, quiet as a cat, past the fishing nets and the sleeping rowboats.

[Spread 8]
*Illustration: The end of a wooden jetty; a glistening wet footprint; the arrows curving toward a blue boathouse with its door ajar.*
At the end of the jetty, a wet footprint glistened — small, like her own.
"Notebook out. Follow the clue, find the answer." The arrows turned toward the old blue boathouse, its door open just a crack.

[Spread 9]
*Illustration: Wren peeking through the narrow gap in the boathouse door, eyes wide, notebook forgotten at her side.*
Wren tiptoed to the boathouse and peeked through the gap in the door. And there — oh! — she stopped, and held her breath, and forgot to write anything down at all.

[Spread 10]
*Illustration: Inside the sunlit boathouse — jars of brushes, a row of drying painted stones (moon, fish, tiny red raincoat), and a boy crouched shyly behind them.*
Jars of paintbrushes. A row of smooth stones drying in a sunbeam: a moon, a fish, a tiny red raincoat. And crouched behind them, a boy about her size, hiding his face behind his knees.

[Spread 11]
*Illustration: The boy looking up, cheeks pink, a paintbrush still in his hand; Wren kneeling gently in the doorway light.*
"I'm sorry," the boy whispered. "I'm Milo. We only just moved here. I wanted to say hello, but the words got stuck. So I painted stones instead. One for each morning, so you'd know somebody was hoping to be your friend."

[Spread 12]
*Illustration: Close-up of the little painted red raincoat stone in Wren's palm; her notebook open beside it, a warm smile spreading.*
Wren looked at the little painted raincoat, red as her own. He had been noticing her, too.
She opened her notebook to the very last clue and smiled. "Milo," she said, "I think I've solved it."

[Spread 13]
*Illustration: The two children side by side in the sunbeam painting one shared stone; final panel — two of them setting a fresh stone on the cobblestone path at dawn.*
So they sat in the sunbeam and painted a stone together — half his blue, half her red — and Wren wrote one last line.
"Notebook out," she said. "Follow the clue... find a friend."
And the very next morning, on the cobblestone path, there were two children leaving a painted stone for somebody new.

*The end.*
