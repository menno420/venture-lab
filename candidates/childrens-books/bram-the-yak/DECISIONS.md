# bram-the-yak — manuscript decisions

Concept source: fresh original, book wave 2, owner delegation 2026-07-12, decide-and-flag (owner vetoes later). Not derived from any existing book or trademark.

Owner-gated note: every choice below is a proposal. The owner (Dutch native) retains veto over titles, character names, refrain wording, and any art/print/commercial step.

## Age band

Read-aloud picture book, ages 4–8. Sentences are short and rhythmic; jokes are physical and sound-based so they land when read aloud to a pre-reader, while the redemptive arc rewards the 6–8 end of the band.

## Length & structure

13 spreads, within the requested 12–15. Body word counts: EN 646, NL 641, DE 636 — all inside the 550–700 target. Structure: setup (spreads 1–2) → four escalating comic disasters, each in an ever-quieter place (library, baby's nap, hide-and-seek, fancy dinner; spreads 3–6) → low point (7) → turn: fog emergency (8–10) → the giant voice becomes the hero (11) → reunion (12) → turnaround coda where the flaw is now the town's treasured asset (13).

## Which story

The "can't whisper" premise seed, kept and sharpened. Bram is a yak (kept — a yak is inherently funny, shaggy, and huggable, and the deep BRRROOONK honk suits a big-chested animal). The escalation uses the four suggested set-pieces. The turnaround is the fog rescue: his enormous voice is the only thing that carries through fog to guide lost little Pip home — flaw reframed as gift.

## Character-name decisions

- **Bram** — kept across all three languages. It is a genuine Dutch/Flemish boy's name (owner is Dutch) and also reads fine in EN/DE, so the hero's name travels without translation. Bonus: "Bram" + "BRRROOONK" share a plosive B, which helps the sound gag.
- **Lost friend:** EN/NL/DE all "Pip" — tiny, high, the opposite of Bram; works phonetically in every language.
- **Baby:** localized for naturalness — EN "Nana", NL "Naatje" (authentic Dutch diminutive), DE "Nele" (common German girl's name). Kept short so the "shot up like toast" gag stays snappy.
- **Authority figure:** Mayor/Bürgermeister Swan/Zwaan/Schwan — a snooty swan is a reliable comic foil; name is just the translated animal.
- The owl librarian and the swan are described by role, not proper name, to keep the cast light.

## Language approach

EN is master and the voice source of truth. NL and DE are native re-tellings, not literal translations — the jokes, sound-words, and refrain were re-authored to land naturally out loud.

**The refrain.**
- EN: "Bram took a tiny breath... / and let out a NOT-tiny sound: / **BRRROOOOONK!**" — the "tiny / NOT-tiny" antithesis is the engine.
- NL (must be funniest — owner is Dutch): "Bram nam een piepklein hapje lucht... / en er kwam een NIET-piepklein geluid uit: / **BROEOEOENK!**" — "piepklein hapje lucht" is warm, very Dutch nursery language; "NIET-piepklein" preserves the antithesis; "hapje lucht" (a little bite of air) is idiomatic and cosier than a literal "kleine adem". The honk is spelled **BROEOENK** because Dutch "oe" = the deep /u/ sound, so a Dutch parent reads the exact low foghorn note automatically.
- DE: "Bram holte ein klitzekleines bisschen Luft... / und heraus kam ein NICHT-klitzekleiner Ton: / **BRÖÖÖÖNK!**" — "klitzeklein" is the natural German nursery superlative for tiny; "NICHT-klitzekleiner Ton" keeps the antithesis. The honk uses **Ö** because the German ö gives the round, comical low vowel a native reader will voice correctly.

**Sound-words (re-authored, not translated).**
- Window rattle: EN *rattle-rattle-CLINK* / NL *rammel-rammel-KLING* / DE *klipper-klapper-KLIRR*.
- Books falling: EN *flap-flap-FLUMP* / NL *flap-flap-PLOF* / DE *flatter-flatter-PLUMPS*.
- Soup: EN *sloosh* / NL *sjoesj* / DE *schwapp*; candles EN *poof* / NL *poef* / DE *puff*.
- Swallowed voices in fog: EN *pfft* / NL *pff* / DE *pfft*.
Each is spelled to the target language's phonics so the read-aloud grown-up produces the intended noise without thinking.

**Wordplay kept natural.** The owl's scolding was localized as an alliterative insult-that-is-secretly-fond: EN "loud, lovely lump" / NL "lieve, luide lomperik" / DE "lieber, lauter Lümmel" — alliteration preserved in each language rather than translated word-for-word.

## Concept gaps filled

- Chose the yak (over other animals) and locked a deep foghorn honk as the signature sound.
- Invented the supporting cast (Pip, the baby, the owl librarian, Mayor Swan) and the "Very Loud Valley / Het Heel Harde Dal / Das Ganz Laute Tal" imprint/series name.
- Selected the fog rescue as the specific turnaround (vs. the alternative "wake everyone for something wonderful"), because fog physically motivates why only a huge voice works and gives a clean visual for the payoff spread.
- Added the coda (spread 13) that institutionalizes the flaw-as-gift: Bram becomes the town's official foghorn/dinner-bell, so the ending reframes rather than "cures" him.
