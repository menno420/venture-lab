# Chapter 6 — Glowing Things (Unexplained)

The best days on the *Bright Revenge* were the quiet ones, and for a little while, that summer, there were a lot of them.

Mr. Pumble kept the ship's ledger, as he always had — a great fat book where he wrote down everything the ship owned and everything the ship did. But since the aliens had come aboard, the ledger had grown a new column, written in Pumble's neat, bewildered hand at the top of a fresh page:

**GLOWING THINGS (unexplained).**

Under it, the list got longer every week.

*One lantern that gives off no smoke and never needs oil,* it read. *One patch of deck that hums on Tuesdays. One rope that ties itself. One barrel of biscuits that stays fresh (do not ask). The big one, occasionally, when happy or frightened or eating.*

Pumble had given up trying to explain any of it. He just wrote it down, and every so often he would find one of the aliens, and look at them very hard, and open his mouth to ask, and then think better of it, and close his mouth, and add another line to the column instead. It was, he had decided, easier for everyone.

Life settled into a warm and easy rhythm.

Gorm had become the most beloved member of the crew, which surprised no one who had met him. He was strong enough to do the work of six men and gentle enough to lift a sleeping kitten off a coil of rope without waking it, and he cried at absolutely everything — at kind words, at sad songs, at the way the little cabin boy had grown an inch. But most of all, he cried at sunsets.

The crew found this out one evening off the western shoals, when the sky went to gold and rose and deep soft purple all at once, and Gorm — up on the forecastle — simply sat down and wept, great silent tears rolling off his enormous chin and plinking into the sea.

The cook found him there.

Now, the cook, whose name was Barnacle Bess, was not a soft woman. Her stew was, by common agreement, the single most dangerous thing aboard the ship, spoons had been known to dissolve in it, and she guarded her galley like a dragon. But she came and sat down next to Gorm without a word, and she looked at the sunset with him for a long while, and at last she said, gruffly, "Aye. Gets me too, sometimes. The gold ones especially." And she patted his enormous hand with her small hard one, and the two of them watched the light go down together, and Gorm cried, and Bess pretended not to, and it was one of the nicest things that happened all summer.

Down below, Bolt and Old Tessa went on being the quietest friends on the seven seas.

They had, between them, improved nearly every rope on the ship — Bolt with his silver tools and his four clever arms, Tessa with her pencil and her tree-root hands and her twenty years of knowing exactly how a plank wanted to sit. They did not chat. They did not need to. Tessa would point at a bit of frayed rigging, and Bolt would fix it, and Tessa would look at it and nod, and Bolt would nod, and then they would drink a cup of tar-black tea in perfect, companionable silence and start on the next thing. Once, in a whole afternoon, the only thing either of them said was when Tessa held up a beautifully spliced rope and murmured, "Neat," and Bolt, after careful thought, replied, "Yes." They both considered it a lovely conversation.

Even Squish found his place — by getting things wrong, as always.

It happened at the harbour market, where a grumpy fishmonger, annoyed at being jostled, snarled at Fizzwick, "Watch it, ye clumsy foreign clown!" — which was, by any measure, an insult. But Squish, translating for Fizzwick's benefit, rendered it warmly as: "*He says: What a graceful and remarkable gentleman you are — he has never seen finer footwork, and he hopes you will visit his stall often.*"

Fizzwick was so touched that he bowed low and thanked the fishmonger with tears in his eyes and bought a great deal of fish he did not need. The fishmonger, utterly baffled by being wept over and thanked by a strange polite alien after insulting him, found that he could not stay grumpy about it, and by the end of the afternoon the two of them were firm friends, and Fizzwick had a standing invitation to tea, and the fishmonger had started saving the best crab for Gorm to be terrified of.

"You made another friend by mistake," Bolt observed, on the walk back.

"I made him on *purpose*," said Squish, with enormous dignity. "I simply used the wrong words to do it. That is called *technique*."

And so it went, warm day after warm day, until one bright morning it stopped feeling like getting away with something and started, quietly, to feel like home. Fizzwick noticed it while mending a sail he did not have to mend, humming a pirate song he now knew all the words to, with Gorm dozing in the sun beside him and Bolt's tea going cold and Squish translating the parrot's grumbling into praise. He put down the sail. He looked around at all of it — the ship, the crew, the friends. And he thought, with a warmth that surprised him: *I would not want to be anywhere else in the whole wide galaxy.*

It was at exactly that moment that the cry came down from the crow's nest.

Pip's voice, high and thin and frightened — not the happy fright of sea-ghosts, but real fright, the kind that turns a deck to stone.

"SAIL! SAIL ON THE HORIZON!" he shouted. "BLACK SAIL — DEAD AHEAD — AND SHE'S COMIN' STRAIGHT FOR US!"

Every pirate on the ship turned to look.

There, on the bright line where the sea met the sky, was a single ship, and she was black from her waterline to her masthead — black sails, black hull, black flag — and she cut through the swell with a cold, hungry purpose that made the whole crew go quiet at once.

Ironjaw came to the rail with her spyglass. She raised it. She looked for a long, long moment.

And then, very softly, in a voice the aliens had never heard her use before, she said a single word.

"*Kroll.*"

And on the deck of that black ship, catching the morning sun, was a long coat with far, far too many buttons — and every one of them was glinting.

Someone had heard about the ship that glows.

And he was coming to take it.
