# Chapter 5 — The Harbour Race

The Gallows Cove Harbour Race was the biggest day of the year, and the whole cove had turned out for it.

Ships crowded the start line, sails flapping, crews jeering happily across the water. Flags snapped. A brass band on the harbour wall played something loud and only mostly in tune. And bobbing at the line beside the *Bright Revenge* was the sleek red galleon of Captain Marisol Drab, who was Ironjaw's oldest rival and enjoyed it very much.

"Ironjaw!" Captain Drab called across the gap, grinning. "Still floating? And what's this — new crew?" She squinted at the four aliens lined up nervously at the *Bright Revenge*'s rail. "Bless me. Where'd you dredge up *those*? The one's got too many arms, the big one's crying already, and — is that one *glowing*?"

"He is not glowing," said Ironjaw, through her teeth.

Gorm, who had been quietly leaking a soft blue light out of sheer nervousness, tried very hard to stop.

"Your glow-crew won't help you today, Roana," laughed Drab. "That old tub of yours couldn't outrun a jellyfish. Try to keep up." And she swung back to her own deck, cackling.

Ironjaw's theatrical eyebrow climbed so high it nearly left her face. She turned and looked, slowly, at the four aliens.

"Anything," she said quietly, "you want to tell me?"

"You have a *lovely* boat," offered Fizzwick. "Very fast, I imagine. Extremely — brown."

Ironjaw held his gaze for a long moment. Then the starting cannon fired.

*BOOM.*

The whole line of ships surged forward — sails filling, oars digging, spray flying — and for the first few seconds the *Bright Revenge* fell behind, exactly as everyone expected. Drab's red galleon leapt ahead. The crowd on the harbour wall groaned in sympathy for poor old Ironjaw.

Then Bolt, standing very still by the wheel, reached down to a board by his foot, and — where no one could see — he pressed his four-fingered hand flat against a hidden plate.

Under the water, the keel began to hum.

The *Bright Revenge* lifted.

Not much. Just enough. The great heavy galleon rose the width of a hand out of the water and stopped shoving the sea aside and started *skimming* across the top of it, light as a skipped stone, and the sails — threaded in the night with a thread finer than hair — bellied full and hard though the wind hadn't changed at all.

And the old brown ship *flew*.

She passed Drab's red galleon so fast that the wind of her going knocked Drab's hat clean off. She passed the sleek grey clipper in third. She passed the twin schooners neck and neck for the lead as though they were standing still and waving. The band on the harbour wall stopped playing, note by note, until there was just one confused trumpet, and then even that gave up.

On the deck of the *Bright Revenge*, the pirates lost their minds.

"WE'RE FLYIN'!" howled Sal, clinging to a rope. "THE OLD GIRL'S *FLYIN'*!"

Pumble had dropped his ledger entirely and was gripping the rail with both hands, laughing a wild, disbelieving laugh. Pip, up in the crow's nest, was shouting "THE DEEP ONES! THE DEEP ONES BLESSED US!" and nobody was listening. The parrot did three loops and screamed a word Squish translated as "*VINDICATION!*"

They crossed the finish line so far ahead of everyone else that the second-place ship hadn't yet rounded the last buoy. The crowd on the wall went silent. Then, all at once, they roared.

Ironjaw stood at the wheel with the wind in her face and the whole cove screaming her name, and she did not smile — a captain does not smile — but her silver jaw flashed, and her eyes were bright, and when she looked back down the line at Captain Drab, drifting far behind with no hat and her mouth hanging open, Ironjaw's theatrical eyebrow gave a small, victorious, entirely magnificent *wiggle*.

The aliens, meanwhile, were the very picture of innocence.

"How extraordinary," said Fizzwick, gazing about with wide eyes. "The ship simply — went. Who can say why. A mystery. A gift. Perhaps the sea likes us."

"Perhaps," said Bolt, whose hand was still, very casually, resting on the hidden plate.

"I felt nothing unusual," said Squish, glowing faintly.

Back at the harbour, Ironjaw gathered the whole crew on the deck. Word was already flying round the cove — *Ironjaw's crew won, Ironjaw's crew won by half a mile, did you SEE the old tub go* — and every pirate aboard was grinning like a child. Ironjaw held up a hand for quiet.

"Now," she said, "I don't know how we won. I've got my suspicions." Her eye slid to the aliens, and Fizzwick studied the sky. "But there's a question in front of this crew, and we settle it the old way. These four —" she nodded at the aliens — "they're strange. They shout the war-cry wrong. One of 'em's frightened of shellfish. And they glow, which I'm told is *unlucky*." She paused. "But they crashed onto our beach with nothing, and they didn't run, and — somehow — today they made us the fastest ship on the sea and the proudest crew in the cove. So." She folded her arms. "All in favour of the newcomers staying — say aye."

There was the smallest silence.

Then Sal bellowed "*AYE!*" so loud that gulls fell off the mast, and the whole crew came in behind her — "*AYE!*" "*AYE!*" "*AYE, AND NO MERCY, BUT PLEASE TAKE A SEAT!*" (that was Pip, getting it wrong, and everyone cheered anyway) — a great rolling wall of *aye* that rocked the ship and echoed off the harbour wall and went out across the whole of Gallows Cove.

Fizzwick did not say anything for a moment.

Then his lower lip wobbled, and his six fingers came up to his face, and he began, very quietly and very politely, to cry.

"Oh," he said, "excuse me. So sorry. It's just —" he dabbed at his enormous eyes — "nobody has ever voted for us before. Anywhere. On any planet. I do apologise for leaking."

"Ye great custard," said Sal, and she clapped him on the back so hard he sat down, which is the pirate way of saying *welcome home*.

That night there was a feast on the beach, and dancing, and a bonfire, and Gorm carried three pirates on his shoulders at once, and Bolt and Old Tessa sat side by side saying nothing in the most contented way imaginable, and for the first time since the crash, the four aliens felt something they had almost forgotten the shape of. They felt like they belonged somewhere.

But belonging is a bright thing, and bright things can be seen from a long way off.

Far out past the cove, beyond the last light of the bonfire, on the dark rim of the sea, a lone ship sat at anchor with its sails furled and its lanterns dark. And on its deck a tall man in a long coat lowered a spyglass, and watched the glowing crew dance on the beach, and smiled a slow and hungry smile.

The story of the ship that flew was already travelling.

And it had reached exactly the wrong ears.
