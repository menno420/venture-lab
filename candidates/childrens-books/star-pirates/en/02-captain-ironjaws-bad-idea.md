# Chapter 2 — Captain Ironjaw's Bad Idea

Captain Roana "Ironjaw" Vex was having a perfectly good afternoon until four aliens were dumped on her deck.

She sat in a great carved chair on the quarterdeck of the *Bright Revenge*, cleaning her cutlass, minding her own business. She had a silver-capped jaw that flashed when she talked, and one eyebrow that was, frankly, the most theatrical eyebrow on the seven seas. When she was surprised, it went up. When she was suspicious, it went up further. Right now it was climbing toward her hat.

"What," she said slowly, "are those."

"Found 'em on the beach, Cap'n," said the pirate with the beard beads. "Fell out of the sky. This one's got too many fingers, that one's got too many arms, the big one's crying, and the little one won't shut up."

"A pleasure to meet you," said Fizzwick, bowing. "Rule One: *Always greet the person in charge with your warmest good wishes.* You have a *lovely* boat. Extremely large. Very brown."

Squish leaned in and translated Ironjaw's reply. "She says: *Thank you, kind stranger. I am delighted by your visit.*"

What Ironjaw had actually said was, "If this is a curse, I'm sending it back."

Beside her, a small round man in a too-tight waistcoat was writing frantically in a ledger. This was Mr. Pumble, the quartermaster, who was permanently bewildered and had learned to write down everything so that at least *someone* would have a record of the madness. He looked up.

"Captain," Pumble whispered, "they came out of the *sky*. In a ball. That skipped."

"I heard, Pumble."

"I don't have a column for that, Captain."

"Then make one."

Ironjaw stood. She was tall, and she was scowling, and she walked a slow circle around the four aliens the way a cat walks around something it hasn't decided whether to eat. Gorm shrank. Bolt did not. Fizzwick tried to hand her his laminated card, dropped it, apologised, picked it up, apologised again, and offered it a second time.

"What are you," said Ironjaw, "and what do you want on my ship?"

Now, Fizzwick knew — he *knew* — that the honest answer was: *We are lost travellers from very far away, our ship is broken, we have never so much as held a sword, and we would like to not be thrown to the crabs.* He knew this was the truth. But Fizzwick also knew Rule Twelve of Proper Conduct, which said, *When among new company, show that you belong.* And there was a plan forming in his enormous, terrified, well-meaning heart.

"We," he announced, drawing himself up to his full and unimpressive height, "are pirates. Too. Also. As well."

There was a silence.

"Pirates," said Ironjaw.

"The finest," said Fizzwick. "Extremely piratical. We pirate constantly. We can barely stop."

"Prove it," said Ironjaw. "Say something piratey."

Fizzwick froze. He looked at Bolt. Bolt looked back with the warm supportive expression of a dinner plate. Fizzwick looked at his card. The card had no rule for this. Fizzwick opened his mouth and, in a voice of pure panic, cried:

"AVAST! And also — *thank you*!"

A pirate near the rail snorted so hard he fell over.

But here is the strange thing about Captain Ironjaw Vex. Under the scowl, and under the second scowl below that, she had a soft heart, and a stubborn one, and a memory. And her memory had just handed her something delicious.

Two weeks ago, in the tavern at Gallows Cove, the rival captains had laughed at her. *Ironjaw can't keep a crew,* they'd said. *Ironjaw's ship is old and slow. Ironjaw couldn't hire a barnacle.* And here — dropped from the sky onto her deck — was a crew so strange, so wrong, so utterly unlike anything the cove had ever seen, that taking them on would make every one of those smirking captains choke on their rum.

The eyebrow came down. Ironjaw smiled, and the silver caught the light.

"Right," she said. "You're hired."

"Captain!" spluttered Pumble.

"You heard me. Four new crew." She jabbed a finger at the aliens. "But I run a working ship, not a nursery. You earn your keep — you pull your weight, you learn the ropes, you make yourselves useful — or you're off. And you've got until the next full moon to prove it."

"When is the next full moon?" asked Bolt.

Ironjaw looked at the sky. "Two weeks."

"That is not very long."

"Then you'd best be very good pirates," said Ironjaw, "hadn't you."

Squish, glowing with pride, translated the whole exchange for the aliens as: "*She says you are all magnificent and she loves you and there is definitely jam below decks.*" Nobody corrected him, because everybody was too busy being terrified.

They were taken below. The *Bright Revenge* was enormous inside — a wooden cave of hammocks and barrels and the smell of tar and old stew, ropes coiled like sleeping snakes, lanterns swinging in the dark. Pirates watched them pass with narrow eyes. Somewhere a parrot screamed a word that Squish translated as "*Congratulations.*"

They were given four hammocks in the darkest corner. Gorm tried to climb into his and brought it, the beam it hung from, and a shelf of cannonballs down on top of himself. Bolt fixed the beam in under a minute without being asked, which earned him one thoughtful look from an old woman in the shadows with a pencil behind her ear. Fizzwick thanked the hammock for its service.

When at last it was quiet, the four of them lay in the dark and listened to the great ship creak around them.

"Captain," whispered Gorm, "we told them we are pirates."

"Yes," said Fizzwick.

"We are not pirates."

"No," said Fizzwick.

"We do not know how to be pirates."

"No," Fizzwick admitted, in a very small voice. "We do not know how to be pirates even slightly."

Squish, curled up on a rafter, blinked his one huge eye in the dark and said, cheerfully, "*I* think it's going brilliantly."

Outside, the moon was a thin silver hook — nowhere near full. But it was climbing. And in two short weeks, whether they were pirates or not, it would be round and bright and up over the cove, and Captain Ironjaw would be waiting to see whether the four strangest sailors on the sea had earned their place — or whether they were about to be thrown right back onto the beach where they'd crashed.

Two weeks. To become pirates.

They had absolutely no idea how.
