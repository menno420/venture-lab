# Chapter 1 — The Wrong Way Down

The ship came down over Gallows Cove doing roughly nine hundred impossible things at once, most of them on fire.

"Captain," said Bolt, "we are about to become part of the local sea floor."

Captain Fizzwick was not listening, because Captain Fizzwick was reading. He held up his laminated card — the card of Proper Conduct, which had a rule for absolutely everything except the thing that was currently happening — and he read it aloud in the calm, wobbly voice of an alien who is trying very hard not to scream.

"'In the event of catastrophic descent,'" he read, "'remain courteous. Thank your crew for their service.'" He swallowed. All six of his trembling fingers gripped the card. "Thank you all for your service. It has been an honour. You are each a delight."

"We're going to hit the water," said Bolt.

"Then thank you for the warning," said Fizzwick.

The *Wanderling* hit the water.

It skipped twice, like a flat stone thrown by a giant with poor manners. On the first skip, the left wing sheared off with a sound like an enormous sneeze. On the second skip, the right wing followed it. Then the little ship slid — smoking, groaning, and entirely out of wings — straight up a beach that was absolutely, wall-to-wall, elbow-to-elbow stuffed with pirates.

It stopped.

Steam hissed. Somewhere inside the ship, something important went *plink* and rolled away forever.

For one long moment, nobody moved. Fifty pirates stared at the smoking silver egg that had just parked itself on their beach. Fifty mouths hung open. Fifty pairs of eyes went very wide.

Then the pirates began to mutter.

This was the moment Squish chose to be helpful.

Squish was a translator-blob roughly the size of a plum, with one enormous eye and a level of confidence that his size did not remotely justify. He oozed up onto Fizzwick's shoulder, blinked at the crowd, and translated the pirates' astonished muttering in a warm and cheerful voice.

"They say," announced Squish, "*Welcome, honoured guests. Please, help yourselves to our finest jam.*"

"Oh, how lovely," said Fizzwick, brightening. "I *adore* jam."

What the pirates had actually been muttering was closer to, *"What in the name of the deep sea is THAT,"* but Squish had never let a small thing like accuracy get in the way of a nice greeting.

"We should introduce ourselves properly," Fizzwick decided. "Rule Nine: *A good first impression opens a hundred doors.*" He straightened his little jacket. He tucked the card away. He stepped out of the wreck with both hands raised high, and he smiled.

He smiled the biggest, widest, warmest smile he had. It stretched right across his face. It nearly went round the back of his head. It showed every single one of his gleaming teeth.

And nobody — nobody in the entire galaxy — had ever thought to tell the aliens that the correct number of teeth to show a beach full of pirates is *zero*.

Fifty cutlasses came out of fifty scabbards at exactly the same time. The sound they made was one bright, terrible *shiiiing*.

"Interesting," said Bolt, who had no facial expression and did not intend to grow one now.

Behind them, deep inside the wreck, something whimpered. This was Gorm. Gorm was seven feet tall and could lift a mainmast with one hand, and at this precise moment he was folded into the smallest possible ball behind a cargo crate, because one of the pirates had a crab on his hat, and Gorm was, above all things in the universe, terrified of crabs.

"It's only a hat crab," Bolt told him.

"It has *eyes on stalks*," Gorm whispered. "Bolt. It is looking into my soul."

The tallest pirate on the beach stepped forward. She had a wooden leg, a beard full of beads, and a scowl you could sharpen a knife on. She pointed her cutlass at the smiling captain and said something short and unfriendly.

Squish translated at once, beaming. "She says: *We are so pleased you could visit. Would you care to stay for supper?*"

"We would be *honoured*," said Fizzwick, and he bowed. He bowed so low that his forehead nearly touched the sand. It was, according to the card, a perfect bow — deep, respectful, and correct in every particular.

The pirates did not know what to do with a perfect bow. Pirates are not bowed at. Pirates do the frightening; they are not, generally, the ones being politely honoured. Several of them lowered their cutlasses an inch, purely out of confusion.

"See?" said Squish smugly. "Jam. I told you. I am *very* good at my job."

"You are the worst translator in the galaxy," said Bolt.

"I am the *only* translator in the galaxy," said Squish, "which makes me the best. That is just mathematics."

The tall pirate had heard enough. She grabbed Fizzwick by the collar with one meaty fist and lifted him clean off the sand, and Fizzwick — dangling, spinning gently, still smiling — said, "Oh! Are we hugging? How forward. I like it."

"Quiet," growled the pirate, in a word even Squish could not sweeten.

She turned and bellowed up the beach toward the great black shape of a galleon riding at anchor in the bay — a ship with a hull like a thundercloud and a name painted in gold along her side.

"Oi!" she shouted. "We've got four weird ones and a talkin' gumdrop! Take 'em to Ironjaw!"

The name went down the beach like a cold wind. *Ironjaw. Take 'em to Ironjaw.* Even the pirates who had been smirking stopped smirking. Even the crab on the hat seemed to reconsider its choices.

"Who," asked Fizzwick, still dangling, still upside down, still terribly polite, "is Ironjaw? They sound *wonderful*. I should like very much to thank them for having us."

"You won't," muttered the pirate, "be thankin' anyone."

She marched them down the sand, all four of them — jittery Fizzwick and deadpan Bolt and enormous trembling Gorm and Squish riding proudly on a shoulder, translating the seagulls into fond compliments as they went. Behind them the *Wanderling* ticked and cooled and settled deeper into the beach, wingless, wrecked, and going absolutely nowhere.

They had crossed half a galaxy. They had crossed it very badly. And now they were stranded on a strange sea, on an island that belonged entirely to pirates, with no ship, no wings, no way home — and fifty cutlasses walking them toward the meanest name on the whole cove.

"Well," said Bolt, to nobody. "At least it can't get worse."

Squish translated this, helpfully, for the seagulls.

The seagulls laughed.
