# Chapter 10 — The Part They Need

They found it three days later, in the salvage.

The whole cove had been picking over the wreck of the *Wanderling* since the crash — for scrap, for curiosities, for bits of glowing metal to sell — and Bolt had been down on the beach every evening, sorting through what was left of the little ship that had carried them across the galaxy. Mostly he found junk. A bent wing-strut. A cracked window. Squish's old jam jar, still faintly sticky.

And then, on the third evening, under a slab of buckled hull, his four hands closed around something small and heavy and warm, and Bolt went very, very still.

He carried it up the beach without a word and set it down in front of the others, and they gathered round to look, and for a long moment nobody spoke at all.

It was the part. *The* part. The one thing the crash had broken and they had never been able to replace — the humming heart of the *Wanderling*, the piece that made her fly. Bolt turned it over in his hands. It was whole. It was perfect. It worked.

"With this," he said quietly, "I can fix her. The keel. The wings. All of it." He looked up. "In a month, maybe less, the *Wanderling* could fly again."

Nobody said anything.

"We could go home," said Bolt.

*Home.* The word sat there on the sand between them.

The next evening, the four of them stood on the beach beside their crashed ship, and behind them — a little way off, quiet, giving them room — stood the whole crew of the *Bright Revenge*. Ironjaw. Pumble with his ledger. Sal. Old Tessa, her pencil behind her ear, watching Bolt. Pip. Bess the cook. The parrot. All of them, waiting, because word had got round the cove — the glow-crew found the part, the glow-crew could go home now — and nobody wanted to say it out loud, and nobody wanted to leave.

Fizzwick took out his laminated card.

He turned it over in his six trembling fingers. He read down one side, and up the other. Rule after rule after rule — a rule for greetings, a rule for bows, a rule for catastrophic descent, a rule for how many teeth to show and (added recently, in his own careful hand) a rule that the number is zero. There was a rule for everything.

There was no rule for this.

"There's no rule," Fizzwick said softly. He looked up at the others. "I've looked. I've looked at every one. There's no rule that tells you whether to go home." He swallowed. "So I suppose... I suppose we just have to decide. Ourselves. With no card."

He looked at Bolt, who was looking at Old Tessa. He looked at Gorm, who was looking at Bess, and at the sunset just starting to go gold behind her, and already, gently, beginning to cry. He looked at Squish, perched on his shoulder, who for once had gone quiet.

And Fizzwick thought about what *home* meant.

He thought about the planet they'd left, so far away — the place the card came from, the place with the right number of rules and the right number of teeth and no crabs and no jam and nobody, in all his life there, who had ever once voted for him.

Then he thought about a beach full of cutlasses that had lowered, one inch, because he bowed. He thought about a barrel he'd caught, and a boy who believed in blessings, and a war-cry sung wrong, and a fishmonger who saved the best crab. He thought about the vote — *all in favour, say aye* — and the great rolling wall of *aye* that had made him leak. He thought about Bolt and Tessa and their cups of black tea and their whole conversations made of one word each. He thought about being carried home safe on Gorm's shoulders, and Ironjaw's hand on his collar in the dark, saying *our crew, yours and mine both.*

He thought: *Home isn't the place with all the right rules.*

*Home is the place that took us in with all our wrong ones.*

Fizzwick looked down at the laminated card one last time. Then, very carefully, he put it away in his pocket, and he did not take it out again.

"We're staying," he said.

The words came out small, but they carried all the way up the beach, and the whole crew heard them, and for a heartbeat nobody moved.

Then Gorm let out a happy sob you could have heard in the next cove, and swept all three of the others up in his enormous arms, and Squish shouted something joyful and untranslatable, and the pirates on the beach erupted — cheering, whooping, throwing hats in the air, Sal doing a hornpipe, Pip crying "THE DEEP ONES ARE STAYING!", the parrot screaming, Old Tessa allowing herself the smallest smile in the history of small smiles and murmuring, to Bolt, four whole words: "Glad you're stopping, lad."

And Captain Roana "Ironjaw" Vex stood at the back of it all with her arms folded, and her silver jaw caught the last of the golden light, and she did a thing the crew had almost never seen her do.

She smiled. A real one. All the way.

"Well," she said gruffly, to cover it. "Somebody's got to shout the war-cry wrong."

They did not scrap the *Wanderling*. Bolt would not have it, and neither would anyone else. Instead they hauled her up above the tide-line, at the top of the beach where the grass began, and they covered her with a good stout tarp and pegged it down tight, and there she sat — whole, and mended, and full of fuel, wingless no longer.

Not gone. Not forgotten.

A promise. A ship that could fly, whenever they chose, kept safe for the day they might want the sky again.

That night there was a feast, the biggest the cove had ever seen, and somewhere near the end of it, when the bonfire had burned low and Gorm was asleep sitting up and Bolt and Tessa were drinking tea in the dark, Fizzwick wandered up to the captain's cabin to thank Ironjaw, properly, for everything — and he stopped in the doorway.

On the wall above her desk, Ironjaw had pinned a chart. It was old and cracked and stained with salt, and it showed the sea north of Gallows Cove — up past the last islands, up where the blue faded to white, to a place marked in faded ink with a shiver of tiny drawn icicles and two words: *THE FROZEN STRAITS.*

"Been meaning to sail up there my whole life," Ironjaw said, not turning round. "Never had a ship that could take me. Never had a crew mad enough to try." She glanced back at him, and her eyebrow rose, and the silver flashed. "Reckon I've got both now."

Fizzwick's heart lifted like a sail full of wind.

"Captain," he said warmly, "we would be *honoured*." And he bowed — low, and perfect, and entirely correct — because some rules, the good ones, you keep.

Squish, half-asleep on his shoulder, cracked open his one enormous eye, took in the frozen chart and the smiling captain and the whole warm glowing room, and translated the moment, one last time, for a crew that no longer needed translating.

"*She says,*" murmured Squish happily, "*welcome home, honoured guests. Please — help yourselves to our finest jam.*"

And for once — the very first time, on the very last night — Squish had got it exactly, perfectly right.
