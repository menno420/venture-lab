# Chapter 4 — The Night They Fixed the Ship

They waited until the ship was asleep.

It took a long time. Pirates do not fall asleep tidily; they fall asleep in stages, with a great deal of snoring, and one of them (the cook) talked to his stew in his dreams. But at last the *Bright Revenge* went quiet, rocking gently at anchor, and four small shadows slid out of their hammocks and crept down into the dark belly of the ship.

"Rule Twenty-Two," whispered Fizzwick, holding a tiny glowing light in his six fingers. "*When you cannot repay a kindness with words, repay it with work.*" He looked round at his crew. "They took us in. We're going to earn it. We're going to make this the finest ship on the sea — and we're going to do it so quietly that nobody ever knows it was us."

"Why can nobody know?" asked Gorm.

"Because," said Bolt, "the last time a pirate saw one of us up close, fifty of them drew swords. They do not trust things that glow. So we hide the glow."

They got to work.

Bolt led. Of course Bolt led — this was Bolt's whole reason for existing. He had spent all evening drawing plans in his head, and now his four arms moved at once, quick and sure and silent. From a satchel of salvaged parts — bits rescued from the wreck of the *Wanderling*, carried up the beach a pocketful at a time — he began to feed alien cleverness into the old wooden ship, gently, the way you'd tuck a warm blanket over someone sleeping.

He worked a smoothed, humming plate along the length of the keel — the great beam that runs like a spine under the whole hull — so that it would slice the water instead of shoving it. He re-threaded the rigging with a thread finer than hair and stronger than chain, angled just so, so that the sails would catch a wind that wasn't there and pull the ship along on nothing but a clear day's breath. And down in the dark, he hung, from a hook by the wheel, a small round device that shed a soft blue light — a device that was quite clearly, absolutely, unmistakably *not a lantern*.

"That is a lantern," whispered Gorm.

"It is not a lantern," said Bolt.

"It looks exactly like a lantern."

"That," said Bolt, "is the point."

Gorm's job was to lift things, which he did beautifully — raising a whole section of deck-planking so Bolt could reach beneath, holding it up for an hour without a wobble, weeping only slightly when he thought about how nice everyone was being. Squish's job was to keep watch. Fizzwick's job was to hand Bolt tools and thank each one for its service, which Bolt tolerated with the patience of a very flat rock.

It was going wonderfully.

And then the lookout came down.

His name was Pip — a thin, jumpy young pirate who believed in every sea-ghost, curse, and haunted fish anyone had ever invented, and who had come below for a snack. He rounded the corner, and he saw, in the dark, a soft blue glow, and four strange shapes bent over it, and something humming under the floor.

Pip's eyes went round as plates.

"SEA-GHOST!" he shrieked. "SEA-GHOST BELOW DECKS! THE SHIP'S HAUNTED! WE'RE ALL DOOMED! DOOMED, I SAY, DOOO—"

"Squish!" hissed Fizzwick. "Do something!"

Squish oozed forward into the blue light, blinked his one enormous eye at the terrified boy, and did the only thing Squish knew how to do. He translated.

The trouble was, there was nothing to translate — Fizzwick had only whispered "help" — so Squish, brimming with confidence and no facts whatsoever, simply announced, in his warmest and most soothing voice:

"*Do not be afraid, good sailor. We are the friendly Deep Ones, and we have come to bless this vessel with soft light and swift waters, and to leave a jam tart under your pillow as thanks for your excellent hospitality.*"

Pip stopped screaming.

"A... jam tart?" he said.

"*The finest jam tart,*" Squish confirmed, with total authority.

Now, Pip had believed in sea-ghosts his whole life, and had always secretly hoped that when he finally met one, it would turn out to be *nice*. And here at last was a glowing thing in the dark that was not roaring or rattling chains, but promising him baked goods and calling him an excellent host. It was, frankly, the best supernatural experience of his life.

"Right," said Pip, deeply moved. "Right. Well. Thank you, Deep Ones. I'll — I'll not tell a soul. Wouldn't want to break the blessing." He backed away, bowing, thrilled to his boots, and crept back up to the crow's nest to lie awake all night in happy terror.

The aliens breathed again.

"A jam tart," said Bolt.

"I panicked," said Squish, not sounding remotely panicked. "It worked, didn't it? He loves us now. That's *two* friends I've made by getting it wrong."

"We are going to owe that boy an actual jam tart," said Fizzwick.

"Then we shall get him one," said Fizzwick, immediately, because a promise was a promise even if you hadn't technically made it.

They worked until the sky in the porthole turned the grey of early morning. Bolt fitted the last piece, sealed the humming keel behind a false board, and hung a real, ordinary, boring lantern right next to the one that was not a lantern, so the eye would slide past it. They swept the sawdust. They hid the glow. They wiped away every trace. By the time the first pirate stirred in his hammock, the belly of the ship looked exactly as it always had — old, wooden, and utterly ordinary.

Nobody would ever know.

Four exhausted aliens climbed back into their hammocks as the ship's bell rang for dawn, and Gorm was asleep before he stopped swinging.

Up on deck, boots began to thump. A rooster somewhere on the island crowed. And Sal's voice came booming down the hatch, bright and rough and pleased.

"Up, ye lazy barnacles! On yer feet! It's race day!" A cheer went up across the ship. "The Gallows Cove Harbour Race, and the *Bright Revenge* sails at noon — so shake a leg, and let's show this whole rotten cove what our old girl can do!"

Down in the dark, four aliens lay very, very still in their hammocks.

Their old girl, as it happened, could now do quite a lot.
