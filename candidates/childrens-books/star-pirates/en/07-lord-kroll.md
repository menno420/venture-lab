# Chapter 7 — Lord Kroll

The black ship followed them all the way back to Gallows Cove, and dropped anchor in the bay bold as brass, and by noon Lord Barnabus Kroll had rowed ashore to pay a visit.

He came up the beach slowly, in no hurry at all, because men like Kroll want you to watch them coming. He was tall and pale, with a smile like a crack in ice, and he wore a long black coat with so many buttons — brass buttons, silver buttons, gold buttons, buttons going all the way up to his chin and down each sleeve — that Gorm, peering out from behind Fizzwick, whispered, "Why does he have so *many*? How does he get *out* of it?"

"Slowly," murmured Bolt, "and with help, I imagine."

Kroll stopped in the middle of the beach, in front of the whole crew, and spread his gloved hands wide.

"Captain Vex," he said. "Roana. What a delight."

"Kroll," said Ironjaw. She had planted herself between the alien crew and the rest of the beach, arms folded, jaw flashing. "You're a long way from your own waters."

"I go where the *interesting* things are," said Kroll, and his cold eyes drifted past her, over her shoulder, and settled on the four aliens. He looked at them the way the fishmonger looked at fish. "And you, Roana, have collected the most interesting things I've heard of in twenty years. A ship that flies. A crew that glows." His smile widened. "Word travels, on the sea. It's traveled all the way to me."

"They're my crew," said Ironjaw. "Not things."

"Everything's a thing," said Kroll pleasantly, "if the price is right." He drew out a heavy purse and let it swing, chinking, from one finger. "So here is my price. I'll take the little clever ones off your hands — all four, and their glowing toys — and I'll leave you enough gold to buy three ships better than that brown wreck you sail. A generous offer. A *kind* offer." He tossed the purse; it landed at Ironjaw's feet with a fat, expensive sound. "Sell them to me."

Squish, riding on Fizzwick's shoulder, translated all this for the aliens with his usual sunny confidence — right up until the word *sell*, at which point even Squish faltered, and for once, translated it exactly right.

The aliens went very still.

Ironjaw looked down at the purse at her feet. It was a great deal of gold. It was more gold than the *Bright Revenge* had made in three years. She looked at it for a long moment.

Then she kicked it back down the beach, so hard the coins burst out and scattered in the sand.

"They're not for sale," she said. "Not for that. Not for anything. Now get off my cove."

Something changed in Kroll's face. The smile stayed, but the ice behind it hardened, and when he spoke again his voice had dropped all its silk.

"You misunderstand me, Roana," he said quietly. "That was the polite version. That was me *asking*." He turned and looked out at his black ship, riding low and heavy in the bay, and the aliens saw now that it was not alone — behind it, coming round the headland one after another, were more black sails, a whole dark fleet of them, sliding into the mouth of the cove. "I have wanted a ship that skims the waves my whole life. I have a fleet that could rule this sea — if I could only make it *fast*. And now I know where the secret lives." His pale eyes came back to the aliens, and lingered. "I always get what I want. I will take those four, and I will take their glowing tricks, and I will bolt them to every hull in my fleet — whether you sell them to me or not." He gave a small, dreadful bow. "Sleep well, Captain."

And he walked back down the beach, unhurried, buttons glinting, and rowed away.

That night, the *Bright Revenge* was quiet and grim. Ironjaw doubled the watch. The crew sharpened their cutlasses and spoke in low voices. And Fizzwick sat alone at the very tip of the bowsprit, out over the black water, holding his laminated card in his six trembling fingers, and did not read it.

Bolt found him there.

"It's my fault," said Fizzwick, not turning round. "All of it. I told them we were pirates. I let Bolt fix the ship. I let it fly. I let everyone *see*." His voice was very small. "That man is going to hurt this whole crew — Sal, and Pip, and the cook who sat with Gorm — and he's going to hurt them because of *us*. Because we glow. Because we came here."

Bolt was silent for a moment, which for Bolt was normal, but this was a different kind of silence.

"Rule Thirty-One," Fizzwick whispered. "*A good guest never brings trouble to the house that shelters him.*" He finally turned, and his enormous eyes were wet. "There's only one way to keep them safe, Bolt. If Kroll wants *us* — then we take that away from him. We slip off tonight. We go and give ourselves up, or we go and hide far away, but either way we *leave*, and we take the danger with us, and the crew is safe." He swallowed hard. "It's the polite thing. It's the *right* thing. It's the only rule that fits."

"They voted for us to stay," said Bolt.

"I know," said Fizzwick, and a tear ran down and fell into the sea. "That's what makes it so hard."

Bolt looked at him for a long time. Then he said, quietly, "Talk to me before you do anything. Promise me that. Just — talk to me first."

Fizzwick did not answer.

He sat out on the bowsprit long after Bolt had gone below, wrestling with the biggest, loneliest decision of his whole small life, while the dark fleet sat waiting on the rim of the bay and the moon climbed toward full.

And so it was that Fizzwick — jittery, over-polite, tearful Fizzwick — was the only one still awake, out at the very front of the ship, when he heard, far too late, the soft dip of muffled oars in the dark, and a rope creak against the hull, and a boot land silent as a cat on the deck behind him.

Kroll was not the kind of man who waited to be told no twice.

He had come that very night to take what he wanted — and he had not come to ask.
