# Chapter 8 — Stolen

Fizzwick opened his mouth to shout, and a rough hand clapped over it.

"Not a peep, little glow-worm," breathed a voice in his ear.

The deck was suddenly full of shadows — Kroll's men, a dozen of them, come up the black side of the ship on silent ropes while the whole cove slept. They moved fast and low. They knew exactly what they had come for.

Fizzwick bit the hand. He bit it as hard as a small polite creature can, and the man yelped and let go, and Fizzwick filled his lungs and screamed the only alarm he could think of:

"CREW! WAKE UP! WE ARE BEING ROBBED AND IT IS TERRIBLY RUDE!"

The ship came awake all at once. Lanterns flared. Pirates spilled up from below in their nightshirts with their cutlasses, and Ironjaw was among them roaring, and for a few wild minutes the deck was pure chaos — shouting, clanging, bodies tumbling in the dark, the parrot shrieking, Sal swinging a frying pan she'd grabbed instead of her sword.

But Kroll's men had not come to fight. They had come to *grab*.

In the dark and the crush, two of them got what they were after. One threw a weighted net over Squish, who was too small and too slow to ooze away in time, and scooped the wriggling, furious blob into a sack. And four of them together rushed Gorm — gentle Gorm, who could have flung them all into the sea, who was strong enough to snap their ropes like threads — but who froze, in the very moment it mattered, because one of the men, clever and cruel, had brought a crab in a jar and thrust it right up in front of Gorm's face.

Gorm saw the crab.

Gorm made his terrible sound, and he curled up, enormous and helpless, hiding his eyes — and while he was curled and shaking they got the chains on him, six loops of heavy iron, and it took all of them straining together to drag him, still whimpering, to the rail and down into the boat.

"GORM!" bellowed Fizzwick. "SQUISH! NO — take *me*, take me instead, I'm the captain, it's the polite thing, take *ME* —"

But they were already gone, over the side and away, oars flashing, the black rowboat cutting for the black ship with two of the crew — the biggest and the smallest, the gentlest and the loudest — stolen away into the dark.

And from the water, faint and fading, came Squish's voice, still translating, still absolutely certain, cheerfully informing his kidnappers: "*He says: What a lovely evening for a boat ride! You all have such kind faces!*"

Then the fog swallowed them, and they were gone.

The deck of the *Bright Revenge* fell silent. Kroll's other men had slipped away in the confusion. The pirates stood in the wreckage of the fight, breathing hard, and Fizzwick sank to his knees on the boards where Gorm had been, staring at a single broken link of iron chain, and could not speak.

It was Ironjaw who broke the quiet. She strode across the deck, her nightshirt flapping, her silver jaw set like a trap, and she hauled Fizzwick up by the collar the exact way Sal had on the very first day — except this time it was not rough. This time it was almost gentle.

"Up," she said. "On your feet. We don't sit on the deck and cry when they take our crew. Do you hear me? *Our* crew. Yours and mine both."

"He wants to bolt them to his fleet," Fizzwick whispered. "It's my fault. I was going to leave tonight — to protect everyone — and I was too slow, and now —"

"Leave?" Ironjaw's eyebrow shot up. "You were going to *leave*?" She shook her head once, hard. "There'll be time to be a fool later. Right now you're going to help me get them back." She turned and raised her voice to the whole shocked crew. "LISTEN UP! Kroll's taken two of ours, and he's going to be very sorry he did! We sail at first light — we sail after that black-hearted, over-buttoned barnacle, and we bring our people HOME!"

The crew roared.

And in the middle of the roar, Bolt appeared at Fizzwick's side, silent as ever, and behind him came Old Tessa with her pencil and a roll of drawings under her arm.

"We have until dawn," said Bolt. "That is enough."

"Enough for *what*?" said Fizzwick.

Bolt looked at him with his flat, unreadable face, and for once there was something underneath it — something hard and bright and absolutely certain.

"You built the fastest ship on the sea by accident," said Bolt. "Now Tessa and I are going to finish it on purpose."

He unrolled the drawings across a barrel-top. Fizzwick looked down at them and his six fingers went to his mouth. They were plans — Bolt's plans, drawn in the night — page after page of strange and wonderful devices, humming and glowing and utterly harmless, each one labelled in Bolt's neat hand and Tessa's stubby pencil. A net that bloomed. A shield shaped like a figurehead. A dozen clever, gentle, glowing tricks.

"None of these hurt anyone," Bolt said. "That was the rule. Fizzwick's rule. Nobody gets hurt — not even Kroll." He picked up a tool. "But by the time the sun comes up, that black fleet is going to wish it had never heard of a ship that glows."

Down below, the forge came alight. Hammers began to ring. Old Tessa rolled up her sleeves. Gorm's empty chains lay on the deck, and out past the headland the dark fleet waited with two frightened friends aboard — but the *Bright Revenge* was not sleeping now.

The *Bright Revenge* was getting ready.

And at the first grey hint of dawn, with every device bolted and humming and hidden, Ironjaw stood at the wheel, and the sails filled with a wind that wasn't there, and the old brown ship rose up onto the water and turned her nose toward the black horizon.

"After them," said Ironjaw softly.

The chase was on.
