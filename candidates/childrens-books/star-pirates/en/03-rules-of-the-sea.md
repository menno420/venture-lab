# Chapter 3 — Rules of the Sea

Being a pirate, it turned out, had rules. This delighted Fizzwick enormously.

"Rules!" he said, at dawn, clutching his laminated card. "I *love* rules. Rules are my favourite. Give me a rule and I will polish it until it shines."

The trouble was that the pirates' rules and Fizzwick's card did not agree about anything at all.

Take the war-cry. Every pirate crew, Pumble explained wearily, had a war-cry — a great roaring shout you bellowed when you swung into battle, to strike fear into your enemies. The *Bright Revenge*'s war-cry was "*NO MERCY!*"

Fizzwick was appalled.

"No *mercy*?" he said. "But — Rule Four clearly states, *Always offer your guest a comfortable chair.* We can't shout NO MERCY. It's terribly rude. What if they've had a long day?"

"They're the *enemy*, ye great custard," said the beaded pirate, whose name was Sal.

"All the more reason to be gracious," said Fizzwick firmly. "First impressions, Rule Nine."

So when the crew ran their morning battle drill — swinging on ropes, waving cutlasses, roaring "*NO MERCY!*" across the deck — four voices came in a beat late and completely wrong. Bolt said nothing. Gorm said "sorry" to the mast he bumped. And Fizzwick and Squish, swinging past on a rope with tremendous dignity, bellowed together at the top of their lungs:

"*NO MERCY — BUT PLEASE, TAKE A SEAT!*"

The whole drill fell apart. Sal laughed so hard she sat down on a bucket. Even the parrot looked embarrassed.

"That," said Pumble, writing it down, "is the worst war-cry in the history of the sea."

Meanwhile, Gorm was having his own morning.

Gorm had been sent to help haul the anchor, which he did easily, one-handed, without noticing he was doing it. The pirates were quietly impressed. Gorm was strong the way a mountain is strong — gently, and without showing off. Then a crab came sideways along the rail.

Gorm saw the crab.

The crab saw Gorm.

Gorm made a sound no seven-foot creature should be able to make and went straight up the mainmast like a startled cat, and stayed there, wrapped around the crow's nest, whimpering, for the better part of an hour, while the crab wandered off looking pleased with itself. It took Fizzwick, Squish, and a bribe of stew to get him down.

"It was *this big*," Gorm insisted, holding his enormous hands the width of a coin.

"It was smaller than a button," said Bolt.

"It had *ambition*," said Gorm.

But it was down in the belly of the ship, among the sawdust and the wood, that the best thing happened all day — and nobody shouted about it, because it happened in complete silence.

Bolt had wandered off to look at the hull. He liked the hull. It was made of oak, cut and fitted and pegged together by hand, and Bolt — who had four arms and the soul of an engineer — thought it was one of the finest pieces of work he had ever seen. He crouched by a half-finished repair where two planks met, and he ran a finger along the join, and he made a small approving click.

A voice behind him said four words.

"Cut it yourself, did ye."

Bolt turned. An old woman stood there, small and weathered, with a pencil behind one ear and hands like tree roots. This was Old Tessa, the ship's carpenter, who had not, as far as anyone could remember, ever said five words in a row.

Bolt looked at the join. Then he took out a small silver tool, and — without a word — he shaved the edge of a rough plank so smoothly and so true that it slotted into its neighbour with a soft, perfect *thock*, tight as a secret.

Old Tessa looked at it for a long moment.

Then she nodded, once.

Bolt nodded back.

And that was it. That was the entire conversation. Two people who did not like talking had just become the best of friends, over nothing but a well-joined plank, and they would go on being the best of friends for years, mostly by standing near each other and saying nothing at all.

By evening, though, the good feeling had worn thin. Because up on deck, a barrel of fresh water had come loose in the swell and was rolling toward the open hatch, gathering speed, about to crash down into the hold and smash everything below — and the pirates were all at the far end, and nobody could reach it in time.

Nobody except Fizzwick, who happened to be standing right there, holding his card, being useless.

He did not think. There was no rule for this, and so for once he did not look for one. He just threw himself flat in front of the barrel, jammed his little foot against a cleat, braced his six trembling fingers, and *held* — and the barrel struck him, and slowed, and stopped, teetering on the very lip of the hatch, one push from disaster.

Bolt reached past him and lashed it down.

For a moment, the deck was silent.

Then Sal shrugged. "Lucky," she said, and went back to her rope.

That was all. No cheer. No thanks. Fizzwick sat up, dusty and shaking, and watched the crew turn away, and something in his chest went small and quiet.

Later, at the rail, Pumble found him. The quartermaster wasn't a cruel man — just a tired and honest one, and he thought the alien deserved the truth.

"Listen," Pumble said, not unkindly. "You held a barrel. Good. But holdin' a barrel don't make a pirate. This crew's been sailin' together twenty years. They don't need four strangers who shout the war-cry wrong and climb the mast from a crab." He sighed. "You'll never earn your keep by the full moon. Not like this. Not by bein' *nice*." He shook his head and turned to go. "You'd have to do somethin' this ship's never seen. And this ship," he said, "has seen everything."

He went below.

Fizzwick stood alone at the rail with his laminated card and looked out at the dark water, and then up at the moon — fatter tonight, curved and climbing — and he thought about *something this ship has never seen*.

Slowly, very slowly, he turned around.

Below decks, in the lantern-light, Bolt was running one careful hand along the smooth oak hull, and his four arms were very still, and behind his blank, expressionless face, something was quietly, secretly beginning to think.
