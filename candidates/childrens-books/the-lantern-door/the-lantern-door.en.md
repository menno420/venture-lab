# The Lantern Door

- **Title:** The Lantern Door
- **Language:** English (master — voice source of truth)
- **Age band:** read-aloud picture book, ages 4–8
- **Word count:** 621 (manuscript body, excluding spread/illustration notes)
- **Pitch:** When the one lantern that keeps a teacup-sized town warm goes dark, a girl slips through a palm-sized door and climbs a tiny tower to relight it — home by bedtime, with proof in her pocket.

> Series: *The Small Doors* — fantasy adventure, stand-alone. Every spread carries an illustration note in italics; the seat generates no art.

---

[Spread 1]

*Illustration: A tall grey lighthouse at dusk; a small girl in a yellow coat crouches at its base, fingers on a butter-yellow door low in the mossy stone.*

At the bottom of the old lighthouse, where the stones were cold and green with moss, Juno found a door. It was no taller than her hand, painted the colour of butter, with a knob the size of a peppercorn and a keyhole shaped like a star.

[Spread 2]

*Illustration: The tiny door swings wide; a wedge of warm gold light spills onto Juno's knees and the wet grass, and she leans in close.*

She knocked, because knocking felt polite, and the little door swung wide. Out poured light — warm and gold, like the inside of a lantern — and it smelled of toast and candlewax. Juno pressed one eye to the gap and gasped.

[Spread 3]

*Illustration: Through the doorway, a whole miniature town the size of a teacup — crooked roofs, cobbled lanes, coin-sized bridges, and a dark tower in the middle.*

Beyond the door lay a whole town, no bigger than a teacup. Crooked roofs. Little cobbled lanes, and bridges you could hide under a coin. In the middle stood a tall tower where a great lantern should have burned — but it stood dark and quiet and cold.

[Spread 4]

*Illustration: Juno steps through and shrinks; now tiny, she meets Wick, a small lamplighter with a soot-smudged nose holding an empty lamp on a pole taller than himself.*

Juno stepped through, and the world went enormous — or she went small. Now she was town-sized too. A boy with a soot-smudged nose ran up, carrying a lamp-pole taller than himself. "You're big!" he said. "I mean — you were. I'm Wick."

[Spread 5]

*Illustration: Townsfolk bundle in scarves and peer about in the dimness; a cat the size of a thimble winds around Juno's ankle.*

"The Great Lantern went dark last night," Wick said. "It keeps our whole town warm. Without it, folks lose their way and their toes go cold." All around, small people hugged their scarves, and a cat no bigger than a thimble mewed at Juno's ankle.

[Spread 6]

*Illustration: Wick counts on his fingers before a chalk sketch of the tower, with three little icons: a spark, a thread, a curl of wind.*

"To wake it," said Wick, "we need three small things: a spark to start it, a thread to hold the flame, and a breath of wind to make it dance. And someone brave to carry them up the tower." Juno stood as tall as she could. "That's me."

[Spread 7]

*Illustration: In a moonlit moss meadow that glitters like scattered stars, Juno cups her hands around a sleepy, glowing beetle.*

First, the spark. In the moss meadow, which sparkled like a spilled sky, slept the glow-beetles. Juno crept close and cupped her hands — soft, soft — around the sleepiest one. It blinked awake and glowed like an ember in her palms. "One," she whispered.

[Spread 8]

*Illustration: Under a little stone bridge, a friendly spider spins a silver thread into Juno's outstretched fingers.*

Next, the thread. Under the stone bridge lived a spider who spun silver from her toes. Juno asked her kindly, and the spider spun a thread so fine it hummed. Juno wound it round her wrist like a bracelet. "Two."

[Spread 9]

*Illustration: On a windy hilltop, Juno traps a gust inside a curled seashell and corks it with a folded leaf, her hair streaming.*

Last, the wind. Juno climbed the little hill where the breezes played, held up a curled seashell, and caught a gust inside. The wind tugged her hair and laughed as she stoppered the shell with a folded leaf. "Three! Now — the tower."

[Spread 10]

*Illustration: Juno and Wick at the foot of the dark tower, craning up at a spiral stair winding into shadow, each step tall as her knee.*

The tower had seemed small from the door. Now it rose and rose, and each step was as tall as her knee. Up wound a staircase into the dark. "I'll be right behind you," said Wick, lifting his lamp-pole. Round and round they climbed.

[Spread 11]

*Illustration: Midway up, a step crumbles; Juno grips the rail, Wick steadies her elbow, both wide-eyed but climbing on.*

Halfway up, a step went crumble beneath her foot. Juno's heart jumped — but she held the rail, and Wick caught her elbow. "Steady," he whispered. She breathed. She climbed on. Brave, she decided, is just scared, still walking.

[Spread 12]

*Illustration: At the top, Juno lays the silver thread, touches the beetle-spark to it, and opens the shell; the Great Lantern blazes gold.*

At the top waited the Great Lantern, dark as a cold kettle. Juno laid the silver thread along the wick. She touched the glowing beetle to it — a spark! Then she opened the shell, and the wind breathed out, and the flame leapt up gold and grand.

[Spread 13]

*Illustration: Light pours from the tower over every rooftop; tiny townsfolk cheer with pink cheeks, and Wick spins in a happy circle.*

Light spilled over the town, warm and gold, into every window and lane. Somewhere a tiny band began to play. The small people cheered and found their way home, cheeks pink again, and Wick spun round in a happy circle. "You did it! You woke it up!"

[Spread 14]

*Illustration: Wick gives Juno a berry-sized glowing lantern; back outside, big again in the dusk, she holds it as the little door clicks shut.*

Wick pressed something into her hand — a lantern the size of a berry, warm and glowing. "So you'll remember." A far bell chimed: bedtime. Juno stepped back through the little door and grew, and grew, until the lighthouse was tall again. In her pocket, something small stayed warm. It had all been real.

*The end.*
