# Chapter 11 — Sending

The tide came in the way Fa‘amanu used to say the good ones came — without hurry, without asking, filling the reef flat until the whole atoll seemed to lift a little off the sea floor. Tuli had chosen this tide months ago, on paper, in the language of ephemerides and lunar tables. Now she stood on the coral apron in front of the control shed and watched it arrive in the language it had always spoken, and understood that the two languages had never been different. She had only, for most of her life, known one of them.

They had waited for the season. She had insisted on it against the advice of nearly everyone with a clipboard, because a millisecond pulsar did not care what month it was on Vaituga and a transmitter would fire on any night at all. But the message did not go out of a transmitter. It went out of a people. And a people had to be gathered.

So they had gathered.

They came along the causeway on foot as the sun went orange over the lagoon, the whole village and half the ones who had left it, in white and in the deep reds of the older cloth, carrying mats and food and small sleeping children over their shoulders. The Fenua array stood above them on its low rise, eight dishes pale against the darkening sky, and Tuli watched the people flow up toward the ears the way water flows up a beach, and she pressed her hand flat against her sternum where the trouble lived, and she breathed carefully, and she stayed on her feet.

Marcus found her there. He did not ask how she was feeling. He had stopped asking weeks ago, which was its own kind of tenderness, and instead he put his shoulder near hers so that if she leaned there would be something to lean on.

"Ropati's ready," he said. "Lani's been checking the lock on the pulsar for an hour. She won't let anyone else touch it."

"Good," Tuli said. "She shouldn't."

"You could sit for the walk up."

"I'll sit," she said, "when I've earned it."

He almost smiled. The light was nearly gone from the west and the first hard stars had come out over the seaward reef, and out there, eight hundred light-years off, a dead star the size of this atoll turned nine hundred times a second and had been turning, and speaking, since before there were islands here with names.

---

They had built the ceremony together, she and Ropati, over many evenings, arguing gently about order. He had wanted the gafa recited first, the whole descent of names from the founding canoe down through the generations to the living children asleep on the mats tonight, before anything electrical happened at all. She had wanted the science explained, so the young ones would know what their hands were being laid to. In the end they had done what the island always did with two true things that would not fit in one place — they had braided them.

Ropati stood at the base of the largest dish. He was very old now, older than she would ever be, his voice worn down to something like the sound the tide made on the far reef, and when he lifted his orator's staff the crowd went quiet in a wave that started at the front and reached the causeway. He did not use the loudspeaker they had set up for him. He never had. His voice found the back of the crowd the way it had always found it, carried on nothing but the shape of the words.

He spoke the fa‘alupega first, the courteous address, the ceremonial greeting of the place and its titles, and Tuli felt the old formality settle over the gathering like cool air off the water. Then he began the gafa. She had heard it many times now and still it moved her — not the meaning of each name, most of which she would never fully hold, but the sheer unbroken reaching of it, generation gripping generation by the wrist across the dark, hand over hand over hand back to a canoe and a star and a first landfall on an empty shore. Nobody had written most of it down until this century. It had lived in mouths. It had crossed nine hundred years on breath alone and lost nothing that mattered.

That was the thing she had come all this way, all this life, to understand. She had spent forty years listening for intelligence in the sky, sure it would announce itself in mathematics, in prime numbers, in the clean cold universals. And the universe had finally spoken to her, and what it had asked was not a sum. It had asked: is anyone there continuous enough to answer? And the only technology on her whole planet that could honestly say yes was standing in front of her in a worn red lavalava, reciting names into the dark from memory.

When Ropati reached the end of the descent, he did not stop where the gafa had always stopped. He paused. The pause was deliberate; she saw him take a breath and hold the crowd in it. And then he spoke the new verse.

It was short. They had made it short on purpose, because it had to be learnable, had to be sayable by a tired child at the end of a long recitation for the next hundred generations without error. It named the ears. It named the star that had spoken first. It named the question. And it named the answer the island had chosen to make with its own continuity — that yes, someone was here, and would keep being here, and had begun to say so. Ropati folded Tuli's forty years of decoding, and the ten-thousand-year reply, and the transmission schedule that reached out past every person now living, into nine lines of chant that a six-year-old could carry.

She heard her own name pass through it. Teuila, who listened. And near it, held in the same breath, Fa‘amanu, who taught her to. Her grandmother had been dead for thirty years and was standing, now, in the mouth of an old man, in a verse that would outlast the shed and the dishes and possibly the atoll itself. That was what the gafa did. That was what it had always been for. It was a machine for keeping the dead speaking, and she had crossed the whole sky to find it in her own family's throat.

She was crying, quietly, without covering her face. Marcus's hand was on her back. She let it stay.

---

Then it was the science's turn, and the science's turn was Lani.

The girl came out of the control shed carrying the logbook against her chest — the real one, the leather-bound one Tuli had started keeping by hand once she understood what they were doing, because a message meant to cross ten thousand years should begin in something a hand could hold. Lani was nineteen and she walked to the front of the crowd like she was carrying water she was afraid to spill, and she set the book on the folding table under the work light, and she looked out at the whole island watching her, and she looked, last, at Tuli.

"Explain it," Tuli said, gently, only to her. "The way I taught you. They should know what we're doing before we do it."

Lani turned to the crowd. Her voice shook at the start and then it stopped shaking, because she knew this cold, because Tuli had made her know it cold.

She told them about the Metronome — the little dead star that flashed nine hundred times a second, so steady you could set the world's clocks by it, and steadier still underneath, a slower rhythm folded into the flashing, one small change every hundred and twelve years or so. She told them it had taken Tuli years to understand that the slow rhythm was not noise. It was language. It was a message so patient that a single one of its sounds took longer to say than the whole of the gafa Ropati had just recited. She told them the message was a question, and that whoever had sent it — the Slow Ones, Tuli called them, the ones who thought in centuries the way people thought in breaths — had been asking it for ten thousand years, long before the founding canoe, and were still asking it now.

"They want to know if anyone will still be here to hear the end of the sentence," Lani said. "That's all. That's the whole question. Is anyone there long enough to answer."

She put her hand flat on the logbook.

"So we're going to answer. Not tonight. Tonight is only the first sound of it. The answer takes longer than any of us. It takes longer than our grandchildren. We started learning how to hold something that long a thousand years ago, when the first of us crossed the sea and kept the names. We already know how to do this. We're the only ones who know how to do this. Tonight we just point it at the sky."

Somewhere back in the crowd, a child asked its mother a question in a loud whisper, and there was soft laughter, and it broke nothing.

Ropati came forward then. He laid his hand on the transmitter housing — the squat grey mass of it, the thing that would push the island's first word out across eight hundred light-years — and he blessed it. He did not bless it as a machine. He blessed it the way he would bless a canoe before a long crossing, as a thing entrusted with something precious that it must carry faithfully to a shore it would never see. He asked the ancestors to keep the va, the space between, clean and true. He asked that the word go out whole. He asked, last, that those who sent it and those who received it be bound now into one genealogy, one long unbroken reaching-across, and Tuli thought: yes. That is exactly what a message is. A hand held out across a gap too big for one life. She had spent her career calling it a signal. It had always been a hand.

---

The send itself took almost no time at all, which she had known it would, and which still undid her.

Lani sat at the console. Tuli stood behind her with one hand on the back of the chair. The lock on the pulsar was green and holding, the phase model was good, the antenna was on target, and the first phoneme of the reply — the first slow sound of a word ten thousand years long — was queued and waiting.

"You do it," Tuli said.

Lani looked up. "It should be you. All of it was you."

"No." Tuli's voice was steady, which cost her something. "If it's me, it's a thing that ended with me. It has to be a thing that started with you. That's the whole answer, Lani. Not the word. The handing of it. Send it."

The girl turned back to the console. The crowd had gone silent again, the real silence this time, the held one. Out on the reef the tide had reached its full and stood, for the slack moment, neither coming nor going.

Lani pressed her hand to the key.

There was no sound. There was never going to be a sound. Somewhere above them the largest dish held dead still on its bearing and a modulated whisper of power left the feed and climbed out through the warm night air and the thin cold air above it and then the air ran out and there was only the going, the light of it already old the instant it left, already on its way to a place none of them would live to point a telescope at again. The console logged the timestamp. Lani wrote it into the leather book by hand, in ink, the first line of the reply, in a nineteen-year-old's careful script.

That was all. The first sound of the answer was said.

Nobody cheered. Ropati had told them not to and they would not have anyway. Instead, softly, starting again at the front, the island began to sing the new verse — the nine lines, the ears and the star and the question and the yes — and it moved back through the crowd to the causeway, and the children who had been taught it that week sang it loudest because they were proud of knowing it, and Tuli stood in the middle of the sound of her own name being folded into a chant that would still be sung when the language it was sung in had changed past her recognizing.

She would never hear the answer to the answer. She had done the arithmetic more times than she could count and it always came out the same and gentle: eight hundred years out, eight hundred back, and that was if they replied the instant they heard, which they would not, because they were slow, because slowness was the whole of what they were. Sixteen hundred years, at least, before any word came back the other way. She would be gone in a season. She would be a name in the verse before the reply had cleared the edge of the solar system.

And this was not a tragedy. This was the point. She understood it now in her body and not only in her head. A meaning you could live to see the end of was too small to be worth sending to the Slow Ones. The only answer large enough for them was one no single person could contain — one that had to be carried, hand over hand, name over name, past death after death after death, and still arrive whole. She had spent her life afraid of ending. The island had known all along that ending was not the opposite of continuing. It was how continuing was done.

---

The people drifted down toward the mats and the food, and the singing loosened into talk, and the work light hummed over the leather book. Marcus brought her a chair at last and she sat in it, and she found she was glad to. Lani stayed at the console a while, watching the lock hold green, then came and crouched by Tuli's knee the way she had as a small girl, and neither of them said anything, because it had all been said and would go on being said for a thousand years.

The light went fully out of the west. The dishes turned, all eight together, tracking the source down toward the horizon on their slow motors, and Tuli watched the ears follow the star across the sky — the ancestors' ears, tilted up into the dark, listening the way the island had always listened, patiently, across a distance no life could cross alone.

She let her breath go all the way out.

She thought: it is done, and it is not done, and that is the same thing now.

The tide, out on the reef, turned, and began, without hurry, to go.
