# Chapter 12 — The Answering

Lani was old now, and the ears still turned.

She woke before the others most mornings, the way old people do, and walked the causeway out to Fenua while the lagoon was still the colour of the inside of a shell. Her knees complained. She let them. The dishes had been re-skinned twice in her lifetime and the shed rebuilt higher after the water came up, and there were fourteen of them now instead of eight, wider ears, but they were the same ears, tilted into the same sky, and the atoll had kept them the way the atoll kept everything worth keeping — by refusing, generation after stubborn generation, to let go.

She had been the girl at the console. She had stopped being able to fully believe that, some years ago. The girl who pressed her hand to the key was someone Lani remembered the way she remembered her own grandmother, with love and a little distance, as an ancestor who happened to share her name. In between there had been a whole life. She had trained three keepers after herself and buried one of them. She had married and been widowed and raised children who had raised children, some of whom had left for the cities on the rim and some of whom had come back, the way island children always both did. She had recited the gafa so many thousands of times that she could feel the new verse coming the way you feel a stair in the dark, her mouth already shaping the ears and the star and the question before her mind arrived.

The transmission had never stopped. That was the thing she was proudest of, more than any paper she had put her name to in the years when there were still papers. Sixty years, now, and more — she had lost the exact count and kept the true one, which was measured not in years but in phonemes: they were most of the way through the eleventh slow sound of the reply. Eleven sounds. A word not yet a hundredth said. And the island had never once let the signal drop. Through storms that took the shed roof, through the year the water rose over the old apron, through the quarrels and the funerals and the two generations who had grown up thinking the whole thing faintly embarrassing before growing old enough to understand it — the word had gone out, night after night, on the right tide, in the right season, added to by hands that had never met the hand that began it.

That was the answer. She had understood, watching Tuli understand it, that the content of the reply was almost beside the point. The reply was the continuity itself. The reply was fourteen dishes and a leather book and a nine-line verse a child could carry, all still here, still saying yes, across a gap of time no single one of them could see across. They were not sending a message. They had become one.

People came from off-island to see it, sometimes — scholars, the descendants of the old Consortium men who had once tried to buy the array out from under the village and now wrote respectful papers about it, students who wanted to stand where the first phoneme had gone out. Lani received them all the same way. She let them sit in on a recitation, and she watched their faces when they realised that the machine and the chant were the same machine, that the science she showed them in the shed and the names Ropati's granddaughter sang under the dishes were two halves of a single instrument for holding meaning across time. Most of them understood it in the head and went away pleased. A few understood it lower down, the way you understand a tide, and those few usually went quiet and looked out at the sea for a while, and Lani knew them for her own kind, and was gentle with them, and let them be.

---

The modulation came on a night in the wet season, and Lani was not even at the console when it came, which felt right to her afterward. A machine had been watching for it for sixty years. It did not need her eyes.

Her great-grandnephew Ioane woke her. He was seventeen and the newest keeper, the one whose hands she had been putting the work into, and he came pounding up the path to her house in the dark with a tablet in his hand and his voice gone high and strange, and for a bad moment she thought the water was coming again.

"The polarization," he said. "Matua Lani. The polarization angle. It's — you have to come. The instruments flagged it an hour ago and I ran it three times, I ran it every way, it's not us, it's not the equipment—"

She was already reaching for her stick.

They took the causeway slowly because she could not take it any faster and because, she thought, some things should not be run to. The lagoon was black and the rain had stopped and the stars were out hard and clean over the seaward reef, the way they had been the night the girl pressed her hand to the key. Ioane kept the tablet turned toward her the whole way, its glow lighting the underside of his young face, and he talked, and she let the words come apart and reassemble as they walked.

The Metronome flashed nine hundred times a second, the way it always had, steady enough to set a world's clocks by. Underneath the flashing was the slow rhythm, the message, one small change every hundred and twelve years, the ancient question turning and turning. That had not changed. What had changed was the light itself — the angle of it, the tilt of the polarization, a property Lani had learned the name of as a girl at Tuli's knee and had taught to Ioane last dry season without ever expecting it to matter. The angle had begun to move.

It was moving in a pattern. It was moving in their pattern. The cadence of the shift — the spacing of it, the shape — answered the cadence of the reply the island had been sending. Not the words. They could not have received the words; the words were still climbing out across eight centuries of dark and would not arrive at the source for lifetimes yet. This was something else. This was older. This was a change that must have left the Metronome sixteen hundred years ago, before the founding canoe, before any of it — a change that had been on its way the whole time the island was learning to listen, the whole time Tuli was being born and living and dying, the whole time the girl grew old into Lani. It had been coming toward them, patiently, at the speed of light, for as long as the island had had a name.

And it matched. The Slow Ones had begun to answer, sixteen hundred years ago, and the answer had arrived tonight, and it fit the shape of the reply the way a hand fits the hand held out to meet it.

They had not been answering the island's word. They could not have been. But they had been answering the same question with the same grammar, reaching across the same gap in the same way, so that when the two reachings finally met in the middle of the dark they knew each other. Someone out there was continuous enough. Someone out there had kept saying yes for ten thousand years, the way the island had kept saying it for sixty, and the two continuities had recognized each other across the whole width of the night.

The reply had been heard. Not the words — the fact of them. The fact that someone was here, and had stayed, and had answered. That was what had come back. That was enough. That was everything.

---

She sat at the console a long time before she touched anything. Ioane hovered, wanting her to confirm it, wanting the old keeper to say the word so it would be real. She made him wait. She watched the trace on the screen, the slow tilt of the light, and she let herself feel the size of it, which was the same size as her whole life and larger.

She thought of Tuli.

She thought of the frail steady woman who had said, *if it's me, it's a thing that ended with me; it has to be a thing that started with you.* Tuli had been right and had been righter than she knew. She had died in the season she said she would, before the word had even cleared the edge of the solar system, and she had died at peace, which Lani had not fully believed at the time and understood completely now. Because here was the peace, sixty years on, tilting quietly across a screen in the wet-season dark: the thing Tuli had begun had not ended with her. It had not ended at all. It had gone out into the night and found its answer, and Tuli had known it would without ever needing to see it, the way you know a name you have added to the gafa will still be spoken long after the mouth that added it has gone silent.

Tuli was in the verse. Fa‘amanu was in the verse, who had taught her to listen. They were ancestors now, held in the chant, spoken every time the gafa was recited, which was to say they were still here, still speaking, still part of the reaching-across. The dead did not leave the genealogy. That was the whole of what the genealogy was for. Lani had understood it as a girl as a beautiful idea and understood it now as a plain fact, the way she understood the tide: the light that left long ago had finally arrived, and the people who sent their word to meet it were not gone, because the island had refused, night after night, to let them be.

Outside, the sky was beginning to grey along the seaward edge. The fourteen dishes stood pale against it and, as the source came up over the horizon, they turned, all together, on their slow motors — following the little dead star that had spoken first, the way the island had always followed it, patiently, listening across a distance no life could cross alone.

Lani put her hand on the leather book.

There were many books now; the leather one was kept in a sealed case and touched only for this. She opened it to where the last keeper had written, and she took the pen, and in a hand gone unsteady with age but sure of what it was doing, she wrote the date, and the angle, and what the angle meant. *The reply was heard,* she wrote. *They have begun to answer. The word held.*

Then she turned to Ioane, who was seventeen, whose hands she had put the work into, and she said the thing she had waited her whole life to be old enough to say.

"Wake Ropati's granddaughter," she said. "Tell her there is a new line for the verse. And tell her to teach it to the children first."

The boy went out into the greying light. Lani stayed with the ears a while longer, listening the way the island had always listened, and out on the reef the tide turned, without hurry, and came in.
