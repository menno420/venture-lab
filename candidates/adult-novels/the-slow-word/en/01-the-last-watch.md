# Chapter 1 — The Last Watch

Dawn came to Vaituga the way it always had, without ceremony and without mercy. Tuli watched it from the control room window: a grey seam splitting the ocean from the sky, then a bruise of colour, then the sudden shameless gold that would, within the hour, turn the whole atoll into a griddle. She had lived through eleven thousand of these dawns on this island and she still could not decide whether the light forgave the place or simply exposed it.

She logged the ears one at a time, as she had every morning for thirty years.

Dish One, azimuth nominal. The old motor groaned as it woke, a sound like a man clearing his throat before bad news. Dish Two, elevation drive sticking again; she keyed the override and it shuddered free. Dish Three, Four, Five, the small ones out along the reef flat, their white bowls tilted skyward like a congregation that had been promised something and was still, patiently, waiting to receive it. Dish Six was dead and had been dead for two years, and the Consortium had declined to authorise the part. She logged it anyway. *Dish Six, no response.* There was a discipline in recording an absence. Her grandmother would have understood that, though her grandmother had never seen a dish in her life.

The coffee was bad. The coffee had always been bad; the good beans went to the mainland stations, the ones with visitors and press releases and futures. Tuli drank it standing, because sitting down at this hour felt like a concession she wasn't ready to make, and she looked at the array laid out below the ridge — six white shapes on the black volcanic apron, the lagoon beyond them going from pewter to turquoise as the sun climbed, and past the lagoon the reef where the swell broke in a line of white that never stopped and never would.

The ears. That was what the islanders called them, the ones who still bothered to have a word for the place. *O taliga o le lagi.* The ears of the sky. She had liked that, once, before she stopped letting herself like things that came from here. Ropati had said it to her the first year, standing in this same room with his hands folded, and she had corrected him — *they're antennas, Matua, they don't hear, they receive* — and he had looked at her with an expression she now recognised, at fifty-eight, as pity, though at twenty-eight she had read it as ignorance.

They were coming to take the ears away in nine days.

The decommission crew was already booked, a contractor out of Suva, three men and a barge and a set of instructions that ran to forty pages, most of which concerned the safe disposal of forty years of accumulated lubricant and the recovery of anything containing rare earths. The dishes themselves would be cut up on site. Steel didn't travel well across an ocean; it was cheaper to leave it as scrap for whoever on Vaituga wanted to cart it off, and Tuli had a private, corrosive certainty that within a decade the bowls would be roofing and rainwater tanks and the frames of fishing boats, and that this was probably the most honest afterlife an instrument of pure knowledge could hope for in a place like this.

Her own afterlife was less clear. She had a flat in Auckland she had not slept in for six years and a pension the Consortium described, in the same email that terminated her post, as *generous under the circumstances.* The circumstances were that Fenua Observatory had produced, in three decades, exactly one result of note — a refinement to the timing model of a millisecond pulsar, published in a journal nobody outside four hundred people had read — and that the money it consumed could keep two graduate students in orbit-mechanics simulations somewhere with a nicer climate and better optics. She did not, in her more rigorous moods, entirely disagree with them. That was the worst of it. She had spent a career learning to follow evidence, and the evidence said Fenua was a beautiful, expensive mistake, and she had given it her whole life.

She set the coffee down and went to work on the archive, because the archive would not box itself.

Thirty years of data. It sat in the server room as a wall of drives and, redundantly, in a rack of tape that nobody had touched since the fibre link went in, and its total volume was both enormous and, by modern standards, a joke — the kind of dataset a single sky survey now generated before breakfast. The Consortium wanted the scientifically significant portions transferred to the central repository in Bonn and the remainder deleted. *Remainder* was doing an enormous amount of work in that sentence. The significant portions were perhaps four terabytes. The remainder was everything else: the calibration runs, the failed observations, the nights of pure static when the receivers were warm and the sky said nothing, the long redundant vigil of an instrument pointed patiently at the same handful of objects for longer than some marriages lasted.

She was to catalogue it, tag what mattered, and mark the rest for erasure. She had been putting it off for a week and she could put it off no longer, because a person came all the way out here to switch off a telescope, and once they had switched it off there was no one left to argue with the deletion script.

So: the boxes. She had brought them up from the store, flat-packed, and she assembled them on the floor of the control room the way her mother had taught her to fold the woven mats for a fine mat exchange, corner to corner, pressure at the crease. Into the boxes went the paper. There was so much paper. Logbooks in a dozen hands, hers and her predecessors', the ink going brown; a run of thermal-printer traces from the nineties, curled like fern fronds, the images on them long since faded to ghosts; a wall calendar from 2034 with a child's drawing of a fish taped to July, and she could not for the life of her remember whose child, or why the fish, and she stood holding it for a long moment before she put it in the box for keeping rather than the box for burning. She was allowed a few irrational preservations. She had earned that much.

Her phone sat face-up on the desk and she did not look at it, which was its own kind of looking.

There were four messages from Lani. She knew there were four because she had read the first two and left the last two unopened, which on the small illuminated square of the phone showed as a number, a red four, a debt. Lani was nineteen and clever in the particular unguarded way of people who have not yet been told no by anyone who mattered, and she had a scholarship offer from a university in Melbourne and a letter of provisional acceptance from another in Wellington, and she wanted her grand-aunt — her *only* relation who had gone away and made a life of the mind and come back, or half come back — to tell her what to do. Tuli had answered the first message with something warm and useless. She had answered the second with something shorter. The third and fourth she had not answered because she did not know what to say that was both true and kind, and she had reached the age where she was no longer willing to lie to the young in order to be loved by them.

There had been, three weeks ago, a *saofa‘i* — a bestowal, the conferring of a chiefly title on one of her cousins — and with it the recital of the *gafa*, the genealogy, the long chanted river of names that ran back past the missionaries and past the first canoes and out, if you believed it, into the founding dark. Ropati would have called it. Ropati was the *tulāfale* now, the orator, the keeper; he was eighty-one and his voice had thinned but the names had not, the names never thinned, and he had sent word through her cousin that a place would be kept for her, that it was fitting, that the family's scientist should stand and hear her own line spoken.

She had not gone. She had sent money and an apology and a lie about the array requiring her, and the array had required nothing of her that day but the same patient staring it required every day, and she had sat in this room while forty generations were spoken aloud eight kilometres away across the lagoon, and she had told herself she was working.

The truth was closer to fear, and she knew it, the way you know a tooth is going bad before it aches. Fa‘amanu had tried to give her the chants when she was small — the small ones, the ones a child could hold, the counting-songs and the naming-songs — and Tuli had learned them in the effortless way children learn everything, and then she had gone to Auckland at seventeen on a scholarship of her own and she had put them down, deliberately, the way you put down a language you have decided is beneath the person you intend to become. She had traded a chant for a spectrum. She had told herself it was the same hunger pointed at a better sky. And now she was fifty-eight and the sky was being switched off and the woman who had taught her the songs was thirty years dead, and Tuli could not have stood up in front of the family and chanted the *gafa* if her life had depended on it, because she had let it fall out of her on purpose, and there is a particular shame reserved for the things we lose not through carelessness but through choice.

She taped the box shut. The tape shrieked in the quiet.

By mid-morning the heat had come into the room despite the labouring aircon, and she moved from the paper to the drives, which was cleaner work and lonelier. This was done at the terminal, the catalogue open on one screen and the deletion manifest on the other, and it consisted of going through the archive tree branch by branch and deciding, for each folder, whether thirty years of one woman's attention had produced anything the species needed to keep.

Mostly it had not. She was ruthless because ruthlessness was the only mercy left; there was no point being sentimental about a static run. She tagged and she flagged and she watched the red total on the deletion manifest climb — a terabyte, two — and she felt, distantly, the way she imagined a person might feel signing the papers to sell a house they had been unhappy in for forty years. Grief and relief so tangled you could not have separated them with a scalpel.

Then she came to the Metronome.

It had a catalogue name, a string of coordinates and a designation, but everyone who had ever worked at Fenua had called it the Metronome, because that was what it was: a millisecond pulsar, a dead star spinning six hundred times a second, sweeping its lighthouse beam across the Earth with a regularity that made atomic clocks look nervous. It was Fenua's one real object, the source of the one real paper, the thing the dishes had returned to night after night for thirty years the way the tide returns, because a pulsar that stable is a gift — a clock you did not build, ticking in the dark, that lets you measure the trembling of space itself.

She had a folder for it that was thirty years deep. The timing data was already flagged for Bonn; that was the significant part, the part that mattered. But there was more than timing. There was polarisation — the angle of the light, the plane in which the radio waves oscillated as they arrived, a quantity Fenua had recorded faithfully and, so far as anyone had ever determined, pointlessly, because the Metronome's polarisation was famously, boringly constant. It did not vary. That was almost the definition of the object. You could set your instruments by it. She had, in fact, spent years using its polarisation angle to calibrate the receivers, the way a carpenter trusts a spirit level: not because the level is interesting but because it does not move.

She opened the polarisation record to tag it for deletion, because a constant is not a discovery, and she pulled thirty years of it onto the screen as a single long trace, the angle plotted against time, expecting a flat line.

It was not quite a flat line.

She saw it and did not believe it and looked away and looked back, which is the oldest reflex in the science, older than the telescope, the animal refusal of the eye to accept what it has been told cannot be there. Across the full width of the plot — across three decades — the polarisation angle drifted. Not much. A fraction of a degree, a lean so slight that on any single night, any single year, it would have vanished under the noise, which was exactly why nobody had ever seen it and why she had used this very quantity as her *level* for half her career. But laid out end to end, all thirty years at once, the drift was there: faint, monotonous, and — she leaned closer, her bad coffee cold at her elbow, the heat forgotten — regular. Impossibly, stupidly regular. As if something were turning, very slowly, somewhere out past the reach of every reasonable idea she had.

Calibration error. It had to be calibration error, a slow creep in the reference, an artefact of thirty years of drifting instruments and swapped receivers and firmware nobody remembered writing. That was the answer. That was the boring, correct, professional answer, and she had built a career on preferring the boring correct answer to the beautiful one, because the beautiful ones were almost always wrong and the graveyard of astronomy was full of people who had fallen in love with an artefact.

She reached for the mouse to tag the folder for deletion.

Her hand did not do it.

Outside, the tide had turned; she could hear it in the changed voice of the reef, the endless white line shifting its note. The dishes below the ridge held their patient upward attitude, listening, as they had listened before she came and would not listen after she was gone, to light that had left its source ten thousand years ago and was only now, this morning, arriving at a switched-off telescope at the edge of the world.

She did not delete the folder.

She flagged it — a single yellow tag, *hold, review* — and told herself it meant nothing, that she would look once with fresh eyes and clear it in the morning, that a careful woman checks even her artefacts before she burns them. Then she sat for a while in the heat and looked at the faint, patient, impossible slope on the screen, and did not go back to the boxes, and let the coffee go entirely cold.
