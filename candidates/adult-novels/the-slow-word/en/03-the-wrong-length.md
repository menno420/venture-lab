# Chapter 3 — The Wrong Length

By morning she had convinced herself it was real, which meant the next thing she had to do was try to kill it.

That was the part nobody outside the work understood — that belief and destruction were the same gesture, aimed twice. Tuli had spent the night not sleeping so much as circling the console, coming back to the plot the way a tongue comes back to a broken tooth, and now, with the lagoon gone the flat pewter of just-before-dawn beyond the louvres, she sat down to do the honest thing. She would build the modulation a model and then she would spend the day looking for the mistake inside it.

The array hummed around her. Fenua's main hall had been designed for forty people and now held one, and the empty desks had taken on the patient quality of pews. Somewhere along the roofline a loose panel ticked in the trade wind. She had grown so used to the sound of the ears — the low electronic respiration of the receivers, the fans, the coolant loop that gurgled like a sleeping man — that silence, when the power cut, frightened her more than any noise.

She pulled the polarization data forward. Thirty years of the Metronome, that patient millisecond pulsar out past the galactic centre, its beam sweeping across the atoll seven hundred and some times a second, faithful past belief. For three decades the observatory had timed it, and in timing it had also — almost incidentally, the way you notice a friend's grey hair — recorded the angle of its polarized light. And the angle drifted.

Not randomly. That was the thing that had kept her up. Noise drifted too; the ionosphere breathed, the instrument aged, cables warmed and cooled and lied by fractions. She knew every one of those liars personally. She had spent the small hours introducing them to the data one by one, the way you'd walk suspects past a witness, and the data had refused to identify any of them. What remained, when she had subtracted every honest error she could name, was a curve. A long, shallow, deliberate-looking curve, bending through her thirty years like the top of a wave too big to see the trough of.

She fitted it. She was good at fitting; it was most of what a life in this work turned out to be, the patient marriage of a shape to a suspicion. She tried a line and it wanted to bend. She tried a parabola and it fit better, which meant nothing — a parabola will flatter anything over a short enough baseline, will lie down obligingly against any gentle arc you show it and call itself the truth. She had seen careers built on parabolas that were really the first thin sip of something enormous, and she had no intention of being one of them. She tried a sinusoid, because that was what her gut had been muttering since two in the morning, a slow clean oscillation, the kind of thing a physical process makes when it is doing one honest thing over and over, and the machine chewed and returned a period.

Tuli read the number and laughed, one short unhappy sound in the empty hall.

The period was longer than the data. The best-fit cycle was something like four hundred years, and she had thirty. She was trying to measure the length of a sentence by staring at a single letter. The fit was real in the sense that the curvature was real, the bend was in the numbers and would not leave, but the *period* was a rumour the software had invented to explain a fragment — it had taken her sliver of arc and drawn, very confidently, the whole circle it might belong to, and the confidence was worth nothing at all.

"You don't know that," she told the screen. "You're guessing."

She widened the error bars herself, by hand, dragging them out until they were honest, and the fit sagged into a grey fan of possibility — four hundred years, or nine hundred, or eleven thousand, the software agreeable to any of them, because from inside a keyhole every corridor looks the same length. That was the whole problem, stated cleanly at last, and stating it cleanly did not make it any smaller. Thirty years was a keyhole. Whatever the Metronome's light was doing, it was doing it on a scale that made the entire radio era — every dish humanity had ever turned to the sky, from the first crude horns to these dying ears of hers — a single held breath in a very long song.

She got up. She made coffee in the chipped press that Marcus had left the last time he came, four years ago now, and she stood at the louvres drinking it while the sun came up hot and immediate over the reef, the way it did here, no dawn to speak of, just night and then the hammer of day. Frigatebirds hung over the pass. The tide was going out; she could smell it, that low green iodine breath the flats gave off when the water pulled back and left the coral to steam. Salt on everything. Salt in the hinges, in her hair, in the solder joints she had to replace twice a year because the sea ate them. Her grandmother had said, once, that the tide was the island breathing, and that you could set your whole life by it if you were patient enough to learn its longer rhythms — not the daily going and coming any child could see, but the slower swellings the moon dragged around across weeks and seasons, tides inside the tides, a nesting of clocks. Tuli had thought it superstition then. She thought now, watching the flats steam, that Fa'amanu had simply been a better observer than her granddaughter had been willing to notice.

The trouble, she thought, was not that she couldn't see the shape. The trouble was that to *prove* the shape she needed to see more of it, and to see more of it she needed time she did not have and would never have — no one would. You could not point a telescope at four centuries. You could not wait. The only way to catch a wave that long was to have already been watching before you were born, and to keep watching after you died, and to have the watching passed hand to hand without a single link lying about what it saw.

Nobody did that. Nobody kept a measurement clean across four hundred years.

She was still standing there, empty cup gone cold in her hand, when the outer door banged and a voice called into the hall, "Aunty? You in here or you finally left me the whole place?"

Lani.

Tuli's grand-niece came in out of the glare in a way that made the room seem, briefly, absurdly full — nineteen and unhurried, sandals slapping, a canvas bag over one shoulder and a green coconut in her free hand with the top already macheted off. She had Fa'amanu's forehead. Tuli noticed it every time and every time refused to say so.

"You're supposed to call ahead," Tuli said. "This is a scientific installation."

"It's a shed full of old aunties talking to God." Lani set the coconut on the least important desk and dropped into a wheeled chair and pushed herself in a slow arc, taking the place in with the frank ownership of the young. "One old aunty. Mum sent breadfruit, it's in the truck, I'm not carrying it in until you're nice to me." She stopped the chair. "You look terrible. You sleep?"

"I was working."

"On the thing that ate your face." Lani nodded at the screen without really looking, and then — because she was Fa'amanu's blood too, and sharper than she let the island see — she did look. "That's a wobble."

"It's a polarization angle."

"It's a wobble." She grinned. "I did one year of physics, Aunty, I get to say wobble." She rolled closer. "What's it do?"

And Tuli, who had told no one, who had not even finished telling herself, found the words coming out anyway, because the alternative was to keep holding them alone. "It bends," she said. "The light from a — from a very steady thing, a lighthouse, out past the middle of the galaxy. It shouldn't bend and it does. Slowly. So slowly that in thirty years of watching I've caught maybe a hundredth of one full bend. Maybe less. I can't tell." She heard her own voice thin out. "I can't tell how long the whole thing is. I can see that it's a curve but I can't see the size of the curve. I need — " She stopped.

"You need more years," Lani said.

"I need more years than there are."

Lani considered this with the seriousness she gave to things that weren't about herself. "Than there are for you," she said, "or than there are?"

Tuli looked at her.

"Because those are different," Lani went on, blithe, wheeling herself another slow half-turn, not knowing she had said anything, which was how the true things usually arrived. "Anyway. That's not why I came." She reached into the canvas bag and produced, of all things, a folded printout, government letterhead, the scholarship's crest at the top — Auckland, the marine biology place, the one that would take her nine thousand kilometres away and give her a life Tuli half-wanted for her and half-couldn't bear. "They moved the deadline up. I have to say yes or no by the end of the month."

"And?"

"And I don't know." Lani said it lightly and it landed heavy. "Everybody knows for me. Mum knows, the pastor knows, the whole village has a strong opinion about what the clever one should do. You're the only one who left, so." She shrugged, a nineteen-year-old's shrug, carrying more than it admitted. "I wanted to ask the one who left."

"Don't ask me," Tuli said, more sharply than she meant. "I'm the worst person on this atoll to ask."

Lani let that sit, and did not push, which was its own kind of skill. Then she stood, and slung the bag, and delivered what she had actually come to deliver. "Matua Ropati's reciting tonight," she said. "The gafa. All of it, the long version, because old Sione finally died and his branch needs saying back into the line. It's at the fale, after the sun. You should come." She raised a hand before Tuli could refuse. "He asked after you. He always asks after you. It kills him a little, you know, that you're up here talking to the sky and you won't come down and hear your own name in the chant."

"I have work."

"You have a wobble you can't measure." Lani said it kindly, which was worse. "It'll keep. It's four hundred years long, you said. One night." She was already walking backward toward the glare of the door, grinning again, forgiving her in advance the way the island always did. "Breadfruit's in the truck. Be nice to me. I'll leave the coconut."

And she was gone, sandals on the concrete, the door banging, the day pouring back in white and enormous where she had stood.

Tuli did not go to the recital. She told herself she would and then the afternoon ate itself the way afternoons did — a receiver dropped out and she spent three hours coaxing it back, cursing the Consortium and Elin Halvorsen and the whole slow strangling of the place, and by the time she had it the sun was down and the fale would be full of people she'd half-avoided for two years and she could not, she found, make her body walk down the hill toward them. She ate breadfruit standing over the sink. She drank two fingers of the bad rum she kept for genuinely bad days.

Then she went back to the hall, and she sat down in front of the too-long curve, and she looked at it in the dark with the receivers breathing around her, and she thought about what Lani had said without knowing she'd said it. *Than there are for you, or than there are.*

She thought: the measurement needs to outlive the measurer.

She thought: that has never once been done. Not cleanly. Not across four centuries, not with a signal you could still trust at the far end. Every long record humanity kept was a game of telephone that ended in nonsense — copies of copies, drift and rot and the well-meaning lie, until the thing that came out the far end bore no honest relation to the thing that went in. You could not carry a number across ten generations without dropping it. Everyone knew that. It was practically a law.

The curve waited on the screen, patient, unmeasurable, real. A wave too long to see the trough of. To catch it she would need a record kept faithfully — call it faithfully, call it without error — across centuries, longer than the radio era, longer than the telescope, longer than the whole apparatus she had given her life to. She needed a thousand-year measurement made by people who had never heard of a pulsar, made and kept and passed on intact, so that she could lay it against her thirty years and finally see how big the wave was.

Somewhere down the hill, faint under the reef's endless collapse, a single voice had begun to carry up on the night wind — old, unhurried, rising and falling in a shape she knew in her body before she knew it in her mind, a shape her grandmother's mouth had once made over a child who hadn't wanted to learn it. Matua Ropati, saying the names. Saying the line. Carrying a list of the dead back thirty generations without a page of writing, exactly as it had been carried before him, and before him, and before that.

Tuli sat very still.

Where in the world, she had been asking herself all day, does one find a record that spans a thousand years.

The wind brought her the answer and she had spent her whole life walking away from it.
