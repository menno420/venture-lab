# Chapter 9 — The Genealogy

Matua Ropati kept his fine mats in a house that smelled of woodsmoke and pandanus, a low fale open on three sides to the wind off the lagoon. Tuli had passed it a thousand times as a girl and a thousand more as a stranger to her own village, and never once had she gone in to truly listen. She had come for other things — a signature on a land document, a word about her grandmother's grave, a favour for the Consortium she was ashamed now to remember. She had come the way a scientist comes to a museum, expecting the exhibits to hold still.

He was ninety-one, or ninety-three; the number moved depending on which cousin you asked. His hands were folded on the head of a fue, the orator's flywhisk, its coconut-fibre lengths gone silver with handling. He watched her cross the threshold and sit, and waited, because waiting was half of what he did.

"Matua," she said. "I need you to teach me the thing I refused to learn."

He smiled without showing his teeth. "You were a very fast child," he said. "Fast children are hard to teach. They arrive at the end of the sentence before you have finished the beginning, and then they are bored, and then they go to sea." He tipped his chin at her. "You went to sea. And now you have come back up the beach." He said it without triumph, the way you note the tide. "Tell me what you refused."

So she told him. She told him about the Metronome — the pulsar that ticked eight hundred times a second, steadier than any clock human beings had built, a dead star spinning at the bottom of a well of time. She told him how its light had left before the atoll existed, before the reef, and how in the angle of that light, the twist of its polarisation, someone had written. Not in the brightness. Not in the timing. In the *lean* of the wave, a hair one way, a hair the other, a change so slow you could stare at it for a human lifetime and swear nothing moved.

"One sound," she said, and she did not have a better word, so she used his, "one word every hundred and twelve years. It takes them ten thousand years to say the whole thing. And the whole thing is a question."

Ropati's hands stilled on the fue. "What do they ask?"

She had translated it a dozen ways in a dozen sleepless nights, and every version embarrassed her with its plainness. "*We have been speaking a long time,*" she said. "*Is anyone there who is continuous enough to answer.*"

The wind came through the fale and turned the pages of the lagoon. Somewhere a child was being scolded, and a dog took up the scolding and improved on it.

"They are not asking whether we are clever," Tuli went on, and this was the part that had broken her, out at the array, the diagnosis sitting in her chest like a stone in a net. "Cleverness is cheap. Any species that can build a radio can be clever for an afternoon. They are asking whether we can *hold*. Whether anything here can carry one thought — unbroken, recognisably itself — across centuries. To answer them you cannot just shout back. You have to send the way they send. A word every hundred years. A message that outlives every person who works on it. You have to keep faith with a sentence for a thousand years." She spread her hands. "And I looked for something on Earth that could do that. A church, a government, a library, my own damned observatory. And there is nothing. Nothing we build lasts. The Consortium is closing Fenua after forty years and calling forty years a long run."

"Forty years," Ropati agreed, gravely, as if she had said forty minutes.

"I was ready to write it down as impossible," she said. "A message we could receive and never answer. The loneliest fact in the sky." She looked at him. "And then I remembered you. At the last fono, saying the fa‘alupega. You called the titles and the lands and the houses in their order, and you did not read from anything, and you did not falter, and when you finished, old Sina corrected you on one name — the wife's line, three generations back — and you thanked her and said it again, corrected. And I sat there thinking, *this is theatre, this is the past being polite to itself.*" Her voice was unsteady now and she let it be. "I did not understand that I was watching the thing I said did not exist."

Ropati was very quiet. Then he set the fue across his knees. "Say what you think you were watching. Say it as a scientist. I want to hear it in your own language, so I know you believe it."

She almost laughed at how well he knew the shape of pride. "All right," she said. "The gafa. The genealogy. It's a message." She was finding it as she spoke, the way you find the floor of a lagoon with your feet. "It carries meaning across thirty, forty generations. Without writing. Which is the part that used to make me condescend to it. No writing, I thought, therefore fragile, therefore folklore. I had it exactly backwards. Writing is what's fragile. Paper burns, servers rot, languages die and leave the marks unreadable. The gafa is not stored anywhere you can burn. It's stored in *people*, in the living, in more than one person at once —"

"Redundancy," Ropati said, trying the word.

"Redundancy. Yes. Every important name is held by many mouths. Lose one orator, the line survives in his cousins, his rivals, the woman who corrected him. And when it's performed —" She stopped. "That's the part I couldn't see. The call and response. It isn't decoration. When you chant and the others answer, and someone says *no, the name was Tuiā, not Tuia* — that's error-correction. Every recital is the message verifying itself against every other copy in every other head in the room. That's how it comes down thirty generations without drifting into nonsense. A system built — over centuries, by people who never wrote a line of code — with exactly the features you need to survive deep time. And it's bound to *identity*, so people will die to keep it accurate, because the message is *who they are.*"

She had run out of breath. Ropati was looking at her with an expression she could not at first read, and then she could, and it undid her, because it was the expression Fa‘amanu used to wear.

"You are describing my whole life," he said slowly. "And you are describing it correctly. And no palagi ever has, and no scientist ever has, and my own grandchildren mostly think I am an old man reciting the phone book." The film of age in his eyes caught the light. "I did not expect to hear it from Va‘a's granddaughter, who ran away to talk to stars."

"I ran away," Tuli said, "to learn the only language in which I could hear you."

—

It came to her then, unbidden, the way a wave you did not see gathers behind you and lifts your whole body before you have decided anything. A chant. Not the grand fa‘alupega but a child's one, the ma‘au her grandmother had drilled into her on the mat at night — the counting of the family back and back, *o ai lou tama, o ai lona tama,* who is your father, who is his father, the ladder of names climbing into the dark. Fa‘amanu's voice in it, the exact pitch, the way she held the second-to-last syllable a half-beat too long so the child would lean in for the fall of the last.

Tuli had not thought of it in fifty years. She had believed it gone — overwritten, the storage reclaimed. And here it was, entire, not a word missing, retrieved across half a century by nothing but the shape of this conversation. She felt her grandmother's dry hand on the back of her neck, steadying her at the place where the names got hard, and she understood in her body what she had only understood in her mind: the message had been running in her the whole time. She was a copy. She was carrying it and had not known.

She wept, which she had promised herself she was too old and too ill to do over anything but the sea. Ropati did not comfort her, which was the correct thing, and the kind thing. He let the va between them hold it — the sacred space between two people that you keep by not crowding it. When she was done he handed her, without comment, a square of faded lavalava to wipe her face, and it smelled of him and of woodsmoke, and it was the most tender thing anyone had done for her since Marcus.

—

"I want to propose something," she said at last, "that you will think is madness. Hear all of it before you decide."

"At my age," Ropati said, "madness and prophecy wear the same shirt. Speak."

"The reply. To the Slow Ones." She had started calling them that out at the array, and it had stuck. "It has to be sent the way they sent — one increment at a time, over centuries. The machine can do the sending. The dishes, retuned, can broadcast the polarisation pattern; the physics is a smaller problem than people think. But the machine cannot *keep* the promise. Someone has to tend it for a thousand years — has to make sure that when the transmitter fails in the year 2400, and it will, somebody knows why it matters and rebuilds it and does not change a single phoneme out of boredom or cleverness or war. No institution can promise that. I told you. We don't build anything that lasts."

"But we sing things that last," Ropati said. He had arrived at the end of her sentence before she finished it. Fast children grow into fast old men.

"But you sing things that last." She leaned forward. "Marry them, Matua. Marry the machine to the lineage. Make the reply — and the *keeping* of the reply — into a new verse of the gafa. Not a footnote. A line in the living genealogy of this family and this village. So that a hundred years from now a child on this mat learns the names of her grandmothers and then, in the same breath, learns that her people are answering the sky, and how, and that it is hers to carry, and that she must correct the one who carries it wrong. Put the star in the chant. Let the chant keep the star." She was almost whispering. "It's the only vault on this planet with a thousand-year track record. I'm asking you to let me store the most important message in human history inside the one technology that can hold it. Yours. Not mine. Yours."

For a long time the old man said nothing. The lagoon breathed in and out of the fale. A rooster, with terrible timing, made a remark, and both ignored it, and that shared ignoring was its own small treaty.

"You honour us," Ropati said finally, his voice gone formal, the register of the tulāfale, and she understood that he was choosing his words the way you choose stones for a wall. "You honour us, and you frighten me. Not the star. The weight. A gafa is not a museum, Teuila. It is not a place we keep dead things safe. It is alive because it costs. Every name in it is defended. There are feuds three centuries old over a single title, because to hold a name is to hold land, and mana, and the dead. If we put your star into the gafa, we do not make it safe. We make it *contested* — something people will fight over, and lie about, and twist to their advantage, for as long as it lives. That is the price of being alive. Are you offering me a treasure, or a war that will never end?"

"Both," Tuli said, because he deserved the truth and because she had thought about exactly this at three in the morning. "It has to be contested. A message no one fights over is a message no one is really carrying. The gafa survived thirty generations *because* it mattered enough to fight about. If the reply is going to last a thousand years, it can't be safe. It has to be loved, and quarrelled over, and defended. It has to cost." She met his eyes. "You taught me that just now, and you didn't know you were teaching it."

Ropati laughed then, a real one, the teeth and all, and it turned into a cough that bent him over the fue, and she half-rose in alarm — the reflex of a woman who now measured every chest by her own — but he waved her down. When he straightened his eyes were wet, but with the laughing.

"Va‘a's granddaughter," he said, shaking his head. "You went to the far side of the ocean of stars, and you came back to tell me my own grandmother was a computer."

"A better one than any I ran," Tuli said.

"Yes," he agreed, without vanity, as a matter of record. Then the humour left him and something heavier took its place, the practical grief of the very old, who have learned that every plan is a plan about who will still be alive to see it.

"There is one question," he said. "The only question. It is the question the whole thing turns on, and you already know it, or you would not have your face arranged so carefully." He set the fue upright, ceremonial, a small gavel. "A verse that is not carried is only a verse. A message no one is willing to spend a life on is already dead. So. Who will carry it, Teuila? Not me. I am the last knot on a rope that is almost paid out. Not you." He said it gently, and it was the gentleness that told her he knew about her heart; the island knew everything, it always had. "You have brought the fire down from the mountain. But you cannot be the one to keep it burning. That is why your face is arranged."

"I know it," she said. The stone in her chest, the tired valve, the months and not years. She did not flinch, and she saw him note that she did not, and approve.

Down the road, past the church, past the graves with their plastic flowers gone pale in the sun, a girl's laugh rang out — bright, impatient, nineteen years old and certain of nothing except that the island was too small and the world was very large. Lani, arguing with her mother about the scholarship again, about Auckland, about leaving. The laugh carried into the fale and hung between the two of them like a name waiting to be called.

Tuli looked toward the sound. And she felt, rather than saw, the old man turn his head to look the same way, at the same moment, the two of them aligned like dishes swinging onto a source — and neither said anything, because neither had to, because they had both, in the same breath, arrived at the end of the sentence.
