# Chapter 4 — Deep Archives

She did not sleep on it so much as let it work on her, the way seawater works on a mooring line, and in the morning she went hunting for a thousand years.

The proper places first, because she was a proper scientist and because the improper place frightened her. She had come to the idea in the night that the Metronome's slow bend, if it was real, must have left its fingerprint on more than her thirty years — that anything modulating the pulsar's light on a scale of centuries would also, faintly, be written into any long record sensitive to the same physics, or to time itself measured cleanly enough. What she needed were proxies. Old, patient, independent measurements that reached back past the radio era and did not know her curve existed and so could not be flattering it.

She started with ice.

The great cores from the polar archives were public, or public enough, and she spent the morning scraping cadence data out of them — not the climate story everyone mined them for, the isotopes and the trapped air, but the *layering*, the annual banding that laid itself down winter on winter like the rings of a slow tree. Some cores held eight hundred thousand years. What she wanted was subtler and probably foolish: any faint periodic modulation in the deposition, anything with a beat in the range her fit had coughed up. She pulled the series down over Fenua's wheezing satellite link, the progress bar crawling while the receivers breathed around her and the reef kept its endless appointment with the shore, and she detrended and windowed and stared, and then detrended again with a longer window because the first result had been too pretty to trust and pretty results were the ones that killed you.

Something. Maybe. A whisper of power in the band she wanted, right at the edge of meaning nothing — the kind of thing that vanished the moment you looked at it straight and came back when you looked away, which was either the signature of a real faint process or the signature of a scientist wanting one too badly. She had been on both sides of that line in her life and knew how thin it was.

She went to the eclipse records next, and these she loved, had always loved — the Babylonian tablets, the Chinese court astronomers, the medieval Arab observers, generations of frightened careful men who had watched the sky misbehave and written down *when*, to the hour, on clay and silk and vellum, across three thousand years. Modern astronomers used those records to measure how the Earth's spin had slowed, prying a deep-time signal out of the anxieties of the dead. If her modulation touched the propagation of the pulsar's light at all, it might — might — leave a residue in the long timing residuals people had already teased from those ancient frightened observations. She overlaid what she could find. She was careful. She widened every error bar until it hurt.

By late afternoon she had three proxies, all faint, all individually dismissible, none of them proof of anything. A single one meant nothing. She knew how this went; she had spent a career watching people fall in love with noise because they wanted the noise to love them back. Three whispers were still just whispers.

But they whispered in the same band. And to test whether they whispered *in phase* — whether they were the same voice reaching her by three different roads — she needed a fourth road, and an anchor, something to fix the phase against reaching back before the ice cores were drilled and after the eclipse-watchers had died. She needed a long clean measurement, faithfully kept, from the part of the world that had no telescopes and no clay tablets and had crossed the largest ocean on Earth anyway, by reading the sky.

She sat with that for a while, the cold rum bottle within reach and untouched. Then she went and washed her face and put on a proper shirt and walked down the hill, in the last of the heat, to Matua Ropati's fale.

He was on the raised platform in the open-sided house, out of the sun, a very old man arranged with great dignity on a pandanus mat, and he watched her come the whole way up the path without pretending not to. He did not get up. Getting up was not what he was for.

"Teuila," he said, when she had slipped off her sandals and sat, low, the way you sat before a matai, the way her body remembered even as her mind protested it. Not *Tuli*. He used the whole name, the christened name, the name that was hers in the chant. "So the sky let you go."

"For an afternoon."

"An afternoon." He turned the word over as though inspecting it for damage. His face was a topography of years, and his eyes, when they found hers, were not soft. "You did not come last night. Your name was said. Your grandmother's name was said. Fa'amanu, who taught you to say them back, before you decided the palagi's machines were a better use of a Va'a mouth." He let that sit in the va between them, the charged space-between, and did not soften it. "Thirty years I have been putting your name in the line for people who ask where you have gone. It is a long time to answer for someone who will not answer for herself."

Tuli looked at the mat. She had come to ask a favour of this man and he was going to make her earn the right to ask it, and she found she could not resent it, because he was right, and because being made to sit low on a mat and hear it said plainly was itself a thing she had crossed an ocean to escape and had missed without knowing.

"I know, Matua," she said. "I'm sorry. I stayed away too long."

"You did." He accepted the apology without discounting it. Then, because he was stern and not cruel, and because his cousin's granddaughter had come down the hill at last: "But you came. Fa'amanu's forehead came down the hill." Something moved at the corner of his mouth. "What does the sky want, that it sends you to an old man's mat?"

So she told him. Haltingly at first, in her science, and then — watching him not follow the words but follow *her*, the way an orator reads a room — she found the shape of it in the only terms that would carry. There is a light, she said, from very far, older than any of us, older than the island. It carries a mark. The mark changes, very slowly, so slowly that one lifetime cannot see the whole of it, and to read it I must lay my thirty years against something much longer, something kept true across many lifetimes. And I have looked in the ice and in the old dead astronomers' books, she said, and they whisper but they will not tell me the phase, they will not tell me *where in the turning* we are. And I remembered — she said, and stopped, and made herself say it — I remembered the gafa. The line. That you keep the names true across thirty generations without a page of writing. That your fathers kept the star-paths, the rising points, the houses of the stars you steered by, in the chant, unchanged, for a thousand years and more.

The old man was quiet for a long moment.

"You want the star-lore," he said. "The fetū. The paths."

"I want to know how you keep it true," she said. "Across so long. I want to understand how it doesn't rot."

And Matua Ropati, tulāfale, keeper of the fa'alupega, looked at the last astronomer of Fenua for a while longer, and then he did the thing she had walked down the hill both hoping and dreading — he closed his eyes, and he began to recite.

She had heard it as a child and slept through it. She had not heard it as what she was now.

The gafa came out of him slow and enormous, name giving birth to name, generation folding into generation, and she listened this time the way she listened to data — for structure, for architecture — and the architecture undid her. It was not a list. A list would have died in three generations; a single dropped name and everything after it is orphaned, unmoored, lost. This was not a list. Each name arrived *braced*. It came with its pairings, its marriage, its place fixed against two or three others so that if one link ever failed the others held the shape of the gap and told you a name was missing and roughly what it must have been. The chant doubled back on itself, call and answer, the young men's voices — she could hear them, the recital was not solitary, there was a second and a third voice now, coming in from the shadows of the fale, catching the lines, throwing them back — so that no single mouth carried the whole load and no single failure could break it. When Ropati's memory reached for a name and hesitated a half-beat, another voice supplied it, and he took it back, and the line went on, self-correcting, redundant, *robust*, a thousand years of the dead marching in ranks so deep that you could shoot great holes in them and the ranks would close.

Tuli sat on the mat with her scientist's heart going hard in her chest, because she was hearing an error-correcting code. Not a metaphor for one. The thing itself. Redundancy, parity, distributed storage, call-and-response checksum — every trick her civilization had invented in the last eighty years to shove a number across a noisy channel without corruption, these people had invented to shove their ancestors across a noisy millennium, and theirs had *worked*, theirs was working right now, in the mouths of teenagers in the dark, the names arriving unrotted from before the first European saw this ocean.

*This* was how you kept a measurement true across a thousand years. Not by writing it down, where it would burn or moulder or be locked in a language no one spoke. You built it into a living structure that repaired itself every time it was spoken, and you made the speaking a duty and a joy and an identity, so that the line would fight to keep saying it long after the reasons were forgotten.

The chant reached the star-paths. She heard the rising points named, the houses of the sky, the fetū that stood over this island and that one, the roads the great canoes had steered by — a sky-clock, calibrated and recalibrated by ten thousand crossings, held in the same self-correcting structure as the names of the dead, and just as true. And she understood, sitting there, that this was her fourth road and her anchor both: a thousand-year record of where the stars had stood, kept clean, kept phased, kept honest, by the only technology on Earth that had ever managed it.

When it was done — when Ropati opened his eyes and the young voices went quiet and the reef came back into the silence — he looked at her and saw that something had happened in her face.

"You heard it," he said.

"I heard it, Matua."

"Not the way a child hears it."

"No." Her voice was not entirely steady. "The way — " She stopped, and gave him the truest thing she had. "The way I should have heard it forty years ago."

He nodded, once, and something in the va between them eased, not all the way, but enough. "Come back," he said. "You have things to learn and I have not much time to teach them. That is a bad joke the two of us are standing in." The corner of his mouth moved again. "Bring your machine's questions. We will see what the old line remembers."

She walked back up the hill in the dark with the star-paths still turning in her head, and she did not go to bed. She went to the console, and she took the rising points from the chant — dated, phased, anchored across centuries — and she added them to the ice and the eclipses and the thirty years of the Metronome's slow bend, all of it forced onto one axis, one long axis reaching back past the radio era, past the telescope, past writing, into the deep time where the light had left its distant lighthouse before any of this island's dead were born.

She overlaid them.

For a moment the screen was only a mess, four ragged fragments at four different scales, and her heart dropped. Then she phased them — anchored the whole thing on the star-clock, the one clean thousand-year measurement she had, and let the others slide into their proper places against it.

They lined up.

Not perfectly. Not without slack. But the ice's faint whisper and the dead astronomers' residue and her own thirty years of bend all fell onto the *same curve*, the same long shallow deliberate wave, each fragment a different arc of one enormous circle, and where they overlapped they agreed, and where they didn't overlap they extended each other, the way the braced names in the chant extended across the gaps, telling her the shape of what she couldn't directly see.

Tuli sat back in the breathing dark of the empty hall and looked at ten thousand years of a single coherent signal assembled out of ice and clay and the mouths of the dead, and she said, out loud, to no one, in a voice gone strange:

"It's not noise."

The curve held. It did not sag into a fan of possibilities. It was one thing, saying one thing, across a span that made her entire life a held breath — real, coherent, not random, waiting.

Somewhere out past the galactic centre a lighthouse had been bending its light on purpose, and she had just, for the first time, seen far enough down the wave to be sure. Now she only had to learn to read it.
