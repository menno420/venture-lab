# Chapter Five — Ten Thousand Years

The trick, in the end, was to stop trusting the clock.

Tuli had spent forty years teaching herself the opposite. A radio astronomer lived and died by timing; the whole apparatus of the field was a cathedral built to the worship of the exact second. The Metronome itself — that small dead star spinning six hundred times a second in the constellation the charts called nothing and Matua Ropati called the heel of the net — kept better time than any atomic standard on Earth. You did not doubt the clock. You doubted yourself, your cables, your own tired hands.

But the modulation she was chasing did not live in the clock. It lived in the polarization angle, in the slow twist of the light's plane as it arrived, and that twist changed on a scale so long that no clock built by anything with a pulse could hold it. One notch per hundred and twelve years, near enough. To read it she had to abandon the second and think in centuries, and to think in centuries she had to borrow other people's clocks — clocks that ran in ice and stone and memorized speech.

She had them spread across the screens now, four in the morning, the station humming its one low note around her.

The ice cores gave her the coarsest ruler: eighty thousand years of layered Antarctic snow, each summer a dark band, each winter a pale one, oxygen isotopes recording the temperature of a vanished sky. If she trusted the ice to be a calendar, she could ask where the Metronome's light had been when each layer fell. The eclipse records gave her the medium ruler: Babylonian tablets, Chinese court astronomers, the terrifying bureaucratic patience of people who wrote down the sky's failures for three thousand years so that a woman on an atoll they could not imagine could borrow the exact instant the shadow crossed a named city. And the oral star-lore — Ropati's chant, the risings and settings and the names of the stars braided into the names of the dead — gave her the finest ruler she had for the last two thousand years, because the chant was not a record of the sky, it was a machine for keeping the sky, and it had kept.

Four clocks. None accurate to the second. All of them, together, accurate to the century.

She overlaid them.

She had done this before, in the days just past — that was how she had first killed the noise hypothesis, how she had proved the twist in the light was coherent across ten thousand years and not the random walk of an instrument or an atmosphere or a mind wanting a pattern. But proving a thing is coherent is not the same as reading it. A drumbeat is coherent. What she had not let herself do, until tonight, was the greedy thing, the thing she had been circling for a week like a dog around a snake: not just confirm the signal, but extract it. Pull the sequence out whole. Line up all four clocks, resample the polarization angle onto a single deep-time axis, and let the numbers fall where they fell.

She wrote the last of the code standing up, because sitting felt like presumption.

When she ran it the first time it failed, and she was almost relieved; the universe reasserting its indifference was a familiar comfort. A sign error in the precession correction. She found it, fixed it, and ran it again.

The plot drew itself across the screen in a stutter of green.

For a moment she did not understand what she was looking at, because she had been braced for a curve — a wave, a slow sinusoid, something continuous and physical, the kind of thing a star does. This was not a curve. This was steps. The polarization angle did not slide from one value to the next; it held, and held, and then it moved — a clean transition, a settling, a new plateau held for its own span, then another move. The angle was not varying. It was being set. Held at one position, then reset to another, the way a hand sets a stone down and leaves it and comes back and sets down another.

Discrete. Quantized. Not a wave. A string of values.

She counted the plateaus across the width of what she had. The average width came out to a hundred and eleven point six years, with a scatter tight enough to make her mouth go dry — tight enough that the width itself was clearly meant, a clock inside the clock, a beat the sender was keeping deliberately so that anyone downstream would know where one unit ended and the next began.

A hundred and twelve years to a symbol. And the symbols were not random. She ran the test she had run a hundred times on a hundred false alarms, the flat honest statistics that had killed every hope she'd ever had, and the sequence came back structured — non-random at a confidence that, in any paper about any ordinary thing, would have ended the argument before it started.

She stood in the hum and looked at it. Discrete symbols. Roughly a hundred of them, in the span she could reconstruct. Each a plateau a century wide. The whole thing reaching back — she checked the ice, checked her own arithmetic twice — something on the order of ten thousand years, and running forward to now, to the light presently falling on the dishes, to this room.

Ten thousand years of held stone.

She had used the word *phoneme* in her notes days ago, a placeholder, and had meant to strike it. She did not strike it now. It was the right word, and the knowing was the most frightening thing that had happened to her in a professional life not short of fright. These were not measurements of anything. A temperature curve, a magnetic field, an orbit — those varied, they did not *hold*. Nothing physical held a value dead level for a century and then stepped cleanly to the next and held that. Holding was expensive. Holding was work. Somewhere out there something was paying, symbol by symbol, the cost of being unambiguous, and it had been paying since before the first city on Earth had a wall.

She was not looking at a star. She was looking at somebody's care.

She sat down after all, because her legs had made the decision for her.

---

The satellite link to Aotearoa took eleven seconds to negotiate, which was long enough for her to reconsider three times and dial anyway. It was past midnight where Marcus was too. He answered on video with his reading glasses pushed up into grey hair, a book face-down on his chest, and the unguarded softness of a man woken who is not yet performing being awake.

"Tuli. It's the middle of the — is it the array? Did the Consortium finally—"

"Marcus. I need you to be a physicist for ten minutes and then you can go back to sleep and decide I've lost my mind."

He looked at her properly. Whatever he saw made him sit up, and the book slid to the floor, and he did not reach for it. "Okay," he said. "I'm awake. Go."

She told him. She was, if anything, colder than the data warranted, because she needed him to try to break it, not to be swept. She gave him the four clocks and the resampling and the sign error she'd caught and the width of the plateaus and the flat statistics. She watched his face do the thing she'd watched it do for thirty years — the skepticism, the hunt for the boring explanation, the small unconscious frown when he couldn't find one.

"Instrumental," he said. "The feed, the receiver, some slow thermal—"

"On a hundred-and-twelve-year period? Marcus, the receiver's nine years old. The dish is younger than my knees."

"Ionosphere. Faraday rotation, you know that better than I do, the plasma twists the—"

"Twists it fast and dirty and randomly. This is slow and clean and it steps. The ionosphere doesn't hold a value for a century and then decide to move. Nothing does. That's the whole point, Marcus, that's why I'm calling you at midnight instead of writing it up like a sane person."

He was quiet. On the screen his hand came up and rubbed his mouth, and she knew he was running his own numbers behind his own eyes. When he came back his voice had changed. The softness was gone and something more frightened had taken its place.

"If it's real," he said slowly, "you can't sit on it. Tuli. If this is real it's not yours. It's not the station's. It's — you have to take it up. Tonight. You call Halvorsen, you call the union, you get it timestamped and witnessed and out of that room before somebody decides to—"

"Before somebody decides to what."

"Before you have an accident with a hard drive," he said. "You know how this goes. The moment the Consortium understands what's on that array, Fenua stops being a decommissioning and becomes an asset. Halvorsen isn't a villain but she has a board, and the board will want it in a vault in Geneva with their name on the door and you thanked in a footnote. Go public *now*, while it's still yours to give away."

It was good advice, exactly the advice she would have given him. She sat with it and found that she did not want it, and made herself examine the wanting, because a scientist who mistakes her ego for her judgment is worse than useless.

"If I go public tonight," she said, "what do I have? I have a hundred plateaus and a claim. I don't have a translation. I don't have a single symbol I can tell you the *meaning* of. I have proof that somebody spoke and no idea what they said. And the instant it goes up the chain, it leaves my hands and goes to people with more machines and more money and more time than I've got, and Marcus — they'll get it. Eventually. In ten years, twenty, some kid who's in nappies right now will stand up at a conference and tell the world what it says." She heard her own voice thin out. "And I will be dead, or close to it, and I will have handed away the only thing I have ever found that mattered, at the exact moment before it meant anything, so that it would be *safe*."

"Tuli—"

"I'm not being romantic. I'm being selfish and I know the difference. I want to understand it before I let go of it. Not own it. Understand it. One symbol, so that when I hand it over I'm handing over a *word* and not a rumour." She looked at the dark beyond the window, the invisible sea saying its one long sentence against the reef. "The station has weeks. I want to spend them reading, not filing."

Marcus was silent for a long time. Eleven seconds of it, at least, was the link, but the rest was him.

"You always were," he said at last, and stopped, and started again gentler. "If you hold it and the drive dies, it's gone. If you hold it and you're wrong, you've thrown away sleep for nothing, and that I can live with. But if you hold it and you're *right*, and something happens to you or the station before you've made a copy that survives you—" He didn't finish. He didn't have to. "Promise me you back it up. Off-island. Tonight. Promise me the message survives you even if nothing else does."

"I promise," she said, and meant it, and the ease of meaning it frightened her more than the data had, because it meant some part of her had already begun to plan around her own absence.

"Then hold it," he said. "God help you. Hold it. And call me. Every day, Tuli. I mean it."

"Go to sleep, Marcus."

"Back it up first."

"Go to sleep."

He almost smiled. Then the link dropped him into pixels and gone.

---

She did back it up. Three copies, one to the encrypted store in Auckland that Marcus didn't know she'd kept the keys to, before she let herself go back to the plot. That was the promise kept, and it steadied her hands, and she was grateful to him in a way she would tell him someday and probably wouldn't.

Then she did the thing she had been saving since the plateaus first drew themselves and she had made herself do the discipline instead.

She ran the sequence.

Not analyzed — *played*. She mapped each phoneme's angle to a value, laid them end to end in time, and let the reconstruction run across the screen from the oldest she could reach to the newest, ten thousand years scrolling past in ninety seconds, the held stones of it setting themselves down one after another after another. And then she ran it backward. And then forward again, slower, watching not the values but the *shape* — the way the symbols grouped, the way certain ones recurred and framed the others, the redundancy Ropati had shown her in his own recited blood, the call and its answer, the structure that was there so that meaning could survive the loss of any single beat.

She had expected, if she'd expected anything, a list. A table. Facts, coordinates, the periodic table in some universal hand, here is where we are and here is what we are made of — the tidy first-contact of every hopeful paper she'd ever half-believed. A shout with information in it.

It was not a list. Lists did not have this shape. She had spent an evening a week ago in Ropati's house with his hand keeping time on the mat, and she knew now what she was seeing because he had taught her the difference without either of them knowing that was the lesson. This had the shape of a *sentence*. It opened, it built, it turned on itself and referred back to its own beginning, it carried one long line of intent from its first held stone to its last. Somebody was not reciting. Somebody was *saying*.

And the end of it — the newest phonemes, the ones still arriving, the light that had left its source when the pyramids were already old and was only reaching the dishes now — the end of it lifted. She saw it and did not trust it and ran it again and saw it again. The final symbols climbed. The angle stepped up and up in the last stretch, a rising cadence, the intonation of a voice going up at the close of a line, the way every human voice in every language she had ever heard goes up, when it has stopped telling you something and started asking.

It was not a broadcast.

It was a question.

And it was not finished. The last phoneme was still being held — out there, now, tonight, a stone the size of a century set down and not yet lifted — and the sea went on against the reef, and Tuli sat very still in the humming dark and understood that somewhere a mouth she would never see was, at this exact moment, in the middle of asking her something, and would go on asking it for another hundred years, long after she had stopped being anyone at all.
