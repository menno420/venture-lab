# Chapter 10 — The Handover

The Meridian Group's lawyer arrived on the Wednesday flight, which meant they were serious, because the Wednesday flight was small and expensive and only people with per diems took it. Tuli watched him come down onto the hot apron in a linen jacket already surrendering at the armpits, and felt something she had not expected — calm.

Marcus, beside her at the fence, felt it too and mistrusted it. "You've got a look. The one you had before you told Halvorsen the noise floor was in her own cabling."

"I was right about the cabling."

"You were right and she never forgave you. That's their acquisitions man. Meridian doesn't send acquisitions unless the science is already classified upstairs, or about to be. What did you find that I haven't?"

"Nothing new in the data. I found the flaw in their whole approach. They think the observatory is a thing you can own." She said it almost fondly, watching the lawyer pat his pockets for a phone that had no signal here and would have none for three days. "Salvage rights, hardware, the IP in the message. A national-security carve-out to relocate the array somewhere with a fence and a flag. All of it built on one assumption everyone in that fight shares — Meridian, the Consortium, even us, until last week. That whoever holds the *machine* holds the *discovery.*" She turned to him. "But the machine just receives. The answer can't live in a machine at all. So let them have the dishes. Let them fight over forty years of rusting aluminium. I'm going to put the actual thing somewhere their lawyers cannot follow."

—

It took nine days, and it was not clean, and it was the best work of her life.

She did not do it. That was the first thing she made herself understand, sitting in the council fale with Matua Ropati at her shoulder and eleven matai arrayed on the mats, their faces giving nothing away. She was not the author here. She was a returning daughter bringing a proposal to people who did not owe her a yes, and the fastest way to lose them was to arrive with the answer already written — the way the missionaries had, the way every ship that ever anchored in the lagoon had arrived with the future already decided and only the signatures left to collect.

So she brought a question instead. She let Ropati open — the fa‘alupega first, the long courteous naming of the house and its titles and its lands, and she sat through all of it without her old impatience, hearing it now as what it was, a system announcing its own integrity before it did business. Then she laid it out plainly, in Samoan where she had the Samoan and English where she did not, and let them ask.

The questions were not the ones the Consortium would have asked. No one asked what it cost or earned. They asked whose name would be on it, and what happened to the fishing ground off the point where the cables came ashore. They asked, sharply, whether this was another way for outsiders to plant a flag on Vaituga and call it partnership — and Tuli said yes, it could become exactly that, and the only defence was that the land and the array and the message all belong to the trust, and the trust belongs to the village and can never be sold, only held. She had brought the instrument for that. From a Suva firm that did customary land trusts — ownership that has no owner, that cannot be bought because there is no seller, that passes down the gafa itself.

"You are proposing," said Afioga Malaefou, the eldest of the matai, a woman with a voice like slow surf, "that we take the palagi machine they are fighting over as if it were a whale on the reef, and make it *aiga.* Family. Un-sellable. So that when they come with their papers, there is no hand to shake."

"Yes," Tuli said. "There can never be a seller. It becomes like the reef itself. You don't own the reef. The reef owns you a little, and you keep it."

The old woman looked at her a long moment. "Your grandmother argued exactly like this. Around a corner. She once won an entire taro plantation from my uncle by making him agree to three small things that added up to the plantation." A dry pause. "He never forgave her either."

"It runs in the family," Marcus murmured from the back, and got a laugh, and the laugh was the sound of the room deciding.

It was not unanimous and it was not quick. Two of the matai wanted the scholarship money Meridian had dangled, and they were not wrong to want it, and Tuli did not pretend they were. But Ropati rose then, with the fue, and he did not argue economics. He recited the line of the house back eleven generations to the canoe that made the first landfall, and stopped at the living edge of it, at the names of the children now in the village, and said: *this is the longest thing we own, and it has never once been for sale, and it is the only thing the ocean has never taken. I am asking you to add one verse.* And he told them the verse — the star, the question in the light, the answer that would take a thousand years to speak — and said, *let the machine hear for us, but let the family keep the promise, the way we have always kept promises, in our mouths and our children.* When he sat down the room was very quiet, and the two matai who wanted the money looked at their hands, and Malaefou said, "Sili." Enough. It is decided.

—

The decommissioning the Consortium had ordered became, on paper, a transfer, and the transfer became, in customary law, an absorption. Fenua Observatory ceased to be a Consortium asset scheduled for salvage and became the property of the Vaituga Sky Trust — a body with no shareholders, no head office, and no legal fiction the acquisitions man could locate, because it was not a fiction at all but a village, four hundred people and a genealogy, holding a thing in common the way they held the reef.

The lawyer met her once, on the second day, in the concrete shade of the control building. He was courteous and tired, not a bad man, only one who had flown a long way to buy something that had stopped being for sale while he was in the air.

"You understand this will be contested," he said. "The national-security angle is real; they'll argue the message can't be left in the hands of a —" he chose the word with care "— a non-state actor."

"Let them," Tuli said. "They'll be litigating against a genealogy. You can classify a machine. You can seize a hard drive. Tell me how you serve an injunction on a chant that lives in the heads of four hundred people who will still be reciting it after your company's name is a trivia question." She was not unkind about it. "You came to buy the discovery. It belongs to somebody your masters have no instrument for. I'm sorry you flew all this way."

He looked at her for a while. "Off the record. Will it work? A thousand years."

"I don't know," she said, and it was the truest thing she said to anyone that week. "But it's the first plan I've ever made that has a chance, and it isn't mine, and that's why."

—

Lani found her at the array on the ninth evening, the light going gold and long across the dishes, the lagoon gone the flat pink of a healing bruise. The girl had been at the edge of all of it for days — carrying kava at the fono, translating a form for the Suva lawyer, saying nothing, watching everything with those quick eyes that missed the beginning of no sentence.

"They're saying," Lani began, and stopped, and started again in the direct way of the young. "Matua Ropati says the verse needs someone to carry it. Someone young, who understands the machine *and* the chant. He looked at me when he said it. So did you. Everyone keeps looking at me and then away like it's rude to ask." She lifted her chin. "So I'm asking. Are you asking me?"

Tuli set down the spanner she had been not-using and faced her grand-niece squarely, because the girl deserved the truth without the softening the family put on things.

"No," she said. "I'm not asking you. And I want to be clear, because all your life people will try to decide it for you — the Consortium wanted you as a statistic, Meridian wants you as a photograph, your mother wants you home, the scholarship wants you gone. I will not stand in that line. I am telling you it exists, that you're one of about three people alive who could carry it, and that if you take the Auckland scholarship on Friday and become whatever the far side of the ocean makes of you, I will be *glad*, and your grandmother would be glad, and no one will have failed. You are not the last resort of a dying woman's plan. You are a girl with a whole life, and the life is yours."

Lani was quiet. The dishes ticked as they cooled, the metal letting go of the day's heat with small sounds like a language.

"But if I ask *you*," the girl said slowly, "you'll teach me."

"If you ask me, I will teach you everything I have, as fast as this heart lets me, and I don't know how fast that is." She would not lie about that either. "It would mean deferring the scholarship, not throwing it away — Auckland will hold the place two years; I made sure of that before I let anyone look at you, because I was not going to let this cost you a door. But it would mean staying. Learning polarimetry and receiver calibration in the same year you learn the fa‘alupega properly for the first time. Being, for the rest of your life, the person the village trusts to know why the star matters. It is a big thing to hang on a nineteen-year-old, and I hate that it's you, and there isn't anyone else, and that is not your problem to solve."

The girl walked out between the dishes, the way Tuli herself used to as a child, into the field of them, the ears of the ancestors tilted up at a sky that had already sent its light and would not know for ten thousand years whether anyone had answered. Lani stood there a long time, small under the great white bowls, and Tuli let her stand, and kept the va, and did not crowd the space where a life was turning.

When Lani came back her face had changed. Not softened — settled. The face of someone who has done the arithmetic and will not redo it every morning.

"My mother thinks I'd be staying because I'm scared to go," she said. "She'll tell people I got frightened of Auckland and dressed it up as duty. That's the only thing that almost put me on the plane. Proving her wrong — which is a stupid reason to leave. Leaving to win an argument."

"It is," Tuli agreed. "As stupid as staying to win one."

"So I'm not doing either." Lani squared herself. "I'm not staying for you, and I'm not leaving for her. I read the message you decoded. *Is anyone there continuous enough to answer.*" Her voice did something then that Tuli had heard only in the very old, a steadiness underneath the youth. "I want to be the answer. Not because you're dying and not because it's owed. Because I read it and can't unread it and I don't want to spend my life anywhere that isn't this. That's my reason. You don't get to have it, and my mother doesn't." She exhaled. "I'm asking you. Teach me."

Tuli found she could not speak for a moment. Then she said, "Yes," and it came out rough, and she did not mind.

—

They began the next morning, and the training was the strange braid Tuli had promised — an hour on the mat with Ropati and the ma‘au, then an hour in the control room with the polarimetry, and the girl's mind moved between them without the seam Tuli's own generation had been taught to feel, as if chant and code were two dialects of the one work: carrying a thing accurately through time.

And Tuli composed the reply, which was two documents that were really one. There was the physical plan: the retuned transmission, the polarisation increments, the maintenance intervals stretching out in a column of dates that ran off the bottom of the century and kept going, each a small act of faith aimed at a person not yet born. And there was the verse — the new line of the gafa, in Ropati's careful orator's Samoan, naming the star and the question and the family's promise, naming the keeping of the transmitter as a duty passed down like a title, the redundancy built in, three lines to hold it where one would fail, the call-and-response so a child a thousand years from now, reciting it wrong, would be corrected by the room.

She finished it on a Sunday and read it through, the machine's cold dates and the warm braided names, and thought: *this is the only thing I have made that might outlive the sound of my own name.* The thought did not frighten her. It was a kind of peace she had spent fifty-eight years looking for.

Marcus found her there with both documents spread on the console, the transmitter ready, the dishes retuned and idle, everything built and tested and one command away from the first increment of a message ten thousand years long.

"It's ready," he said. "You could send the first phoneme tonight. God knows your heart isn't promising anyone next spring."

"No," Tuli said, and she smiled, and it was not the smile of a tired woman refusing rest. "It's not mine to start alone in a room at night like something shameful. This message asks *is anyone there together enough to answer* — you don't answer that by yourself in the dark." She looked out at the dishes, at the lagoon beyond, gauging the moon on the water the way her grandmother had taught her before she ever learned a wavelength. "There's a right tide for it. The spring tide, when the whole lagoon breathes at once. The elders present, the chant sung out loud so the first word goes up with four hundred people knowing why. Lani will send it with me. Ropati will speak the verse into the gafa the same night, so the machine and the family start the promise in the same hour." She touched the console lightly, the way you touch something you are lending, not keeping. "Three weeks to the tide. I intend to be here for it."

She said it as a plan, not a hope, and Marcus, who knew the difference, said nothing, and stood beside her, and watched the light leave the water — light that had left the sun eight minutes before and would keep going, past the atoll, past the reef, out to where, if she had done all of this right, an answer was on its slow way home.
