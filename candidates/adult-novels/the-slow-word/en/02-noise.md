# Chapter 2 — Noise

She gave it one day. That was the bargain she made with herself over breakfast, standing at the same window with the same bad coffee: one day to kill it properly, to trace the drift back to whatever loose cable or lazy assumption had spawned it, and then she would tag the folder, run the deletion, and spend her last week on the island doing the dignified thing, which was packing.

One day. She had said the same thing to herself about the island once, at seventeen. *One year in Auckland and I'll know.* She was aware of the echo and chose to ignore it.

The first suspect was always RFI — radio-frequency interference, the junk the human world poured into the sky. A telescope like Fenua lived in a permanent war against its own species: microwave ovens, phone masts, the ignition of an outboard motor crossing the lagoon, a satellite passing over with its beacon lit, all of it screaming across the very frequencies the dishes strained to hear. Half of radio astronomy was subtraction. You spent your life learning the precise signature of human noise so that you could remove it and hear the faint true thing underneath.

But RFI was dirty and this was clean. That was the first wrongness, the one that had kept her hand off the mouse the evening before. Interference was spiky, bursty, tied to the clock of human life — it came at the frequencies of our machines, it pulsed with the working day, it vanished at three in the morning when the island slept and roared back when the generator kicked in. It did not produce a smooth, patient, decades-long lean in a single quantity. RFI was a drunk shouting in the street. This was something breathing very slowly in another room.

She checked anyway, because checking was the job. She pulled the interference logs and laid them against the drift and found no correlation — the angle leaned on quiet nights and loud ones alike, through the years the mast went up on the far motu and the years it was down, through generator failures and fuel shortages and the long clean COVID silence of the early twenties when half the world's machines went dark. If anything the drift was clearer in the quiet stretches, which was precisely backwards for interference and precisely right for a real signal from the sky.

She wrote that down. Then she crossed out the word *signal*, because she had not earned it, and wrote *feature* instead.

The second suspect was the hardware. Thirty years is a long time in the life of an instrument; over three decades Fenua had worn through four receiver packages, two entire cabling replacements, a firmware history that read like a genealogy of its own — this version begat that version begat the patch nobody documented. Any of it could produce a slow instrumental drift. A polarising element aging in the feed. A reference oscillator sliding by fractions with temperature over the seasons. That was, in fact, her leading theory, and the elegant thing about it, the thing that should have let her sleep, was that instrumental drift and a real celestial drift would look identical on the plot. You could not tell them apart by staring.

But you could tell them apart by the seams.

Because if the drift lived in the hardware, then it would jump when the hardware jumped. Every time a receiver was swapped, every time the cabling was replaced, the instrumental baseline would step — discontinuously, a cliff in the record — because the new component would have its own offset, its own private lie. She had the dates of every one of those changes; they were in the logbooks she had spent yesterday boxing, in her own hand and her predecessors', brown ink and blue. She spent the morning transcribing them and marking them on the plot as vertical lines, a picket fence of interventions across thirty years, and then she looked to see whether the drift jumped at the fences.

It did not.

The receiver swap of 2039 — nothing; the angle leaned through it as though the new hardware had inherited the old hardware's secret. The great recabling of 2047, when they had torn out every run and laid new fibre and she had personally recalibrated the whole polarisation chain from scratch, from *zero*, with a known reference source — nothing. The drift crossed that boundary smooth as water crossing a stone. And that was the wrongness that made the back of her neck go cold in the heat of the control room, because there was no honest way for an instrumental artefact to survive the complete replacement of the instrument. She had rebuilt the level itself in 2047. And the lean had simply continued from where it left off, as though it were not in the instrument at all but in the sky the instrument was pointed at, waiting on the other side of every repair.

She was still sitting with that when the call came in.

She almost let it ring out. Then she saw the name and answered, because there were perhaps three people left on Earth she would interrupt this for, and Marcus was two of them.

His face assembled itself on the screen in the particular way of a bad connection routed through a satellite and a sea — a beat behind his voice, so that he seemed always to be reacting a half-second late to his own life. He was in his office in Auckland, or what passed for his office, a window behind him full of the wrong hemisphere's grey, and he looked older than she let herself remember, which meant she looked older too.

"You're up early," she said.

"You're *answering*," he said. "I'd resigned myself to leaving voicemails until the barge came. Elin says you've gone feral out there. Her word."

"Elin can come and switch off her own telescope."

"That's roughly what I told her." He tilted his head, and even a half-second late she could see him reading her, the old reflex, thirty years of learning her weather. They had been together for four of those years, a long time ago, and then colleagues for the rest, which was either a failure or a rare success depending on the day you asked her. "You've got a face on," he said. "That's your found-something face. I know that face. I proposed to you with that face, once, and you told me you needed to check the data."

"I did not tell you that."

"You told me you needed to *sleep on it*, which from you is the same sentence." He was smiling. "Go on. What's got you."

She should have deflected. Instead she told him — carefully, without the forbidden words, the way you carry a full cup — the polarisation of the Metronome, the constant that wasn't, the drift too clean for RFI, the seams that didn't jump. She turned the screen and shared the plot, thirty years leaning gently across his display in Auckland, and watched his face do what hers had done: the flick away, the flick back, the animal refusal.

"Huh," he said, which from Marcus was a paragraph.

"Say the thing," she said. "Say calibration error. I need someone to say it who isn't me."

"Calibration error," he said obligingly. And then, because he was honest, which was the thing she had loved most and forgiven least: "Except you've already killed calibration error, or you wouldn't have shared your screen. The recabling was a clean reset. If it survived '47 it's not in your chain." He rubbed his jaw. "Reference source? Your calibrator's not variable, is it? If the thing you're measuring *against* is drifting —"

"Extragalactic. Rock steady. I checked it before you rang because I knew you'd ask." She heard the sharpness in her own voice and softened it. "Sorry. Yes. I checked."

"Course you did." He was quiet a moment, the connection humming. "Tuli. It's almost certainly *something*. Some slow galactic foreground, some rotation-measure thing, the interstellar medium doing something dull between you and the source over thirty years. That's not nothing — that could be a nice little paper, foreground variation, someone's thesis. But it's the boring kind of real." He said it gently, the way you offer someone a smaller grief to spare them a larger hope. "Don't build it into a cathedral. Not in your last week. You'll break your heart on an artefact and then the barge comes."

"I know," she said. "I know all of that. I taught you half of it."

"You taught me all of it and let me keep half." The old line; it had stopped being a joke years ago and become a kind of endearment, a thing they said instead of the thing they meant. "Send me the reduced series. I'll look tonight, properly, sober. And Tuli —" the half-second lag, his face grave and kind and far away — "eat something. Ring the girl. Lani. She messaged me, did you know that? Asking whether her grand-aunt was all right out there alone. She's frightened for you and she's using me as a relay because you won't answer your phone."

That landed somewhere she wasn't ready for it to land. "I'll ring her," she said, and did not.

They said the rest of it, the small closing courtesies of two people who had run out of large things to say to each other decades ago and kept the small ones burning like a pilot light. Then his face folded away and she was alone in the control room again with the heat and the reef and the leaning line.

*The boring kind of real.* He was right, of course. He was almost always right, which was its own quiet insult across thirty years. Foreground variation. The interstellar medium turning over in its sleep between here and there. She wanted it to be that. She was a careful woman and she wanted the boring answer and she reached, again, to close the folder on it.

And again her hand stopped, because a thought had surfaced while Marcus talked, a small cold thought she had been keeping underwater, and now it would not stay down.

She pulled more of the archive.

Not thirty years now — everything. She reached past her own tenure into the records of the dead men who had run Fenua before her, the founding data from the fifties reduced by hand and half-trusted, the gaps where the funding lapsed and the dishes stood idle, the whole patched imperfect history of this instrument's attention. She stitched it together through the afternoon while the light moved across the floor, correcting for every receiver, every recabling, every offset she could reconstruct, building the longest baseline that had ever been built for this one quantity — not thirty years but nearly seventy, the entire working life of the ears, every night anyone had ever pointed them at the Metronome.

And she plotted the polarisation angle across all of it, and sat back, and understood, slowly, that she had made a mistake in how she'd been thinking.

She had been looking for the *period*. That was the reflex — you see a drift, you assume it turns, you look for the cycle, the point where it swings back and repeats, because everything in her science eventually came round again: pulsars, orbits, seasons, the long patient wheel of recurrence that let you predict and therefore trust. She had assumed that somewhere in the record the angle would crest and begin its return, and that the length of that swing would tell her what she was looking at.

It never crested.

Seventy years of data, the longest such record on Earth, and the line only leaned — one direction, unhurried, unbroken, unturned. Whatever was moving out there had not, in seventy years of human watching, gotten far enough through its motion to show her so much as the top of a single curve. She was not looking at a cycle. She was looking at a *fragment* of one — a nearly straight segment, a chord cut from a circle so vast that seventy years of continuous observation rendered it, effectively, a line. The way the horizon at sea is a line, and is not.

She did the crude arithmetic in her head, the back-of-the-envelope thing you do before you let yourself believe, and it refused to come out small. If the drift she had measured was even a slice of a slow curve — if the whole record she possessed, everything the ears had ever heard, was a single early portion of one turn — then the period of that turn was not years. It was not decades. It was longer than the instrument. It was, on the most conservative estimate she could bear to make standing up, longer than the entire span of human radio astronomy, longer than the science itself, reaching back past the invention of the telescope and forward past every date she would live to write.

The coffee was cold again. She had not noticed making it.

Outside the tide had gone all the way out, and the reef lay exposed and steaming in the last of the light, the whole living shelf of it that had taken ten thousand years to grow one careful inch at a time, and beyond it the ocean held its enormous patience, and above it the sky was beginning to show the first stars — old light, light that had left its sources before she was born, before the island was named, before the first canoe. She had spent her whole life telling herself she understood how long *long* was. She had built a career on deep time, on the ages of stars, and she had carried the numbers the way you carry a foreign currency, confident of the arithmetic and numb to the value.

She looked at the line that would not turn, and for the first time in a very long time she was afraid — not the small professional fear of being wrong in a journal, but something older and colder, the vertigo of a small warm animal that has just been shown the true size of the room it has been living in. Something out there was turning. It was turning so slowly that seventy years of the most patient watching humanity had managed showed only a lean, a hint, the first faint syllable of a word too long to say in a lifetime.

She reached for a clean page in the logbook, the one box she had not yet taped shut, and wrote the date, and then, in the careful hand her grandmother would not have recognised and would have understood completely, a single question, so that in the morning she would know she had not dreamed it:

*How long?*
