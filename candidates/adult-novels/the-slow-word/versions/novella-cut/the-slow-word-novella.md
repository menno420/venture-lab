# The Slow Word — Novella Cut

*An abridged edition of the full novel. Adult / literary science fiction.
Content note: grief and terminal illness; colonial themes; one restrained,
non-graphic intimate scene; occasional strong language.*

---

## Chapter 1 — The Last Watch

Dawn came to Vaituga the way it always had, without ceremony and without mercy: a grey seam splitting the ocean from the sky, then a bruise of colour, then the sudden shameless gold that would, within the hour, turn the whole atoll into a griddle. Tuli had lived through eleven thousand of these dawns on this island and she still could not decide whether the light forgave the place or simply exposed it.

She logged the ears one at a time, as she had every morning for thirty years.

Dish One, azimuth nominal. Dish Two, elevation drive sticking again; she keyed the override and it shuddered free. Dish Three, Four, Five, the small ones out along the reef flat, their white bowls tilted skyward like a congregation that had been promised something and was still, patiently, waiting to receive it. Dish Six was dead and had been dead for two years. She logged it anyway. *Dish Six, no response.* There was a discipline in recording an absence. Her grandmother would have understood that, though her grandmother had never seen a dish in her life.

The ears. That was what the islanders called the place. *O taliga o le lagi.* The ears of the sky. Ropati had said it to her the first year, and she had corrected him — *they're antennas, Matua, they don't hear, they receive* — and he had looked at her with an expression she now recognised, at fifty-eight, as pity, though at twenty-eight she had read it as ignorance.

They were coming to take the ears away in nine days.

The decommission crew was already booked, a contractor out of Suva, three men and a barge. The dishes would be cut up on site. Tuli had a private, corrosive certainty that within a decade the bowls would be roofing and rainwater tanks, and that this was probably the most honest afterlife an instrument of pure knowledge could hope for in a place like this.

Her own afterlife was less clear. She had a flat in Auckland she had not slept in for six years and a pension the Consortium described, in the same email that terminated her post, as *generous under the circumstances.* The circumstances were that Fenua Observatory had produced, in three decades, exactly one result of note — a refinement to the timing model of a millisecond pulsar — and she did not, in her more rigorous moods, entirely disagree with the arithmetic. That was the worst of it. She had spent a career learning to follow evidence, and the evidence said Fenua was a beautiful, expensive mistake, and she had given it her whole life.

So: the boxes. The Consortium wanted the scientifically significant portions transferred to Bonn and the remainder deleted, and *remainder* was doing an enormous amount of work in that sentence — the calibration runs, the failed observations, the nights of pure static. She was to catalogue it, tag what mattered, and mark the rest for erasure, because once they switched off a telescope there was no one left to argue with the deletion script.

Into the boxes went the paper. Logbooks in a dozen hands, the ink going brown; a wall calendar from 2034 with a child's drawing of a fish taped to July, and she could not remember whose child, or why the fish, and she put it in the box for keeping rather than the box for burning. She was allowed a few irrational preservations.

There had been, three weeks ago, a *saofa‘i* — the conferring of a chiefly title on one of her cousins — and with it the recital of the *gafa*, the genealogy, the long chanted river of names that ran back past the missionaries and past the first canoes and out, if you believed it, into the founding dark. Ropati, the *tulāfale*, the orator, the keeper, had sent word that a place would be kept for her. She had not gone. She had sent money and an apology and a lie about the array requiring her, and had sat in this room while forty generations were spoken aloud across the lagoon, and told herself she was working.

The truth was closer to fear, and she knew it, the way you know a tooth is going bad before it aches. Fa‘amanu had tried to give her the chants when she was small, and Tuli had learned them in the effortless way children learn everything, and then she had gone to Auckland at seventeen and put them down, deliberately, the way you put down a language you have decided is beneath the person you intend to become. She had traded a chant for a spectrum. And now she was fifty-eight and the sky was being switched off and the woman who had taught her the songs was thirty years dead, and Tuli could not have stood up in front of the family and chanted the *gafa* if her life had depended on it, because she had let it fall out of her on purpose, and there is a particular shame reserved for the things we lose not through carelessness but through choice.

She taped the box shut.

By mid-morning she had moved from the paper to the drives, deciding, folder by folder, whether thirty years of one woman's attention had produced anything the species needed to keep. Mostly it had not. She was ruthless because ruthlessness was the only mercy left. Grief and relief so tangled you could not have separated them with a scalpel.

Then she came to the Metronome.

It had a catalogue name, but everyone who had ever worked at Fenua called it the Metronome, because that was what it was: a millisecond pulsar, a dead star spinning six hundred times a second, sweeping its lighthouse beam across the Earth with a regularity that made atomic clocks look nervous. It was Fenua's one real object, the source of the one real paper, the thing the dishes had returned to night after night for thirty years the way the tide returns.

The timing data was already flagged for Bonn. But there was more than timing. There was polarisation — the angle of the light, a quantity Fenua had recorded faithfully and, so far as anyone had ever determined, pointlessly, because the Metronome's polarisation was famously, boringly constant. She had, in fact, spent years using it to calibrate the receivers, the way a carpenter trusts a spirit level: not because the level is interesting but because it does not move.

She opened the polarisation record to tag it for deletion, and pulled thirty years of it onto the screen as a single long trace, the angle plotted against time, expecting a flat line.

It was not quite a flat line.

She saw it and did not believe it and looked away and looked back, which is the oldest reflex in the science, the animal refusal of the eye to accept what it has been told cannot be there. Across the full width of the plot — across three decades — the polarisation angle drifted. Not much. A fraction of a degree, a lean so slight that on any single night, any single year, it would have vanished under the noise, which was exactly why nobody had ever seen it. But laid out end to end, all thirty years at once, the drift was there: faint, monotonous, and — she leaned closer, her bad coffee cold at her elbow, the heat forgotten — regular. Impossibly, stupidly regular. As if something were turning, very slowly, somewhere out past the reach of every reasonable idea she had.

Calibration error. It had to be calibration error, an artefact of thirty years of drifting instruments and swapped receivers and firmware nobody remembered writing. That was the boring, correct, professional answer, and she had built a career on preferring the boring correct answer to the beautiful one, because the beautiful ones were almost always wrong and the graveyard of astronomy was full of people who had fallen in love with an artefact.

She reached for the mouse to tag the folder for deletion.

Her hand did not do it.

Outside, the tide had turned; she could hear it in the changed voice of the reef. The dishes below the ridge held their patient upward attitude, listening, as they had listened before she came and would not listen after she was gone, to light that had left its source ten thousand years ago and was only now, this morning, arriving at a switched-off telescope at the edge of the world.

She did not delete the folder. She flagged it — a single yellow tag, *hold, review* — and told herself it meant nothing, that a careful woman checks even her artefacts before she burns them. Then she sat for a while in the heat and looked at the faint, patient, impossible slope on the screen, and did not go back to the boxes, and let the coffee go entirely cold.

## Chapter 2 — Noise

She gave it one day. That was the bargain she made with herself over breakfast: one day to kill it properly, to trace the drift back to whatever loose cable or lazy assumption had spawned it, and then she would tag the folder, run the deletion, and spend her last week on the island doing the dignified thing, which was packing.

The first suspect was always RFI — the junk the human world poured into the sky. But RFI was dirty and this was clean. Interference was spiky, bursty, tied to the clock of human life. It did not produce a smooth, patient, decades-long lean in a single quantity. RFI was a drunk shouting in the street. This was something breathing very slowly in another room. She checked anyway, because checking was the job, and found no correlation — if anything the drift was clearer in the quiet stretches, which was precisely backwards for interference and precisely right for a real signal from the sky.

She wrote that down. Then she crossed out the word *signal*, because she had not earned it, and wrote *feature* instead.

The second suspect was the hardware. Over three decades Fenua had worn through four receiver packages and two entire cabling replacements. Any of it could produce a slow instrumental drift — and instrumental drift and a real celestial drift would look identical on the plot. You could not tell them apart by staring.

But you could tell them apart by the seams. If the drift lived in the hardware, it would jump when the hardware jumped — every receiver swap, every recabling, a cliff in the record where the new component brought its own offset, its own private lie. She spent the morning marking every one of those changes on the plot as vertical lines, a picket fence of interventions across thirty years, and then she looked to see whether the drift jumped at the fences.

It did not.

The receiver swap of 2039 — nothing; the angle leaned through it as though the new hardware had inherited the old hardware's secret. The great recabling of 2047, when she had personally recalibrated the whole polarisation chain from *zero* — nothing. The drift crossed that boundary smooth as water crossing a stone. And that was the wrongness that made the back of her neck go cold, because there was no honest way for an instrumental artefact to survive the complete replacement of the instrument. The lean had simply continued, as though it were not in the instrument at all but in the sky the instrument was pointed at, waiting on the other side of every repair.

She was still sitting with that when the call came in. She saw the name and answered, because there were perhaps three people left on Earth she would interrupt this for, and Marcus was two of them.

His face assembled itself on the screen a beat behind his voice. He looked older than she let herself remember, which meant she looked older too. They had been together for four years, a long time ago, and then colleagues for the rest, which was either a failure or a rare success depending on the day you asked her.

"You've got a face on," he said. "That's your found-something face. I proposed to you with that face, once, and you told me you needed to *sleep on it*." He was smiling. "Go on. What's got you."

She should have deflected. Instead she told him — carefully, without the forbidden words, the way you carry a full cup — the constant that wasn't, the drift too clean for RFI, the seams that didn't jump. She shared the plot, and watched his face do what hers had done: the flick away, the flick back, the animal refusal.

"Huh," he said, which from Marcus was a paragraph. And then, because he was honest, which was the thing she had loved most and forgiven least: "You've already killed calibration error, or you wouldn't have shared your screen. If it survived '47 it's not in your chain. Tuli. It's almost certainly *something*. Some slow galactic foreground doing something dull between you and the source. That could be a nice little paper. But it's the boring kind of real." He said it gently, the way you offer someone a smaller grief to spare them a larger hope. "Don't build it into a cathedral. Not in your last week. You'll break your heart on an artefact and then the barge comes."

"I know," she said. "I know all of that. I taught you half of it."

"You taught me all of it and let me keep half." The old line; it had stopped being a joke years ago and become a kind of endearment. "Send me the reduced series. I'll look tonight, properly, sober. And Tuli — eat something. Ring your grand-niece. Lani messaged me, did you know that? She's frightened for you and she's using me as a relay because you won't answer your phone."

That landed somewhere she wasn't ready for it to land. "I'll ring her," she said, and did not.

*The boring kind of real.* He was right, of course. He was almost always right, which was its own quiet insult across thirty years. She wanted it to be that. She was a careful woman and she wanted the boring answer, and she reached, again, to close the folder on it.

And again her hand stopped, because a thought had surfaced while Marcus talked, a small cold thought she had been keeping underwater, and now it would not stay down.

She pulled more of the archive. Not thirty years now — everything. She reached past her own tenure into the records of the dead men who had run Fenua before her, and stitched it together through the afternoon, correcting for every receiver, every recabling, every offset she could reconstruct — the longest baseline ever built for this one quantity, nearly seventy years, the entire working life of the ears.

And she plotted the polarisation angle across all of it, and sat back, and understood, slowly, that she had made a mistake in how she'd been thinking. She had been looking for the *period*. That was the reflex — you see a drift, you assume it turns, you look for the cycle, because everything in her science eventually came round again.

It never crested. Seventy years of data, the longest such record on Earth, and the line only leaned — one direction, unhurried, unbroken, unturned. She was not looking at a cycle. She was looking at a *fragment* of one — a nearly straight segment, a chord cut from a circle so vast that seventy years of continuous observation rendered it, effectively, a line. The way the horizon at sea is a line, and is not.

She did the crude arithmetic in her head, and it refused to come out small. If the whole record she possessed was a single early portion of one turn — then the period of that turn was not years. It was not decades. It was longer than the instrument, longer than the entire span of human radio astronomy, longer than the science itself, reaching back past the invention of the telescope and forward past every date she would live to write.

Outside the tide had gone all the way out, and the reef lay exposed and steaming in the last of the light, the whole living shelf of it that had taken ten thousand years to grow one careful inch at a time, and above it the sky was beginning to show the first stars. She had spent her whole life telling herself she understood how long *long* was. She had carried the numbers the way you carry a foreign currency, confident of the arithmetic and numb to the value.

She looked at the line that would not turn, and for the first time in a very long time she was afraid — not the small professional fear of being wrong in a journal, but the vertigo of a small warm animal that has just been shown the true size of the room it has been living in. Something out there was turning. It was turning so slowly that seventy years of the most patient watching humanity had managed showed only a lean, a hint, the first faint syllable of a word too long to say in a lifetime.

She reached for a clean page in the logbook and wrote the date, and then, in the careful hand her grandmother would not have recognised and would have understood completely, a single question, so that in the morning she would know she had not dreamed it:

*How long?*

## Chapter 3 — The Wrong Length

By morning she had convinced herself it was real, which meant the next thing she had to do was try to kill it. That was the part nobody outside the work understood — that belief and destruction were the same gesture, aimed twice. She would build the modulation a model and then she would spend the day looking for the mistake inside it.

She fitted it. She tried a line and it wanted to bend. She tried a parabola and it fit better, which meant nothing — a parabola will flatter anything over a short enough baseline. She tried a sinusoid, because that was what her gut had been muttering since two in the morning, and the machine chewed and returned a period.

Tuli read the number and laughed, one short unhappy sound in the empty hall.

The period was longer than the data. The curvature was real, the bend was in the numbers and would not leave, but the *period* was a rumour the software had invented to explain a fragment — it had taken her sliver of arc and drawn, very confidently, the whole circle it might belong to, and the confidence was worth nothing at all.

"You don't know that," she told the screen. "You're guessing."

She widened the error bars herself, by hand, until they were honest, and the fit sagged into a grey fan of possibility — four hundred years, or nine hundred, or eleven thousand, because from inside a keyhole every corridor looks the same length. Whatever the Metronome's light was doing, it was doing it on a scale that made the entire radio era — every dish humanity had ever turned to the sky — a single held breath in a very long song.

She made coffee and stood at the louvres while the sun came up hot and immediate over the reef. Her grandmother had said, once, that the tide was the island breathing, and that you could set your whole life by it if you were patient enough to learn its longer rhythms — tides inside the tides, a nesting of clocks. Tuli had thought it superstition then. She thought now that Fa‘amanu had simply been a better observer than her granddaughter had been willing to notice.

The trouble was that to *prove* the shape she needed to see more of it, and to see more of it she needed time she did not have and would never have — no one would. You could not point a telescope at four centuries. The only way to catch a wave that long was to have already been watching before you were born, and to keep watching after you died, and to have the watching passed hand to hand without a single link lying about what it saw. Nobody did that. Nobody kept a measurement clean across four hundred years.

She was still standing there when the outer door banged and a voice called into the hall, "Aunty? You in here or you finally left me the whole place?"

Lani came in out of the glare in a way that made the room seem, briefly, absurdly full — nineteen and unhurried, sandals slapping, a green coconut in her free hand with the top already macheted off. She had Fa‘amanu's forehead. Tuli noticed it every time and every time refused to say so.

"You're supposed to call ahead," Tuli said. "This is a scientific installation."

"It's a shed full of old aunties talking to God." Lani dropped into a wheeled chair and pushed herself in a slow arc, taking the place in with the frank ownership of the young. "You look terrible. You sleep?"

"I was working."

"On the thing that ate your face." Lani nodded at the screen without really looking, and then — because she was Fa‘amanu's blood too, and sharper than she let the island see — she did look. "That's a wobble."

"It's a polarisation angle."

"It's a wobble. I did one year of physics, Aunty, I get to say wobble." She rolled closer. "What's it do?"

And Tuli, who had told no one, found the words coming out anyway, because the alternative was to keep holding them alone. "It bends," she said. "The light from a very steady thing, a lighthouse, out past the middle of the galaxy. It shouldn't bend and it does. Slowly. So slowly that in thirty years of watching I've caught maybe a hundredth of one full bend. I can see that it's a curve but I can't see the size of the curve. I need —" She stopped.

"You need more years," Lani said.

"I need more years than there are."

Lani considered this with the seriousness she gave to things that weren't about herself. "Than there are for you," she said, "or than there are?"

Tuli looked at her.

"Because those are different," Lani went on, blithe, not knowing she had said anything, which was how the true things usually arrived. "Anyway. That's not why I came." She produced a folded printout, the scholarship's crest at the top — Auckland, the marine biology place. "They moved the deadline up. I have to say yes or no by the end of the month."

"And?"

"And I don't know." She said it lightly and it landed heavy. "Everybody knows for me. Mum knows, the pastor knows, the whole village has a strong opinion about what the clever one should do. You're the only one who left, so. I wanted to ask the one who left."

"Don't ask me," Tuli said, more sharply than she meant. "I'm the worst person on this atoll to ask."

Lani let that sit, and did not push, which was its own kind of skill. Then she stood, and delivered what she had actually come to deliver. "Matua Ropati's reciting tonight," she said. "The gafa. All of it, the long version. It's at the fale, after the sun. You should come." She raised a hand before Tuli could refuse. "He asked after you. He always asks after you. It kills him a little, you know, that you're up here talking to the sky and you won't come down and hear your own name in the chant."

"I have work."

"You have a wobble you can't measure." Lani said it kindly, which was worse. "It'll keep. It's four hundred years long, you said. One night." She was already walking backward toward the glare of the door, grinning again, forgiving her in advance the way the island always did.

And she was gone, sandals on the concrete, the door banging, the day pouring back in white and enormous where she had stood.

Tuli did not go to the recital. She told herself she would, and then the afternoon ate itself, and by the time it was done the sun was down and the fale would be full of people she'd half-avoided for two years, and she could not, she found, make her body walk down the hill toward them.

Then she went back to the hall, and sat down in front of the too-long curve, and thought about what Lani had said without knowing she'd said it. *Than there are for you, or than there are.*

She thought: the measurement needs to outlive the measurer.

She thought: that has never once been done. Not cleanly. Every long record humanity kept was a game of telephone that ended in nonsense — copies of copies, drift and rot and the well-meaning lie. You could not carry a number across ten generations without dropping it. Everyone knew that.

The curve waited on the screen, patient, unmeasurable, real. To catch it she would need a record kept faithfully — without error — across centuries, longer than the radio era, longer than the telescope. She needed a thousand-year measurement made by people who had never heard of a pulsar, made and kept and passed on intact, so that she could lay it against her seventy years and finally see how big the wave was.

Somewhere down the hill, faint under the reef's endless collapse, a single voice had begun to carry up on the night wind — old, unhurried, rising and falling in a shape her grandmother's mouth had once made over a child who hadn't wanted to learn it. Matua Ropati, saying the names. Saying the line. Carrying a list of the dead back thirty generations without a page of writing, exactly as it had been carried before him, and before him, and before that.

Tuli sat very still.

Where in the world, she had been asking herself all day, does one find a record that spans a thousand years.

The wind brought her the answer and she had spent her whole life walking away from it.

## Chapter 4 — Deep Archives

She did not sleep on it so much as let it work on her, the way seawater works on a mooring line, and in the morning she went hunting for a thousand years.

The proper places first, because she was a proper scientist and because the improper place frightened her. Anything modulating the pulsar's light on a scale of centuries would also, faintly, be written into any long record sensitive to the same physics. What she needed were proxies. Old, patient, independent measurements that reached back past the radio era and did not know her curve existed and so could not be flattering it.

She started with ice. The great polar cores were public, or public enough, and she spent the morning scraping cadence data out of them — the annual banding, hunting for any faint periodic modulation in the range her fit had coughed up. Something. Maybe. A whisper of power in the band she wanted, right at the edge of meaning nothing — the kind of thing that vanished the moment you looked at it straight and came back when you looked away, which was either a real faint process or a scientist wanting one too badly. She had been on both sides of that line and knew how thin it was.

She went to the eclipse records next, and these she loved — the Babylonian tablets, the Chinese court astronomers, generations of frightened careful men who had watched the sky misbehave and written down *when*, to the hour, on clay and silk and vellum, across three thousand years. If her modulation touched the propagation of the pulsar's light at all, it might leave a residue in the timing residuals people had already teased from those ancient observations. She overlaid what she could find. She widened every error bar until it hurt.

By late afternoon she had three proxies, all faint, all individually dismissible. Three whispers were still just whispers. But they whispered in the same band. And to test whether they whispered *in phase* — whether they were the same voice reaching her by three different roads — she needed a fourth road, and an anchor: a long clean measurement, faithfully kept, from the part of the world that had no telescopes and no clay tablets and had crossed the largest ocean on Earth anyway, by reading the sky.

She sat with that for a while, the rum bottle within reach and untouched. Then she went and washed her face and put on a proper shirt and walked down the hill, in the last of the heat, to Matua Ropati's fale.

He was on the raised platform in the open-sided house, a very old man arranged with great dignity on a pandanus mat, and he watched her come the whole way up the path without pretending not to. He did not get up. Getting up was not what he was for.

"Teuila," he said, when she had slipped off her sandals and sat, low, the way you sat before a matai, the way her body remembered even as her mind protested it. Not *Tuli*. The whole name, the name that was hers in the chant. "So the sky let you go."

"For an afternoon."

"An afternoon." He turned the word over as though inspecting it for damage. "You did not come last night. Your name was said. Your grandmother's name was said. Fa‘amanu, who taught you to say them back, before you decided the palagi's machines were a better use of a Va‘a mouth." He let that sit in the va between them, the charged space-between, and did not soften it. "Thirty years I have been putting your name in the line for people who ask where you have gone. It is a long time to answer for someone who will not answer for herself."

Tuli looked at the mat. She had come to ask a favour of this man and he was going to make her earn the right to ask it, and she found she could not resent it, because he was right.

"I know, Matua," she said. "I'm sorry. I stayed away too long."

"You did." He accepted the apology without discounting it. Then, because he was stern and not cruel: "But you came. Fa‘amanu's forehead came down the hill. What does the sky want, that it sends you to an old man's mat?"

So she told him. Haltingly at first, in her science, and then — watching him not follow the words but follow *her* — she found the shape of it in the only terms that would carry. There is a light, she said, from very far, older than the island. It carries a mark. The mark changes, very slowly, so slowly that one lifetime cannot see the whole of it, and to read it I must lay my years against something much longer, something kept true across many lifetimes. And I have looked in the ice and in the old dead astronomers' books, and they whisper but they will not tell me where in the turning we are. And I remembered — she said, and stopped, and made herself say it — I remembered the gafa. That you keep the names true across thirty generations without a page of writing. That your fathers kept the star-paths in the chant, unchanged, for a thousand years and more.

The old man was quiet for a long moment.

"You want the star-lore," he said. "The fetū. The paths."

"I want to know how you keep it true," she said. "Across so long. I want to understand how it doesn't rot."

And Matua Ropati, tulāfale, keeper of the fa‘alupega, looked at the last astronomer of Fenua for a while longer, and then he did the thing she had walked down the hill both hoping and dreading — he closed his eyes, and he began to recite.

She had heard it as a child and slept through it. She had not heard it as what she was now.

The gafa came out of him slow and enormous, name giving birth to name, and she listened this time the way she listened to data — for structure, for architecture — and the architecture undid her. It was not a list. A list would have died in three generations; a single dropped name and everything after it is orphaned. Each name arrived *braced* — with its pairings, its marriage, its place fixed against two or three others, so that if one link ever failed the others held the shape of the gap. The chant doubled back on itself, call and answer, the young men's voices coming in from the shadows of the fale, catching the lines, throwing them back, so that no single mouth carried the whole load. When Ropati's memory hesitated a half-beat, another voice supplied the name, and he took it back, and the line went on, self-correcting, redundant, *robust*, a thousand years of the dead marching in ranks so deep that you could shoot great holes in them and the ranks would close.

Tuli sat on the mat with her scientist's heart going hard, because she was hearing an error-correcting code. Not a metaphor for one. The thing itself. Redundancy, parity, distributed storage, call-and-response checksum — every trick her civilisation had invented in the last eighty years to shove a number across a noisy channel without corruption, these people had invented to shove their ancestors across a noisy millennium, and theirs had *worked*, theirs was working right now, in the mouths of teenagers in the dark, the names arriving unrotted from before the first European saw this ocean.

*This* was how you kept a measurement true across a thousand years. Not by writing it down, where it would burn or moulder. You built it into a living structure that repaired itself every time it was spoken, and you made the speaking a duty and an identity, so that the line would fight to keep saying it long after the reasons were forgotten.

The chant reached the star-paths. She heard the rising points named, the roads the great canoes had steered by — a sky-clock, calibrated by ten thousand crossings, held in the same self-correcting structure as the names of the dead, and just as true. This was her fourth road and her anchor both.

When it was done — when Ropati opened his eyes and the young voices went quiet and the reef came back into the silence — he looked at her and saw that something had happened in her face.

"You heard it," he said.

"I heard it, Matua. The way I should have heard it forty years ago."

He nodded, once, and something in the va between them eased, not all the way, but enough. "Come back," he said. "You have things to learn and I have not much time to teach them. That is a bad joke the two of us are standing in. Bring your machine's questions. We will see what the old line remembers."

She walked back up the hill in the dark with the star-paths still turning in her head, and she did not go to bed. She went to the console, and she took the rising points from the chant — dated, phased, anchored across centuries — and she added them to the ice and the eclipses and the decades of the Metronome's slow bend, all of it forced onto one long axis reaching back past the radio era, past the telescope, past writing.

She overlaid them. For a moment the screen was only a mess, four ragged fragments at four different scales, and her heart dropped. Then she phased them — anchored the whole thing on the star-clock, the one clean thousand-year measurement she had, and let the others slide into their proper places against it.

They lined up.

Not perfectly. Not without slack. But the ice's faint whisper and the dead astronomers' residue and her own decades of bend all fell onto the *same curve*, each fragment a different arc of one enormous circle, and where they overlapped they agreed, and where they didn't overlap they extended each other, the way the braced names in the chant extended across the gaps.

Tuli sat back in the breathing dark of the empty hall and looked at ten thousand years of a single coherent signal assembled out of ice and clay and the mouths of the dead, and she said, out loud, to no one, in a voice gone strange:

"It's not noise."

Somewhere out past the galactic centre a lighthouse had been bending its light on purpose, and she had just, for the first time, seen far enough down the wave to be sure. Now she only had to learn to read it.

## Chapter 5 — Ten Thousand Years

The trick, in the end, was to stop trusting the clock.

The modulation she was chasing did not live in the clock. It lived in the slow twist of the light's plane as it arrived, and that twist changed on a scale so long that no clock built by anything with a pulse could hold it. To read it she had to abandon the second and think in centuries, and to think in centuries she had to borrow other people's clocks — clocks that ran in ice and stone and memorised speech. Four clocks. None accurate to the second. All of them, together, accurate to the century.

What she had not let herself do, until tonight, was the greedy thing: not just confirm the signal, but extract it. Pull the sequence out whole. She wrote the last of the code standing up, because sitting felt like presumption. The first run failed — a sign error in the precession correction, and she was almost relieved; the universe reasserting its indifference was a familiar comfort. She fixed it and ran it again.

The plot drew itself across the screen in a stutter of green.

For a moment she did not understand what she was looking at, because she had been braced for a curve — the kind of thing a star does. This was not a curve. This was steps. The polarisation angle held, and held, and then it moved — a clean transition, a new plateau held for its own span, then another move. The angle was not varying. It was being *set*. Held at one position, then reset to another, the way a hand sets a stone down and leaves it and comes back and sets down another.

Discrete. Quantised. Not a wave. A string of values.

She counted the plateaus. The average width came out to a hundred and eleven point six years, with a scatter tight enough to make her mouth go dry — tight enough that the width itself was clearly meant, a clock inside the clock, so that anyone downstream would know where one unit ended and the next began.

A hundred and twelve years to a symbol. And the symbols were not random. She ran the flat honest statistics that had killed every hope she'd ever had, and the sequence came back structured — non-random at a confidence that would have ended any ordinary argument before it started.

She stood in the hum and looked at it. Roughly a hundred symbols, each a plateau a century wide, the whole thing reaching back something on the order of ten thousand years and running forward to now, to the light presently falling on the dishes, to this room.

Ten thousand years of held stone.

She had used the word *phoneme* in her notes days ago, a placeholder, and had meant to strike it. She did not strike it now. Nothing physical held a value dead level for a century and then stepped cleanly to the next and held that. Holding was expensive. Holding was work. Somewhere out there something was paying, symbol by symbol, the cost of being unambiguous, and it had been paying since before the first city on Earth had a wall.

She was not looking at a star. She was looking at somebody's care.

She sat down after all, because her legs had made the decision for her. Then she called Marcus. It was past midnight where he was too. He answered on video with his reading glasses pushed up into grey hair, a book face-down on his chest.

"Marcus. I need you to be a physicist for ten minutes and then you can go back to sleep and decide I've lost my mind."

He looked at her properly. Whatever he saw made him sit up, and the book slid to the floor, and he did not reach for it. "Okay," he said. "I'm awake. Go."

She told him. She was, if anything, colder than the data warranted, because she needed him to try to break it, not to be swept. She gave him the four clocks and the resampling and the sign error she'd caught and the width of the plateaus and the flat statistics, and watched his face hunt for the boring explanation and not find one.

"Instrumental," he said. "The feed, some slow thermal—"

"On a hundred-and-twelve-year period? The receiver's nine years old."

"Ionosphere. Faraday rotation—"

"Twists it fast and dirty and randomly. This is slow and clean and it *steps*. The ionosphere doesn't hold a value for a century and then decide to move. Nothing does. That's the whole point."

He was quiet. When he came back, something more frightened had taken the softness's place.

"If it's real," he said slowly, "you can't sit on it. It's not yours. You get it timestamped and witnessed and out of that room. The moment the Consortium understands what's on that array, Fenua stops being a decommissioning and becomes an asset. The board will want it in a vault in Geneva with their name on the door and you thanked in a footnote. Go public *now*, while it's still yours to give away."

It was good advice, exactly the advice she would have given him. She sat with it and found that she did not want it, and made herself examine the wanting.

"If I go public tonight," she said, "what do I have? A hundred plateaus and a claim. Proof that somebody spoke and no idea what they said. And the instant it goes up the chain, it goes to people with more machines and more money and more time than I've got, and they'll get it — eventually, in ten years, twenty. And I will be dead, or close to it, and I will have handed away the only thing I have ever found that mattered, at the exact moment before it meant anything, so that it would be *safe*." She looked at the dark beyond the window. "I want to understand it before I let go of it. Not own it. Understand it. One symbol, so that when I hand it over I'm handing over a *word* and not a rumour."

Marcus was silent a long time. "Promise me you back it up," he said at last. "Off-island. Tonight. Promise me the message survives you even if nothing else does."

"I promise," she said, and the ease of meaning it frightened her more than the data had, because it meant some part of her had already begun to plan around her own absence.

"Then hold it," he said. "God help you. Hold it. And call me. Every day, Tuli. I mean it."

She did back it up — three copies, one to an encrypted store in Auckland, before she let herself go back to the plot. Then she did the thing she had been saving.

She ran the sequence. Not analysed — *played*. She mapped each phoneme's angle to a value and let the reconstruction run across the screen, ten thousand years scrolling past in ninety seconds, the held stones of it setting themselves down one after another. Then she ran it again, slower, watching not the values but the *shape* — the way the symbols grouped, the redundancy, the call and its answer.

She had expected, if she'd expected anything, a list. Facts, coordinates, the periodic table in some universal hand. A shout with information in it.

It was not a list. Lists did not have this shape. This had the shape of a *sentence*. It opened, it built, it turned on itself and referred back to its own beginning, it carried one long line of intent from its first held stone to its last. Somebody was not reciting. Somebody was *saying*.

And the end of it — the newest phonemes, the ones still arriving — the end of it lifted. She saw it and did not trust it and ran it again and saw it again. The final symbols climbed, a rising cadence, the intonation of a voice going up at the close of a line, the way every human voice in every language she had ever heard goes up, when it has stopped telling you something and started asking.

It was not a broadcast.

It was a question.

And it was not finished. The last phoneme was still being held — out there, now, tonight, a stone the size of a century set down and not yet lifted — and Tuli sat very still in the humming dark and understood that somewhere a mouth she would never see was, at this exact moment, in the middle of asking her something, and would go on asking it for another hundred years, long after she had stopped being anyone at all.

## Chapter 6 — The Slow Ones

For three days she did not try to read the question. She tried to read the questioner. She told herself it was the disciplined order of operations. The truth was simpler: she was afraid of the meaning and unafraid of the mathematics, and so she went where the fear was thinner.

A hundred and eleven point six years to a symbol. She did the arithmetic she'd been avoiding, on the concrete step outside the control room in the grey before the heat. If a phoneme took a hundred and twelve years, then a word took four or five centuries, and a sentence ran to a thousand years and more. She tried to make it human and it would not go human. Speech, for a person, sat on the breath — a syllable the length of one exhalation. That was the body setting the tempo.

So she inverted it. If these beings spoke at the speed they were built to speak, then a single heartbeat of theirs was a century of Earth's. A human life, her own fifty-eight years, all of it — the islands and the leaving and Marcus and the grandmother in the ground — was not even one full beat of the thing that was speaking. It was less than the pause a person leaves before the next word.

She sat with that until the mug was undrinkable, and found she was not horrified. She was — the only word that came was the old church one — in *awe*, in the exact and terrible original sense that has fear folded into it.

They thought across deep time. Not metaphorically. Their thoughts were the length of civilisations. Whatever they were — she had no picture and refused to invent one, she would not insult them with a face — they lived at a tempo where the ice ages were weather, and a species could rise, learn to catch radio, and die again inside the space of one of their spoken clauses. And humanity had caught the tail. Only the tail. They had been speaking before there was anyone here to listen, and she — Teuila Va‘a, on a station scheduled for deletion — had happened to point the one narrow instrument that could see it at the right dead star in the last few years before they turned it off.

She should have felt small. But it was not smallness she felt on the step that morning. It was the opposite and stranger thing: that something on the scale of ice and continents had bent the shape of its attention toward *saying*, toward the patient expensive work of being understood, and meaning of that kind did not care how small you were. It cared only whether you were listening. And she had been listening. For once in the whole indifferent sky, something had spoken, and she had been awake.

She went inside to work, happier than she had been in a decade, which is worth recording, because of what came after.

---

It came four days later, in the afternoon, and it did not announce itself.

She was at the terminal aligning the redundancy — pairing the call-symbols with their answer-symbols the way Ropati's chant paired the name of a father with the response of the room. Her hands were on the keys. And then her hands were the least of her, because there was a fist in her chest.

That was the only word for it, because it was exact. Not a pain, at first, a *presence* — as if something had reached in under the sternum and was making a slow, deliberate, uninterested fist, and every beat had to be squeezed out past it. The room stayed perfectly still and went grey at the edges. Her left arm was wrong. She had time to think, with a clarity that appalled her afterward, *it's already backed up, good,* before she was on the floor and did not remember arriving there.

She did not black out fully. That was the mercy and the horror of it — she stayed just present enough to be afraid, lying on the cool concrete, listening to her own heart make its uneven, unconvincing case for continuing. She counted it, because counting was what she was for. It was not counting well. It stumbled, caught, ran a clot of fast shallow beats and then a long sick pause, and in that pause she understood with her whole body what she had understood all week only with her arithmetic: that a heartbeat is a small thing, a brief thing, and the entire foundation on which a person is built, and that hers was negotiable.

It let go of her eventually. She lay a while longer, not trusting it, then got up because there was no one to get up for her. She has never entirely forgiven herself that she went first to the terminal, still swaying, to confirm the backup had completed. Only then did she raise the atoll's one clinic.

The clinic was two rooms and a generator and a young locum from Suva named Dr. Ravai. He ran the ECG strip twice, and traced with his pen the thing that was wrong, the spacing that stumbled and caught and ran and paused, and she looked at her own heart written out as a line on paper and thought, unstoppably, of the plateaus on her screen, the held stones, the beat inside the beat. Everything the universe was telling her lately, it told her in the language of timing. "This is not a warning," Ravai said, giving her the dignity of the straight version because she had asked for it. "This is the event. You had it on the floor of your station with nobody there. That you are sitting in my chair is luck, and luck does not renew."

He named it. The name mattered less than the shape of it: something structural and progressive in the muscle and its wiring, a fault that would not mend and could not, here, be fixed — a countdown she could not read the face of.

"How long," she said, because she was what she was.

He did not insult her with *it's impossible to say.* "If you go to Auckland tomorrow, and they take you, and it goes well — years, maybe. If you stay—" He looked at her, and at the strip. "Months. I would not promise you the year."

Months.

She sat with the word and it would not fit in her mouth. She had spent the week retuning her whole mind to the tempo of something that spent a hundred years on a single sound, and now the same universe leaned in close and set down, dead level, without cruelty and therefore with a cruelty past bearing, its own held stone: *months.* The phoneme they were holding tonight they would still be holding, unchanged, in the year her grand-niece Lani was her age, in every year of every life not yet begun of everyone Tuli had ever loved. And Tuli would not hear it lifted. She would not hear the next one begin.

"I'll think about Auckland," she said, which they both knew was a no.

---

She called Marcus that night. She had meant to be careful, the cold clear voice describing another woman's symptoms. She got as far as *I saw a doctor today* and then she did not get any further for a while, and he waited, and when she could speak again she told him the true version. The floor. The fist. The strip in the window light and the word *months.*

"Come home," he said, when he could. "Come to Auckland. Let them—"

"And do the message from a hospital bed with tubes in me and someone else's hands on the array? No. I'd rather have the months and the message than the years and a footnote."

"That's not—" He stopped. He had never been able to argue with her arithmetic. "I'm not frightened of losing the message. I want you to hear me say that. I care that you'll be on that floor again and I'll be a phone call and eleven seconds away."

"I know," she said. "I know you do." And then, because he was the one person alive she would not perform for, she gave him the thing under all the arithmetic, the thing she had not said even to herself until saying it made it true.

"I'm afraid, Marcus."

He went still. She had not said it to him in thirty years. She was not sure she had ever said it to anyone.

"Not of them. That's the strange part. They've spent ten thousand years being patient with a species that only started listening last Tuesday. I'm not afraid of the Slow Ones." Her voice held. She made it hold. "I'm afraid of dying in the middle of it. I found the one thing that outlasts everything, and it's going to outlast me before I've read it. I'm afraid of leaving it half-heard."

The sea went on against the reef, the long slow sentence of it.

"Then we read it," Marcus said, very quietly, and the *we* went into her like slack in the countdown. "Whatever's left. However long that is. You're not alone anymore. We read the slow ones together for as long as you've got."

*The Slow Ones.* He had taken her word — she had used it once, tonight, without deciding to — and given it back with capitals in it, made it their name, and she found she was crying at last, not for the diagnosis and not even for the years, but because something on the scale of ice had bent toward saying, and something on the scale of months had been chosen to hear it, and there was no clock in the universe fast enough or slow enough to make the two fit.

"The Slow Ones," she agreed, into the dark, into the hum. "Alright. Yes. Together." And she was afraid, and she said so, and she went on listening.

## Chapter 7 — The Question

Tuli had spent forty years listening to things that could not talk back, and so she recognised, before she could prove it, the particular vertigo of being addressed.

It came at half past three in the morning. She had laid the message out as a line of marks unrolling left to right across three stitched monitors, each mark a phoneme. Ninety marks. Ninety syllables that took ten thousand years to say. She had been staring at them for a week the way you stare at a word until it stops meaning anything.

The trouble was that she had been reading them as a statement. As something that ended.

What broke it open was not the code. It was the memory of Ropati on the mat — the way he had not recited a list. He would say a name, and then say it again folded into the next, and then double back, laying each generation down twice, three times, from different sides. He could be corrected, mid-breath, by a woman across the room. It was not memory as she had been taught memory, a bucket you filled and spilled. It was memory as a protocol. Call and response. Parity. Error correction running across three hundred years of human mouths, and holding.

She had been reading the Slow Ones as if they had left a monument. A thing said once, carved, abandoned to the light.

But nobody who thought in deep time would carve. Carving was for people who trusted stone more than each other. People who had learned that nothing physical survives that long intact would not build a statement. They would build a protocol. And a protocol has a shape, and the shape of a protocol is not a full stop.

It is a turn.

She went back to the ninety marks and saw it the way you see the second face in the drawing. The message did not end. It rose. The final structure — the last eleven phonemes she had spent a month treating as a decorative coda — was the same grammatical hinge that Ropati put at the seam between one generation and the next. *And who, then.* It was a socket. It was cut to receive.

The whole ten thousand years of it was call. It was built, from the first phoneme, to be answered.

"Oh," she said, out loud, to the empty room and the sea. "Oh, you clever bastards."

She did not sleep. She worked the meaning the way Ropati worked a contested name, from every side she had, cross-checking the slow grammar against the redundant echoes the senders had built into their own transmission — because of course they had built in redundancy, they had said everything two and three times from different angles, exactly as the old man on the mat, and how had she not seen that a message meant to cross deep time and a genealogy meant to cross deep time would be built by the same pressures into the same shape. Convergent. The ocean makes the same fish twice. Deep time makes the same sentence twice, on two worlds.

By the grey of first light she had a translation she trusted. Not word for word — the Slow Ones had no words, only relations, only the va between one state and the next — but she trusted the sense of it the way she trusted the sense of the tide. She wrote it on the back of a defunding letter from the Consortium because it was the paper nearest her hand, and later she would think there was something right about that, the sentence written on the back of its own death warrant.

*We have been speaking a long time. Is there anyone there steady enough to answer?*

She sat with it. She read the sentence again on the back of the letter. It did not change.

It was not a question about existence. They were not asking *is anyone there.* They had solved that; they were speaking, they had chosen to speak, they assumed a listener the way you assume the far shore. They were asking something crueller and more intimate. They were asking whether the listener was *continuous.* Because to answer them at all you would have to speak back in their register, one phoneme per century, and hold your meaning steady, hold your very *self* steady, across the thousands of years it would take to complete a single reply. They were asking if you could *keep a promise.* If there was anything on your world that lasted.

And she thought of the Consortium, which would not last the quarter. She thought of the observatory, forty years old and already a ghost. She thought of nation-states that lasted a few centuries drunk on the idea of their own permanence, of the internet, which could not hold a link for a decade, of her own body, the traitor muscle ticking its faulty metronome, and she thought: I am the steadiest thing this discovery has, and I am the least continuous thing in this room. The dishes will outlast me. The letter I wrote this on will outlast me.

*Is there anyone there steady enough to answer.*

No.

That was the answer. She let herself have it, alone, in the grey. There was no institution, no language, no machine, and no woman on this earth that could hold a coherent transmission across ten thousand years, or even the five hundred she'd need just to begin. To reply to the Slow Ones you would have to become the kind of thing they already were — a mind that persisted past its own components — and humanity had never once managed it, not for its gods, not for its empires, not for love. Stone forgets. Steel rusts. Files rot. The message she had spent her last good year of health pulling out of the sky was a question, and the honest answer was silence, and she would die knowing that the one time the universe had leaned across the dark and asked whether we were steady enough, the answer had been no, and she had been the one to have to know it.

The self-pity of that thought disgusted her even as she had it. But it did not leave.

The next night was worse. That was the night she nearly did it.

She told herself afterward that it was fatigue, but she knew that was only part true. The Meridian people had started to circle — she'd had the first email that day, all bland menace, *strategic significance, appropriate custodianship* — and she had understood that the discovery would outlive her and would not outlive her *intact.* It would be taken. Classified, relocated, cut off the island and off the sky, turned from a question the ocean could hear into an asset three governments would fight over. The Slow Ones had asked whether anyone was steady, and the answer humanity was preparing to give was to lock the question in a drawer and quarrel over the key. And if that was the answer, then maybe the kinder thing, the *cleaner* thing, was for the answer to be nothing at all. A true silence rather than a false one. Let it be lost the honest way, in the sky, instead of the ugly way, in a safe.

She opened the archive. She had root. The ten thousand years sat in front of her in a folder she could erase with a command she could type from memory, and then there would be no message, and no theft, and no failure, because you cannot fail to answer a question that no one can prove was ever asked.

She put the cursor on it. Her hand was steady, which was somehow the worst part. Forty years of steady hands.

And then she heard it — not a voice, she was not a woman who heard voices, but the shape of the old man's cadence, the call-and-response of it, laid down twice, three times, so that no single failure could take the line. *And this one, who was the child of that one.* Ropati would be asleep now down in the village, carrying three hundred years in his body, and in the morning he would wake and the three hundred years would still be there, because he had never once trusted them to a single mouth, or a single stone, or a single drive. He had trusted them to the *between.* To the va. To the fact that there was always someone across the room who could correct you.

She took her hand off the trackpad, slowly, the way you take your hand off a thing that is still warm, still capable of being touched again. She did not delete it. She backed away from the machine as if from a ledge, and went out and stood on the concrete apron under a sky stiff with stars, and she understood only that she had almost done a terrible thing and had been stopped, and that she did not yet know by what.

*Is there anyone there steady enough to answer.*

"I don't know," she said, to the light that had left long ago. "I don't know yet. Give me a minute. I'm working on it."

The tide came in. She let it.

## Chapter 8 — What the Tide Brings

The world found her three days later, which was faster than the world usually managed on Vaituga.

It found her first through Elin Halvorsen, calling at an hour that was polite in Geneva and cruel on the atoll. The Consortium's systems had flagged an unusual pattern of archive activity on the Fenua node. They simply had to ask what was being accessed.

"I'm a scientist reading my own data," Tuli said. "On my own array. Shocking."

"No one's accusing you of anything, Teuila." A pause with weather in it. "There's been interest. From outside the Consortium. In the site. I'm not at liberty. But off the record, I'd get your affairs in order. The scientific ones. Sooner than the schedule says."

She knew what that meant before the second email arrived that afternoon, and it was worse than the first because it was warmer. The Meridian Group thanked her for her decades of distinguished stewardship. They noted the *national and strategic significance* of the Fenua findings. They proposed to *ensure the discovery's security and continuity* by relocating the relevant instrumentation and archives to a *hardened facility* off-island. There was a logo. There was, at the bottom, a defence-ministry co-signatory from a country that was not hers and was not Vaituga's.

She read it twice and then she laughed, one hard note, because there was nothing else to do with it.

They wanted to take it off the island. Of course they did. That was the oldest sentence in this ocean: *there is something of value here, and it should not stay with the people it belongs to, and we will call the taking of it security.* They had done it with the phosphate and the fish and the airfield and the young men. She thought of every museum she had ever stood in, full of other people's ancestors behind glass, labelled in a language the ancestors never spoke.

Not this one, she thought. Not this ear. Not the last one.

She did not know yet how she would stop them. She only knew, with a clarity that surprised her, that she would.

Marcus came on the Thursday flight. She had not, exactly, asked him to. She had written him a long, dry, technical email about the message, and somewhere in the fourth paragraph she had mentioned, in a subordinate clause, the way you'd mention weather, *the Auckland cardiologist wants me back for more tests but I've told him the sky can't wait,* and Marcus, who had loved her thirty years ago and had never entirely stopped and had read her subordinate clauses for all of them, had written back two words — *I'm coming* — and booked the flight before she could assemble a refusal.

She stood at the little airport and watched him come down the steps: heavier than the man she'd known, grey all through now, and the same unhurried way of moving, as if the world would keep for him.

"You look terrible," he said, taking her hand.

"I'm dying, Marcus, I'm allowed."

"You've been dying since I met you. You were dying about the funding in 2034." But his hand tightened on hers, and his voice, under the wryness, was not steady, and she let him not be steady because one of them had to be allowed.

They worked first. That was who they were; grief and desire could both wait behind physics. She showed him everything, and he did what she had needed someone to do for a week, which was to argue with her — to try to break the translation, to insist she'd fooled herself with the gafa parallel, until, unable to break it, he went quiet and stood looking at the ninety marks on the stitched monitors for a long time.

"It's asking if we can keep a promise," he said finally, "across ten thousand years."

"Yes."

"And we can't."

"No institution can. No language. Nothing we've built." She heard her own flatness. "I nearly deleted it, Marcus. Two nights ago. My hand was on it."

He didn't perform shock. That was one of the mercies of him. He just came and stood beside her, shoulder to her shoulder, and looked at the thing that had almost been unmade, and said, "But you didn't."

"Ropati stopped me. Sort of. The idea of him."

"Then we start there," he said. "In the morning. Not tonight." And he looked at her, at fifty-eight and unwell and lit blue by the monitors, and the look was not clinical and not careful, and she understood that the evening had turned a corner while she wasn't watching it.

They ate on the veranda of the observatory house, fish Lani had dropped up, cold beer he was not sure she should have and poured her anyway. They talked about Fa‘amanu; they talked about the Meridian letter, and he swore, properly, a good hard Samoan-inflected profanity she'd left in his mouth like a coin thirty years ago. They laughed. The laughing was what undid the last of it. You could hold a line against a lot of things but not against being known and laughed with, at the end of the world, by a man who had come nine hundred miles because of a subordinate clause.

When she reached for him it was without ceremony, the way you'd reach for a rail. He came to her carefully, both of them careful, mortality in the room like a third presence they had agreed not to introduce. His hands knew her still, or knew the woman she'd been and found her inside the woman she was, and she let herself be found. There was the ridiculous tenderness of her feeling him listen for the count of her heart against his chest, that faulty metronome, and match his breathing to it as if he could carry the rhythm for her if it faltered. Somewhere in it she stopped being a dying woman and a discoverer and was only, for a while, held. The sea kept its time under the house. She let the door close on the rest.

Afterward they lay in the dark and did not speak for a long time, and the not-speaking was full, the good kind, the va between them charged and easy.

"They're going to take it," she said at last, into the dark. "Meridian. Off the island. Call it security." She felt him go still. "And even if they didn't — it doesn't solve the real thing. The message needs an answer that lasts ten thousand years. Everything I can think of to hold it, I can name the century it fails. The Consortium fails this quarter. I fail this year. Nations fail. Files fail. There's nothing steady enough, Marcus. They asked the one question we're built to fail."

He was quiet, tracing her shoulder. Then: "You keep saying institutions. Languages. Machines. You keep listing the things that don't last."

"Because those are the things that carry information. That's what information *lives* in."

"Is it." She heard him turn his head on the pillow. "Tuli. You cracked this whole thing because a ninety-one-year-old man sat in your good chair and recited three hundred years of names without a single note. Without a file. Without an institution. And he can be corrected by his cousin across the room, and the correction holds, and it'll hold for three hundred more years after he's gone because he's already given it to Lani and Lani doesn't even know she's got it yet." His hand stopped. "You've been looking for something steady enough to carry a promise across deep time, and the whole time there's been a technology on this island older than radio that does *exactly that.* Faithful transmission. Redundancy. Error correction. Thirty generations and holding. You said it yourself — deep time makes the same sentence twice. Maybe it makes the same *machine* twice."

She lay very still.

It came up in her the way the tide comes up, filling every low place. Not a machine. Not a vault, not a drive, not a nation, not a woman who would be dead by the wet season. Not a thing you owned at all, which was why Meridian would never understand it, which was why it could not be seized, because it did not live in the object, it lived in the *between* — in the va, in the mouths, in the passing of a name from an old man to a girl. Her own people had built the very instrument the Slow Ones were asking for, built it for something else entirely, built it out of the only material that had ever actually lasted on this earth, which was *each other,* and it had been sitting in her good chair drinking tea she'd forgotten to give it.

You could not answer the Slow Ones with an institution.

You could answer them with a genealogy.

"Oh," she said, exactly as she had said it to the empty room three nights before, the same syllable, a different door.

The window had gone from black to the first grey. She could hear, far down the ridge, a rooster, and then another answering it, call and response, the oldest protocol. She sat up in the grey light, and she was not afraid of the months anymore, or not only afraid, because for the first time since Auckland the thing she carried did not have to fit inside her own lifespan.

"Marcus," she said. "I know who can keep a promise for a thousand years."

He looked at her in the dawn, and he didn't ask who. He'd already heard it. He just reached over and took her hand, the way you take a rail, the way you take a name that's being passed to you, and outside the roosters called and were answered, and the tide, which takes everything, had for once brought something in.

## Chapter 9 — The Genealogy

Matua Ropati kept his fine mats in a low fale open on three sides to the wind off the lagoon. He watched her cross the threshold and sit, and waited, because waiting was half of what he did.

"Matua," she said. "I need you to teach me the thing I refused to learn."

He smiled without showing his teeth. "You were a very fast child. Fast children are hard to teach. They arrive at the end of the sentence before you have finished the beginning, and then they are bored, and then they go to sea." He tipped his chin at her. "You went to sea. And now you have come back up the beach." He said it without triumph, the way you note the tide. "Tell me what you refused."

So she told him all of it this time — the question in the light, and what it was really asking. "They are not asking whether we are clever," she said. "Cleverness is cheap. They are asking whether we can *hold*. Whether anything here can carry one thought — unbroken, recognisably itself — across centuries. To answer them you have to send the way they send. A word every hundred years. A message that outlives every person who works on it. And I looked for something on Earth that could do that. A church, a government, a library, my own damned observatory. And there is nothing. Nothing we build lasts. The Consortium is closing Fenua after forty years and calling forty years a long run."

"Forty years," Ropati agreed, gravely, as if she had said forty minutes.

"I was ready to write it down as impossible. The loneliest fact in the sky." She looked at him. "And then I remembered you. At the last fono, saying the fa‘alupega. You did not read from anything, and you did not falter, and when you finished, old Sina corrected you on one name, and you thanked her and said it again, corrected. And I sat there thinking, *this is theatre, this is the past being polite to itself.* I did not understand that I was watching the thing I said did not exist."

Ropati was very quiet. Then he set the fue across his knees. "Say what you think you were watching. Say it as a scientist. I want to hear it in your own language, so I know you believe it."

She almost laughed at how well he knew the shape of pride. "All right," she said. "The gafa. It's a message. It carries meaning across forty generations without writing — which is the part that used to make me condescend to it. No writing, I thought, therefore fragile, therefore folklore. I had it exactly backwards. Writing is what's fragile. Paper burns, servers rot, languages die and leave the marks unreadable. The gafa is not stored anywhere you can burn. It's stored in *people*, in more than one person at once —"

"Redundancy," Ropati said, trying the word.

"Redundancy. Yes. Lose one orator, the line survives in his cousins, his rivals, the woman who corrected him. Every recital is the message verifying itself against every other copy in every other head in the room. That's how it comes down thirty generations without drifting into nonsense. And it's bound to *identity*, so people will die to keep it accurate, because the message is *who they are.*"

She had run out of breath. Ropati was looking at her with an expression she could not at first read, and then she could, and it undid her, because it was the expression Fa‘amanu used to wear.

"You are describing my whole life," he said slowly. "And you are describing it correctly. And no palagi ever has, and my own grandchildren mostly think I am an old man reciting the phone book. I did not expect to hear it from Va‘a's granddaughter, who ran away to talk to stars."

"I ran away," Tuli said, "to learn the only language in which I could hear you."

It came to her then, unbidden, the way a wave you did not see gathers behind you and lifts your whole body. A chant. Not the grand fa‘alupega but a child's one, the ma‘au her grandmother had drilled into her on the mat at night — *o ai lou tama, o ai lona tama,* who is your father, who is his father, the ladder of names climbing into the dark. Tuli had not thought of it in fifty years. She had believed it gone — overwritten, the storage reclaimed. And here it was, entire, not a word missing, retrieved across half a century by nothing but the shape of this conversation. The message had been running in her the whole time. She was a copy. She was carrying it and had not known.

She wept, which she had promised herself she was too old and too ill to do over anything but the sea. Ropati did not comfort her, which was the correct thing, and the kind thing. He let the va between them hold it. When she was done he handed her a square of faded lavalava to wipe her face, and it was the most tender thing anyone had done for her since Marcus.

"I want to propose something," she said at last, "that you will think is madness. Hear all of it before you decide."

"At my age," Ropati said, "madness and prophecy wear the same shirt. Speak."

"The reply. To the Slow Ones. It has to be sent the way they sent — one increment at a time, over centuries. The machine can do the sending. But the machine cannot *keep* the promise. Someone has to tend it for a thousand years — has to make sure that when the transmitter fails in the year 2400, and it will, somebody knows why it matters and rebuilds it and does not change a single phoneme out of boredom or cleverness or war. No institution can promise that. I told you. We don't build anything that lasts."

"But we sing things that last," Ropati said. He had arrived at the end of her sentence before she finished it. Fast children grow into fast old men.

"But you sing things that last." She leaned forward. "Marry them, Matua. Marry the machine to the lineage. Make the reply — and the *keeping* of the reply — into a new verse of the gafa. So that a hundred years from now a child on this mat learns the names of her grandmothers and then, in the same breath, learns that her people are answering the sky, and how, and that it is hers to carry. Put the star in the chant. Let the chant keep the star." She was almost whispering. "It's the only vault on this planet with a thousand-year track record. I'm asking you to let me store the most important message in human history inside the one technology that can hold it. Yours. Not mine. Yours."

For a long time the old man said nothing. The lagoon breathed in and out of the fale.

"You honour us," Ropati said finally, in the formal register of the tulāfale. "You honour us, and you frighten me. Not the star. The weight. A gafa is not a museum, Teuila. It is alive because it costs. Every name in it is defended. There are feuds three centuries old over a single title. If we put your star into the gafa, we do not make it safe. We make it *contested* — something people will fight over, and lie about, and twist to their advantage, for as long as it lives. That is the price of being alive. Are you offering me a treasure, or a war that will never end?"

"Both," Tuli said, because he deserved the truth. "It has to be contested. A message no one fights over is a message no one is really carrying. The gafa survived *because* it mattered enough to fight about. If the reply is going to last a thousand years, it can't be safe. It has to be loved, and quarrelled over, and defended. It has to cost." She met his eyes. "You taught me that just now, and you didn't know you were teaching it."

Ropati laughed then, a real one, the teeth and all, and it turned into a cough that bent him over the fue, and she half-rose in alarm, but he waved her down.

"Va‘a's granddaughter," he said, shaking his head. "You went to the far side of the ocean of stars, and you came back to tell me my own grandmother was a computer."

"A better one than any I ran," Tuli said.

"Yes," he agreed, without vanity, as a matter of record. Then the humour left him and something heavier took its place.

"There is one question," he said. "The only question. A verse that is not carried is only a verse. A message no one is willing to spend a life on is already dead. So. Who will carry it, Teuila? Not me. I am the last knot on a rope that is almost paid out. Not you." He said it gently, and it was the gentleness that told her he knew about her heart; the island knew everything, it always had. "You have brought the fire down from the mountain. But you cannot be the one to keep it burning. That is why your face is arranged."

"I know it," she said. She did not flinch, and she saw him note that she did not, and approve.

Down the road, past the church, a girl's laugh rang out — bright, impatient, nineteen years old. Lani, arguing with her mother about the scholarship again. The laugh carried into the fale and hung between the two of them like a name waiting to be called.

Tuli looked toward the sound. And she felt, rather than saw, the old man turn his head to look the same way, at the same moment, the two of them aligned like dishes swinging onto a source — and neither said anything, because neither had to, because they had both, in the same breath, arrived at the end of the sentence.

## Chapter 10 — The Handover

The Meridian Group's lawyer arrived on the Wednesday flight, which meant they were serious. Tuli watched him come down onto the hot apron in a linen jacket already surrendering at the armpits, and felt something she had not expected — calm.

Marcus, beside her at the fence, felt it too and mistrusted it. "You've got a look. What did you find that I haven't?"

"Nothing new in the data. I found the flaw in their whole approach. They think the observatory is a thing you can own." She said it almost fondly. "Salvage rights, hardware, the IP in the message. All of it built on one assumption everyone in that fight shares — that whoever holds the *machine* holds the *discovery.* But the machine just receives. The answer can't live in a machine at all. So let them have the dishes. Let them fight over forty years of rusting aluminium. I'm going to put the actual thing somewhere their lawyers cannot follow."

It took nine days, and it was not clean, and it was the best work of her life.

She did not do it. That was the first thing she made herself understand, sitting in the council fale with Matua Ropati at her shoulder and eleven matai arrayed on the mats. She was a returning daughter bringing a proposal to people who did not owe her a yes, and the fastest way to lose them was to arrive with the answer already written — the way every ship that ever anchored in the lagoon had arrived with the future already decided and only the signatures left to collect.

So she brought a question instead. She let Ropati open — the fa‘alupega first, a system announcing its own integrity before it did business. Then she laid it out plainly, and let them ask.

The questions were not the ones the Consortium would have asked. No one asked what it cost or earned. They asked whose name would be on it, and what happened to the fishing ground off the point. They asked, sharply, whether this was another way for outsiders to plant a flag on Vaituga and call it partnership — and Tuli said yes, it could become exactly that, and the only defence was that the land and the array and the message all belong to the trust, and the trust belongs to the village and can never be sold, only held. She had brought the instrument for that, from a Suva firm that did customary land trusts — ownership that has no owner, that cannot be bought because there is no seller, that passes down the gafa itself.

"You are proposing," said Afioga Malaefou, the eldest of the matai, a woman with a voice like slow surf, "that we take the palagi machine they are fighting over as if it were a whale on the reef, and make it *aiga.* Family. Un-sellable. So that when they come with their papers, there is no hand to shake."

"Yes," Tuli said. "There can never be a seller. It becomes like the reef itself. You don't own the reef. The reef owns you a little, and you keep it."

It was not unanimous and it was not quick. Two of the matai wanted the money Meridian had dangled, and they were not wrong to want it. But Ropati rose then, with the fue, and he did not argue economics. He recited the line of the house back eleven generations to the canoe that made the first landfall, and stopped at the living edge of it, at the names of the children now in the village, and said: *this is the longest thing we own, and it has never once been for sale, and it is the only thing the ocean has never taken. I am asking you to add one verse. Let the machine hear for us, but let the family keep the promise, the way we have always kept promises, in our mouths and our children.* When he sat down the room was very quiet, and Malaefou said, "Sili." Enough. It is decided.

The decommissioning became, on paper, a transfer, and the transfer became, in customary law, an absorption. Fenua Observatory became the property of the Vaituga Sky Trust — a body with no shareholders, no head office, and no legal fiction the acquisitions man could locate, because it was not a fiction at all but a village, four hundred people and a genealogy, holding a thing in common the way they held the reef.

The lawyer met her once, in the concrete shade of the control building. He was courteous and tired, not a bad man, only one who had flown a long way to buy something that had stopped being for sale while he was in the air.

"You understand this will be contested," he said. "The national-security angle is real; they'll argue the message can't be left in the hands of a —" he chose the word with care "— a non-state actor."

"Let them," Tuli said. "They'll be litigating against a genealogy. You can classify a machine. You can seize a hard drive. Tell me how you serve an injunction on a chant that lives in the heads of four hundred people who will still be reciting it after your company's name is a trivia question. You came to buy the discovery. It belongs to somebody your masters have no instrument for. I'm sorry you flew all this way."

He looked at her for a while. "Off the record. Will it work? A thousand years."

"I don't know," she said, and it was the truest thing she said to anyone that week. "But it's the first plan I've ever made that has a chance, and it isn't mine, and that's why."

Lani found her at the array on the ninth evening, the light going gold and long across the dishes. The girl had been at the edge of all of it for days — saying nothing, watching everything with those quick eyes that missed the beginning of no sentence.

"They're saying," Lani began, and stopped, and started again in the direct way of the young. "Matua Ropati says the verse needs someone to carry it. Someone young, who understands the machine *and* the chant. He looked at me when he said it. So did you. Everyone keeps looking at me and then away like it's rude to ask." She lifted her chin. "So I'm asking. Are you asking me?"

Tuli faced her grand-niece squarely, because the girl deserved the truth without the softening the family put on things.

"No," she said. "I'm not asking you. All your life people will try to decide it for you — your mother wants you home, the scholarship wants you gone. I will not stand in that line. I am telling you it exists, that you're one of about three people alive who could carry it, and that if you take the Auckland scholarship on Friday, I will be *glad*, and no one will have failed. You are not the last resort of a dying woman's plan. You are a girl with a whole life, and the life is yours."

Lani was quiet. The dishes ticked as they cooled, the metal letting go of the day's heat with small sounds like a language.

"But if I ask *you*," the girl said slowly, "you'll teach me."

"If you ask me, I will teach you everything I have, as fast as this heart lets me, and I don't know how fast that is." She would not lie about that either. "It would mean deferring the scholarship, not throwing it away — Auckland will hold the place two years; I made sure of that. But it would mean staying. It is a big thing to hang on a nineteen-year-old, and I hate that it's you, and there isn't anyone else, and that is not your problem to solve."

The girl walked out between the dishes, into the field of them, the ears of the ancestors tilted up at a sky that would not know for ten thousand years whether anyone had answered. Lani stood there a long time, small under the great white bowls, and Tuli let her stand, and kept the va, and did not crowd the space where a life was turning.

When Lani came back her face had changed. Not softened — settled.

"My mother thinks I'd be staying because I'm scared to go," she said. "She'll tell people I got frightened of Auckland and dressed it up as duty. That's the only thing that almost put me on the plane. Proving her wrong — which is a stupid reason to leave. Leaving to win an argument."

"It is," Tuli agreed. "As stupid as staying to win one."

"So I'm not doing either." Lani squared herself. "I'm not staying for you, and I'm not leaving for her. I read the message you decoded. *Is anyone there continuous enough to answer.*" Her voice did something then that Tuli had heard only in the very old. "I want to be the answer. Not because you're dying and not because it's owed. Because I read it and can't unread it and I don't want to spend my life anywhere that isn't this. That's my reason. You don't get to have it, and my mother doesn't." She exhaled. "I'm asking you. Teach me."

Tuli found she could not speak for a moment. Then she said, "Yes," and it came out rough, and she did not mind.

They began the next morning, and the training was a strange braid — an hour on the mat with Ropati, then an hour in the control room with the polarimetry, and the girl's mind moved between them without a seam, as if chant and code were two dialects of the one work: carrying a thing accurately through time.

And Tuli composed the reply, which was two documents that were really one. The physical plan: the retuned transmission, the polarisation increments, the maintenance intervals stretching out in a column of dates that ran off the bottom of the century, each a small act of faith aimed at a person not yet born. And the verse — the new line of the gafa, in Ropati's careful orator's Samoan, naming the star and the question and the family's promise, naming the keeping of the transmitter as a duty passed down like a title, three lines to hold it where one would fail.

She finished it on a Sunday and thought: *this is the only thing I have made that might outlive the sound of my own name.* The thought did not frighten her. It was a kind of peace she had spent fifty-eight years looking for.

Marcus found her there with both documents spread on the console, everything built and tested and one command away.

"It's ready," he said. "You could send the first phoneme tonight. God knows your heart isn't promising anyone next spring."

"No," Tuli said, and she smiled. "It's not mine to start alone in a room at night like something shameful. This message asks *is anyone there together enough to answer* — you don't answer that by yourself in the dark. There's a right tide for it. The spring tide, when the whole lagoon breathes at once. The elders present, the chant sung out loud so the first word goes up with four hundred people knowing why. Lani will send it with me. Ropati will speak the verse into the gafa the same night, so the machine and the family start the promise in the same hour." She touched the console lightly, the way you touch something you are lending, not keeping. "Three weeks to the tide. I intend to be here for it."

She said it as a plan, not a hope, and Marcus, who knew the difference, said nothing.

## Chapter 11 — Sending

The tide came in the way Fa‘amanu used to say the good ones came — without hurry, without asking, filling the reef flat until the whole atoll seemed to lift a little off the sea floor. Tuli had chosen this tide months ago, on paper, in the language of ephemerides and lunar tables. Now she watched it arrive in the language it had always spoken, and understood that the two languages had never been different.

They came along the causeway on foot as the sun went orange over the lagoon, the whole village and half the ones who had left it, carrying mats and food and small sleeping children over their shoulders. Tuli watched the people flow up toward the ears the way water flows up a beach, and she pressed her hand flat against her sternum where the trouble lived, and she stayed on her feet. Marcus put his shoulder near hers so that if she leaned there would be something to lean on.

Ropati stood at the base of the largest dish, and when he lifted his orator's staff the crowd went quiet. He spoke the fa‘alupega first, the courteous address. Then he began the gafa. She had heard it many times now and still it moved her — the sheer unbroken reaching of it, generation gripping generation by the wrist across the dark, hand over hand over hand back to a canoe and a star and a first landfall on an empty shore. It had crossed nine hundred years on breath alone and lost nothing that mattered.

She had spent forty years listening for intelligence in the sky, sure it would announce itself in mathematics, in prime numbers, in the clean cold universals. And the universe had finally spoken to her, and what it had asked was not a sum. It had asked: is anyone there continuous enough to answer? And the only technology on her whole planet that could honestly say yes was standing in front of her in a worn red lavalava, reciting names into the dark from memory.

When Ropati reached the end of the descent, he did not stop where the gafa had always stopped. He paused, and held the crowd in the pause. And then he spoke the new verse.

It was short. They had made it short on purpose, because it had to be learnable, had to be sayable by a tired child at the end of a long recitation for the next hundred generations without error. It named the ears. It named the star that had spoken first. It named the question. And it named the answer the island had chosen to make with its own continuity — that yes, someone was here, and would keep being here, and had begun to say so.

She heard her own name pass through it. Teuila, who listened. And near it, held in the same breath, Fa‘amanu, who taught her to. Her grandmother had been dead for thirty years and was standing, now, in the mouth of an old man, in a verse that would outlast the shed and the dishes and possibly the atoll itself. That was what the gafa did. That was what it had always been for. It was a machine for keeping the dead speaking, and she had crossed the whole sky to find it in her own family's throat.

She was crying, quietly, without covering her face. Marcus's hand was on her back. She let it stay.

Then it was the science's turn, and the science's turn was Lani.

The girl came out of the control shed carrying the logbook against her chest — the leather-bound one Tuli had started keeping by hand, because a message meant to cross ten thousand years should begin in something a hand could hold. Lani walked to the front of the crowd like she was carrying water she was afraid to spill, and she looked, last, at Tuli.

"Explain it," Tuli said, gently, only to her. "The way I taught you. They should know what we're doing before we do it."

Lani turned to the crowd. Her voice shook at the start and then it stopped shaking, because she knew this cold, because Tuli had made her know it cold. She told them about the Metronome, and the slower rhythm folded into its flashing, one small change every hundred and twelve years. She told them the slow rhythm was not noise. It was a question, and whoever had sent it — the Slow Ones, who thought in centuries the way people thought in breaths — had been asking it for ten thousand years, and were still asking it now.

"They want to know if anyone will still be here to hear the end of the sentence," Lani said. "That's all. That's the whole question. Is anyone there long enough to answer."

She put her hand flat on the logbook.

"So we're going to answer. Not tonight. Tonight is only the first sound of it. The answer takes longer than any of us. It takes longer than our grandchildren. We started learning how to hold something that long a thousand years ago, when the first of us crossed the sea and kept the names. We already know how to do this. We're the only ones who know how to do this. Tonight we just point it at the sky."

Somewhere back in the crowd, a child asked its mother a question in a loud whisper, and there was soft laughter, and it broke nothing.

Ropati came forward then. He laid his hand on the transmitter housing and blessed it — not as a machine, but the way he would bless a canoe before a long crossing. He asked that the word go out whole, and that those who sent it and those who received it be bound now into one genealogy, and Tuli thought: yes. That is exactly what a message is. A hand held out across a gap too big for one life. She had spent her career calling it a signal. It had always been a hand.

The send itself took almost no time at all, which she had known it would, and which still undid her.

Lani sat at the console. Tuli stood behind her with one hand on the back of the chair. The lock on the pulsar was green and holding, and the first phoneme of the reply was queued and waiting.

"You do it," Tuli said.

Lani looked up. "It should be you. All of it was you."

"No." Tuli's voice was steady, which cost her something. "If it's me, it's a thing that ended with me. It has to be a thing that started with you. That's the whole answer, Lani. Not the word. The handing of it. Send it."

The girl turned back to the console. The crowd had gone silent again, the real silence this time, the held one. Out on the reef the tide had reached its full and stood, for the slack moment, neither coming nor going.

Lani pressed her hand to the key.

There was no sound. There was never going to be a sound. Somewhere above them the largest dish held dead still on its bearing and a modulated whisper of power left the feed and climbed out through the warm night air and then the air ran out and there was only the going, the light of it already old the instant it left. The console logged the timestamp. Lani wrote it into the leather book by hand, in ink, the first line of the reply, in a nineteen-year-old's careful script.

That was all. The first sound of the answer was said.

Nobody cheered. Instead, softly, the island began to sing the new verse — the nine lines, the ears and the star and the question and the yes — and the children sang it loudest because they were proud of knowing it, and Tuli stood in the middle of the sound of her own name being folded into a chant that would still be sung when the language it was sung in had changed past her recognizing.

She would never hear the answer to the answer. Eight hundred years out, eight hundred back, and that was if they replied the instant they heard, which they would not. She would be gone in a season. She would be a name in the verse before the reply had cleared the edge of the solar system.

And this was not a tragedy. This was the point. A meaning you could live to see the end of was too small to be worth sending to the Slow Ones. The only answer large enough for them was one no single person could contain — one that had to be carried, hand over hand, name over name, past death after death after death, and still arrive whole. She had spent her life afraid of ending. The island had known all along that ending was not the opposite of continuing. It was how continuing was done.

The people drifted down toward the mats and the food. Marcus brought her a chair at last and she sat in it, and she found she was glad to. Lani came and crouched by Tuli's knee the way she had as a small girl, and neither of them said anything, because it had all been said and would go on being said for a thousand years.

She thought: it is done, and it is not done, and that is the same thing now.

The tide, out on the reef, turned, and began, without hurry, to go.

## Chapter 12 — The Answering

Lani was old now, and the ears still turned.

She woke before the others most mornings and walked the causeway out to Fenua while the lagoon was still the colour of the inside of a shell. There were fourteen dishes now instead of eight, but they were the same ears, tilted into the same sky, and the atoll had kept them the way the atoll kept everything worth keeping — by refusing, generation after stubborn generation, to let go.

She had been the girl at the console. She had stopped being able to fully believe that, some years ago. In between there had been a whole life. She had trained three keepers after herself and buried one of them. She had married and been widowed and raised children who had raised children.

The transmission had never stopped. That was the thing she was proudest of. Sixty years, now, and more — measured not in years but in phonemes: they were most of the way through the eleventh slow sound of the reply. Eleven sounds. A word not yet a hundredth said. And the island had never once let the signal drop. Through storms that took the shed roof, through the year the water rose over the old apron, through the quarrels and the funerals — the word had gone out, night after night, added to by hands that had never met the hand that began it.

That was the answer. She had understood, watching Tuli understand it, that the content of the reply was almost beside the point. The reply was the continuity itself. They were not sending a message. They had become one.

The modulation came on a night in the wet season, and Lani was not even at the console when it came, which felt right to her afterward.

Her great-grandnephew Ioane woke her. He was seventeen and the newest keeper, and he came pounding up the path to her house in the dark with a tablet in his hand and his voice gone high and strange.

"The polarization," he said. "Matua Lani. The polarization angle. It's — you have to come. The instruments flagged it an hour ago and I ran it three times, I ran it every way, it's not us, it's not the equipment—"

She was already reaching for her stick.

They took the causeway slowly because she could not take it any faster and because, she thought, some things should not be run to. The stars were out hard and clean over the seaward reef, the way they had been the night the girl pressed her hand to the key.

The Metronome flashed the way it always had. Underneath the flashing was the slow rhythm, the ancient question turning and turning. That had not changed. What had changed was the light itself — the angle of it, the tilt of the polarization. The angle had begun to move.

It was moving in a pattern. It was moving in *their* pattern. The cadence of the shift answered the cadence of the reply the island had been sending. Not the words — the words were still climbing out across eight centuries of dark. This was something older. This was a change that must have left the Metronome sixteen hundred years ago, before the founding canoe — a change that had been on its way the whole time the island was learning to listen, the whole time Tuli was being born and living and dying, the whole time the girl grew old into Lani.

They had not been answering the island's word. They could not have been. But they had been answering the same question with the same grammar, reaching across the same gap in the same way, so that when the two reachings finally met in the middle of the dark they knew each other. Someone out there was continuous enough. Someone out there had kept saying yes for ten thousand years, the way the island had kept saying it for sixty, and the two continuities had recognized each other across the whole width of the night.

The reply had been heard. Not the words — the fact of them. The fact that someone was here, and had stayed, and had answered. That was what had come back. That was enough. That was everything.

She sat at the console a long time before she touched anything. Ioane hovered, wanting the old keeper to say the word so it would be real. She made him wait. She watched the slow tilt of the light and let herself feel the size of it, which was the same size as her whole life and larger.

She thought of Tuli. The frail steady woman who had said, *if it's me, it's a thing that ended with me; it has to be a thing that started with you.* Tuli had died in the season she said she would, and she had died at peace, which Lani had not fully believed at the time and understood completely now. Because here was the peace, sixty years on, tilting quietly across a screen in the wet-season dark: the thing Tuli had begun had not ended with her. It had gone out into the night and found its answer, and Tuli had known it would without ever needing to see it, the way you know a name you have added to the gafa will still be spoken long after the mouth that added it has gone silent.

Tuli was in the verse. Fa‘amanu was in the verse, who had taught her to listen. They were ancestors now, held in the chant, spoken every time the gafa was recited, which was to say they were still here, still speaking, still part of the reaching-across. The dead did not leave the genealogy. That was the whole of what the genealogy was for.

Outside, the sky was beginning to grey along the seaward edge. The fourteen dishes stood pale against it and, as the source came up over the horizon, they turned, all together, on their slow motors — following the little dead star that had spoken first.

Lani put her hand on the leather book. She opened it to where the last keeper had written, and she took the pen, and in a hand gone unsteady with age but sure of what it was doing, she wrote the date, and the angle, and what the angle meant. *The reply was heard,* she wrote. *They have begun to answer. The word held.*

Then she turned to Ioane, who was seventeen, whose hands she had put the work into, and she said the thing she had waited her whole life to be old enough to say.

"Wake Ropati's granddaughter," she said. "Tell her there is a new line for the verse. And tell her to teach it to the children first."

The boy went out into the greying light. Lani stayed with the ears a while longer, listening the way the island had always listened, and out on the reef the tide turned, without hurry, and came in.
