# The Slow Word — Novella Cut

*An abridged edition of the full novel. Adult / literary science fiction —
first contact, deep-time communication. Complete and self-contained. Content
note: grief and terminal illness; colonial themes; one restrained, non-graphic
intimate scene; occasional strong language.*

---

## Chapter 1 — The Last Watch

Dawn came to Vaituga the way it always had, without ceremony and without mercy. Tuli watched it from the control room window: a grey seam splitting the ocean from the sky, then a bruise of colour, then the sudden shameless gold that would, within the hour, turn the whole atoll into a griddle. She had lived through eleven thousand of these dawns on this island and she still could not decide whether the light forgave the place or simply exposed it.

She logged the ears one at a time, as she had every morning for thirty years.

Dish One, azimuth nominal. The old motor groaned as it woke, a sound like a man clearing his throat before bad news. Dish Two, elevation drive sticking again; she keyed the override and it shuddered free. Dish Three, Four, Five, the small ones out along the reef flat, their white bowls tilted skyward like a congregation that had been promised something and was still, patiently, waiting to receive it. Dish Six was dead and had been dead for two years, and the Consortium had declined to authorise the part. She logged it anyway. *Dish Six, no response.* There was a discipline in recording an absence. Her grandmother would have understood that, though her grandmother had never seen a dish in her life.

The ears. That was what the islanders called the place, the ones who still bothered to have a word for it. *O taliga o le lagi.* The ears of the sky. Ropati had said it to her the first year, standing in this same room with his hands folded, and she had corrected him — *they're antennas, Matua, they don't hear, they receive* — and he had looked at her with an expression she now recognised, at fifty-eight, as pity, though at twenty-eight she had read it as ignorance.

They were coming to take the ears away in nine days.

The decommission crew was already booked, a contractor out of Suva, three men and a barge and forty pages of instructions, most of which concerned the safe disposal of forty years of lubricant and the recovery of anything containing rare earths. The dishes themselves would be cut up on site. Tuli had a private, corrosive certainty that within a decade the bowls would be roofing and rainwater tanks and the frames of fishing boats, and that this was probably the most honest afterlife an instrument of pure knowledge could hope for in a place like this.

Her own afterlife was less clear. She had a flat in Auckland she had not slept in for six years and a pension the Consortium described, in the same email that terminated her post, as *generous under the circumstances.* The circumstances were that Fenua Observatory had produced, in three decades, exactly one result of note — a refinement to the timing model of a millisecond pulsar, published in a journal nobody outside four hundred people had read — and that the money it consumed could keep two graduate students in simulations somewhere with a nicer climate and better optics. She did not, in her more rigorous moods, entirely disagree. That was the worst of it. She had spent a career learning to follow evidence, and the evidence said Fenua was a beautiful, expensive mistake, and she had given it her whole life.

So: the boxes. The archive would not box itself. Thirty years of data sat in the server room as a wall of drives, enormous and, by modern standards, a joke — the kind of dataset a single sky survey now generated before breakfast. The Consortium wanted the scientifically significant portions transferred to Bonn and the remainder deleted. *Remainder* was doing an enormous amount of work in that sentence. The significant portions were perhaps four terabytes. The remainder was everything else: the calibration runs, the failed observations, the nights of pure static when the receivers were warm and the sky said nothing. She was to catalogue it, tag what mattered, and mark the rest for erasure — because a person came all the way out here to switch off a telescope, and once they had switched it off there was no one left to argue with the deletion script.

Into the boxes went the paper. Logbooks in a dozen hands, hers and her predecessors', the ink going brown; thermal-printer traces from the nineties, curled like fern fronds, faded to ghosts; a wall calendar from 2034 with a child's drawing of a fish taped to July, and she could not for the life of her remember whose child, or why the fish, and she stood holding it a long moment before she put it in the box for keeping rather than the box for burning. She was allowed a few irrational preservations. She had earned that much.

Her phone sat face-up on the desk and she did not look at it, which was its own kind of looking. There were four messages from Lani. Lani was nineteen and clever in the particular unguarded way of people who have not yet been told no by anyone who mattered, and she had a scholarship offer from Auckland, and she wanted her grand-aunt — her only relation who had gone away and made a life of the mind and come back, or half come back — to tell her what to do. Tuli had answered the first message with something warm and useless, the second with something shorter, and the last two not at all, because she did not know what to say that was both true and kind, and she had reached the age where she was no longer willing to lie to the young in order to be loved by them.

There had been, three weeks ago, a *saofa‘i* — the conferring of a chiefly title on one of her cousins — and with it the recital of the *gafa*, the genealogy, the long chanted river of names that ran back past the missionaries and past the first canoes and out, if you believed it, into the founding dark. Ropati was the *tulāfale* now, the orator, the keeper; he was over ninety and his voice had thinned but the names had not, the names never thinned, and he had sent word that a place would be kept for her, that it was fitting, that the family's scientist should stand and hear her own line spoken.

She had not gone. She had sent money and an apology and a lie about the array requiring her, and had sat in this room while forty generations were spoken aloud eight kilometres away across the lagoon, and told herself she was working.

The truth was closer to fear, and she knew it, the way you know a tooth is going bad before it aches. Fa‘amanu had tried to give her the chants when she was small — the counting-songs and the naming-songs, the ones a child could hold — and Tuli had learned them in the effortless way children learn everything, and then she had gone to Auckland at seventeen and put them down, deliberately, the way you put down a language you have decided is beneath the person you intend to become. She had traded a chant for a spectrum. She had told herself it was the same hunger pointed at a better sky. And now she was fifty-eight and the sky was being switched off and the woman who had taught her the songs was thirty years dead, and Tuli could not have stood up in front of the family and chanted the *gafa* if her life had depended on it, because she had let it fall out of her on purpose, and there is a particular shame reserved for the things we lose not through carelessness but through choice.

She taped the box shut. The tape shrieked in the quiet.

By mid-morning she had moved from the paper to the drives, going through the archive tree branch by branch and deciding, for each folder, whether thirty years of one woman's attention had produced anything the species needed to keep. Mostly it had not. She was ruthless because ruthlessness was the only mercy left, and she watched the red total on the deletion manifest climb and felt the way she imagined a person might feel signing the papers to sell a house they had been unhappy in for forty years. Grief and relief so tangled you could not have separated them with a scalpel.

Then she came to the Metronome.

It had a catalogue name, a string of coordinates and a designation, but everyone who had ever worked at Fenua called it the Metronome, because that was what it was: a millisecond pulsar, a dead star spinning six hundred times a second, sweeping its lighthouse beam across the Earth with a regularity that made atomic clocks look nervous. It was Fenua's one real object, the source of the one real paper, the thing the dishes had returned to night after night for thirty years the way the tide returns.

The timing data was already flagged for Bonn. But there was more than timing. There was polarisation — the angle of the light, the plane in which the radio waves oscillated as they arrived, a quantity Fenua had recorded faithfully and, so far as anyone had ever determined, pointlessly, because the Metronome's polarisation was famously, boringly constant. It did not vary. That was almost the definition of the object. She had, in fact, spent years using its polarisation angle to calibrate the receivers, the way a carpenter trusts a spirit level: not because the level is interesting but because it does not move.

She opened the polarisation record to tag it for deletion, and pulled thirty years of it onto the screen as a single long trace, the angle plotted against time, expecting a flat line.

It was not quite a flat line.

She saw it and did not believe it and looked away and looked back, which is the oldest reflex in the science, older than the telescope, the animal refusal of the eye to accept what it has been told cannot be there. Across the full width of the plot — across three decades — the polarisation angle drifted. Not much. A fraction of a degree, a lean so slight that on any single night, any single year, it would have vanished under the noise, which was exactly why nobody had ever seen it and why she had used this very quantity as her *level* for half her career. But laid out end to end, all thirty years at once, the drift was there: faint, monotonous, and — she leaned closer, her bad coffee cold at her elbow, the heat forgotten — regular. Impossibly, stupidly regular. As if something were turning, very slowly, somewhere out past the reach of every reasonable idea she had.

Calibration error. It had to be calibration error, a slow creep in the reference, an artefact of thirty years of drifting instruments and swapped receivers and firmware nobody remembered writing. That was the boring, correct, professional answer, and she had built a career on preferring the boring correct answer to the beautiful one, because the beautiful ones were almost always wrong and the graveyard of astronomy was full of people who had fallen in love with an artefact.

She reached for the mouse to tag the folder for deletion.

Her hand did not do it.

Outside, the tide had turned; she could hear it in the changed voice of the reef. The dishes below the ridge held their patient upward attitude, listening, as they had listened before she came and would not listen after she was gone, to light that had left its source ten thousand years ago and was only now, this morning, arriving at a switched-off telescope at the edge of the world.

She did not delete the folder. She flagged it — a single yellow tag, *hold, review* — and told herself it meant nothing, that a careful woman checks even her artefacts before she burns them. Then she sat for a while in the heat and looked at the faint, patient, impossible slope on the screen, and did not go back to the boxes, and let the coffee go entirely cold.

## Chapter 2 — Noise

She gave it one day. That was the bargain she made with herself over breakfast: one day to kill it properly, to trace the drift back to whatever loose cable or lazy assumption had spawned it, and then she would tag the folder, run the deletion, and spend her last week on the island doing the dignified thing, which was packing.

One day. She had said the same thing to herself about the island once, at seventeen. *One year in Auckland and I'll know.* She was aware of the echo and chose to ignore it.

The first suspect was always RFI — radio-frequency interference, the junk the human world poured into the sky. A telescope like Fenua lived in a permanent war against its own species: microwave ovens, phone masts, the ignition of an outboard motor crossing the lagoon. Half of radio astronomy was subtraction. But RFI was dirty and this was clean. Interference was spiky, bursty, tied to the clock of human life — it pulsed with the working day and vanished at three in the morning. It did not produce a smooth, patient, decades-long lean in a single quantity. RFI was a drunk shouting in the street. This was something breathing very slowly in another room.

She checked anyway, because checking was the job. She pulled the interference logs and laid them against the drift and found no correlation — the angle leaned on quiet nights and loud ones alike. If anything the drift was clearer in the quiet stretches, which was precisely backwards for interference and precisely right for a real signal from the sky.

She wrote that down. Then she crossed out the word *signal*, because she had not earned it, and wrote *feature* instead.

The second suspect was the hardware. Thirty years is a long time in the life of an instrument; over three decades Fenua had worn through four receiver packages, two entire cabling replacements, a firmware history that read like a genealogy of its own. Any of it could produce a slow instrumental drift. And the elegant thing about that theory, the thing that should have let her sleep, was that instrumental drift and a real celestial drift would look identical on the plot. You could not tell them apart by staring.

But you could tell them apart by the seams. If the drift lived in the hardware, it would jump when the hardware jumped — every receiver swap, every recabling, a cliff in the record where the new component brought its own offset, its own private lie. She had the dates of every one of those changes in the logbooks she had spent yesterday boxing. She spent the morning marking them on the plot as vertical lines, a picket fence of interventions across thirty years, and then she looked to see whether the drift jumped at the fences.

It did not.

The receiver swap of 2039 — nothing; the angle leaned through it as though the new hardware had inherited the old hardware's secret. The great recabling of 2047, when they had torn out every run and laid new fibre and she had personally recalibrated the whole polarisation chain from scratch, from *zero*, with a known reference source — nothing. The drift crossed that boundary smooth as water crossing a stone. And that was the wrongness that made the back of her neck go cold in the heat of the control room, because there was no honest way for an instrumental artefact to survive the complete replacement of the instrument. She had rebuilt the level itself in 2047. And the lean had simply continued from where it left off, as though it were not in the instrument at all but in the sky the instrument was pointed at, waiting on the other side of every repair.

She was still sitting with that when the call came in. She saw the name and answered, because there were perhaps three people left on Earth she would interrupt this for, and Marcus was two of them.

His face assembled itself on the screen in the particular way of a bad connection routed through a satellite and a sea — a beat behind his voice, so that he seemed always to be reacting a half-second late to his own life. He was in his office in Auckland, a window behind him full of the wrong hemisphere's grey, and he looked older than she let herself remember, which meant she looked older too. They had been together for four years, a long time ago, and then colleagues for the rest, which was either a failure or a rare success depending on the day you asked her.

"You've got a face on," he said. "That's your found-something face. I know that face. I proposed to you with that face, once, and you told me you needed to check the data."

"I did not tell you that."

"You told me you needed to *sleep on it*, which from you is the same sentence." He was smiling. "Go on. What's got you."

She should have deflected. Instead she told him — carefully, without the forbidden words, the way you carry a full cup — the polarisation of the Metronome, the constant that wasn't, the drift too clean for RFI, the seams that didn't jump. She shared the plot, thirty years leaning gently across his display in Auckland, and watched his face do what hers had done: the flick away, the flick back, the animal refusal.

"Huh," he said, which from Marcus was a paragraph. And then, because he was honest, which was the thing she had loved most and forgiven least: "You've already killed calibration error, or you wouldn't have shared your screen. If it survived '47 it's not in your chain." He rubbed his jaw. "Tuli. It's almost certainly *something*. Some slow galactic foreground, the interstellar medium doing something dull between you and the source over thirty years. That could be a nice little paper. But it's the boring kind of real." He said it gently, the way you offer someone a smaller grief to spare them a larger hope. "Don't build it into a cathedral. Not in your last week. You'll break your heart on an artefact and then the barge comes."

"I know," she said. "I know all of that. I taught you half of it."

"You taught me all of it and let me keep half." The old line; it had stopped being a joke years ago and become a kind of endearment. "Send me the reduced series. I'll look tonight, properly, sober. And Tuli — eat something. Ring the girl. Lani. She messaged me, did you know that? She's frightened for you and she's using me as a relay because you won't answer your phone."

That landed somewhere she wasn't ready for it to land. "I'll ring her," she said, and did not.

*The boring kind of real.* He was right, of course. He was almost always right, which was its own quiet insult across thirty years. She wanted it to be that. She was a careful woman and she wanted the boring answer, and she reached, again, to close the folder on it.

And again her hand stopped, because a thought had surfaced while Marcus talked, a small cold thought she had been keeping underwater, and now it would not stay down.

She pulled more of the archive. Not thirty years now — everything. She reached past her own tenure into the records of the dead men who had run Fenua before her, the founding data from the fifties reduced by hand and half-trusted, the gaps where the funding lapsed and the dishes stood idle. She stitched it together through the afternoon, correcting for every receiver, every recabling, every offset she could reconstruct, building the longest baseline that had ever been built for this one quantity — not thirty years but nearly seventy, the entire working life of the ears.

And she plotted the polarisation angle across all of it, and sat back, and understood, slowly, that she had made a mistake in how she'd been thinking.

She had been looking for the *period*. That was the reflex — you see a drift, you assume it turns, you look for the cycle, the point where it swings back and repeats, because everything in her science eventually came round again: pulsars, orbits, seasons, the long patient wheel of recurrence that let you predict and therefore trust.

It never crested. Seventy years of data, the longest such record on Earth, and the line only leaned — one direction, unhurried, unbroken, unturned. She was not looking at a cycle. She was looking at a *fragment* of one — a nearly straight segment, a chord cut from a circle so vast that seventy years of continuous observation rendered it, effectively, a line. The way the horizon at sea is a line, and is not.

She did the crude arithmetic in her head, and it refused to come out small. If the whole record she possessed, everything the ears had ever heard, was a single early portion of one turn — then the period of that turn was not years. It was not decades. It was longer than the instrument, longer than the entire span of human radio astronomy, longer than the science itself, reaching back past the invention of the telescope and forward past every date she would live to write.

Outside the tide had gone all the way out, and the reef lay exposed and steaming in the last of the light, the whole living shelf of it that had taken ten thousand years to grow one careful inch at a time, and above it the sky was beginning to show the first stars — old light, light that had left its sources before she was born, before the island was named, before the first canoe. She had spent her whole life telling herself she understood how long *long* was. She had carried the numbers the way you carry a foreign currency, confident of the arithmetic and numb to the value.

She looked at the line that would not turn, and for the first time in a very long time she was afraid — not the small professional fear of being wrong in a journal, but something older and colder, the vertigo of a small warm animal that has just been shown the true size of the room it has been living in. Something out there was turning. It was turning so slowly that seventy years of the most patient watching humanity had managed showed only a lean, a hint, the first faint syllable of a word too long to say in a lifetime.

She reached for a clean page in the logbook, the one box she had not yet taped shut, and wrote the date, and then, in the careful hand her grandmother would not have recognised and would have understood completely, a single question, so that in the morning she would know she had not dreamed it:

*How long?*

## Chapter 3 — The Wrong Length

By morning she had convinced herself it was real, which meant the next thing she had to do was try to kill it.

That was the part nobody outside the work understood — that belief and destruction were the same gesture, aimed twice. She had spent the night circling the console, coming back to the plot the way a tongue comes back to a broken tooth, and now she sat down to do the honest thing. She would build the modulation a model and then she would spend the day looking for the mistake inside it.

She fitted it. She was good at fitting; it was most of what a life in this work turned out to be, the patient marriage of a shape to a suspicion. She tried a line and it wanted to bend. She tried a parabola and it fit better, which meant nothing — a parabola will flatter anything over a short enough baseline, will lie down obligingly against any gentle arc you show it and call itself the truth. She tried a sinusoid, because that was what her gut had been muttering since two in the morning, and the machine chewed and returned a period.

Tuli read the number and laughed, one short unhappy sound in the empty hall.

The period was longer than the data. The best-fit cycle was something like four hundred years, and she had seventy at the outside. She was trying to measure the length of a sentence by staring at a single letter. The curvature was real, the bend was in the numbers and would not leave, but the *period* was a rumour the software had invented to explain a fragment — it had taken her sliver of arc and drawn, very confidently, the whole circle it might belong to, and the confidence was worth nothing at all.

"You don't know that," she told the screen. "You're guessing."

She widened the error bars herself, by hand, dragging them out until they were honest, and the fit sagged into a grey fan of possibility — four hundred years, or nine hundred, or eleven thousand, the software agreeable to any of them, because from inside a keyhole every corridor looks the same length. That was the whole problem, stated cleanly at last. Thirty years was a keyhole. Whatever the Metronome's light was doing, it was doing it on a scale that made the entire radio era — every dish humanity had ever turned to the sky — a single held breath in a very long song.

She made coffee and stood at the louvres while the sun came up hot and immediate over the reef, no dawn to speak of, just night and then the hammer of day. The tide was going out; she could smell it, that low green iodine breath the flats gave off when the water pulled back. Her grandmother had said, once, that the tide was the island breathing, and that you could set your whole life by it if you were patient enough to learn its longer rhythms — not the daily going and coming any child could see, but the slower swellings the moon dragged around across weeks and seasons, tides inside the tides, a nesting of clocks. Tuli had thought it superstition then. She thought now, watching the flats steam, that Fa‘amanu had simply been a better observer than her granddaughter had been willing to notice.

The trouble was not that she couldn't see the shape. The trouble was that to *prove* the shape she needed to see more of it, and to see more of it she needed time she did not have and would never have — no one would. You could not point a telescope at four centuries. The only way to catch a wave that long was to have already been watching before you were born, and to keep watching after you died, and to have the watching passed hand to hand without a single link lying about what it saw.

Nobody did that. Nobody kept a measurement clean across four hundred years.

She was still standing there, empty cup gone cold in her hand, when the outer door banged and a voice called into the hall, "Aunty? You in here or you finally left me the whole place?"

Lani came in out of the glare in a way that made the room seem, briefly, absurdly full — nineteen and unhurried, sandals slapping, a canvas bag over one shoulder and a green coconut in her free hand with the top already macheted off. She had Fa‘amanu's forehead. Tuli noticed it every time and every time refused to say so.

"You're supposed to call ahead," Tuli said. "This is a scientific installation."

"It's a shed full of old aunties talking to God." Lani set the coconut on the least important desk and dropped into a wheeled chair and pushed herself in a slow arc, taking the place in with the frank ownership of the young. "Mum sent breadfruit, it's in the truck, I'm not carrying it in until you're nice to me." She stopped the chair. "You look terrible. You sleep?"

"I was working."

"On the thing that ate your face." Lani nodded at the screen without really looking, and then — because she was Fa‘amanu's blood too, and sharper than she let the island see — she did look. "That's a wobble."

"It's a polarisation angle."

"It's a wobble. I did one year of physics, Aunty, I get to say wobble." She rolled closer. "What's it do?"

And Tuli, who had told no one, who had not even finished telling herself, found the words coming out anyway, because the alternative was to keep holding them alone. "It bends," she said. "The light from a — from a very steady thing, a lighthouse, out past the middle of the galaxy. It shouldn't bend and it does. Slowly. So slowly that in thirty years of watching I've caught maybe a hundredth of one full bend. Maybe less. I can't tell how long the whole thing is. I can see that it's a curve but I can't see the size of the curve. I need —" She stopped.

"You need more years," Lani said.

"I need more years than there are."

Lani considered this with the seriousness she gave to things that weren't about herself. "Than there are for you," she said, "or than there are?"

Tuli looked at her.

"Because those are different," Lani went on, blithe, wheeling herself another slow half-turn, not knowing she had said anything, which was how the true things usually arrived. "Anyway. That's not why I came." She reached into the canvas bag and produced a folded printout, the scholarship's crest at the top — Auckland, the marine biology place, the one that would take her nine thousand kilometres away and give her a life Tuli half-wanted for her and half-couldn't bear. "They moved the deadline up. I have to say yes or no by the end of the month."

"And?"

"And I don't know." She said it lightly and it landed heavy. "Everybody knows for me. Mum knows, the pastor knows, the whole village has a strong opinion about what the clever one should do. You're the only one who left, so. I wanted to ask the one who left."

"Don't ask me," Tuli said, more sharply than she meant. "I'm the worst person on this atoll to ask."

Lani let that sit, and did not push, which was its own kind of skill. Then she stood, and slung the bag, and delivered what she had actually come to deliver. "Matua Ropati's reciting tonight," she said. "The gafa. All of it, the long version, because old Sione finally died and his branch needs saying back into the line. It's at the fale, after the sun. You should come." She raised a hand before Tuli could refuse. "He asked after you. He always asks after you. It kills him a little, you know, that you're up here talking to the sky and you won't come down and hear your own name in the chant."

"I have work."

"You have a wobble you can't measure." Lani said it kindly, which was worse. "It'll keep. It's four hundred years long, you said. One night." She was already walking backward toward the glare of the door, grinning again, forgiving her in advance the way the island always did. "Breadfruit's in the truck. Be nice to me. I'll leave the coconut."

And she was gone, sandals on the concrete, the door banging, the day pouring back in white and enormous where she had stood.

Tuli did not go to the recital. She told herself she would, and then the afternoon ate itself the way afternoons did — a receiver dropped out and she spent three hours coaxing it back — and by the time she had it the sun was down and the fale would be full of people she'd half-avoided for two years, and she could not, she found, make her body walk down the hill toward them. She ate breadfruit standing over the sink. She drank two fingers of the bad rum she kept for genuinely bad days.

Then she went back to the hall, and sat down in front of the too-long curve, and looked at it in the dark with the receivers breathing around her, and thought about what Lani had said without knowing she'd said it. *Than there are for you, or than there are.*

She thought: the measurement needs to outlive the measurer.

She thought: that has never once been done. Not cleanly. Not across four centuries, not with a signal you could still trust at the far end. Every long record humanity kept was a game of telephone that ended in nonsense — copies of copies, drift and rot and the well-meaning lie, until the thing that came out the far end bore no honest relation to the thing that went in. You could not carry a number across ten generations without dropping it. Everyone knew that. It was practically a law.

The curve waited on the screen, patient, unmeasurable, real. To catch it she would need a record kept faithfully — call it faithfully, call it without error — across centuries, longer than the radio era, longer than the telescope, longer than the whole apparatus she had given her life to. She needed a thousand-year measurement made by people who had never heard of a pulsar, made and kept and passed on intact, so that she could lay it against her seventy years and finally see how big the wave was.

Somewhere down the hill, faint under the reef's endless collapse, a single voice had begun to carry up on the night wind — old, unhurried, rising and falling in a shape she knew in her body before she knew it in her mind, a shape her grandmother's mouth had once made over a child who hadn't wanted to learn it. Matua Ropati, saying the names. Saying the line. Carrying a list of the dead back thirty generations without a page of writing, exactly as it had been carried before him, and before him, and before that.

Tuli sat very still.

Where in the world, she had been asking herself all day, does one find a record that spans a thousand years.

The wind brought her the answer and she had spent her whole life walking away from it.

## Chapter 4 — Deep Archives

She did not sleep on it so much as let it work on her, the way seawater works on a mooring line, and in the morning she went hunting for a thousand years.

The proper places first, because she was a proper scientist and because the improper place frightened her. She had come to the idea in the night that the Metronome's slow bend, if it was real, must have left its fingerprint on more than her decades — that anything modulating the pulsar's light on a scale of centuries would also, faintly, be written into any long record sensitive to the same physics. What she needed were proxies. Old, patient, independent measurements that reached back past the radio era and did not know her curve existed and so could not be flattering it.

She started with ice. The great cores from the polar archives were public, or public enough, and she spent the morning scraping cadence data out of them over Fenua's wheezing satellite link — not the climate story everyone mined them for, but the layering, the annual banding, hunting for any faint periodic modulation in the range her fit had coughed up. Something. Maybe. A whisper of power in the band she wanted, right at the edge of meaning nothing — the kind of thing that vanished the moment you looked at it straight and came back when you looked away, which was either the signature of a real faint process or the signature of a scientist wanting one too badly. She had been on both sides of that line in her life and knew how thin it was.

She went to the eclipse records next, and these she loved, had always loved — the Babylonian tablets, the Chinese court astronomers, the medieval Arab observers, generations of frightened careful men who had watched the sky misbehave and written down *when*, to the hour, on clay and silk and vellum, across three thousand years. Modern astronomers used those records to measure how the Earth's spin had slowed, prying a deep-time signal out of the anxieties of the dead. If her modulation touched the propagation of the pulsar's light at all, it might leave a residue in the long timing residuals people had already teased from those ancient observations. She overlaid what she could find. She was careful. She widened every error bar until it hurt.

By late afternoon she had three proxies, all faint, all individually dismissible, none of them proof of anything. Three whispers were still just whispers. But they whispered in the same band. And to test whether they whispered *in phase* — whether they were the same voice reaching her by three different roads — she needed a fourth road, and an anchor: a long clean measurement, faithfully kept, from the part of the world that had no telescopes and no clay tablets and had crossed the largest ocean on Earth anyway, by reading the sky.

She sat with that for a while, the rum bottle within reach and untouched. Then she went and washed her face and put on a proper shirt and walked down the hill, in the last of the heat, to Matua Ropati's fale.

He was on the raised platform in the open-sided house, out of the sun, a very old man arranged with great dignity on a pandanus mat, and he watched her come the whole way up the path without pretending not to. He did not get up. Getting up was not what he was for.

"Teuila," he said, when she had slipped off her sandals and sat, low, the way you sat before a matai, the way her body remembered even as her mind protested it. Not *Tuli*. The whole name, the name that was hers in the chant. "So the sky let you go."

"For an afternoon."

"An afternoon." He turned the word over as though inspecting it for damage. His face was a topography of years, and his eyes, when they found hers, were not soft. "You did not come last night. Your name was said. Your grandmother's name was said. Fa‘amanu, who taught you to say them back, before you decided the palagi's machines were a better use of a Va‘a mouth." He let that sit in the va between them, the charged space-between, and did not soften it. "Thirty years I have been putting your name in the line for people who ask where you have gone. It is a long time to answer for someone who will not answer for herself."

Tuli looked at the mat. She had come to ask a favour of this man and he was going to make her earn the right to ask it, and she found she could not resent it, because he was right, and because being made to sit low on a mat and hear it said plainly was itself a thing she had crossed an ocean to escape and had missed without knowing.

"I know, Matua," she said. "I'm sorry. I stayed away too long."

"You did." He accepted the apology without discounting it. Then, because he was stern and not cruel: "But you came. Fa‘amanu's forehead came down the hill." Something moved at the corner of his mouth. "What does the sky want, that it sends you to an old man's mat?"

So she told him. Haltingly at first, in her science, and then — watching him not follow the words but follow *her*, the way an orator reads a room — she found the shape of it in the only terms that would carry. There is a light, she said, from very far, older than any of us, older than the island. It carries a mark. The mark changes, very slowly, so slowly that one lifetime cannot see the whole of it, and to read it I must lay my years against something much longer, something kept true across many lifetimes. And I have looked in the ice and in the old dead astronomers' books, and they whisper but they will not tell me where in the turning we are. And I remembered — she said, and stopped, and made herself say it — I remembered the gafa. That you keep the names true across thirty generations without a page of writing. That your fathers kept the star-paths, the rising points, the houses of the stars you steered by, in the chant, unchanged, for a thousand years and more.

The old man was quiet for a long moment.

"You want the star-lore," he said. "The fetū. The paths."

"I want to know how you keep it true," she said. "Across so long. I want to understand how it doesn't rot."

And Matua Ropati, tulāfale, keeper of the fa‘alupega, looked at the last astronomer of Fenua for a while longer, and then he did the thing she had walked down the hill both hoping and dreading — he closed his eyes, and he began to recite.

She had heard it as a child and slept through it. She had not heard it as what she was now.

The gafa came out of him slow and enormous, name giving birth to name, generation folding into generation, and she listened this time the way she listened to data — for structure, for architecture — and the architecture undid her. It was not a list. A list would have died in three generations; a single dropped name and everything after it is orphaned, unmoored, lost. This was not a list. Each name arrived *braced*. It came with its pairings, its marriage, its place fixed against two or three others, so that if one link ever failed the others held the shape of the gap and told you a name was missing and roughly what it must have been. The chant doubled back on itself, call and answer, the young men's voices — she could hear them, the recital was not solitary, there was a second and a third voice now, coming in from the shadows of the fale, catching the lines, throwing them back — so that no single mouth carried the whole load and no single failure could break it. When Ropati's memory reached for a name and hesitated a half-beat, another voice supplied it, and he took it back, and the line went on, self-correcting, redundant, *robust*, a thousand years of the dead marching in ranks so deep that you could shoot great holes in them and the ranks would close.

Tuli sat on the mat with her scientist's heart going hard in her chest, because she was hearing an error-correcting code. Not a metaphor for one. The thing itself. Redundancy, parity, distributed storage, call-and-response checksum — every trick her civilisation had invented in the last eighty years to shove a number across a noisy channel without corruption, these people had invented to shove their ancestors across a noisy millennium, and theirs had *worked*, theirs was working right now, in the mouths of teenagers in the dark, the names arriving unrotted from before the first European saw this ocean.

*This* was how you kept a measurement true across a thousand years. Not by writing it down, where it would burn or moulder or be locked in a language no one spoke. You built it into a living structure that repaired itself every time it was spoken, and you made the speaking a duty and a joy and an identity, so that the line would fight to keep saying it long after the reasons were forgotten.

The chant reached the star-paths. She heard the rising points named, the houses of the sky, the fetū that stood over this island and that one, the roads the great canoes had steered by — a sky-clock, calibrated and recalibrated by ten thousand crossings, held in the same self-correcting structure as the names of the dead, and just as true. And she understood, sitting there, that this was her fourth road and her anchor both.

When it was done — when Ropati opened his eyes and the young voices went quiet and the reef came back into the silence — he looked at her and saw that something had happened in her face.

"You heard it," he said.

"I heard it, Matua."

"Not the way a child hears it."

"No." Her voice was not entirely steady. "The way — " She stopped, and gave him the truest thing she had. "The way I should have heard it forty years ago."

He nodded, once, and something in the va between them eased, not all the way, but enough. "Come back," he said. "You have things to learn and I have not much time to teach them. That is a bad joke the two of us are standing in." The corner of his mouth moved again. "Bring your machine's questions. We will see what the old line remembers."

She walked back up the hill in the dark with the star-paths still turning in her head, and she did not go to bed. She went to the console, and she took the rising points from the chant — dated, phased, anchored across centuries — and she added them to the ice and the eclipses and the decades of the Metronome's slow bend, all of it forced onto one long axis reaching back past the radio era, past the telescope, past writing, into the deep time where the light had left its distant lighthouse before any of this island's dead were born.

She overlaid them.

For a moment the screen was only a mess, four ragged fragments at four different scales, and her heart dropped. Then she phased them — anchored the whole thing on the star-clock, the one clean thousand-year measurement she had, and let the others slide into their proper places against it.

They lined up.

Not perfectly. Not without slack. But the ice's faint whisper and the dead astronomers' residue and her own decades of bend all fell onto the *same curve*, the same long shallow deliberate wave, each fragment a different arc of one enormous circle, and where they overlapped they agreed, and where they didn't overlap they extended each other, the way the braced names in the chant extended across the gaps, telling her the shape of what she couldn't directly see.

Tuli sat back in the breathing dark of the empty hall and looked at ten thousand years of a single coherent signal assembled out of ice and clay and the mouths of the dead, and she said, out loud, to no one, in a voice gone strange:

"It's not noise."

The curve held. It did not sag into a fan of possibilities. It was one thing, saying one thing, across a span that made her entire life a held breath — real, coherent, not random, waiting.

Somewhere out past the galactic centre a lighthouse had been bending its light on purpose, and she had just, for the first time, seen far enough down the wave to be sure. Now she only had to learn to read it.

## Chapter 5 — Ten Thousand Years

The trick, in the end, was to stop trusting the clock.

Tuli had spent forty years teaching herself the opposite. A radio astronomer lived and died by timing; the whole apparatus of the field was a cathedral built to the worship of the exact second. But the modulation she was chasing did not live in the clock. It lived in the slow twist of the light's plane as it arrived, and that twist changed on a scale so long that no clock built by anything with a pulse could hold it. To read it she had to abandon the second and think in centuries, and to think in centuries she had to borrow other people's clocks — clocks that ran in ice and stone and memorised speech. Four clocks. None accurate to the second. All of them, together, accurate to the century.

What she had not let herself do, until tonight, was the greedy thing, the thing she had been circling for a week like a dog around a snake: not just confirm the signal, but extract it. Pull the sequence out whole. Line up all four clocks, resample the polarisation angle onto a single deep-time axis, and let the numbers fall where they fell.

She wrote the last of the code standing up, because sitting felt like presumption. The first run failed — a sign error in the precession correction, and she was almost relieved; the universe reasserting its indifference was a familiar comfort. She fixed it and ran it again.

The plot drew itself across the screen in a stutter of green.

For a moment she did not understand what she was looking at, because she had been braced for a curve — a slow sinusoid, something continuous and physical, the kind of thing a star does. This was not a curve. This was steps. The polarisation angle did not slide from one value to the next; it held, and held, and then it moved — a clean transition, a settling, a new plateau held for its own span, then another move. The angle was not varying. It was being *set*. Held at one position, then reset to another, the way a hand sets a stone down and leaves it and comes back and sets down another.

Discrete. Quantised. Not a wave. A string of values.

She counted the plateaus. The average width came out to a hundred and eleven point six years, with a scatter tight enough to make her mouth go dry — tight enough that the width itself was clearly meant, a clock inside the clock, a beat the sender was keeping deliberately so that anyone downstream would know where one unit ended and the next began.

A hundred and twelve years to a symbol. And the symbols were not random. She ran the flat honest statistics that had killed every hope she'd ever had, and the sequence came back structured — non-random at a confidence that, in any paper about any ordinary thing, would have ended the argument before it started.

She stood in the hum and looked at it. Roughly a hundred symbols, each a plateau a century wide, the whole thing reaching back something on the order of ten thousand years and running forward to now, to the light presently falling on the dishes, to this room.

Ten thousand years of held stone.

She had used the word *phoneme* in her notes days ago, a placeholder, and had meant to strike it. She did not strike it now. It was the right word, and the knowing was the most frightening thing that had happened to her in a professional life not short of fright. Nothing physical held a value dead level for a century and then stepped cleanly to the next and held that. Holding was expensive. Holding was work. Somewhere out there something was paying, symbol by symbol, the cost of being unambiguous, and it had been paying since before the first city on Earth had a wall.

She was not looking at a star. She was looking at somebody's care.

She sat down after all, because her legs had made the decision for her. Then she called Marcus.

It was past midnight where he was too. He answered on video with his reading glasses pushed up into grey hair, a book face-down on his chest, and the unguarded softness of a man woken who is not yet performing being awake.

"Marcus. I need you to be a physicist for ten minutes and then you can go back to sleep and decide I've lost my mind."

He looked at her properly. Whatever he saw made him sit up, and the book slid to the floor, and he did not reach for it. "Okay," he said. "I'm awake. Go."

She told him. She was, if anything, colder than the data warranted, because she needed him to try to break it, not to be swept. She gave him the four clocks and the resampling and the sign error she'd caught and the width of the plateaus and the flat statistics, and watched his face do the thing she'd watched it do for thirty years — the scepticism, the hunt for the boring explanation, the small unconscious frown when he couldn't find one.

"Instrumental," he said. "The feed, some slow thermal—"

"On a hundred-and-twelve-year period? The receiver's nine years old."

"Ionosphere. Faraday rotation—"

"Twists it fast and dirty and randomly. This is slow and clean and it *steps*. The ionosphere doesn't hold a value for a century and then decide to move. Nothing does. That's the whole point. That's why I'm calling you at midnight instead of writing it up like a sane person."

He was quiet. When he came back his voice had changed. The softness was gone and something more frightened had taken its place.

"If it's real," he said slowly, "you can't sit on it. It's not yours. You call Halvorsen, you get it timestamped and witnessed and out of that room before somebody decides to—"

"Before somebody decides to what."

"Before you have an accident with a hard drive," he said. "You know how this goes. The moment the Consortium understands what's on that array, Fenua stops being a decommissioning and becomes an asset. Halvorsen isn't a villain but she has a board, and the board will want it in a vault in Geneva with their name on the door and you thanked in a footnote. Go public *now*, while it's still yours to give away."

It was good advice, exactly the advice she would have given him. She sat with it and found that she did not want it, and made herself examine the wanting.

"If I go public tonight," she said, "what do I have? A hundred plateaus and a claim. I don't have a translation. I have proof that somebody spoke and no idea what they said. And the instant it goes up the chain, it goes to people with more machines and more money and more time than I've got, and they'll get it — eventually, in ten years, twenty. And I will be dead, or close to it, and I will have handed away the only thing I have ever found that mattered, at the exact moment before it meant anything, so that it would be *safe*." She looked at the dark beyond the window, the invisible sea saying its one long sentence against the reef. "I want to understand it before I let go of it. Not own it. Understand it. One symbol, so that when I hand it over I'm handing over a *word* and not a rumour."

Marcus was silent a long time. "Promise me you back it up," he said at last. "Off-island. Tonight. Promise me the message survives you even if nothing else does."

"I promise," she said, and meant it, and the ease of meaning it frightened her more than the data had, because it meant some part of her had already begun to plan around her own absence.

"Then hold it," he said. "God help you. Hold it. And call me. Every day, Tuli. I mean it."

She did back it up — three copies, one to an encrypted store in Auckland, before she let herself go back to the plot. Then she did the thing she had been saving.

She ran the sequence. Not analysed — *played*. She mapped each phoneme's angle to a value, laid them end to end, and let the reconstruction run across the screen from the oldest she could reach to the newest, ten thousand years scrolling past in ninety seconds, the held stones of it setting themselves down one after another after another. Then she ran it again, slower, watching not the values but the *shape* — the way the symbols grouped, the way certain ones recurred and framed the others, the redundancy, the call and its answer, the structure that was there so that meaning could survive the loss of any single beat.

She had expected, if she'd expected anything, a list. A table. Facts, coordinates, the periodic table in some universal hand — the tidy first contact of every hopeful paper she'd ever half-believed. A shout with information in it.

It was not a list. Lists did not have this shape. She had sat on a mat while an old man's hand kept time, and she knew now what she was seeing because he had taught her the difference without either of them knowing that was the lesson. This had the shape of a *sentence*. It opened, it built, it turned on itself and referred back to its own beginning, it carried one long line of intent from its first held stone to its last. Somebody was not reciting. Somebody was *saying*.

And the end of it — the newest phonemes, the ones still arriving, the light that had left its source when the pyramids were already old — the end of it lifted. She saw it and did not trust it and ran it again and saw it again. The final symbols climbed. The angle stepped up and up in the last stretch, a rising cadence, the intonation of a voice going up at the close of a line, the way every human voice in every language she had ever heard goes up, when it has stopped telling you something and started asking.

It was not a broadcast.

It was a question.

And it was not finished. The last phoneme was still being held — out there, now, tonight, a stone the size of a century set down and not yet lifted — and the sea went on against the reef, and Tuli sat very still in the humming dark and understood that somewhere a mouth she would never see was, at this exact moment, in the middle of asking her something, and would go on asking it for another hundred years, long after she had stopped being anyone at all.

## Chapter 6 — The Slow Ones

For three days she did not try to read the question. She tried to read the questioner.

It was, she told herself, the disciplined order of operations: you do not translate a language before you know something of the mouth that speaks it. But the truth was simpler. She was afraid of the meaning and unafraid of the mathematics, and so she went where the fear was thinner.

A hundred and eleven point six years to a symbol. She did the arithmetic she'd been avoiding, on the concrete step outside the control room in the grey before the heat. If a phoneme took a hundred and twelve years, then a word took four or five centuries, and a sentence ran to a thousand years and more. She tried to make it human and it would not go human. Speech, for a person, sat on the breath — a syllable the length of one exhalation, the span of a single heartbeat. That was the body setting the tempo.

So she inverted it. If these beings spoke at the speed they were built to speak — if a phoneme sat on one breath of theirs, one beat of whatever kept them going — then a single heartbeat of theirs was a century of Earth's. A human life, her own fifty-eight years, all of it — the islands and the leaving and Marcus and the grandmother in the ground and every single thing she had ever done or failed to do — was not even one full beat of the thing that was speaking. It was less than the pause a person leaves before the next word.

She sat with that on the cold step until the mug was undrinkable, and found she was not horrified. She was — the only word that came was the old church one — in *awe*, in the exact and terrible original sense that has fear folded into it and does not apologise for the fear.

They thought across deep time. Not metaphorically. Their thoughts were the length of civilisations. Whatever they were — she had no picture and refused to invent one, she would not insult them with a face — they lived at a tempo where the ice ages were weather, the pyramids a recent noise, and a species could rise, learn to catch radio, and die again inside the space of one of their spoken clauses. And humanity had caught the tail. Only the tail. They had been speaking before there was anyone here to listen, and the species had happened to build the one narrow instrument that could see the twist in the light, and she — Teuila Va‘a, on a station scheduled for deletion — had happened to point it at the right dead star in the last few years before they turned it off.

She should have felt small; she had been braced, all her life, for the cosmos to make her feel small. But it was not smallness she felt on the step that morning. It was the opposite and stranger thing: that something on the scale of ice and continents had bent the shape of its attention toward *saying*, toward the patient expensive work of being understood, and meaning of that kind did not care how small you were. It cared only whether you were listening. And she had been listening. For once in the whole indifferent sky, something had spoken, and she had been awake.

She went inside to work, happier than she had been in a decade, which is worth recording, because of what came after.

---

It came four days later, in the afternoon, and it did not announce itself.

She was at the terminal aligning the redundancy — pairing the call-symbols with their answer-symbols the way Ropati's chant paired the name of a father with the response of the room. Her hands were on the keys. And then her hands were the least of her, because there was a fist in her chest.

That was the only word for it, and she used it later, to the doctor, to Marcus, because it was exact. Not a pain, at first, a *presence* — as if something had reached in under the sternum and taken hold and was making a slow, deliberate, uninterested fist, and every beat had to be squeezed out past it. The room did not spin. It did something worse; it stayed perfectly still and went grey at the edges, the colour draining inward the way the tide leaves the flats. Her left arm was wrong. Her jaw ached. She had time to think, with a clarity that appalled her afterward, *it's already backed up, good,* before she was on the floor and did not remember arriving there.

She did not black out fully. That was the mercy and the horror of it — she stayed just present enough to be afraid, lying on the cool concrete with her cheek against the grit of coral dust, listening to her own heart make its uneven, unconvincing case for continuing. She counted it, because counting was what she was for. It was not counting well. It stumbled, caught, ran a clot of fast shallow beats and then a long sick pause that went on past when it should have ended, and in that pause she understood with her whole body what she had understood all week only with her arithmetic: that a heartbeat is a small thing, a brief thing, and the entire foundation on which a person is built, and that hers was negotiable.

It let go of her eventually. She lay a while longer, not trusting it, then got up because there was no one to get up for her. She has never entirely forgiven herself the fact that she went first to the terminal, still swaying, to confirm the backup had completed. Only then did she raise the atoll's one clinic.

The clinic was two rooms and a generator and a young locum from Suva named Dr. Ravai who was better than the building deserved. He ran the ECG strip twice, held the second up to the window light, and was quiet in the particular way she had learned, over one long week, to read as *structure, not noise* — the quiet of a man looking at a pattern he wished were random and knowing it was not.

"Dr. Va‘a," he said. "You should not have been standing up, four days ago or now." He traced with his pen the thing that was wrong, the spacing that stumbled and caught and ran and paused, and she looked at her own heart written out as a line on paper and thought, unstoppably, of the plateaus on her screen, the held stones, the beat inside the beat. Everything the universe was telling her lately, it told her in the language of timing. "This is not a warning," Ravai said, giving her the dignity of the straight version because she had asked for it. "This is the event. You had it on the floor of your station with nobody there. That you are sitting in my chair is luck, and luck does not renew."

He named it. The name mattered less than the shape of it: something structural and progressive in the muscle and its wiring, a fault that would not mend and could not, here, be fixed — that in a city with a catheter lab might have been managed for years, and that here was simply a countdown she could not read the face of.

"How long," she said, because she was what she was.

He did not insult her with *it's impossible to say.* "If you go to Auckland tomorrow, and they take you, and it goes well — years, maybe. If you stay—" He looked at her, and at the strip, and out the window at the dishes. "Months. I would not promise you the year."

Months.

She sat with the word and it would not fit in her mouth. She had spent the week learning to think in centuries, retuning her whole mind to the tempo of something that spent a hundred years on a single sound, and now the same universe leaned in close and set down, dead level, without cruelty and therefore with a cruelty past bearing, its own held stone: *months.* The phoneme they were holding tonight — the one out there, a century wide, set down and not yet lifted — they would still be holding it, unchanged, in the year her grand-niece Lani was her age, in every year of every life not yet begun of everyone Tuli had ever loved. And Tuli would not hear it lifted. She would not hear the next one begin.

"I'll think about Auckland," she said, which they both knew was a no, and Ravai had the grace not to push, only to write her a script for the pills that would buy the days a little slack.

---

She called Marcus that night. She had meant to be careful, the cold clear voice describing another woman's symptoms, to protect him the way she had protected everyone her whole competent solitary life. She got as far as *I saw a doctor today* and then she did not get any further for a while, and he waited, and when she could speak again she told him the true version. The floor. The fist. The strip in the window light and the word *months.*

"Come home," he said, when he could. "Come to Auckland. Let them—"

"And do the message from a hospital bed with tubes in me and someone else's hands on the array? No." She was not angry. She was past angry, out the other side of it into somewhere very quiet. "I'm not going to trade the reading for the year, Marcus. I'd rather have the months and the message than the years and a footnote."

"That's not—" He stopped. He had never been able to argue with her arithmetic and he did not try now. "I'm not frightened of losing the message. I want you to hear me say that. I care that you'll be on that floor again and I'll be a phone call and eleven seconds away."

"I know," she said. "I know you do." And then, because he had earned it, because he was the one person alive she would not perform for, she gave him the thing under all the arithmetic, the thing she had not said even to herself until saying it made it true.

"I'm afraid, Marcus."

He went still. She had not said it to him in thirty years. She was not sure she had ever said it to anyone.

"Not of them. That's the strange part. They've spent ten thousand years being patient with a species that only started listening last Tuesday. I'm not afraid of the Slow Ones." Her voice held. She made it hold. "I'm afraid of dying in the middle of it. There's a question out there a hundred years wide and it's still being asked and I am going to die before I understand it, and it will go on being asked, exactly the same, over the top of my grave and everyone's. I found the one thing that outlasts everything, and it's going to outlast me before I've read it. I'm afraid of leaving it half-heard."

The sea went on against the reef, the long slow sentence of it.

"Then we read it," Marcus said, very quietly, and the *we* went into her like the pills, like slack in the countdown. "Whatever's left. However long that is. You're not alone anymore. Back it up, don't be alone, and we read the slow ones together for as long as you've got."

*The Slow Ones.* He had taken her word — she had used it once, tonight, without deciding to — and given it back with capitals in it, made it their name, and she found she was crying at last, not for the diagnosis and not even for the years, but because something on the scale of ice had bent toward saying, and something on the scale of months had been chosen to hear it, and there was no clock in the universe fast enough or slow enough to make the two fit.

"The Slow Ones," she agreed, into the dark, into the hum, into the long light that had left its source before any of this and was only now, tonight, arriving. "Alright. Yes. Together." And she was afraid, and she said so, and she went on listening.

## Chapter 7 — The Question

Tuli had spent forty years listening to things that could not talk back, and so she recognised, before she could prove it, the particular vertigo of being addressed.

It came at half past three in the morning, in the concrete cool of the control room. She had laid the message out the only way that had ever made sense to her: a line of marks unrolling left to right across three stitched monitors, each mark a phoneme, each phoneme a full setting of the angle that the Slow Ones had cut into the Metronome's light across a hundred and twelve years of their patience. Ninety marks. Ninety syllables that took ten thousand years to say. She had been staring at them for a week the way you stare at a word until it stops meaning anything.

The trouble was that she had been reading them as a statement. As something that ended.

What broke it open was not the code. It was the memory of Ropati on the mat — the way he had not recited a list. He would say a name, and then say it again folded into the next, and then double back — *and this one, who was the child of that one, whose mother was* — laying each generation down twice, three times, from different sides, so that if you lost a name to a cough or a passing truck you could still reconstruct it from the names that leaned against it. He could be corrected, mid-breath, by a woman across the room who had heard her own grandmother do it differently, and the two of them would negotiate the discrepancy until the line came clean again. It was not memory as she had been taught memory, a bucket you filled and spilled. It was memory as a protocol. Call and response. Parity. Error correction running across three hundred years of human mouths, and holding.

She had been reading the Slow Ones as if they had left a monument. A thing said once, carved, abandoned to the light.

But nobody who thought in deep time would carve. Carving was for people who trusted stone more than each other. People who thought across ten thousand years, who had learned — as Ropati's people had learned across their own smaller ocean of time — that nothing physical survives that long intact, would not build a statement. They would build a protocol. And a protocol has a shape, and the shape of a protocol is not a full stop.

It is a turn.

She went back to the ninety marks and saw it the way you see the second face in the drawing. The message did not end. It rose. The final structure — the last eleven phonemes she had spent a month treating as a decorative coda, a signing-off — was not a coda. It was the same grammatical hinge that Ropati put at the seam between one generation and the next. *And who, then.* It was a socket. It was cut to receive.

The whole ten thousand years of it was call. It was built, from the first phoneme, to be answered.

"Oh," she said, out loud, to the empty room and the sea. "Oh, you clever bastards."

She did not sleep. She worked the meaning the way Ropati worked a contested name, from every side she had, cross-checking the slow grammar against the redundant echoes the senders had built into their own transmission — because of course they had built in redundancy, of course they had, they had said everything two and three times from different angles, exactly as the old man in the good chair, and how had she not seen that a message meant to cross deep time and a genealogy meant to cross deep time would be built by the same pressures into the same shape. Convergent. The ocean makes the same fish twice. Deep time makes the same sentence twice, on two worlds.

By the grey of first light she had a translation she trusted. Not word for word — the Slow Ones had no words, only relations, only the va between one state and the next — but she trusted the sense of it the way she trusted the sense of the tide. She wrote it on the back of a defunding letter from the Consortium because it was the paper nearest her hand, and later she would think there was something right about that, the sentence written on the back of its own death warrant.

*We have been speaking a long time. Is there anyone there steady enough to answer?*

She sat with it. Outside, the frigatebirds came off the roost and went out over the lagoon, black scissors against the paling sky. She read the sentence again on the back of the letter. It did not change.

It was not a question about existence. That was what took the breath out of her, slowly, over the next hour, as the full weight of it arrived the way water arrives, without hurry, filling every low place. They were not asking *is anyone there.* They had solved that; they were speaking, they had chosen to speak, they assumed a listener the way you assume the far shore. They were asking something crueller and more intimate. They were asking whether the listener was *continuous.* Because to answer them at all you would have to speak back in their register, one phoneme per century, and hold your meaning steady, hold your very *self* steady, across the thousands of years it would take to complete a single reply. They were not asking if you existed. They were asking if you could *keep a promise.* If there was anything on your world that lasted.

And she thought of the Consortium, which would not last the quarter. She thought of the observatory, forty years old and dying under her, the youngest institution she could name and already a ghost. She thought of the languages she had watched die on this ocean, and the ones her own family had let go of, the words for tides her grandmother had and she did not. She thought of nation-states that lasted a few centuries drunk on the idea of their own permanence. She thought of the internet, which could not hold a link for a decade. She thought of her own body, the traitor muscle in her chest ticking its faulty metronome, and she thought: I am the steadiest thing this discovery has, and I am the least continuous thing in this room. The dishes will outlast me. The letter I wrote this on will outlast me.

*Is there anyone there steady enough to answer.*

No.

That was the answer. She let herself have it, alone, in the grey. No. There was no institution, no language, no memory, no machine, and no woman on this earth that could hold a coherent transmission across ten thousand years, or one thousand, or even the five hundred she'd need just to begin. To reply to the Slow Ones you would have to become the kind of thing they already were — a mind that persisted past its own components, that survived the death of every cell that first thought the thought — and humanity had never once managed it, not for its gods, not for its empires, not for love. Everything humans made to outlast themselves, they made out of the wrong material. Stone forgets. Steel rusts. Files rot. The message she had spent her last good year of health pulling out of the sky was a question, and the honest answer was silence, and she would die knowing that the one time the universe had leaned across the dark and asked whether we were steady enough, the answer had been no, and she had been the one to have to know it.

The self-pity of that thought disgusted her even as she had it. But it did not leave.

The next night was worse. That was the night she nearly did it.

She told herself afterward that it was fatigue, that the medication thinned everything, but she knew that was only part true. Part of it was cleaner and colder than that. The Meridian people had started to circle — she'd had the first email that day, all bland menace, *strategic significance, appropriate custodianship* — and she had understood, sitting alone with the second glass of wine she was not supposed to have, that the discovery would outlive her and would not outlive her *intact.* It would be taken. It would be classified, relocated, cut off the island and off the sky and stapled into some vault, turned from a question the ocean could hear into an asset three governments would fight over. The Slow Ones had asked whether anyone was steady, and the answer humanity was preparing to give was to lock the question in a drawer and quarrel over the key. And if that was the answer, then maybe the kinder thing, the *cleaner* thing, was for the answer to be nothing at all. A true silence rather than a false one. Let it be lost the honest way, in the sky, instead of the ugly way, in a safe.

She opened the archive. She had root. She had built the whole thing; there was no version of it she could not reach. The ten thousand years sat in front of her in a folder she could put in the trash with two clicks, and empty the trash with a third, and overwrite the sectors with a command she could type from memory, and then there would be no message, and no theft, and no failure, because you cannot fail to answer a question that no one can prove was ever asked.

She put the cursor on it. Her hand was steady, which was somehow the worst part. Forty years of steady hands.

And then she heard it — not a voice, she was not a woman who heard voices, but the shape of the old man's cadence, the call-and-response of it, laid down twice, three times, so that no single failure could take the line. *And this one, who was the child of that one.* Ropati would be asleep now down in the village, carrying three hundred years in his body, and in the morning he would wake and the three hundred years would still be there, because he had never once trusted them to a single mouth, or a single stone, or a single drive. He had trusted them to the *between.* To the va. To the fact that there was always someone across the room who could correct you.

She took her hand off the trackpad. She took it off slowly, the way you take your hand off a thing that is still warm, still capable of being touched again.

She did not delete it. She backed away from the machine as if from a ledge, and she left the room, and she stood on the concrete apron under a sky stiff with more stars than any city person would believe, and she understood only that she had almost done a terrible thing and had been stopped, and that she did not yet know by what.

*Is there anyone there steady enough to answer.*

"I don't know," she said, to the light that had left long ago. "I don't know yet. Give me a minute. I'm working on it."

The tide came in. She let it.

## Chapter 8 — What the Tide Brings

The world found her three days later, which was faster than the world usually managed on Vaituga.

It found her first through Elin Halvorsen, calling at an hour that was polite in Geneva and cruel on the atoll, her face composed into the particular sympathy of a person about to make your life harder. The Consortium's systems had flagged an unusual pattern of archive activity on the Fenua node. Very high pulls. Repeated. Off-schedule. Given the decommissioning timeline, they simply had to ask what was being accessed.

"I'm a scientist reading my own data," Tuli said. "On my own array. Shocking."

"No one's accusing you of anything, Teuila." A pause with weather in it. "There's been interest," Halvorsen said, "from outside the Consortium. In the site. I'm not at liberty. But I'd — off the record, I'd get your affairs in order. The scientific ones. Sooner than the schedule says."

She knew what that meant before the second email arrived that afternoon, and it did arrive, and it was worse than the first because it was warmer. The Meridian Group thanked her for her decades of distinguished stewardship. They noted the *national and strategic significance* of the Fenua findings. They proposed, at no cost to her or to the government of Vaituga, to *ensure the discovery's security and continuity* by relocating the relevant instrumentation and archives to a *hardened facility* off-island. A team could arrive within the month. There was a logo. There was, at the bottom, a defence-ministry co-signatory from a country that was not hers and was not Vaituga's.

She read it twice and then she laughed, one hard note, because there was nothing else to do with it.

They wanted to take it off the island. Of course they did. That was the oldest sentence in this ocean, older than the Metronome's message, older than the gafa: *there is something of value here, and it should not stay with the people it belongs to, and we will call the taking of it security.* They had done it with the phosphate and the fish and the airfield and the young men, and now a thing had fallen out of deep space onto a decommissioned dish on a poor atoll, and men with logos had smelled it from the far side of the world and were coming to put it in a vault. She thought of every museum she had ever stood in, full of other people's ancestors behind glass, labelled in a language the ancestors never spoke.

Not this one, she thought. Not this ear. Not the last one.

She did not know yet how she would stop them. She only knew, with a clarity that surprised her, that she would.

Marcus came on the Thursday flight.

She had not, exactly, asked him to. She had written to him the way she wrote to him about everything — a long, dry, technical email about the message, the question-structure, the redundancy, Ropati's gafa — and somewhere in the fourth paragraph she had mentioned, in a subordinate clause, the way you'd mention weather, *the Auckland cardiologist wants me back for more tests but I've told him the sky can't wait,* and Marcus, who had loved her thirty years ago and had never entirely stopped and had read her subordinate clauses for all of them, had written back two words — *I'm coming* — and booked the flight before she could assemble a refusal.

She stood at the little airport with the heat coming off the tarmac and watched him come down the steps: heavier than the man she'd known, grey all through now, a linen shirt already losing to the humidity, and the same unhurried way of moving, as if the world would keep for him.

"You look terrible," he said, taking her hand.

"I'm dying, Marcus, I'm allowed."

"You've been dying since I met you. You were dying about the funding in 2034." But his hand tightened on hers, and his voice, under the wryness, was not steady, and she let him not be steady because one of them had to be allowed.

They worked first. That was who they were; grief and desire could both wait behind physics. She showed him everything in the control room with the louvres open and the sea in the background, and he did what she had needed someone to do for a week, which was to argue with her — to try to break the translation, to attack the question-reading, to insist she'd fooled herself with the gafa parallel, until, unable to break it, he went quiet and stood looking at the ninety marks on the stitched monitors for a long time.

"It's asking if we can keep a promise," he said finally, "across ten thousand years."

"Yes."

"And we can't."

"No institution can. No language. Nothing we've built." She heard her own flatness. "I nearly deleted it, Marcus. Two nights ago. My hand was on it."

He didn't perform shock. That was one of the mercies of him. He just came and stood beside her, shoulder to her shoulder, and looked at the thing that had almost been unmade, and said, "But you didn't."

"Ropati stopped me. Sort of. The idea of him."

"Then we start there," he said. "In the morning. Not tonight." And he looked at her, at fifty-eight and unwell and lit blue by the monitors, and the look was not clinical and not careful, and she understood that the evening had turned a corner while she wasn't watching it.

They ate on the veranda of the observatory house, fish Lani had dropped up, cold beer he was not sure she should have and poured her anyway. The tide came up under the palms. They talked about Fa‘amanu, who had feared the sea and lived beside it eighty years; they talked about the Meridian letter, and he swore, properly, a good hard Samoan-inflected profanity that she'd carried to Auckland thirty years ago and left in his mouth like a coin. They laughed. The laughing was what undid the last of it. You could hold a line against a lot of things but not against being known and laughed with, at the end of the world, by a man who had come nine hundred miles because of a subordinate clause.

When she reached for him it was without ceremony, the way you'd reach for a rail. He came to her carefully, both of them careful, mortality in the room like a third presence they had agreed not to introduce. His hands knew her still, or knew the woman she'd been and found her inside the woman she was, and she let herself be found. There was the slow negotiation of two aged and imperfect bodies that had once been young and certain together and were neither now; there was the ridiculous tenderness of him pausing, of her feeling him listen for the count of her heart against his chest, that faulty metronome, and match his breathing to it as if he could carry the rhythm for her if it faltered. Somewhere in it she stopped being a dying woman and a discoverer and the last scientist at Fenua and was only, for a while, held. The sea kept its time under the house. She let the door close on the rest.

Afterward they lay in the dark with the fan turning and the reef breathing and did not speak for a long time, and the not-speaking was full, the good kind, the va between them charged and easy.

"They're going to take it," she said at last, into the dark. "Meridian. Off the island. Call it security." She felt him go still. "And even if they didn't — it doesn't solve the real thing. The message needs an answer that lasts ten thousand years. Everything I can think of to hold it, I can name the century it fails. The Consortium fails this quarter. I fail this year. Nations fail. Files fail. There's nothing steady enough, Marcus. That's the whole cruelty of it. They asked the one question we're built to fail."

He was quiet, tracing her shoulder. Then: "You keep saying institutions. Languages. Machines." A pause. "You keep listing the things that don't last."

"Because those are the things that carry information. That's what information *lives* in."

"Is it." She heard him turn his head on the pillow. "Tuli. You cracked this whole thing because a ninety-one-year-old man sat in your good chair and recited three hundred years of names without a single note. Without a file. Without an institution. And he can be corrected by his cousin across the room, and the correction holds, and it's held for three hundred years, and it'll hold for three hundred more after he's gone because he's already given it to Lani and Lani doesn't even know she's got it yet." His hand stopped. "You've been looking for something steady enough to carry a promise across deep time, and you've been looking at the sky and the archives, and the whole time there's been a technology on this island older than radio that does *exactly that.* Faithful transmission. Redundancy. Error correction. Thirty generations and holding. You said it yourself — deep time makes the same sentence twice. Maybe it makes the same *machine* twice."

She lay very still.

It came up in her the way the tide comes up, without hurry, filling every low place. Not a machine. Not a vault, not a drive, not a nation, not a woman who would be dead by the wet season. Not a thing you owned at all, which was why Meridian would never understand it, which was why it could not be seized and carried off to a hardened facility, because it did not live in the object, it lived in the *between* — in the va, in the mouths, in the passing of a name from an old man to a girl who complained the whole time she was learning it. A technology for keeping a promise across a thousand years that had never once been recognised as a technology because it did not look like one to people who thought stone and steel were how you spoke to the future. Her own people had built the very instrument the Slow Ones were asking for, built it for something else entirely, built it out of the only material that had ever actually lasted on this earth, which was *each other,* and it had been sitting in her good chair drinking tea she'd forgotten to give it.

You could not answer the Slow Ones with an institution.

You could answer them with a genealogy.

"Oh," she said, exactly as she had said it to the empty room three nights before, the same syllable, a different door.

Marcus waited. He was good at waiting; the ocean would keep for him.

The window had gone from black to the first grey. She could hear, far down the ridge, a rooster, and then another answering it, call and response, the oldest protocol. She sat up in the grey light, her heart doing its faulty steady work, and she was not afraid of the months anymore, or not only afraid, because for the first time since Auckland the thing she carried did not have to fit inside her own lifespan.

"Marcus," she said. "I know who can keep a promise for a thousand years."

He looked at her in the dawn, and he didn't ask who. He'd already heard it. He just reached over and took her hand, the way you take a rail, the way you take a name that's being passed to you, and outside the roosters called and were answered, and the tide, which takes everything, had for once brought something in.
