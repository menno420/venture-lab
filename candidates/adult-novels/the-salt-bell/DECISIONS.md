- **Premise fidelity** — Delivers the concept as scored (24/30): Zeeland, the North Sea flood of 31 January–1 February 1953 and the reconstruction after. A village *koster* (sexton) and *klokkenist* — trained young on a city carillon, returned home to keep one bell and a tower clock — in the oyster country of the Oosterschelde. When the dike goes in the night, the bell he tends becomes the alarm, then the refuge-beacon, then the tolling for the dead, then — in the rebuilding — a bell that has to learn its ordinary job again. The book's question, from the brief verbatim: **what a bell is for when the water is already in the streets.** No villain — the antagonist is the sea and, behind it, the low ground itself; the "public work of genuine national good" (the Delta Works, born of this disaster) is watched with the house's standing ambivalence (the dike is right and ruinous at once, as in Sweetwater's Afsluitdijk and Wire Garden's Dodendraad — here the pattern is the great sea-answer that comes only after the count).

- **Historical accuracy — verified spine** (checked this session against the standard record via web search, incl. [North Sea flood of 1953 — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Sea_flood_of_1953), accessed 2026-07-16): the disaster fell in the night of **Saturday 31 January into the morning of Sunday 1 February 1953**, when a severe northwesterly storm over the North Sea coincided with a **spring tide**, driving a storm surge to record levels; sea dikes failed in many places across Zeeland, South Holland and North Brabant; **about 1,836 people died in the Netherlands** (with hundreds more around the North Sea in England, Belgium, and at sea), roughly **100,000 evacuated**, some **150,000 hectares** (~340,000 acres) flooded, **~47,300 buildings damaged** and thousands destroyed, and on the order of **30,000 livestock** drowned; the warning failed to reach many villages because it came late, at night, on a weekend, with the radio off air, telephone and telegraph lines down, and no national alarm reaching the countryside; church bells and human voices were in places the only alarm; churches, church towers, high houses, and dike-remnants served as refuges; rescue came over days by an improvised armada of inland fishing boats and army amphibious craft; the final large breaches in Zeeland were closed later that year with sunken **caissons** (the last, at Ouwerkerk on Schouwen-Duiveland, in **November 1953**, with four surplus Second-World-War Phoenix caissons); the disaster launched the **Delta Commission** (installed February 1953) and the **Delta Works / Deltaplan**, the great programme that dammed the sea arms and was decades in the building. Two grounded background facts carried in prose: the German wartime **bell requisition** (the *klokkenvordering* / "klokkenroof" of 1942–43, when occupying forces took down and melted many Dutch church bells for their metal, some later recovered from depots after the liberation), which is why the village's bell in the story is a **post-war recast (1949)**; and Zeeland's provincial motto **"Luctor et Emergo"** (I struggle and emerge), which the text uses once, plainly.
- **Historical accuracy — marked inventions** — All named characters are fictional (Marinus and Neeltje de Ridder; their daughter Jacomina "Mina" and her husband Leendert Verschuure; the grandson Bram; dominee Aris Vinke; the sluice-keeper Kees Boot; the widow Janne; the schoolmaster); **no real person appears or speaks anywhere in the text** — the Queen, the Delta Commission, and the relief effort act only as reported facts, never staged, and no real victim is named or depicted. **The village of Zoutkerke and its polder (the Sint-Maartenspolder) are wholly invented** — a plausible low polder set in the Oosterschelde oyster country, an hour by bicycle from the real Yerseke, laid over no specific real drowned village; the real Zeeland places that suffered (Schouwen-Duiveland, Tholen, the Kruiningen/Kortgene shore, Ouwerkerk) are named only as the surrounding province, never as the site of the invented action. The specific dike breach, the harbour, the church of Zoutkerke, its bell and inscription, and every scene are fiction. The caisson closing of *Zoutkerke's* breach dramatizes the real technique used across Zeeland that year without documentary basis as a specific case. Ration/relief particulars and the timing of the second tide are handled at newspaper precision, not archival precision.
