# THE SALT BELL

*A novella of the Zeeland flood*

> Zoutkerke, on the low polder behind the sea dike, the winter of 1953. Marinus de Ridder has kept the church, wound the tower clock, and rung the one bell of his village for thirty years — a bell recast after the war to replace the two the occupiers took for their metal. He has spent a life giving order to time, an hour struck for every hour there is. On the last night of January the sea comes over the dike in the dark, before any warning can reach the polder, and the bell that has counted so many ordinary hours is asked at last what a bell is truly for.

---

**Content note:** Adult literary-historical fiction set during and after the North Sea flood of 1953 (the Dutch Watersnoodramp), a real disaster. Contains a drowning and its aftermath (restrained, largely off-page), bereavement and grief, the loss of a community and its rebuilding. No graphic violence; no explicit content. The village and every character are invented; see the author's note. For adult readers.

# Chapter One — The Hours

The bell was cast to call the living and mourn the dead, and for thirty years Marinus de Ridder had mostly asked it to do neither, but only to keep the time.

He wound the tower clock every third day, in the grey of the afternoon, climbing the ladder-stair inside the brick with the crank-key warm in his pocket where he had carried it from the kitchen to take the chill off the iron. There were sixty-one rungs from the church floor to the clock chamber; he had counted them the first week his father handed him the keys in 1922 and had never needed to count them since, his knees keeping the tally for him now, one complaint per rung. In the chamber the works stood in their oak cage — the great toothed wheels, the going train and the striking train, the stone weights on their frayed ropes going down the dark of the tower like plumb-lines dropped into a well — and he wound the two weights up hand over hand until the pawls clacked home, and oiled what wanted oil, and stood a moment in the smell of iron and pigeon and old grease that was the smell of his whole working life, and listened to the escapement tick the seconds off into the cold. Above him, in the belfry, hung the bell.

There was one bell now. There had been two when he was a boy — the great one and a smaller, the *voorslag*, that struck the halves — and both of them had gone down the tower on a Tuesday in the spring of 1943, lowered on the occupiers' tackle by men who would not meet the eyes of the village, and been carted to the station with the bells of Kruiningen and Wemeldinge and half the province, to be melted into the war. He had stood in the churchyard that day with his hat in his hands as though at a burial, which it was. For two years the tower said nothing. You did not know, he had learned then, how much of a village lived in its bell until the bell was gone and the hours came and went unmarked and the people grew, without saying so, a little more lost. When the war ended they got word that some of the stolen bells had been found in a yard in the north, laid out in their thousands like a cemetery of bronze, but theirs were not among the saved; theirs had gone into the furnace early. So the village did what villages do. They gathered — guilders and cheese-money and a collection at every wedding for three years — and in 1949 the founder at Heiligerlee cast them a new one, a single great bell, and around its shoulder in a raised band he set the old words the tradition gives a bell, which Marinus, who had learned his Latin from a carillon and not a school, could say in either tongue: *Levenden roep ik, de doden beklaag ik.* The living I call, the dead I mourn.

He had rung it up the tower himself the day it came, and wept, and told no one, and had asked it since only to strike the hours.

That was the strange arithmetic of his trade, and he had made his peace with it long ago. A koster gives his life to a building and a bell that exist for the great occasions — the births and the deaths, the danger and the deliverance — and spends that life almost entirely on the small ones: the sweeping, the winding, the opening and the shutting, the hour struck for every hour there is whether anyone is listening or not. Marinus had wanted, once, to be a different kind of man of bells. As a young one he had gone to the city and learned the carillon — the great keyboard of batons and pedals in the tower at Goes, four octaves of bronze you played with the sides of your fists, so that a man alone in a stone room could put a hymn or a folk-tune or the whole of his heart out over the roofs of a town that never saw him. He had been good at it. But his father had sickened, and the village had no one, and a stiffness had come into his left hand the winter he turned twenty-six that took the run out of his playing; and he had come home to Zoutkerke to keep one bell and a clock, and had married Neeltje Boogaard, and had not, on the whole, been sorry. A man who has once made music for a whole town does not despise the striking of a single true note on the hour. He had come to think it was the more honest of the two.

Zoutkerke lay where no sensible people would have built and where people had built anyway, because the land was there and the land was rich. It sat on the Sint-Maartenspolder, a fat low square of ground diked out of the Oosterschelde generations back, on the north shore of Beveland an hour by bicycle from Yerseke where the oysters went to market. The polder lay below the sea. Everyone knew this and no one thought about it; you cannot think about it and also carry on. Standing in his own churchyard, which was the highest ground in the village by the length of a man because the dead had been buried there in their raised ring for six hundred years, Marinus could look north over the roofs to the green wall of the sea dike, and know that the grey water sliding along the far side of it at high tide stood higher than his own boots, higher than the weathercocks, level with the middle of the church windows; and that only the dike, and the sluice, and the mercy of the weather, kept the one from the other. The village turned its back on this the way you turn your back on a debt. It faced inward, to the little harbour on the creek where the oyster boats and the mussel boats lay, to the oyster pits by the quay where the shellfish were laid to fatten, to the one street and the shop and the school and the church, and it lived, and was, on the whole, content.

He had his people. Neel in the kitchen of the koster's house against the churchyard wall, going grey now and sharp as ever and the truest bell he owned. Their daughter Jacomina — Mina — married these seven years to Leendert Verschuure, who worked the oyster beds and the mussel plots out on the flats and came home smelling of the sea's cellar, of brine and mud and cold shell. And the boy, Bram, six years old, who had lately discovered the tower and would climb the first flight of the ladder-stair after his grandfather with his tongue between his teeth, and be lifted the last rungs, and stand in the clock chamber holding the crank-key in both hands as though it were a sword, and ask, every time, the same question: *why does it have to keep the time even at night, Opa, when everyone is asleep and nobody hears it?* And Marinus, every time, told him the truth, which was the only answer he had: *because then, when you wake in the dark and you are afraid, and you hear it strike, you know the world is still going on, and it is not so far to morning.*

He wound the clock that Thursday, the twenty-ninth of January, in the last of a hard cold light, and came down the sixty-one rungs one complaint at a time, and did not know that he had a handful of ordinary hours left, and that the boy's question was the whole book of what was coming, and that he himself would give the last true answer to it.

# Chapter Two — Spring Tide

The wind came up out of the northwest on the Friday and by the Saturday it had a sound in it that Marinus did not like, and he was a man who did not, as a rule, dislike weather.

He had lived his life inside weather. A koster reads the sky the way a sailor does, because everything he does out of doors — the digging, the roof, the churchyard, the walk to the far graves — is done in it, and because the bell itself is a weather-instrument in its way, its voice carrying clean on a still frost and torn to rags on a gale. This gale was building steadily, without gusting, which was the wrong way for a winter storm to build; it did not bluster and drop and bluster again but simply leaned harder and harder out of the same quarter, hour upon hour, as though something very large and very patient had set its shoulder to the whole coast and meant to keep it there. By the Saturday afternoon the poplars along the Kerkweg were streaming like a woman's hair pulled sideways and the smoke off the chimneys did not rise at all but ran flat along the ridge-tiles and away.

And it was spring tide. Marinus knew this the way the whole polder knew it, in the bone, the way you know the phase of the moon without looking: the tides at the new and the full run highest, and this was the full, and the almanac in the shop window had the figure for the Saturday-night high water ringed for the mussel men. High water on a spring tide, and a full northwest gale driving the whole grey weight of the North Sea down into the funnel of the Channel and up against the islands — Marinus was no engineer, but he had stood on the dike in enough weathers to feel the size of what those two things were when they came together, the wind holding the tide up so it could not fall, and the next tide climbing onto the back of the one that had not gone out.

At the sluice by the harbour he found Kees Boot, the sluice-keeper, standing on the lock-gate in an oilskin gone black with spray, watching the creek. Boot was not a man for talk and he did not talk now; he only nodded north, to the dike, where the water on the far side stood far up the green in the failing light, higher than Marinus had seen it stand, and did not seem to be going down though the tide by the almanac was two hours past its turn.

"It's not running off," Marinus said.

"No," said Boot. "The wind won't let it." He spat into the churning creek. "I've sent word up the line to the polder board. If the two-o'clock tide tonight comes on top of this—" He did not finish. He did not need to. He went back to watching the water with the flat attention of a man watching a fire he cannot yet put out.

Marinus walked home along the inside of the dike, the wind shoving him, and stopped once to lay his hand flat against the cold grass of the great bank as a man lays a hand on the flank of a horse he is not sure of, and felt nothing he could name, only the size of the thing on the other side. Then he went in to his supper.

The evening was ordinary, which he would remember. Leendert came in late from the flats, having spent the last of the light hauling the mussel boat higher up the hard and doubling her lines, because a man who works the water respects it without dramatizing it; he ate two platefuls and held Bram upside down by the ankles until the boy shrieked, and said the tide out on the flats was the highest working water he had ever poled through, black and full and wrong, and said it the way you report a curiosity, and then said it was nothing a good dike had not held a hundred times, and believed it, because it was true, because a good dike had. Mina washed the boy and put him down. Neel sat with her mending in the lamplight and the wind boomed in the tower like a slow drum and rattled the leads of the church windows, and Marinus, who could not have said why, went out once more before bed, into the roaring dark, and stood in the churchyard and looked up at the black bulk of the tower against the racing sky, and listened to the bell that was not ringing, and came in, and bolted the door, and did not sleep well.

The radio had gone off the air at midnight with the ordinary sign-off, the anthem and then the hiss, the way it did every night of the world. There was no one, that night, to say the word that would have carried down the wind to the sleeping polders, no bell but the bells the villages might ring for themselves, and the villages were asleep. The last ordinary Saturday of Zoutkerke ended at some hour Marinus never afterward could fix, with the whole family under one roof and the sea standing higher than the windows a quarter of a mile to the north, held up by the wind, climbing.

# Chapter Three — The Night

He woke because Neel's hand was on his chest and she was saying his name, not loudly, and under her voice was a sound that should not have been there, and it took him the length of a breath to understand that the sound was water where no water was — a rushing, flat and wide and close, in the street, in the dark, filling the low places of the village with a noise like a great slow river that had not been there when he lay down.

The clock in the tower struck three.

He knew it for the striking train doing its patient work in the black above them, indifferent, three strokes and done, and even as he swung his legs from the bed and his bare feet met the cold he understood two things at once with a clarity that never afterward left him: that the dike was gone, and that the bell had just told the hour to a village that was drowning, because that was the only thing it had ever been asked to do.

The floor of the koster's house was dry still — the house stood high against the churchyard, on the raised ground — but when he opened the street door the wind took it out of his hand and flung it back on its hinges and the water was there, a foot of it and rising, black and cold and moving, sliding past the doorstep with things carried in it, and beyond the gatepost the whole low bowl of the village where the street ran down to the harbour was gone under a sheet of broken water that heaved and gleamed under the racing cloud. Somewhere a beast was screaming. A door banged and banged. Down the Kerkweg a lamp showed in an upper window and went out.

Marinus stood one instant on his own step with the sea in his village and felt the whole of his life narrow to a single point, the way they say it does in the falling, and the point was not his wife or his daughter or the boy — they were high, they were behind him on the high ground, they had a moment yet — the point was every soul down there in the dark in the low houses by the harbour who did not know, who were asleep, or waking now as he had waked, to a sound they could not name, and who had, if they were to have it at all, one warning left in the world.

He did not decide. There was no deciding in it. He turned back into the house and took the tower key off its nail — his hand found it in the dark without looking, thirty years of it — and he said to Neel, who stood white in the inner doorway with the lamp, *get them up the church, all of them, the loft, go now,* and she did not argue, she was a koster's wife, she knew what the man of the bell was for; and he went out into the water in his nightshirt and his bare feet with the key in his fist and waded across the churchyard to the tower door.

The water was to his thighs at the bottom of the churchyard slope and only to his ankles at the tower door, the six hundred years of raised dead lifting him that much above the drowning street. He got the great key into the lock with fingers he could not feel and turned it and put his shoulder to the door, and was inside, in the roaring dark of the tower, at the foot of the ladder-stair, with the bell-rope hanging down out of the black above him, the long tail of it he had spliced himself, swaying a little in the draught of the tower like something alive and waiting.

He took it in both hands. He set his feet on the dry stone. And Marinus de Ridder, koster of Zoutkerke, fifty-seven years old, with the sea in the streets of his village and the night as black as the inside of the furnace that had taken the old bells, began to ring — not the hour, not the slow toll for the dead, but the wild broken clangour that has only one meaning in any village on any coast in any tongue, the alarm, the bronze voice thrown out over the water again and again with the whole weight of his body on the rope, *up, up, get up, the water, the water,* the one thing a bell is for when the hour no longer matters; and high in the belfry the great recast bell that had been cast to call the living swung out on its wheel into the storm and did, at last, the work its makers had written on it, and threw its voice down the wind over the black and rising water to every soul who had ears yet above the flood.
