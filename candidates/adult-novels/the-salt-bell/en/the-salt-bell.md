# THE SALT BELL

*A novella of the Zeeland flood*

> Zoutkerke, on the low polder behind the sea dike, the winter of 1953. Marinus de Ridder has kept the church, wound the tower clock, and rung the one bell of his village for thirty years — a bell recast after the war to replace the two the occupiers took for their metal. He has spent a life giving order to time, an hour struck for every hour there is. On the last night of January the sea comes over the dike in the dark, before any warning can reach the polder, and the bell that has counted so many ordinary hours is asked at last what a bell is truly for.

---

**Content note:** Adult literary-historical fiction set during and after the North Sea flood of 1953 (the Dutch Watersnoodramp), a real disaster. Contains a drowning and its aftermath (restrained, largely off-page), bereavement and grief, the loss of a community and its rebuilding. No graphic violence; no explicit content. The village and every character are invented; see the author's note. For adult readers.

# Chapter One — The Hours

The bell was cast to call the living and mourn the dead, and for thirty years Marinus de Ridder had mostly asked it to do neither, but only to keep the time.

He wound the tower clock every third day, in the grey of the afternoon, climbing the ladder-stair inside the brick with the crank-key warm in his pocket where he had carried it from the kitchen to take the chill off the iron. There were sixty-one rungs from the church floor to the clock chamber; he had counted them the first week his father handed him the keys in 1922 and had never needed to count them since, his knees keeping the tally for him now, one complaint per rung. In the chamber the works stood in their oak cage — the great toothed wheels, the going train and the striking train, the stone weights on their frayed ropes going down the dark of the tower like plumb-lines dropped into a well — and he wound the two weights up hand over hand until the pawls clacked home, and oiled what wanted oil, and stood a moment in the smell of iron and pigeon and old grease that was the smell of his whole working life, and listened to the escapement tick the seconds off into the cold. Above him, in the belfry, hung the bell.

There was one bell now. There had been two when he was a boy — the great one and a smaller, the *voorslag*, that struck the halves — and both of them had gone down the tower on a Tuesday in the spring of 1943, lowered on the occupiers' tackle by men who would not meet the eyes of the village, and been carted to the station with the bells of Kruiningen and Wemeldinge and half the province, to be melted into the war. He had stood in the churchyard that day with his hat in his hands as though at a burial, which it was. For two years the tower said nothing. You did not know, he had learned then, how much of a village lived in its bell until the bell was gone and the hours came and went unmarked and the people grew, without saying so, a little more lost. When the war ended they got word that some of the stolen bells had been found in a yard in the north, laid out in their thousands like a cemetery of bronze, but theirs were not among the saved; theirs had gone into the furnace early. So the village did what villages do. They gathered — guilders and cheese-money and a collection at every wedding for three years — and in 1949 the founder at Heiligerlee cast them a new one, a single great bell, and around its shoulder in a raised band he set the old words the tradition gives a bell, which Marinus, who had learned his Latin from a carillon and not a school, could say in either tongue: *Levenden roep ik, de doden beklaag ik.* The living I call, the dead I mourn.

He had rung it up the tower himself the day it came, and wept, and told no one, and had asked it since only to strike the hours.

That was the strange arithmetic of his trade, and he had made his peace with it long ago. A koster gives his life to a building and a bell that exist for the great occasions — the births and the deaths, the danger and the deliverance — and spends that life almost entirely on the small ones: the sweeping, the winding, the opening and the shutting, the hour struck for every hour there is whether anyone is listening or not. Marinus had wanted, once, to be a different kind of man of bells. As a young one he had gone to the city and learned the carillon — the great keyboard of batons and pedals in the tower at Goes, four octaves of bronze you played with the sides of your fists, so that a man alone in a stone room could put a hymn or a folk-tune or the whole of his heart out over the roofs of a town that never saw him. He had been good at it. But his father had sickened, and the village had no one, and a stiffness had come into his left hand the winter he turned twenty-six that took the run out of his playing; and he had come home to Zoutkerke to keep one bell and a clock, and had married Neeltje Boogaard, and had not, on the whole, been sorry. A man who has once made music for a whole town does not despise the striking of a single true note on the hour. He had come to think it was the more honest of the two.

Zoutkerke lay where no sensible people would have built and where people had built anyway, because the land was there and the land was rich. It sat on the Sint-Maartenspolder, a fat low square of ground diked out of the Oosterschelde generations back, on the north shore of Beveland an hour by bicycle from Yerseke where the oysters went to market. The polder lay below the sea. Everyone knew this and no one thought about it; you cannot think about it and also carry on. Standing in his own churchyard, which was the highest ground in the village by the length of a man because the dead had been buried there in their raised ring for six hundred years, Marinus could look north over the roofs to the green wall of the sea dike, and know that the grey water sliding along the far side of it at high tide stood higher than his own boots, higher than the weathercocks, level with the middle of the church windows; and that only the dike, and the sluice, and the mercy of the weather, kept the one from the other. The village turned its back on this the way you turn your back on a debt. It faced inward, to the little harbour on the creek where the oyster boats and the mussel boats lay, to the oyster pits by the quay where the shellfish were laid to fatten, to the one street and the shop and the school and the church, and it lived, and was, on the whole, content.

He had his people. Neel in the kitchen of the koster's house against the churchyard wall, going grey now and sharp as ever and the truest bell he owned. Their daughter Jacomina — Mina — married these seven years to Leendert Verschuure, who worked the oyster beds and the mussel plots out on the flats and came home smelling of the sea's cellar, of brine and mud and cold shell. And the boy, Bram, six years old, who had lately discovered the tower and would climb the first flight of the ladder-stair after his grandfather with his tongue between his teeth, and be lifted the last rungs, and stand in the clock chamber holding the crank-key in both hands as though it were a sword, and ask, every time, the same question: *why does it have to keep the time even at night, Opa, when everyone is asleep and nobody hears it?* And Marinus, every time, told him the truth, which was the only answer he had: *because then, when you wake in the dark and you are afraid, and you hear it strike, you know the world is still going on, and it is not so far to morning.*

The bell's proper offices, the ones it was cast for, came round rarely and unpredictably, the way the great things of a life do, and between them lay the ordinary striking that was the true substance of the work. He had rung that January a burial and a wedding within the one fortnight, which was the whole span of the trade laid out for anyone who cared to read it. The burial was old Willem Kloet from the far end of the polder, ninety and ready, and Marinus had tolled him into the churchyard on a still frost morning with the slow single stroke and the long count between, and the sound had carried clean over the frozen fields for kilometres, the way a bell only carries on a hard still cold, so that men working on the dikes three villages off had straightened their backs and known that Zoutkerke was burying someone and taken their caps off where they stood, though they could not have said who. And the wedding was young Adriaan Boot, the sluice-keeper's nephew, married to a Wemeldinge girl on a bright blowing Saturday, and for that Marinus had rung the full glad peal, the bell swung right over on its wheel so it spoke fast and high and tumbling, a sound with a laugh built into it; and the difference between the two ringings was made with the same bronze and the same rope and the same pair of hands, and lay entirely in how a man leaned his weight and where he let the wheel carry. That was the koster's art, such as it was: one bell, and the whole register of a human life to be got out of it, from the laugh to the lament, by the body alone.

Leendert he saw most at the oyster pits by the quay, where the trade of the village lay soaking in the brackish creek water in its stone-walled beds — the flat Zeeland oysters laid there by the thousand to fatten and clear before they went up to Yerseke and on to the tables of cities Marinus would never eat in. He liked to go down of an afternoon and stand with his son-in-law at the pits while the younger man worked, the two of them not talking much, Leendert lifting the flat shells one at a time out of the cold water with hands gone raw and blue-nailed with the winter of it, culling and grading by a knowledge that lived in his fingers and not his head. It was a patient underwater husbandry, the oyster trade, more like Marinus's own than either man would have said — a matter of tending a slow thing faithfully through years for the sake of a harvest that came, when it came, all at once and briefly; a man laid his shell for the spat to strike on and waited half a decade to know whether he had judged the ground right. Leendert had the temperament for it, the deep unhurried patience of a man who plants what he will not soon reap, and Marinus, watching him grade oysters in the failing light with the sea a green wall at his back, had thought more than once that his daughter had married, without either of them naming it so, another sort of keeper.

He wound the clock that Thursday, the twenty-ninth of January, in the last of a hard cold light, and came down the sixty-one rungs one complaint at a time, and did not know that he had a handful of ordinary hours left, and that the boy's question was the whole book of what was coming, and that he himself would give the last true answer to it.

# Chapter Two — Spring Tide

The wind came up out of the northwest on the Friday and by the Saturday it had a sound in it that Marinus did not like, and he was a man who did not, as a rule, dislike weather.

He had lived his life inside weather. A koster reads the sky the way a sailor does, because everything he does out of doors — the digging, the roof, the churchyard, the walk to the far graves — is done in it, and because the bell itself is a weather-instrument in its way, its voice carrying clean on a still frost and torn to rags on a gale. This gale was building steadily, without gusting, which was the wrong way for a winter storm to build; it did not bluster and drop and bluster again but simply leaned harder and harder out of the same quarter, hour upon hour, as though something very large and very patient had set its shoulder to the whole coast and meant to keep it there. By the Saturday afternoon the poplars along the Kerkweg were streaming like a woman's hair pulled sideways and the smoke off the chimneys did not rise at all but ran flat along the ridge-tiles and away.

And it was spring tide. Marinus knew this the way the whole polder knew it, in the bone, the way you know the phase of the moon without looking: the tides at the new and the full run highest, and this was the full, and the almanac in the shop window had the figure for the Saturday-night high water ringed for the mussel men. High water on a spring tide, and a full northwest gale driving the whole grey weight of the North Sea down into the funnel of the Channel and up against the islands — Marinus was no engineer, but he had stood on the dike in enough weathers to feel the size of what those two things were when they came together, the wind holding the tide up so it could not fall, and the next tide climbing onto the back of the one that had not gone out.

At the sluice by the harbour he found Kees Boot, the sluice-keeper, standing on the lock-gate in an oilskin gone black with spray, watching the creek. Boot was not a man for talk and he did not talk now; he only nodded north, to the dike, where the water on the far side stood far up the green in the failing light, higher than Marinus had seen it stand, and did not seem to be going down though the tide by the almanac was two hours past its turn.

"It's not running off," Marinus said.

"No," said Boot. "The wind won't let it." He spat into the churning creek. "I've sent word up the line to the polder board. If the two-o'clock tide tonight comes on top of this—" He did not finish. He did not need to. He went back to watching the water with the flat attention of a man watching a fire he cannot yet put out.

The village was battening down in the ordinary way, the way it did before any hard blow, and Marinus went the length of the street once in the dusk to see it done, because a koster is a kind of shepherd whether he owns the office or not. The mussel men were doubling the lines on the boats in the little harbour and lifting the light gear up out of reach of a high working tide; the shopkeeper was taking in the almanac and the enamel signs that would fly in a gale; a woman was calling a straying child in out of the wind and getting no answer and going, cross and unafraid, to fetch it. Nobody was frightened. That was the thing Marinus kept and turned over long after: that there was no fear in the village that evening, none at all, because there had been a hundred northwest gales and the dike had held through every one, and a people does not fear the thing that has never yet happened, cannot fear it, is not built to. He met the dominee, young Vinke, coming out of the church with his coat collar up, having been to see the stove banked for the Sunday, and the two of them stood a moment in the lee of the tower and looked north to where the water stood high and grey on the dike in the last light. "Rough night for it," Vinke said, meaning the Sunday, meaning the walk his people would have to church in the morning; and Marinus said yes, rough night, and neither of them meant anything by it, and they parted, and Marinus went in and up the tower on an impulse he could not have named and stood in the belfry with his hand on the cold bell and looked out through the louvres at the racing dark and the high water, and came down, and told himself he was a foolish old man, and half believed it.

Marinus walked home along the inside of the dike, the wind shoving him, and stopped once to lay his hand flat against the cold grass of the great bank as a man lays a hand on the flank of a horse he is not sure of, and felt nothing he could name, only the size of the thing on the other side. Then he went in to his supper.

The evening was ordinary, which he would remember. Leendert came in late from the flats, having spent the last of the light hauling the mussel boat higher up the hard and doubling her lines, because a man who works the water respects it without dramatizing it; he ate two platefuls and held Bram upside down by the ankles until the boy shrieked, and said the tide out on the flats was the highest working water he had ever poled through, black and full and wrong, and said it the way you report a curiosity, and then said it was nothing a good dike had not held a hundred times, and believed it, because it was true, because a good dike had. Mina washed the boy and put him down. Neel sat with her mending in the lamplight and the wind boomed in the tower like a slow drum and rattled the leads of the church windows, and Marinus, who could not have said why, went out once more before bed, into the roaring dark, and stood in the churchyard and looked up at the black bulk of the tower against the racing sky, and listened to the bell that was not ringing, and came in, and bolted the door, and did not sleep well.

The radio had gone off the air at midnight with the ordinary sign-off, the anthem and then the hiss, the way it did every night of the world. There was no one, that night, to say the word that would have carried down the wind to the sleeping polders, no bell but the bells the villages might ring for themselves, and the villages were asleep. The last ordinary Saturday of Zoutkerke ended at some hour Marinus never afterward could fix, with the whole family under one roof and the sea standing higher than the windows a quarter of a mile to the north, held up by the wind, climbing.

# Chapter Three — The Night

He woke because Neel's hand was on his chest and she was saying his name, not loudly, and under her voice was a sound that should not have been there, and it took him the length of a breath to understand that the sound was water where no water was — a rushing, flat and wide and close, in the street, in the dark, filling the low places of the village with a noise like a great slow river that had not been there when he lay down.

The clock in the tower struck three.

He knew it for the striking train doing its patient work in the black above them, indifferent, three strokes and done, and even as he swung his legs from the bed and his bare feet met the cold he understood two things at once with a clarity that never afterward left him: that the dike was gone, and that the bell had just told the hour to a village that was drowning, because that was the only thing it had ever been asked to do.

The floor of the koster's house was dry still — the house stood high against the churchyard, on the raised ground — but when he opened the street door the wind took it out of his hand and flung it back on its hinges and the water was there, a foot of it and rising, black and cold and moving, sliding past the doorstep with things carried in it, and beyond the gatepost the whole low bowl of the village where the street ran down to the harbour was gone under a sheet of broken water that heaved and gleamed under the racing cloud. Somewhere a beast was screaming. A door banged and banged. Down the Kerkweg a lamp showed in an upper window and went out.

Marinus stood one instant on his own step with the sea in his village and felt the whole of his life narrow to a single point, the way they say it does in the falling, and the point was not his wife or his daughter or the boy — they were high, they were behind him on the high ground, they had a moment yet — the point was every soul down there in the dark in the low houses by the harbour who did not know, who were asleep, or waking now as he had waked, to a sound they could not name, and who had, if they were to have it at all, one warning left in the world.

He did not decide. There was no deciding in it. He turned back into the house and took the tower key off its nail — his hand found it in the dark without looking, thirty years of it — and he said to Neel, who stood white in the inner doorway with the lamp, *get them up the church, all of them, the loft, go now,* and she did not argue, she was a koster's wife, she knew what the man of the bell was for; and he went out into the water in his nightshirt and his bare feet with the key in his fist and waded across the churchyard to the tower door.

The water was to his thighs at the bottom of the churchyard slope and only to his ankles at the tower door, the six hundred years of raised dead lifting him that much above the drowning street. He got the great key into the lock with fingers he could not feel and turned it and put his shoulder to the door, and was inside, in the roaring dark of the tower, at the foot of the ladder-stair, with the bell-rope hanging down out of the black above him, the long tail of it he had spliced himself, swaying a little in the draught of the tower like something alive and waiting.

He took it in both hands. He set his feet on the dry stone. And Marinus de Ridder, koster of Zoutkerke, fifty-seven years old, with the sea in the streets of his village and the night as black as the inside of the furnace that had taken the old bells, began to ring — not the hour, not the slow toll for the dead, but the wild broken clangour that has only one meaning in any village on any coast in any tongue, the alarm, the bronze voice thrown out over the water again and again with the whole weight of his body on the rope, *up, up, get up, the water, the water,* the one thing a bell is for when the hour no longer matters; and high in the belfry the great recast bell that had been cast to call the living swung out on its wheel into the storm and did, at last, the work its makers had written on it, and threw its voice down the wind over the black and rising water to every soul who had ears yet above the flood.

# Chapter Four — Ringing

There is no music in an alarm and Marinus, who had been a musician, understood in the first minutes that this was the whole of its power. A tune asks to be listened to. An alarm cannot be listened to; it can only be obeyed or refused; it goes into the body under the mind, the way the smell of smoke does, and puts the sleeper on his feet before he knows he is standing. So he did not ring in any measure. He hauled the rope down with the whole falling weight of himself and let the bell fling it back up and hauled it down again, fast and hard and ragged, no two strokes the same, the great bronze voice tumbling over itself out of the tower in a broken roar that no one in the polder, waking to it, could mistake for the hour or the Sunday or anything but what it was.

He could not see what it did. That was the hardest part, in the ringing, and for years after: that he stood in the black stone throat of the tower with the rope tearing his hands and could not see the single thing he had climbed into the water to do, whether anyone rose to it, whether anyone came. He had only the rope and the dark and the sound and, under everything, the flat wide rushing of the sea moving through his village. Once the tower door, which he had not barred, slammed inward on the flood and a wash of cold water came across the stone to his ankles and he rang on. Once he heard, or thought he heard, under the storm, a human voice call out somewhere near — a man's voice, wordless, the sound a voice makes when it is being used as a bell itself, to say only *here, here I am* — and he could not go to it, could not leave the rope, because the rope was worth more than his two hands could ever be; one man wading could save one man; the bell could wake the polder. So he rang, and wept, which he did not notice, and the salt on his face was not the sea.

They came to the high ground because the bell told them where it was. That was the mercy of a church in a Zeeland village, the thing the six hundred years had built without knowing they built it: the dead lay highest, and the tower stood over the dead, and the tower had a voice, so that in the drowning dark the one dry place and the one sound in the world were the same place, and a person floundering in the black with the water at his chest had only to go toward the bell. They came wading while they could and swimming when they could not, in nightclothes, carrying children, carrying nothing, an old man on a door his sons pushed before them, a woman with a baby held in her teeth by its wrapping so her arms were free — Marinus learned all this afterward, in the loft, in the pieces people gave; in the ringing he knew only that hands began to come out of the dark to the tower door and up past him into the stair, cold hands, gasping people, and that Neel was suddenly there at the foot of the ladder with the lamp turned low, taking them, sending them up, counting them with her eyes, a woman doing at the bottom of the tower exactly what her husband did at the rope — gathering the living.

One of them he did see, and kept. Old Janne, the widow from the cottage by the churchyard gate, seventy if she was a day, came to the tower door while he was ringing and could not get up the step for the water and the weakness, and clung there in the black with the flood to her waist and her thin arms about the doorpost, too spent to climb and too stubborn to let go; and Marinus, who could not leave the rope, roared at her over the storm and the bell the only thing that would serve, which was not comfort but command — *hold on, Janne, hold on, do not you dare let go* — the way you speak to a beast or a child or anyone whose whole task in the world has narrowed to the not-letting-go; and she held, out of spite as much as anything, spite being a great underrated engine of survival, until Neel got a girl to her and the two of them hauled the old woman streaming up over the step and into the stair, where she sat on the third rung and swore at everybody with a vigour that told the whole tower she would live. Marinus rang on, and did not stop, and thought, in the part of the mind that goes on thinking through anything, that he had just saved a life with his voice and not his hands, which was the bell's own trick, the thing it did, and that Janne would be at the front of the church complaining about the draught within the year, and was almost right; it was within eighteen months.

Not all of them came. He made himself hold this too, in the years after, without flinching from it, because to flinch from it would have been a kind of lie about them. The harbour end of the village, the low houses along the creek where the mussel men lived, had taken the water first and taken it worst, in the first minutes, in their sleep, before the bell had well begun; and the very old and the very young in the outlying houses could not, some of them, cross what lay between their doors and the tower, however the bell called; and the current in the streets, where the sea poured through the broken dike and ran seeking its level, was a live and dragging thing that took the strong as readily as the weak and cared nothing for how near a man had come to the light. The bell was not a boat. It could tell you where to go. It could not carry you there. Marinus rang for every soul in the polder equally, the reached and the unreachable alike, and this was the truth of the bell that his thirty ordinary years had never taught him and that one night taught him for good: that it gave what it had, which was a voice, all of it, to everyone, and that a voice is a great thing and not everything, and that the distance between those two is where the dead are.

He rang until his hands would not close, and then he wrapped them in the tail of the rope and rang with his wrists and his forearms and the crook of his elbows, and the split skin printed the rope dark, and he rang past the time when new hands stopped coming to the door, on into the grey, because he could not know that no one was left to come, and would not stop while there was a chance that one more soul was out there in the water turning its face toward the sound. Somewhere in the greyness Neel came up behind him in the dark and put her two hands over his two hands on the rope and stopped them, gently, and held them still, and he understood from her holding, before she said a word, that it was light now, and that the ones who were coming had come, and that the rest of what the night had done was not a thing a bell could change.

# Chapter Five — The Loft

The morning came up grey and enormous over a village that was gone, and from the louvres of the belfry, where he climbed on legs that did not belong to him to look, Marinus saw the size of it, and had, for the length of that looking, no thought in his head at all.

There was no village. There was a sea. It stood over everything the eye could reach, a flat pewter sheet from the tower to the world's edge, wind-torn, moving, with the tops of things standing up out of it here and there like the last of a drowned country refusing to go under all at once — ridge-lines of roofs, the black crowns of the churchyard elms, a haystack, the crossbar of a telegraph pole with a row of gulls on it, and to the north, where the green wall of the sea dike had been, nothing, only a wider running of the water where the whole province of the sea came pouring in through a gap he could not see the ends of. The harbour was not there. The oyster pits, the quay, the low street of the mussel men, the school — under it, all of it, the depth of a man, the depth of two. Only the high ground held: the churchyard ring, the tower, and up the Kerkweg a scatter of the higher houses standing to their windows in the flood with faces at the attic gables. His whole world had been reduced, in one tide, to the few square yards of stone he stood on and the few dozen people it held.

There were sixty-three of them in the church and the tower and the loft over the porch, and he knew this because Neel had counted them and named them where she could, in the grey light, going among the soaked and shivering people with the lamp; and he took the number from her the way he took everything from her, as the truest reckoning to be had, and he did not let himself yet set it against the other number, the number of the village, the number that had gone to bed in the polder the night before. That arithmetic could wait. What could not wait was the cold, and the children, and the long day, and the thing that no one in the loft was saying aloud and everyone was carrying: that the tide which had done this would come again. The sea breathes twice a day. It had come in the black of the small hours; it would come again in the evening; and no one on that high ground knew whether the second coming, onto a broken dike and a full polder, would rise higher than the first, and reach the church floor, and then the loft, and then the bell.

So the day had to be got through, and Marinus learned in the getting-through of it a thing he had not known about his own people, which was how they behaved when there was nothing left to do but wait to see whether they would live. They were quiet. That was the first surprise, and the deepest: no wailing, no railing, none of the loud grief a man might brace for; only a low steady murmur, and the small ceaseless business of keeping sixty-three cold wet human bodies from the worst of the cold and the wet, which turned out to be nearly the whole of what could be done and to be, in the doing, a kind of prayer. Neel ran it without appearing to run anything. She had the women wring out what could be wrung and hang it in the tower draught; she had the driest of the coats put on the smallest of the children and the oldest of the people, and the wettest borne by the strong; she set two of the older girls to keeping the little ones told stories in the corner of the loft so their fear had somewhere to go; and she went among them all the grey forenoon with the lamp turned low to save the oil, saying almost nothing, touching a shoulder here and a wet head there, and the touching did more than any words could have, because a hand laid on you in the dark says the one thing worth saying, which is *I am here and you are not alone in this,* the same thing, Marinus thought, watching her, that the bell says, only quieter, only closer.

The dominee did the far graves of it, the deep places. Young Vinke, who had stood battening his stove twelve hours before with nothing worse to fear than a wet walk to a Sunday, went among the people that day and did the work he had been ordained for and had surely never thought to do so soon or so hard: he sat with the broken-legged man from the harbour and held his hand through the worst of the pain and did not tell him it would be well, only stayed; he prayed with those who wanted praying with, plainly, in the Zeeland way, more speaking than chanting, and sat in silence with those who wanted only silence, and knew the difference, which is the whole of the art; and once, in the middle of that endless forenoon, when a woman near the porch began at last to shake and could not stop, her house and her people all gone under the water below her and the shaking coming up out of her like a tide of its own, he simply gathered her in and let her shake against him and said nothing at all, an unmarried man of twenty-eight holding a drowning grief in his arms in a cold loft, and Marinus, seeing it, forgave the young man every clumsy sermon he had ever preached, and understood that the parish had got, without knowing it, the right man for the once-in-a-lifetime thing.

Leendert was not among the sixty-three.

Marinus came to this slowly, going among the faces with Neel's list in his head, and then not slowly at all. Mina was there, at the top of the ladder-stair with Bram wrapped in the altar-cloth asleep against her, dry, safe, the two of them got up into the tower in the first minutes by Neel's own hands — but Leendert was not with them, and had not been in the house when the water came, because Leendert had gone out at the turn of the night, in the worst of it, down to the sluice: Kees Boot had sent the boy who helped him to knock up the men along the dike when the water would not fall, and Leendert, who worked the water and respected it, had pulled on his boots and gone to stand with the others on the bank in the dark and do what men do on a dike in a rising tide, which is fill and carry and shore and hold, until the dike does not hold. He had gone to the one place in the whole polder that the sea, when it broke, broke first. Mina had watched him go from the door and had not called him back because you do not call a man back from that, and had then taken her boy up the tower to her father's bell, and had been counting, since the grey came, the same terrible sum her father was counting, and had not let herself reach the total, and looked at Marinus now across the loft with a question in her face that he could not answer and would not lie to, and so he only crossed to her and put his ruined hands on her head and on the boy's, and said nothing, which she understood, and which was the worst thing he had ever had to say to his child.

The clock had stopped. He noticed it in the loft in the long grey forenoon, in a lull in the wind, the way you notice a familiar breathing has gone out of a room — the tower over him was silent in its works, the tick gone, the going train drowned somewhere below in the flooded base of the tower or wound down at last after thirty years because there was no one to wind it and the world it kept time for was under water. Time itself had stopped in Zoutkerke. There was no hour now. There was only the light going one way across the sky, and the tide, which kept its own time and needed no clock, coming back.

# Chapter Six — The Second Tide

All that Sunday the water stood, and the people in the church waited for the evening the way you wait for a thing you have been told is coming and cannot see, and Marinus went twice an hour up into the belfry to read the sea, because reading the weather was the one useful thing left that he knew how to do.

He read it off the drowned world itself, having no gauge but that. In the forenoon the flood stood so-and-so high against the churchyard wall, against a particular stone he chose and kept his eye to, and through the middle of the day it did not much move, the tide out in the wide water turning somewhere beyond sight; and then in the afternoon, with the light already failing under the wrack of cloud, he saw the chosen stone go under, and the water begin to climb the wall again, slow and certain, the second tide feeling its way back into the ruined polder over the broken dike. He did not tell the loft in words. He did not have to. They read it in his face when he came down, and the murmur in the church changed its pitch, and the dominee — Vinke, a young man for the work, not two years in the parish — began, quietly, without being asked, to pray aloud in the Zeeland way, plainly, more speaking than chanting, and the people took it up under their breath, not because all of them believed but because a human voice going steadily on in the dark is its own kind of dike.

The evening tide did not reach the church floor. It came to the third step of the porch and hung there through the top of the flood, in the roaring dark, while sixty-three people stood in the nave in their wet clothes and listened to the sea trying the door, and it did not come in; the churchyard ring, the six hundred years of raised dead, held the living up out of the water by the length of a man one more time. But it was in that highest hour of the second tide, when the wind was full again and the dark absolute, that Marinus rang the bell for the last purpose it had left to serve that night, and the strangest, and the one he had never in thirty years imagined asking of it.

There was a light on the water. Mina saw it first from the belfry louvre where she had gone to be alone with her watching — a low yellow point away to the southeast, toward the high land, rising and dropping, that was not a star and not a house: a lantern, on a boat, out on the flood in the dark, come from somewhere the water had not taken, feeling its way across a drowned country with no channel and no mark and every danger under the black surface, a chimney, a wire, a wall, that could hole a hull or foul a screw and drown the men who had come to help. It was coming, and it could not see, and in all that flooded dark there was one thing standing that could tell a boat where the deep safe water of the drowned street lay and where the church stood on its ground — one voice that carried over wind and water and meant, to anyone who had grown up on that coast, *here, come to here.*

So Marinus rang the bell as a beacon. Not the alarm now, not the wild broken clangour, but slow and steady and spaced, one deep stroke and then the count and then another, a sound to steer by, a sound with a bearing in it, the tower speaking across the black to the small brave light to say *this way, and this way, and mind the current where the dike was, and come.* He rang it with his wrapped and broken hands through the whole top of that tide, marking the safe water with his bell for men he could not see, and the light came on, slow, checking, feeling, an hour to cross what a boy could bicycle in ten minutes on a summer road — and came at last under the very tower, out of the dark into the loom of it, an oyster boat with three fishermen in it from the high land beyond the flood who had heard, two nights and a day, a village bell that would not stop, and had come to it across the drowned polder the only way anyone comes anywhere on that coast: by the sound of the bell over the water, toward the light in the tower, home.

# Chapter Seven — The Reach

The rescue of Zoutkerke took three days, because there were a hundred Zoutkerkes that week and the water was everywhere and the help, when it came, had to be invented as it went.

The oyster boat that had steered in by the bell on the Sunday night took nine of them off before the tide turned under it and it had to run for the high land while it still had water to run on — the two smallest children and their mothers, the oldest people, a man whose leg had been broken by a beam in the first minutes and who had lain two days in the loft grey and uncomplaining while Neel kept the wet cold off him with the altar-cloths. Marinus watched them go down into the boat in the lantern-light, handed down the porch steps into the pitching dark by the fishermen with a gentleness that had no words in it, and he rang the bell to see them out across the flood as he had rung it to bring the boat in, marking the safe water for them until the light of the lantern was small and then gone, and then he climbed back up and told the fifty-four who were left that boats came to a bell, that they had proved it, that they had only to hold on and keep the bell going and the boats would keep coming. And they did. On the Monday the sky was full of the drone of aeroplanes going over toward the worse places, and by the Monday afternoon the first of the real rescue reached them: fishing smacks and eel boats and a flat green army thing that swam and then crawled up the churchyard slope on wheels, come across the drowned island from the high land where a whole ragged armada had gathered — every man with a boat on that coast, and the soldiers, and men who had driven through the night from the dry provinces towing dinghies behind their cars, all of it flung at the water without a plan and finding its plan in the doing.

They took the village off in loads over the Monday and the Tuesday, and Marinus stood at the tower door and helped hand every soul of them down into the boats, and would not go himself. He would not say why and could not have said why; it was past reasons. Neel went on the Tuesday with Mina and the boy, because the boy had a cough now that frightened them and had to be got to a dry bed and a stove, and Marinus put them into the boat with his ruined hands and told Neel he would come on the last boat, and Neel looked at him for a long moment in the grey and did not argue, because she knew what the tower was to him and knew there are things you cannot make a man leave until he has finished leaving them himself. So he stayed to the end, the last man but the soldiers on the highest ground in Zoutkerke, and when the last boat was loading in the dusk of the Tuesday a young soldier climbed to the belfry with him, kindly, to bring him down, and found the koster standing with his hand flat on the cold bronze of the bell as a man stands with his hand on the shoulder of someone he is saying goodbye to, and had the sense, being a country boy himself, to wait, and say nothing, and let him finish; and then Marinus came down the sixty-one rungs one complaint at a time, and got into the boat, and did not look back at the tower, and was taken off his drowned village at last across the flat grey water in the last of the light.

They found Leendert on the Wednesday. Marinus was on the high land by then and it was Kees Boot who came and told him — Boot, who had been washed off the dike in the first minutes with the men who had stood on it and who had come to the top of a haystack in the dark and lived, one of four out of the eleven who had gone down to hold the bank; and Boot did not soften it because there was no soft way and Marinus would not have wanted one, and told him only that the sea had given Leendert back onto the flats it had taken him from, out where the oyster beds were, and that he had been found whole and quiet, as though sleeping, and had been brought in and would be buried when there was ground dry enough to bury him in. Marinus took the news standing, the way he had taken the news of the bells in 1943, with his hat in his hands. Then he went and found Mina in the schoolroom where the refugees were laid, and sat with her, and told her; and did not tell her, because it was not a thing that could be told and only a thing that could be slowly learned, that the man she had married had drowned on the dike doing the one thing the whole village had needed doing, holding the bank in the dark so that others might have the minutes the bell then gave them — that Leendert had been, in his way, out on the black water, another kind of bell, spending everything he had to buy the polder time, and had not been rung for, because there had been no one left to ring.

The village counted its dead that week the way villages do, by name, at the size of days: not the great number that the wireless and the papers were beginning to say, the number for the whole coast, the number too round and too enormous to be felt or believed — but its own small terrible sum, the mussel man and his wife from the low end of the street, the two old sisters, the baby of the family by the sluice, the men off the dike, Leendert. Zoutkerke never once, in all the years after, said aloud how the water had emptied it; it said instead the names, on the days the names fell, and let the arithmetic make itself. Marinus, who had spent his life striking a number on the hour, understood the refusal exactly. Some counts are not for saying. You ring them one at a time, slowly, with a space between, so that each one is heard for itself before the next; and that, it turned out, was also a thing a bell was for, and he would come to it.

# Chapter Eight — Dry Land

They were billeted forty kilometres inland, on the sandy high ground of Brabant that had never in its life feared the sea, in a village that opened its houses and its one hall to the drowned people of the islands with a generosity that shamed and steadied them; and it was there, on dry land, in a warm room, that Marinus de Ridder came nearest in his whole life to going under.

It was the quiet that did it. He had a bed, and a stove, and clean dry clothes given by strangers, and enough to eat, and his wife beside him and his daughter and his grandson under the same safe roof; and he lay awake in it night after night and could not bear it, and did not understand at first what it was he could not bear, until he understood that it was the silence. There was no bell. He had lived his entire life inside the sound of a bell — the hours through the day, the halves, the slow toll and the glad peal, the striking that went on through the night so that a man waking in the dark would know the world was still going on — and now, for the first time since he was a child, no bell marked his hours, and the nights were long and shapeless and without floor, and he lay in the dark of the safe dry room with the terrible smooth silence pressing on him and did not know when it was, or how far it was to morning, and was, he realised, afraid, in exactly the way he had spent thirty years telling his grandson the bell existed to cure. A klokkenist with no tower is only a man who cannot sleep.

They were four in two borrowed rooms, and the strangeness of it was a labour in itself. The Brabant family that had taken them in — a farmer, his wife, three children of their own — could not have been kinder and could not make it anything but strange, because there is no easy way for the drowned to be guests of the dry, and both households knew it and both went carefully. Neel made herself useful in the farm kitchen within a day, because to be useful was the only bearable way to be given to, and the two women found their feet together over the bread and the washing in the wordless freemasonry of work. Bram, with the terrible resilience of six, was running half-wild with the farm children inside a week, and his laughter in the yard was a thing Marinus could hardly bear to hear and could not have borne to stop, the sound of the future insisting on itself in a house of the bereaved. It was Mina who could not find her feet, and Marinus watched his daughter that spring with a helplessness worse than any he had felt in the water. She did not weep much, after the first. She went very quiet and very careful, folding and refolding the little the flood had left her, tending the boy with a fierce closed attention, sitting long in the evenings with her hands still in her lap and her eyes on nothing; and grief in the young, Marinus knew, is not the storm it is in the old but a long low water that stands and will not run off, held up by something, the way the tide had stood on the dike held up by the wind. He could not lift it off her. No one could. He could only sit with her in the evenings, as Vinke had sat with the shaking woman, and be there, and let her know by his being there that she was not alone on the high strange ground, and wait for the wind that held her grief up to slacken, in its own time, over years, as he had to trust it would.

The scale of it reached them slowly, in the dry village, and was in its way as hard to hold as the silence. The wireless played in the hall and the newspapers came, and the numbers in them grew through the first week — the flooded hectares, the drowned beasts in their tens of thousands, the villages named that Marinus had known all his life, some of them gone worse than Zoutkerke, some spared, the whole grey catastrophe of the province and the coast laid out in figures — and the figure for the dead climbed and settled at last on a number that no one in the hall could do anything with at all, a number so large it stopped meaning people and became only weight, a thing that sat on the chest. Marinus watched the others take it and saw that they could not, that nobody could; that the mind is built to grieve at the size of a village and not at the size of a province; and that this was not a failing but a mercy, the same mercy that had made Zoutkerke count only its own. The Queen went to the islands, the papers said, and stood in the water in her boots; help was coming from over the whole world, tinned food and blankets and money, from countries that owed them nothing; the dry provinces were emptying their cupboards. It was all true and all good and none of it could be felt at the right size, and Marinus stopped trying to feel it at the right size, and let it be simply a great goodness happening at a distance, the way the great sorrow was happening at a distance, and turned his attention to the one thing he could feel, which was the grief in the room with him, his daughter's, his own, at the size of a single drowned man.

Bram asked the question again in the dry village, one night, waking from the cough into the strange room, afraid. *Opa,* he said, into the dark, not knowing his grandfather lay awake three feet away with the same fear, *there's no bell here. How do you know it isn't so far to morning?* And Marinus, who had told the boy the true answer a hundred times in the tower, lay in the borrowed bed with the smooth silence on his chest and found he could not, for a moment, answer at all — because he did not know, that was the truth, he did not know how far it was to morning without a bell to tell him, no one did, that was the whole of what the flood had taught him — and then he heard himself say, out of somewhere older than his despair, the thing he would spend the next year making true: *We'll go home,* he said, *and I'll wind the clock, and hang the bell right, and then you'll hear it, and then we'll both know.* And the boy, satisfied, went back to sleep, and Marinus lay in the dark having promised his grandson the future, and understood that he had thereby promised it to himself, and would now have to go and build it.

# Chapter Nine — The Salvage

They let the polder go dry again by the summer, the sea stopped out at last with a plug of clay and sunken barges across the broken dike, the water lifted off the drowned fields by pumps working day and night; and in the drained and salt-killed ruin of Zoutkerke the church stood up out of the wreck of the village with its tower whole and its one bell hanging silent in the belfry, exactly as Marinus had left it, waiting for him.

He went back before he was strictly allowed to, on a borrowed bicycle over the mended dike, and stood in the wreck of his churchyard in a silence broken only by the wind and the far knock of the pumps, and made himself look at what the sea leaves. It does not leave a clean thing. It leaves salt — white blooms of it dried on every wall to the tide-line, in the grass, on the dead trees, so that the whole village stood rimed and pale as though frost had fallen and not gone; and it leaves the wreck of ordinary life turned wrong, a chair in a hedge, a boat in a bedroom, the school clock stopped at the black hour, mud a foot deep and stinking in the church itself where the pews stood askew and the psalm-boards floated face down. The land was poisoned with the salt for years to come; the fields would grow nothing that season or the next; the oyster beds and the mussel plots out on the flats, Leendert's beds, were choked and killed and buried under the silt the flood had carried in, the whole slow-built underwater husbandry of the village undone in a night — the trade dead with the salt, as a trade in that country had died once before, in his grandfather's time, when a different sea was closed off up the coast and the salt went out of it and took the fishery with it. Marinus knew that old story. Every man on that water knew it. He stood in the ruin and thought that his country's whole history was the two directions of one bargain with the salt — the sea let in, the sea shut out, and either way a trade drowned and a people made to begin again — and then he stopped thinking, because thinking was not the work, and went to find the bell.

The clock was dead past saving. He climbed the tower — sixty-one rungs, one complaint per rung, some rungs slimed and treacherous now with the tide's leavings — and found the works in their oak cage seized solid, every bearing rusted in an afternoon by the salt water that had stood to the second stage, the going train frozen, the escapement a lump of orange scale, thirty years of his oiling undone in one tide. Time had not merely stopped in Zoutkerke; the instrument of it was killed. But above the dead clock the bell hung sound. Salt water does not hurt a bell; a bell is made to live in the weather; and the great recast bronze swung true on its wheel when he leaned his weight into the rim, dull now with a green-white bloom of dried salt filming its lower curve where the flood had reached it and dried on it, but whole, and its voice, when he let it speak once, a single stroke into the ruined afternoon, as clean and deep and true as the day the founder poured it.

They came back to it in ones and twos through that broken summer, the people of Zoutkerke who were coming back at all, over the mended dike on bicycles and in carts, to houses filmed white with salt and floored with stinking mud, to begin the long filthy work of reclaiming a village from the sea's leavings. There is no dignity in it and Marinus would not pretend there was; the salvage of a drowned house is mud and stench and ruin, the swollen furniture carried out and burned, the walls hacked back to the brick to dry, the dead beast dug out of the ditch, the well fouled and to be pumped and pumped and pumped. But there was a stubborn plain courage in the doing of it that Marinus came to honour more than he had ever honoured the great things — the courage of a woman scrubbing salt off a doorstep that led into a wrecked house on a poisoned polder below a sea that had already once come over the wall, scrubbing it anyway, because it was her doorstep; the courage of the ordinary against the enormous, which is the only courage most people are ever asked for and the one least sung. He dug the church out himself, with Vinke and what men could be spared, barrowing the mud out of God's house through the summer heat with the flies rising off it, and set the pews to rights, and dried the psalm-boards in the sun of the churchyard among the new graves, and it was hard hot filthy work and he was glad of it, gladder than he could say, because it was work, and work was the one answer to the standing grief that a man could take up in his two hands, and he had his hands back now, healed and hard, and a use for them.

He rang it, in the end, for the burials. They buried Zoutkerke's dead through that broken summer as the ground came dry enough and the coffins could be got out, not all at once but as each was ready, in the raised churchyard that had held the living up out of the water and now took the drowned back into itself; and Marinus tolled the bell for every one of them, by hand, alone in the tower, the slow single stroke and the long count and the next, the oldest and simplest thing a bell does, *de doden beklaag ik,* the dead I mourn. He tolled the mussel man and his wife. He tolled the two old sisters and the baby by the sluice and the men off the dike. And on a still grey day in that summer he came up the tower and tolled the bell for Leendert Verschuure, his daughter's husband, the boy's father, drowned holding the dike — tolled it slow and even and complete, one stroke at a time with a space between so that each stroke was heard for itself, the count that is not for saying said in the only way it can be said, over the salt-killed fields and the ruined harbour and the small dark group at the graveside, his own grief rung out on his own bell by his own broken hands; and standing to the rope in the belfry with the tears running unremarked into the salt already dried on his face, Marinus understood that he had been a klokkenist all his life and had only now, at fifty-seven, in the wreck of everything, learned the whole of what the instrument was for.

# Chapter Ten — The Caisson

The sea was answered that autumn, and Marinus went to watch it done, because a man who has heard the sea ask its question is owed the sight of the answer, and because the answering was a thing worth the seeing whatever it cost to feel.

The great breaches did not close of themselves and could not be closed by hand; the gaps the flood had torn in the dikes were rivers now, deep and tide-scoured, and every day the sea ran in and out through them twice and kept the land drowned and would not let the men near enough to build. So they answered the sea in the sea's own scale. All that summer and autumn, up and down the drowned islands, the work went on with a size to it that Marinus had never seen turned on the water before — the endless files of trucks and clay, the dredgers, the pile-drivers, the dijkleger of soldiers and hired men and volunteers labouring on the broken banks in their thousands; and for the worst of the gaps, the ones the tide would not let them fill, they brought the caissons. Marinus stood on a mended dike a half-day's journey from Zoutkerke on a raw November morning and watched them close such a gap, and did not, for a long while, have any words in him. They were great hollow boxes of concrete, the size of houses, the size of churches — surplus from the war, he was told, towed from the far coast, the very caissons that had once been sunk to make a harbour for an army landing on a foreign shore and were now to be sunk to shut a Zeeland sea — and they floated them into the mouth of the breach on the slack of the tide, a line of grey giants riding impossibly on the water, and held them, and at the exact turn of the tide, in the one still minute the sea gives, they opened the valves and let the sea into them and the giants went down all together onto the sill the divers had made, and settled, and stopped the gap; and the next tide came against them and broke, and did not come through. The sea had run through that place twice a day since the last night of January. It ran through it no more. Marinus watched the first tide break against the sunk caissons and hold, and found that he was gripping the rail of the dike with both his healed hands, and that he was, without a sound, weeping again, and could not have told anyone whether it was grief or the other thing that has no better name than awe.

For it was a public work of genuine and enormous good, and that was the whole difficulty of feeling it. There was no villain in what had happened to Zoutkerke and no villain in the answer; there was only the low land and the high sea and the six-hundred-year bargain between them, and the men now setting themselves, with everything a country had, to change the terms of the bargain for good. They were not only mending the dikes that autumn. Word was going about, on the wireless and in the papers and in the mouths of the engineers who came and went, that the country had resolved that this would never come again — that a commission had been named within a fortnight of the flood, in the very first shock of it, and had set itself to a work beyond any dike-mending: to close the sea arms themselves, the whole reach of open water between the islands, with dams and barriers on a scale the world had not attempted, so that the sea could never again get its hand behind the dikes and into the polders in the dark. The Delta plan, they called it, the great sea-answer, a work of decades, a work that would outlive every man then labouring at it. Marinus, standing on the dike, understood two things about it at once and held them both, because his whole trade had taught him to hold two things about one bell. The first was that it was right; that it was a good and a great thing; that it would save, in the long unimaginable run of it, more Zoutkerkes than a man could count, keep more grandsons in more towers hearing the hour struck through the night. And the second was that it came, as the great answers always come, after the count; that not one soul the flood had taken would be given back by it; that his son-in-law lay in the raised churchyard and the caissons could not raise him; that the plan answered the sea and could not answer the dead, who are answered, if at all, only one at a time, slowly, with a space between, by a bell. He held the two things, the rightness and the lateness, and did not try to make them into one thing, because they were not one thing; they were the two directions of the bargain, and a man who keeps a bell learns to live in the space between the strokes.

When they closed the last of the great gaps that November — the famous one, up on the northern island, the four sunk caissons that shut the final breach and gave the drowned country back its ring of dry land — the news of it went round the islands like a peal, and the villages that had bells rang them. Zoutkerke had a bell. Marinus climbed his tower on the evening of the day the word came, up the sixty-one rungs past the dead clock he had not yet had the heart or the means to mend, and he rang the great recast bell over the salt-killed polder — not the alarm, and not the toll, but the third thing, the glad loud ringing-up, the peal a bell is given for the deliverances, that he had not asked of it in all the months since the night — and he rang it long, into the dark, for the closing of the sea, for the answer given however late, for the country that had emptied its cupboards and named its commission and towed its grey giants down the coast to shut the water out; and the few families who had come back to camp in the wreck and begin the mending came out of their salt-white doorways to stand in the ruined street and listen, and some of them, he was told after, took off their hats.

# Chapter Eleven — The New Job

The hardest question of that year was not how to rebuild Zoutkerke but whether to, and it was asked in earnest, by serious and kindly men, and Marinus de Ridder set himself against it with a stubbornness that surprised the whole committee and not least himself.

For it was not certain, in the reckoning that came after, that every drowned village should rise again where it had drowned. The engineers and the land men came with their maps and their reason, and their reason was not cruel, it was only reason: some of the low polders had been emptied so entirely, the people so scattered and so few now come back, the land so poisoned and the harbours so silted and the whole hard bargain so plainly against them, that the sensible course — they said the word gently but they said it — might be not to rebuild at all, but to let the polder go to grass and consolidate the people onto higher, safer ground, and draw a line under the old drowned place, and start the survivors clean somewhere the sea could not reach. It was said of Zoutkerke, among others. And it was not, Marinus had to grant, a foolish thing to say. There was little enough to come back to. Half the village lay in the churchyard or scattered inland and not returning; the oyster trade was dead in the silted flats; the salt would keep the fields barren for years; a reasonable man, counting it up, might well shut the ledger on Zoutkerke and call it a mercy.

Marinus was not, on this one matter, a reasonable man, and he found when it came to it that he knew exactly why, and could say it, which surprised him most of all. He stood up in the schoolroom of the dry Brabant village where the committee met, a koster among engineers, a man with no standing but his thirty years and his broken hands, and he said that a village was not its ledger. He said that the sea had already taken everything the sea could take, the people and the beasts and the trade and the fields, and that if they now took the place itself, the ground, the church, the ring of the dead — if they let Zoutkerke be a name on an old map and nothing more — then the sea would have taken the one last thing it had not managed to drown on its own, which was the going-on, the continuing, the thing his whole trade stood for; and that he for one would not hand the sea that victory after it had been denied it by the bell. He said that the dead in the churchyard had held the living up out of the water on the worst night in the history of the coast, and that you did not repay the dead for that by abandoning them to the grass. And he said — and here the reasonable men looked at their hands — that his grandson had asked him, in the dry village, how you know it is not so far to morning when there is no bell; and that he had promised the boy they would go home and wind the clock and hang the bell right and then they would both know; and that he did not intend to teach the boy, as the first lesson of his life, that a promise made in the dark is a thing you keep only when the ledger allows it. Then he sat down, having said more words at one time than he had said in a year, and the committee was quiet, and the thing turned, not on his eloquence, which was small, but on the fact that a man had stood up and refused, and that refusal, it turned out, was catching. Zoutkerke would be rebuilt.

The mending of the clock was the part of the rebuilding that was most truly his, and he saved it, half consciously, for last, the way you save the thing you most want and most fear to spoil. The clockmaker who came from Goes was Piet Dekker, an old man now, who had been a young man with Marinus in the carillon tower thirty-odd years before, in the years of the batons and the four octaves of bronze and the whole heart put out over a town's roofs; and the two of them had not worked side by side since, and fell into it that fortnight in the cold high clock chamber as though no time had passed at all, which is the gift old friends of a craft give one another and almost no one else can. They took the salt-seized works apart wheel by wheel and bearing by bearing, the two pairs of old hands that had once run over a keyboard of bells together now freeing a rusted pallet and scouring orange scale by lamplight, and they talked as they worked, the slow sparse talk of men whose hands are busy, of the old tower and the old tunes and who was dead now of the men they had known; and once Dekker, holding the cleaned escape-wheel up to the light through the louvre, said without looking round that he had heard how the koster of Zoutkerke had rung his bell through the whole night of the water, and asked, in the plain way of an old friend, whether it was true; and Marinus said yes, it was true, and Dekker nodded, and set the wheel down, and said only, "Good," and they went on with the work; and that was the whole of the praise Marinus ever wanted or got, and it was enough, and more than enough, from that man, in that place, over that work.

It came back slowly and it came back changed. The dike went up higher and broader than it had ever stood, built now to the new reckoning, the great sea-answer's reckoning, so that the green wall to the north of the village stood like a rampart and the water behind it, when Marinus climbed to look, seemed for the first time in his life not quite so surely his master. The salt went slowly out of the fields under the rain of two winters and the land came grudgingly back to a thin green. The oystermen — the few, the young, the ones who would not be told their trade was dead — went out onto the scoured flats and began the patient underwater husbandry again from the beginning, laying fresh shell for the spat to strike on, betting years of their lives on a harvest a decade off, because that was the trade and the trade was in the blood and a man who works the water is by nature a man who plants what he will not soon reap. And in the church, when the mud was dug out and the pews set right and the psalm-boards found and dried, Marinus de Ridder got his clock mended. A clockmaker came from Goes, an old friend from his carillon days, and the two of them spent a fortnight in the tower over the salt-seized works, freeing the bearings, cutting a new pallet, scouring the orange scale from thirty years of iron, coaxing the going train back to life; and on a bright cold morning near the turn of that second year Marinus wound the two weights up hand over hand, one complaint per rung already forgotten in the doing, and set the hands, and let the escapement go — and the clock ticked. It ticked in the tower over the mending village as it had ticked over the drowning one, indifferent, patient, keeping the time whether anyone listened or not; and that afternoon, for the first time since the last night of January two years before, the bell of Zoutkerke struck the hour, of itself, by the clock, the way it was meant to, four plain strokes into the winter air over the raised churchyard and the rebuilt street and the high new dike; and the boy Bram, eight now, standing in the churchyard with his grandfather, counted them under his breath to the end and looked up, and did not need to ask the question, because he had his answer, and so, at last, did the old man beside him.

# Chapter Twelve — The Salt Bell

On the first Sunday the church was fit to use again, a full year and more after the water, Marinus de Ridder climbed his tower to ring the village in to worship, and stood a while first in the belfry with his hand on the bell, as he had stood the evening the soldier came to take him off, and looked at the salt.

It was still there. He had never scoured it off. Around the lower curve of the great bronze, where the flood had risen and stood and dried, the bell wore a bloom of salt, green-white, faint, a tide-line printed on it forever the way the tide-lines were printed on the mended walls of the village that no whitewash quite hid; and Marinus, who kept every metal in his church bright as a rule, who had oiled and scoured and polished for thirty years, had found that he could not bring himself to take the salt off the bell, and had come to understand why, and was at peace with it. The salt was the bell's inscription now, the true one, the one the founder had not cast but the sea had. Around its shoulder the old raised words still ran, *Levenden roep ik, de doden beklaag ik,* the living I call, the dead I mourn, and Marinus had spent his whole life reading them as two separate offices a bell might be asked to perform on separate days — the glad peal and the slow toll, the wedding and the burial, the two different faces of the trade. He knew better now. He had learned on one night and in the long year after it that they were not two offices but one; that in the worst hour there is no line between the calling of the living and the mourning of the dead, that the same bronze voice does both in the same breath, that to ring for the living to rise is already to mourn the ones who will not, and that this — this, and not the ordered striking of the hours he had given his life to — was what a bell was finally for. The hours were the small change of the trade. This was the trade. A bell was a human voice made loud enough and lasting enough to be heard over the sea, saying the only two things a human voice has ever finally had to say to the dark and the water: *come, you who can,* and *I remember you, you who could not.* The salt on the bronze was the day it had said them both at once. He would leave the salt.

He rang the village in. Not the alarm and not the toll but the plain glad Sunday ringing, the bell swinging out clean and deep over the polder, over the high new dike with the grey sea safe behind it, over the raised churchyard where Leendert lay and the mussel man and his wife and the two old sisters and the baby by the sluice and the men off the dike, over the thin green fields coming slowly back and the scoured flats where the young men had laid their shell and their patience, over the rebuilt street where the doors were opening and the people coming out in their Sunday black to walk up to the church by the sound of the bell as their grandparents had done and their grandchildren would; and Marinus stood to the rope in the belfry with his healed hands and rang, and thought of nothing, and was, for the length of the ringing, wholly at his work. Below him in the churchyard Neel would be waiting, and Mina in her widow's black that she wore plainly and would one day, he hoped, set aside, and the boy Bram who had counted the hour to the end and got his answer. The clock ticked in its cage behind him, keeping the ordinary time it kept, and would strike, when the hour came round, of itself. But this was not the clock's ringing. This was his, the man's, the last true kind, the kind no clock can strike and no machine can learn, the human hand on the human bell over the water; and he gave it everything the morning wanted, and let the great salt-bloomed bronze fling its voice out over the drowned and risen country to every soul who had ears yet above the water, the living he called and the dead he mourned, in the one sound, with the salt of the sea dried into the metal like a signature on a receipt for a debt that had been paid, both ways, in full.

They gathered in the churchyard below to the sound of him, the way they always had, coming up the mended lane in their Sunday black between the new graves and the old, and Marinus, ringing, could picture them without looking, the whole shape of a village reassembling itself around the pull of a bell: Janne first and loudest, upright and furious and alive, complaining already of something; the young oystermen with the sea-cold still in their knuckles and their whole harvest a bet years off; Vinke at the door in his black, older by a lifetime than the man who had battened a stove against a wet Sunday two winters gone. And Neel and Mina and the boy, on the raised ground among the dead who had held them all up out of the water, their three faces turned to the tower. It was not a large congregation. The flood had seen to that, and the flood would go on seeing to it for a generation, in the empty chairs and the small school and the names that fell on their days. But it was a village, and it had come back, and it was walking to church by the sound of its bell, and the sea stood safe behind the high new wall, and that — Marinus thought, leaning his weight into the rope and feeling the great bronze answer — that was not nothing. That was, in fact, very nearly everything, and had been bought at very nearly everything's price, and was worth ringing, and he rang it.

The boy would keep the bell after him. That was not yet settled and did not need settling; Marinus only knew it, the way he knew the phase of the moon, ringing that first Sunday morning with his grandson in the churchyard below turning his face up to the sound. Bram would learn the sixty-one rungs and the winding and the striking, and would learn, God grant it not too young, the other thing too, the thing the salt on the bell was there to teach whoever should ring it after; and the bell would go on, calling the living and mourning the dead over the low land behind the high dike, one true note at a time, so that a child waking in the dark of the polder and afraid would hear it strike and know that the world was still going on, and that it was not, after all, so far to morning. Marinus de Ridder rang his bell over the sea that had taken his village and given it back, and the salt shone faint on the bronze, and the sound went out over the water, and was, in all that low grey country, the loudest and the most human thing there was.

THE END

**Author's note.** Zoutkerke, the Sint-Maartenspolder, and every person in this book are invented. There was no such village and no such koster; Marinus de Ridder, his family, and his neighbours are fiction, and no real person appears in these pages. The disaster behind the story is not fiction. On the night of Saturday 31 January into Sunday 1 February 1953, a severe northwesterly storm drove a North Sea surge onto a spring tide and broke the sea dikes of the southwestern Netherlands; the flood killed 1,836 people in the Netherlands — with hundreds more in England, in Belgium, and at sea — drove some hundred thousand from their homes, drowned tens of thousands of animals, and put much of Zeeland, South Holland, and western North Brabant under salt water in a single night. Warning reached many villages too late or not at all; church bells, in places, were the only alarm. The last great breaches were closed later that year with sunken caissons, and the disaster gave rise to the Delta Works, the decades-long sea-defence that has guarded the region since. This novella sets an invented village and invented people beside that real event; it names no real victim and stages no real death, because the real dead are not the author's to use. It is offered in respect for them, and for the province that struggled and emerged. *Luctor et emergo.*
