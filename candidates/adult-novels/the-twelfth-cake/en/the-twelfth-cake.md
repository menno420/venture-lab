# THE TWELFTH CAKE

*A Twelfth Night Novella*

> By the old rule of the feast, the Twelfth Night cake holds one dried bean, and whoever finds it in their slice rules the evening — apprentice or alderman, no appeals. On the last night of Pridd's bakery, with the sale papers signed and the ovens going cold, twelve strangers shelter from the snow, the great cake is brought in from the window at last, and the bean turns up in the most inconvenient slice in London. A Twelfth Night novella about endings, flour, and the one night a year the world agrees to turn upside down.

---

**Content note:** Adult seasonal historical fiction, set in London on the fifth of January, 1847. Contains the sale of a family business, remembered bereavements, remembered hard winters, and one snowbound night in which nobody comes to harm. No crime, no violence, no explicit content, no swearing. A warm, candlelit read for the week after Christmas.

# Chapter One — The Last Batch

There is a particular quiet that comes into a bakery when the fire is drawn, and Josiah Pridd, who had heard every other sound the trade possessed, was hearing it now for the first time in his life.

It was not silence. A bakehouse is never silent, any more than a church is empty. The great oven at the back of Pridd's — brick, black-mouthed, older than the King before the King before the Queen — went on ticking and settling like an old dog by a dead hearth, giving back by pennyworths the heat it had been lent by the hundredweight. The copper hissed where the last of the water cooled. Overhead, along Pannier Court, the wind was beginning to say something about snow. But under all of it was the quiet, the new quiet, the quiet of no tomorrow's sponge set to rise, no sacks standing opened for the morning, no morning — and Josiah Pridd stood in the middle of it with his sleeves still rolled, the way a man stands in a room where somebody has died and the clock has not yet been stopped, because stopping it makes it true.

"Mr. Pridd," said Barney, from the doorway. "It's coming on to snow."

"It generally does," said Pridd, "when it means to."

Barnabas Trott was thirteen, the last apprentice of a house that had trained forty-one of them, and he had spent the whole of this fifth of January being brave in the manner of boys, which is to say loudly about small things and not at all about large ones. He had swept the trough. He had scoured the peels and hung them in their rack by height, the way Pridd's father had hung them, and his father before that, back to the year 1762 when Obadiah Pridd first lit the oven and Pannier Court first learned what mornings were for. He had chalked the day's date on the flour board, because it was his job to chalk it, and then he had stood looking at it a long while, because under the date, where the orders went, there was nothing to write.

The papers had been signed at three o'clock, in Old Jewry, at the offices of Gudgeon & Sharp, in a room that smelled of ink and sealing-wax and no bread whatsoever. Pridd had worn his church coat. He had written his name — a good clear baker's hand; you could have read it across the shop — on a line prepared for it, below several paragraphs in which the premises known as Pridd's, in Pannier Court, off Bread Street, in the City of London, together with the ovens, fixtures, and goodwill thereof, ceased with certain formalities to be his. A clerk with cold-reddened knuckles had passed the sheets, one by one, to the fire to dry the ink, holding each page like something he was sorry for. Completion at Lady Day; possession of the shop to the purchaser's men at the end of the month. The purchaser himself had not troubled to attend. Purchasers of his sort never did. The name on the papers was a firm's name, Crail & Mowl, of Leadenhall Street, agents and contractors, and what a firm of agents and contractors wanted with a bakehouse was nothing; what it wanted was the corner, and the frontage, and the court behind it, for offices. The ovens would be broken out like teeth.

Pridd had signed, shaken one dry hand, and walked back through the icy streets to draw his fire for the last time — for you may not leave a fire banked in premises you have sold, whatever your heart says; his father had taught him that a baker's first duty and his last are one and the same, and both of them are the fire — and now the fire was out, and the ovens were going cold, and it was Twelfth Night.

And in the window stood the cake.

It filled the window. It had been built to fill it — that was the whole art and vanity of the thing — three tiers of it on its gilt board, white as the weather coming, iced hard as a skating pond and twice as smooth, hooped with sugar ropes, walled with almond paste, crowned at its summit with a gilt paper crown sitting upon a wreath of sugar bay-leaves, and stuck all about with figures: a sugar king and a sugar queen, a fiddler, a dancing bear, a Grenadier, a milkmaid, a swan with a green ribbon neck, and one small sugar baker in a sugar apron, holding a loaf the size of a caraway seed. Forty-six years running there had been a great cake in Pridd's window on Twelfth Night. The first had been Ezra Pridd's, in the year One, made to spite Napoleon and dazzle Cheapside; the crowds had stood six deep, and boys had been pinned to the window-boards by their coat-skirts by other boys, that being the traditional wit of the season, and a Lord Mayor's footman had come down with a card and carried away the top tier for the Mansion House. Every year since, war or peace, dear flour or cheap, there had been a cake, and children at the glass with their breath making fog-roses on it, and their mothers behind them saying *come away now* in voices that meant *look a little longer*.

This year there had been no order for it. Who orders a Twelfth cake from a shop that is shut by Epiphany? The sensible thing had been to make no cake at all, and Pridd and Barney had made it anyway, the two of them, over three days, in a shop already sold — the fruit picked over by candlelight, the almonds pounded, the icing floated on and combed and left to set in the cold like ice on a pond — because there are things a man does sensibly and things a man does because he is what he is, and Josiah Pridd was a baker of Bread Street ward, third of his name, and the window of Pridd's did not go dark on Twelfth Night while he held the lease. It stood there now, lit by the two best lamps, worth eleven guineas of anybody's money and ordered by nobody, the grandest cake in the City of London, in the window of a shop that was already, in every way that signified to lawyers, dead.

"Who's to eat it?" Barney had asked, on the first day, up to his elbows in currants.

"That," Pridd had said, "is the cake's affair."

Now the boy came and stood by him in the doorway between shop and bakehouse, and they watched, together, past the great white shoulder of the cake, as the first of the snow came down Pannier Court — a few flakes, sauntering, insolent, like the advance men of a very large procession. The lamplighter had been and gone; the two gas lamps at the court's mouth wore little wavering haloes. Somewhere toward the river a church clock — St. Nicholas Cole Abbey, by the sound of it, which Pridd's father had always called the baker's church, Saint Nicholas being in the trade himself, in a manner of speaking — let go of five strokes, and the wind took each one away in a different direction.

"Mr. Pridd," said Barney, "what happens tomorrow?"

Pridd was some time answering. In the window, the sugar Grenadier presented arms at the weather. The gilt crown atop the cake caught the lamplight and gave it gently back, the way gilt paper will, as if to say: *I am not gold, but I am not nothing, either.*

"Tomorrow," said Pridd, "is Twelfth Day, and we are Twelfth Night. We'll let tonight come first."

And the snow, as if it had been waiting all along for some such invitation, stopped sauntering, and set to work.

By six it lay ankle-deep the length of Bread Street, and Cheapside had gone the colour of unbleached flour. By half past, the omnibuses had given it up; the last of them went by the end of the court with its horses steaming like puddings and its conductor calling something about Holborn Hill that was clearly a lie. The snow came down not in flakes now but in goose-feathers, in handfuls, in whole pillowfuls, with the businesslike air of weather that has been ordered in quantity and means to deliver. Wheels ceased. Hooves ceased. Footsteps lasted a little longer, hurrying, and then they ceased too, and there was only the hissing of gas, and the bells of the City counting the quarters to one another over the roofs, further apart in the snow than they had ever sounded, like lighthouses.

Josiah Pridd, sixty-three years old and master of nothing after Lady Day, stood in his lit shop behind his last great cake and watched his street disappear. And because he was watching — because on any other Twelfth Night he would have been ten deep in customers and seen nothing of the weather until it was on his own doorstep — he saw the small figure come up the middle of Bread Street, where the drifts were shallowest: a boy, a broom over his shoulder like a musket, stopping at the mouth of the court to stamp, and beat his cap against his knee, and look — as everything in that street had looked, every January for forty-six years — at the lit window, and the cake.

Pridd went to the door and opened it. The cold came in like a creditor.

"You're Kit," he said. "Watling Street corner. You swept for me Michaelmas quarter, when the mud was bad."

"Yes, sir," said the boy, through the chatter of his own teeth. "Only there's no crossings left, sir. It's all one." He looked past Pridd, helpless, at the window. "I weren't begging, sir. I were only looking at it. Same as ever year."

"Same as every year," said Pridd, and stood aside, and held the door wide, and in that moment — though he did not know it yet, and would not have believed you if you had told him — the last night of Pridd's bakery began. "Come in off my step, then, before you spoil it. Mind the mat. There's the stove in the back still warm, and Barney'll find you the heel of a loaf."

Kit came in, snow and broom and all. The door shut behind him with its little familiar chime.

Out in the whitening dark, the bells of the City struck seven, and did not know it, but they were ringing the feast in.

# Chapter Two — The Weather's Opinion

The second knock came at a quarter past seven, and it was a large, official-sounding knock, the knock of a man accustomed to doors being answered promptly and warmly, and it belonged to Alderman Pettifer.

Alderman Pettifer of Bread Street ward was built on the lines of the monument in his own favourite corner of the Guildhall — broad in the base, imposing about the middle, and surmounted by a face which, at this moment, resembled a boiled ham that had lost its way. He had dined early with the Cordwainers and been bound for a second engagement of a turtle-soup character in Broad Street, and his coachman, after certain adventures by Bow Church which the Alderman did not intend to relate and never afterwards did, had put it to him respectfully that the horses could go no further, and that His Worship might do worse than wait out the storm somewhere with a fire.

"Pridd," said the Alderman, shedding snow all over the good mat like a shaken counterpane, "your window is lit."

"It is, Alderman."

"You've been sold. It's all over the ward. Signed this very day, I heard."

"You heard right," said Pridd. "The papers are signed and the ovens are going cold. The window is lit because it is Twelfth Night, and it is my window until Lady Day."

The Alderman looked at him a moment, and something surfaced in the boiled ham that had known Pridd's shop, man and boy, for fifty years, and had once, in a plush childhood he mentioned to nobody, been lifted up by a nursemaid to see the cake, the year of Trafalgar, when the sugar figures had all been little ships.

"Quite right," he said, in a different voice. "Quite right. Is that stove I smell?"

"It is. And you'll take a chair by it, Alderman, for nobody's going to Broad Street tonight, nor anywhere else."

That was the beginning of the flood — for a lit window on a night when every other window is going dark is a lighthouse, whatever it sells; and Pridd's stood at the foot of the court like a lamp at the end of a mine, with its cake shining in it. The city emptied itself of its last stranded souls, and the snow herded them, as a dog herds, without malice and without argument, toward the light.

There came Joseph Huggins, hackney coachman, badge number 1109, who had got his cab as far as the Swan yard and his horse Duchess into a stall of that establishment, and himself no further than Pridd's doorstep, where he stood apologising for the state of his boots for fully a minute before anyone could persuade him over the sill. There came old Mrs. Gamage, who had been at a granddaughter's in Milk Street since Sunday and had chosen — against counsel, as she cheerfully admitted — this evening of all evenings to walk home to Aldersgate, because Twelfth Night at other people's firesides made her miss her Samuel more than the whole rest of the calendar together. She came in small and upright and frosted like the cake itself, and when she had been put in the warmest chair and given the first of the tea, she looked a long time at the window from the inside, and said, to nobody in particular:

"Ezra Pridd made my wedding cake. Twelfth Night, the year one-thousand-eight-hundred. Snowing then, too. There was a boy here carried it to the church for us on his head, through the weather, and wouldn't take a penny."

"His father wouldn't let him take the penny," said Pridd, setting the kettle back. "His father held that a wedding cake carried for luck must be carried free, or the luck stays in the shop." He was quiet a moment. "It was a good cake. Saffron in the crumb. He let me stone the fruit."

Mrs. Gamage looked at him — at the grey in him, and the flour that never quite leaves a baker's forearms, and the sixty-three years of him — and she smiled like a girl looking through a church door.

"It was you," she said. "You've snow on you still, I declare."

By eight o'clock the shop held nine souls and the stove had been fed to roaring, and Barney had been three times down the cellar for coals, taking a candle and coming back each time a little slower, because a cellar you have raced up and down four years of mornings becomes, on the last night, a place a boy says goodbye to in instalments.

The nine were these: Kit, thawed now, and installed by the stove with the heel of a quartern loaf and the entire, devout attention of a person eating; the Alderman, filling the best chair and beginning, under the influence of heat, to give off steam and opinions in about equal volume; Huggins the coachman, who would not sit down, but stood holding his tea like the reins of it, respectfully, near the door; Mrs. Gamage; a young clergyman, the Reverend Decimus Snell, curate, bound for his first parish in Kent by a coach that assuredly was not running, who had knocked in a flurry of apology and asked, could he just consult somebody's clock — and had been inside and holding buttered toast before he could finish constructing his sentence, and looked as though nothing of the kind had ever happened to him before, and kept glancing about himself like a man taking notes for a sermon he did not yet know he would give; a dressmaker's apprentice, Sally Sprat, aged fifteen, sent out at five o'clock — on such an evening! the room said it as one — with a banded box containing a ball-dress for a house in Bow Lane whose knocker nobody could find in the drifts, and who had cried a little with the cold when she first came in, and was now quite recovered and holding the box on her knees like a lapdog; a one-armed old soldier, Sergeant Culpepper, late of the 45th, who had said, "By your leave, master, I'll stand at the warm end," and taken up a position by the bakehouse door as if the stove were regimental colours; and — arrived within a minute of one another, off the same drift, shaking the same weather from their shoulders — a large, mild, sad-eyed foreign gentleman in a comforter, who bowed and named himself Tobias Finck, sugar-baker, of Whitechapel; and a small pen-worn person in black, of the sort the City breeds by the gross and never looks at twice, who gave his name in a voice like a dry nib as Nib — "Elias Nib, sir, of Gudgeon & Sharp's — that is — of Old Jewry — a law-stationer's writer, sir, nothing more" — and then appeared to wish he had said none of it, and sat down in the darkest corner available, and looked at the floor.

Pridd stood behind his own counter and surveyed his shop — his sold shop, his shop full of firelight and steam and other people's evenings — with an expression Barney could not read, though the boy tried.

"Mr. Pridd," Barney whispered, on his way past with the coal, "it's a good job we baked, after all."

"It is always a good job we baked," said Pridd. "Mind your sparks."

And the snow came down. It came down as if it had heard the shop was closing and meant to buy everything left; it packed Pannier Court to the second window-boards; it silenced Cheapside from the Poultry to St. Paul's; it lay on the dome of the cathedral itself like icing let down from heaven on a string. And it drove before it, up the middle of a Bread Street that no longer had any edges, the last three guests: a lady in a plaid travelling-cloak over what appeared, at the hem, to be the costume of a fairy queen; a young woman carrying a carpet-bag, walking not like the lost but like the arriving, who stopped at the window and set her bare hand flat against the glass; and, some distance behind her, picking his way with a stick and a lantern gone out, a spare, upright, grey gentleman in a great-coat of the first quality, who looked up at the sign over the door for a long moment — PRIDD'S. ESTABLISHED 1762 — with the snow settling on his shoulders, in the attitude less of a man seeking shelter than of a man verifying an entry in a ledger.

The chime went. The door opened. The cold came in, and the last of them with it.

"Room by the stove," called Pridd, over the general shuffle. "Barney, the kettle. Ladies first, and the gentleman shut the door, if you'd be so kind — the weather's had my flour, it shan't have my warmth too."

The grey gentleman shut the door. Asked his name — the Alderman did the asking; the Alderman always did the asking — he paused for the smallest slice of a second, no longer than it takes a clerk to turn a page.

"Cork," he said.

# Chapter Three — Twelve by Candlelight

It was Kit who did the counting. He did it from the top of the flour bin, where he had been perched out of the way of feet, going round the room with one grubby finger, lips moving, twice over to be sure, and announcing the result with the gravity of a returning officer.

"Twelve," he said. "There's twelve of us, not counting Mr. Pridd nor Barney, on account of it's their house. Twelve strangers."

"Not strangers now," said Mrs. Gamage comfortably. "You can't be strangers past nine o'clock. It isn't done."

"Twelve," repeated the Reverend Mr. Snell, half to himself, in the tone of a man to whom the number suggested several improving parallels, all of which, being shy, he decided to keep. But he took out a little memorandum-book, and wrote in it, and blushed when he saw Sally watching, and put it away.

The lady in the travelling-cloak had proved to be Mrs. Fan Tarleton, of the Theatre Royal, Drury Lane — "of the pantomime, my dears, this fortnight past and five weeks yet to come, if the snow spares us" — driven into the City that afternoon to a Cheapside dressmaker about repairs to the fairy queen's train, in which garment's lower portions she was even now obliged to appear, the dressmaker having kept the skirt. She had a laugh that took the whole room in, a way of standing that made the counter a proscenium, and a flask of something restorative in her muff which she offered round with perfect impartiality, beginning with Mrs. Gamage, who took some.

The young woman with the carpet-bag gave her name quietly: Comfort Pell, of Rochester. She said very little else. She had chosen, of all the seats in the shop, the plain stool at the counter's end nearest the bakehouse door, where the old warmth of the ovens still breathed through; and she sat there with her bag at her feet and her hands folded, and watched the room — not unkindly, not shyly either; the way, though nobody present could have named it, a baker watches an oven through the flue-glass. When Barney staggered past with the coal-scuttle, she rose without a word, took its other handle, and shared the weight to the stove as one who had carried scuttles all her life; and when the tea went round again she somehow had the cutting of the bread and butter, and the slices came off her knife so even you could have sold them by weight.

"You've a trade, Miss Pell," said Pridd, watching the knife. It was not a question.

"My father was a baker," she said. "Noah Pell, of Rochester. The Golden Sheaf, by the bridge." A small pause, exactly the length of a grave. "Until Michaelmas."

"Ah," said Pridd. And then, because bakers say things to one another in loaves and silences that other trades say in letters of condolence: "The Golden Sheaf. I knew the sign. There was a twist they made there, a saffron twist, came up to the Maidstone show one year and took the ribbon from better men."

"From bigger men," said Comfort Pell, and the ghost of her father went round the ring of firelight, bowing.

Herr Finck, meanwhile, had gravitated to the window as a compass needle gravitates north, and stood before the great cake with his hands clasped behind him, in the unmistakable attitude — Mrs. Tarleton recognised it at once, professionally — of an artist in front of somebody else's masterpiece: one part homage, one part inventory. He was heard to murmur to himself in two languages. At last he turned.

"Sir," he said to Pridd, with a small bow, "the swan. The swan with the green ribbon. That is a Kummel mould, or I am a pastrycook." He said *pastrycook* the way a frigate captain might say *ferryman*.

"It is," said Pridd, surprised. "Old Herr Kummel of Goodman's Fields supplied my father thirty years, and me after him, till he died. The swan, the Grenadier, and the crown — the gilt crown's his too; the wreath under it. I've kept his moulds in use every Twelfth Night since. There's no better work in London, though I've had to do the casting myself these last years, and he'd box my ears for my seams."

"He boxed mine for the same," said Tobias Finck. "I was his apprentice, sir. I buried him. I have his other moulds in Whitechapel, and his lease, and his debts, and his name over my door in letters I cannot yet make myself paint out." He looked back at the cake, and his sad eyes shone in the lamplight. "I came tonight through your City on the way from a customer who did not pay, in weather that wished me dead, and I stop at a lit window, and there is my master's swan, swimming. Sir, I will shake your hand."

They shook hands, the baker and the sugar-baker, solemnly, across the counter, as across a border; and nobody in the shop understood precisely what had been transacted, except possibly Comfort Pell, but everybody felt the warmer for it.

It was the Alderman — give the Alderman his due; the night would owe him this much — who said the necessary thing.

The clocks had gone nine. The tea was three rounds old; the bread and butter had fallen back before organised appetite like infantry before cavalry; Kit had been fed twice and was working on diplomatic relations toward a third; and the stove, mighty as it was, had begun to lose ground against fourteen souls' worth of thawing boots and the draught under the shop door. Mrs. Gamage had her shawl up round her ears. The snow outside had reached the first window-boards and was auditing the second.

"Pridd," said Alderman Pettifer, "this won't answer. We're fourteen in a shop built for passing trade, half of us wet through, and the fire's fighting the door. You've a bakehouse back there with an oven in it drew its fire only this afternoon, if my nose is any judge, and a bakehouse oven holds its warmth the way the Bank holds bullion. I've sat on committees, sir, that reasoned worse than that. I move we adjourn."

For a moment Josiah Pridd stood quite still. The bakehouse was his own place as no other spot on earth was — the room he had been born over, the room his father had died over, the room whose ovens he had drawn that afternoon with his own hands, closing the iron door on forty years of mornings — and to open it now to a room full of strangers was to let the world walk on a grave not one day old. Barney, who understood perhaps a third of this and felt all of it, held his breath.

Then Pridd looked round them: at Kit's blue lips gone pink; at Mrs. Gamage small in her shawl; at Sally hugging her band-box; at Miss Pell sitting where the oven's breath came through the door, as near the warmth of the trade as the shop would let her.

"My father," he said, "held that a bakehouse is like a pulpit: nobody idle in it, and nobody cold near it. Barney — lamps. Sergeant, if you'd bring the coal. Alderman, your committee may carry its own chairs. Ladies and gentlemen —" and he opened the bakehouse door, and the deep, dry, cliff-face warmth of the great oven rolled out over them all like a benediction — "walk in. Mind the trough. Welcome to the back of the shop."

They filed through, twelve strangers and a boy and an Alderman's chair, into the long brick room with its scoured tables and its hanging peels and its oven like a sleeping whale; and the lamplight ran along the old wood, and the warmth folded them in, and Mrs. Tarleton, entering last but one, stopped on the threshold and said, in a voice that filled the room to its corners without once being loud, "Oh, my dears. It's better than the theatre."

Last of all came the grey gentleman who had given his name as Cork. He paused in the doorway; he looked down the bakehouse — at the oven, at the peels, at the flour board with its chalked date and its empty order column, at all of it — with that same air of a man verifying a ledger; and something crossed his face that no one saw except Elias Nib, who had been all evening in his corner trying not to look at him, and looking at him, and trying not to, like a man with a sore tooth and a tongue.

Mr. Nib turned quite pale, and found a seat in the new room as far from the gentleman as its geography allowed.

# Chapter Four — The Cake in the Window

It will have been observed by persons of experience that a room containing fourteen people, a warm oven, and no possible means of departure before morning will, by ten o'clock, arrive at one of two conditions: quarrelling, or telling stories. Which of the two it shall be is generally decided by whether anybody feeds it.

Pridd fed it. He went round the bakehouse shelves by lamplight — the last shelves of a shop sold over his head — and he set out on the great scoured table everything the trade had left him: a basket of Twelfth-buns, spiced and sugared, baked two days since for a custom that the weather had now cancelled; a crock of butter; a jar of honey; the end of a Stilton cheese sent at Christmas by his daughter Susan's husband, the corn factor at Ipswich; a string of figs; a paper of almonds; and, going down himself to the cellar and coming up with cobwebs on his shoulders and a look Barney had never seen on him, two bottles of a cordial his wife had made — elder and spice — the autumn before she died, which he had been keeping, he discovered as his hand closed on them in the dark, for absolutely nothing at all.

Sergeant Culpepper mulled ale at the stove-plate with his one hand and a soldier's patience, grating in nutmeg from Mrs. Gamage's own pocket-grater — she was of a generation that no more went abroad without a nutmeg than without a prayer-book — and dropping in roasted apples till the pot wore a lamb's-wool collar and the whole room smelled like the inside of a carol. Sally handed cups. Kit was made lamp-trimmer and took the office seriously. Mrs. Tarleton, discovering the bakehouse's acoustics, gave them the fairy queen's speech from the pantomime with a peel for a sceptre, and then, because Mrs. Gamage asked, something older and softer that had been sung when Mrs. Gamage was married, and the old lady kept time on the table-edge with two fingers, forty-seven years away.

And the talk, as it will around a scoured table when the wind has the streets, came home by and by to the thing standing lit in the window at the front of the shop, whiter than the weather: the cake.

"What I say," said the Alderman, who had dined, but for whom dinner was less an event than a condition, "is that it is a monstrous fine cake, Pridd, the finest since the year of the Coronation cake, which I judged, sir, at this very window, in my capacity — and I ask, as I have asked in the Court of Common Council on questions of graver public moment but never, I confess, of keener interest: *what is to be done with it?*"

"It's ordered by nobody," said Pridd, "and owed to nobody. It stands where it stands because it's Twelfth Night, and this house puts a cake in that window on Twelfth Night. Tomorrow —" he stopped; and the room heard the sentence he did not finish, which was: *tomorrow there is no this house.*

"Begging pardon," said Kit, from among the lamps, in the ringing treble of a person who has not yet learned that some questions are not asked, "but ain't anyone going to EAT it?"

There was a silence.

"Kit," said Sally, scandalised, "you can't eat a cake like that. It's — it's like eating the Lord Mayor's Show."

"You can, though," said Kit doggedly. "That's what it's FOR. I've watched that window ever year since I can remember anything, and I always says to myself, that's the realest thing in London, that is, on account of grand as it looks, it's dinner underneath. It ain't a monument. It's dinner in its Sunday best. And it's got one night left same as the shop has, and seems to me —" his courage ran out all at once, as boys' courage does, with the whole room looking at him — "seems to me it oughtn't to be wasted, is all," he finished, very small, and became extremely busy with a lamp-wick.

"The child," said Mrs. Tarleton, "has delivered the prologue. I could not have placed it better myself."

But Pridd had turned away, to the oven, and put his broad hand flat against the warm brick of it, as a man puts his hand on a horse that is being talked about; and when he spoke it was to the oven as much as the room.

"You'll think it strange in a tradesman," he said, "to bake a thing and grudge the eating of it. I don't grudge it. Only — while that cake stands in the window, this is Pridd's, and it's Twelfth Night, and nothing's ended yet. The minute it's cut —" his hand moved a little on the brick — "the minute it's cut, it's the last cake, and eaten, and swept up, and the shop's a corner property in Bread Street ward, vacant possession at Lady Day. A man wants one more night where nothing's ended yet. That's the whole of it. It's not sense. I never said it was sense."

Nobody answered him directly, because there was nothing in it to answer; it was as watertight as grief ever is. It was Comfort Pell who spoke at last, and she did not answer him either. She had gone, while the others watched Pridd, to the doorway between bakehouse and shop, and was looking down the length of the counter at the back of the great cake where it stood against the whitened glass — the unseen side, the baker's side, the side with the little door in the third tier's ornament where the trials of the icing had been made.

"Mr. Pridd," she said. "Who is it for?"

"I told you. There was no order. It's for nobody."

"No," said Comfort Pell, turning round, "that's who was ordered to pay for it. I asked who it's *for*." She came back into the lamplight, and her voice was still quiet, but it had a baker's-daughter certainty in it, learned at a shop door on winter mornings, that held the room like the warm brick held its heat. "My father made a great cake every Twelfth Night at the Golden Sheaf, in a town where half the trade thought a fancy window was above a bridge-end bakery, and every year the same men asked him what it cost and what it fetched, and every year he told them it fetched nothing and cost a fortnight, and made it again. I asked him why, the year I was tall enough to comb the icing. He said: *Comfort, three hundred and sixty-four days a year, this shop feeds the town its bread. One day a year, it feeds them something they'll stand in the snow to look at. The cake's not for the customers. The cake is the shop, saying what it thinks bread is.*" She looked at the window, and back at Pridd. "Your shop's been saying it forty-six years, and it's said it once more with the fire drawn and the papers signed, and I walked from the Borough through this snow tonight because I heard, in a bakehouse where I was being told no for the eleventh time, that Pridd's of Pannier Court had its cake in the window though the house was sold — and I wanted to stand in front of one shop in London that talked that way, once, before I went home again." She stopped; she had surprised the colour into her own cheeks; but she did not sit down. "You asked what trade I'm of, and I told you my father's. I'm of the same. Eleven London houses this fortnight have told me a bakehouse is no place for a woman to be master in, and I don't notice the trade's opinion of me keeps the snow off. So I'll say the rest. A cake in a window is the shop speaking. A cake on the table is the shop *feeding people*. And the second is the honester sentence, and you know it, Mr. Pridd, and your father knew it, and mine — and if this house has one night left, I'd sooner it ended feeding fourteen people snowed out of their lives than standing grand and hungry in a window with nothing ended yet. Nothing ended is nothing finished. There's a difference between keeping a thing and refusing to give it."

The lamp-flames stood up straight in the still air. Barney was looking from her to his master with his mouth open. Old Mrs. Gamage said, "Amen," placidly, as at church.

Josiah Pridd looked at the last apprentice of the Golden Sheaf for a long moment, across his scoured table, across the whole sold kingdom of his bakehouse; and those nearest him said afterwards that what came into his face was not surrender, and not grief either, but something that had been out of it since three o'clock that afternoon, in a lawyer's office in Old Jewry: interest.

"Barney," he said. "Fetch the bean."
