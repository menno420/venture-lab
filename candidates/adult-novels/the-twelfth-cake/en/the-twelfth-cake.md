# THE TWELFTH CAKE

*A Twelfth Night Novella*

> By the old rule of the feast, the Twelfth Night cake holds one dried bean, and whoever finds it in their slice rules the evening — apprentice or alderman, no appeals. On the last night of Pridd's bakery, with the sale papers signed and the ovens going cold, twelve strangers shelter from the snow, the great cake is brought in from the window at last, and the bean turns up in the most inconvenient slice in London. A Twelfth Night novella about endings, flour, and the one night a year the world agrees to turn upside down.

---

**Content note:** Adult seasonal historical fiction, set in London on the fifth of January, 1847. Contains the sale of a family business, remembered bereavements, remembered hard winters, and one snowbound night in which nobody comes to harm. No crime, no violence, no explicit content, no swearing. A warm, candlelit read for the week after Christmas.

# Chapter One — The Last Batch

There is a particular quiet that comes into a bakery when the fire is drawn, and Josiah Pridd, who had heard every other sound the trade possessed, was hearing it now for the first time in his life.

It was not silence. A bakehouse is never silent, any more than a church is empty. The great oven at the back of Pridd's — brick, black-mouthed, older than the King before the King before the Queen — went on ticking and settling like an old dog by a dead hearth, giving back by pennyworths the heat it had been lent by the hundredweight. The copper hissed where the last of the water cooled. Overhead, along Pannier Court, the wind was beginning to say something about snow. But under all of it was the quiet, the new quiet, the quiet of no tomorrow's sponge set to rise, no sacks standing opened for the morning, no morning — and Josiah Pridd stood in the middle of it with his sleeves still rolled, the way a man stands in a room where somebody has died and the clock has not yet been stopped, because stopping it makes it true.

"Mr. Pridd," said Barney, from the doorway. "It's coming on to snow."

"It generally does," said Pridd, "when it means to."

Barnabas Trott was thirteen, the last apprentice of a house that had trained forty-one of them, and he had spent the whole of this fifth of January being brave in the manner of boys, which is to say loudly about small things and not at all about large ones. He had swept the trough. He had scoured the peels and hung them in their rack by height, the way Pridd's father had hung them, and his father before that, back to the year 1762 when Obadiah Pridd first lit the oven and Pannier Court first learned what mornings were for. He had chalked the day's date on the flour board, because it was his job to chalk it, and then he had stood looking at it a long while, because under the date, where the orders went, there was nothing to write.

The papers had been signed at three o'clock, in Old Jewry, at the offices of Gudgeon & Sharp, in a room that smelled of ink and sealing-wax and no bread whatsoever. Pridd had worn his church coat. He had written his name — a good clear baker's hand; you could have read it across the shop — on a line prepared for it, below several paragraphs in which the premises known as Pridd's, in Pannier Court, off Bread Street, in the City of London, together with the ovens, fixtures, and goodwill thereof, ceased with certain formalities to be his. A clerk with cold-reddened knuckles had passed the sheets, one by one, to the fire to dry the ink, holding each page like something he was sorry for. Completion at Lady Day; possession of the shop to the purchaser's men at the end of the month. The purchaser himself had not troubled to attend. Purchasers of his sort never did. The name on the papers was a firm's name, Crail & Mowl, of Leadenhall Street, agents and contractors, and what a firm of agents and contractors wanted with a bakehouse was nothing; what it wanted was the corner, and the frontage, and the court behind it, for offices. The ovens would be broken out like teeth.

Pridd had signed, shaken one dry hand, and walked back through the icy streets to draw his fire for the last time — for you may not leave a fire banked in premises you have sold, whatever your heart says; his father had taught him that a baker's first duty and his last are one and the same, and both of them are the fire — and now the fire was out, and the ovens were going cold, and it was Twelfth Night.

And in the window stood the cake.

It filled the window. It had been built to fill it — that was the whole art and vanity of the thing — three tiers of it on its gilt board, white as the weather coming, iced hard as a skating pond and twice as smooth, hooped with sugar ropes, walled with almond paste, crowned at its summit with a gilt paper crown sitting upon a wreath of sugar bay-leaves, and stuck all about with figures: a sugar king and a sugar queen, a fiddler, a dancing bear, a Grenadier, a milkmaid, a swan with a green ribbon neck, and one small sugar baker in a sugar apron, holding a loaf the size of a caraway seed. Forty-six years running there had been a great cake in Pridd's window on Twelfth Night. The first had been Ezra Pridd's, in the year One, made to spite Napoleon and dazzle Cheapside; the crowds had stood six deep, and boys had been pinned to the window-boards by their coat-skirts by other boys, that being the traditional wit of the season, and a Lord Mayor's footman had come down with a card and carried away the top tier for the Mansion House. Every year since, war or peace, dear flour or cheap, there had been a cake, and children at the glass with their breath making fog-roses on it, and their mothers behind them saying *come away now* in voices that meant *look a little longer*.

This year there had been no order for it. Who orders a Twelfth cake from a shop that is shut by Epiphany? The sensible thing had been to make no cake at all, and Pridd and Barney had made it anyway, the two of them, over three days, in a shop already sold — the fruit picked over by candlelight, the almonds pounded, the icing floated on and combed and left to set in the cold like ice on a pond — because there are things a man does sensibly and things a man does because he is what he is, and Josiah Pridd was a baker of Bread Street ward, third of his name, and the window of Pridd's did not go dark on Twelfth Night while he held the lease. It stood there now, lit by the two best lamps, worth eleven guineas of anybody's money and ordered by nobody, the grandest cake in the City of London, in the window of a shop that was already, in every way that signified to lawyers, dead.

"Who's to eat it?" Barney had asked, on the first day, up to his elbows in currants.

"That," Pridd had said, "is the cake's affair."

Now the boy came and stood by him in the doorway between shop and bakehouse, and they watched, together, past the great white shoulder of the cake, as the first of the snow came down Pannier Court — a few flakes, sauntering, insolent, like the advance men of a very large procession. The lamplighter had been and gone; the two gas lamps at the court's mouth wore little wavering haloes. Somewhere toward the river a church clock — St. Nicholas Cole Abbey, by the sound of it, which Pridd's father had always called the baker's church, Saint Nicholas being in the trade himself, in a manner of speaking — let go of five strokes, and the wind took each one away in a different direction.

"Mr. Pridd," said Barney, "what happens tomorrow?"

Pridd was some time answering. In the window, the sugar Grenadier presented arms at the weather. The gilt crown atop the cake caught the lamplight and gave it gently back, the way gilt paper will, as if to say: *I am not gold, but I am not nothing, either.*

"Tomorrow," said Pridd, "is Twelfth Day, and we are Twelfth Night. We'll let tonight come first."

And the snow, as if it had been waiting all along for some such invitation, stopped sauntering, and set to work.

By six it lay ankle-deep the length of Bread Street, and Cheapside had gone the colour of unbleached flour. By half past, the omnibuses had given it up; the last of them went by the end of the court with its horses steaming like puddings and its conductor calling something about Holborn Hill that was clearly a lie. The snow came down not in flakes now but in goose-feathers, in handfuls, in whole pillowfuls, with the businesslike air of weather that has been ordered in quantity and means to deliver. Wheels ceased. Hooves ceased. Footsteps lasted a little longer, hurrying, and then they ceased too, and there was only the hissing of gas, and the bells of the City counting the quarters to one another over the roofs, further apart in the snow than they had ever sounded, like lighthouses.

Josiah Pridd, sixty-three years old and master of nothing after Lady Day, stood in his lit shop behind his last great cake and watched his street disappear. And because he was watching — because on any other Twelfth Night he would have been ten deep in customers and seen nothing of the weather until it was on his own doorstep — he saw the small figure come up the middle of Bread Street, where the drifts were shallowest: a boy, a broom over his shoulder like a musket, stopping at the mouth of the court to stamp, and beat his cap against his knee, and look — as everything in that street had looked, every January for forty-six years — at the lit window, and the cake.

Pridd went to the door and opened it. The cold came in like a creditor.

"You're Kit," he said. "Watling Street corner. You swept for me Michaelmas quarter, when the mud was bad."

"Yes, sir," said the boy, through the chatter of his own teeth. "Only there's no crossings left, sir. It's all one." He looked past Pridd, helpless, at the window. "I weren't begging, sir. I were only looking at it. Same as ever year."

"Same as every year," said Pridd, and stood aside, and held the door wide, and in that moment — though he did not know it yet, and would not have believed you if you had told him — the last night of Pridd's bakery began. "Come in off my step, then, before you spoil it. Mind the mat. There's the stove in the back still warm, and Barney'll find you the heel of a loaf."

Kit came in, snow and broom and all. The door shut behind him with its little familiar chime.

Out in the whitening dark, the bells of the City struck seven, and did not know it, but they were ringing the feast in.

# Chapter Two — The Weather's Opinion

The second knock came at a quarter past seven, and it was a large, official-sounding knock, the knock of a man accustomed to doors being answered promptly and warmly, and it belonged to Alderman Pettifer.

Alderman Pettifer of Bread Street ward was built on the lines of the monument in his own favourite corner of the Guildhall — broad in the base, imposing about the middle, and surmounted by a face which, at this moment, resembled a boiled ham that had lost its way. He had dined early with the Cordwainers and been bound for a second engagement of a turtle-soup character in Broad Street, and his coachman, after certain adventures by Bow Church which the Alderman did not intend to relate and never afterwards did, had put it to him respectfully that the horses could go no further, and that His Worship might do worse than wait out the storm somewhere with a fire.

"Pridd," said the Alderman, shedding snow all over the good mat like a shaken counterpane, "your window is lit."

"It is, Alderman."

"You've been sold. It's all over the ward. Signed this very day, I heard."

"You heard right," said Pridd. "The papers are signed and the ovens are going cold. The window is lit because it is Twelfth Night, and it is my window until Lady Day."

The Alderman looked at him a moment, and something surfaced in the boiled ham that had known Pridd's shop, man and boy, for fifty years, and had once, in a plush childhood he mentioned to nobody, been lifted up by a nursemaid to see the cake, the year of Trafalgar, when the sugar figures had all been little ships.

"Quite right," he said, in a different voice. "Quite right. Is that stove I smell?"

"It is. And you'll take a chair by it, Alderman, for nobody's going to Broad Street tonight, nor anywhere else."

That was the beginning of the flood — for a lit window on a night when every other window is going dark is a lighthouse, whatever it sells; and Pridd's stood at the foot of the court like a lamp at the end of a mine, with its cake shining in it. The city emptied itself of its last stranded souls, and the snow herded them, as a dog herds, without malice and without argument, toward the light.

There came Joseph Huggins, hackney coachman, badge number 1109, who had got his cab as far as the Swan yard and his horse Duchess into a stall of that establishment, and himself no further than Pridd's doorstep, where he stood apologising for the state of his boots for fully a minute before anyone could persuade him over the sill. There came old Mrs. Gamage, who had been at a granddaughter's in Milk Street since Sunday and had chosen — against counsel, as she cheerfully admitted — this evening of all evenings to walk home to Aldersgate, because Twelfth Night at other people's firesides made her miss her Samuel more than the whole rest of the calendar together. She came in small and upright and frosted like the cake itself, and when she had been put in the warmest chair and given the first of the tea, she looked a long time at the window from the inside, and said, to nobody in particular:

"Ezra Pridd made my wedding cake. Twelfth Night, the year one-thousand-eight-hundred. Snowing then, too. There was a boy here carried it to the church for us on his head, through the weather, and wouldn't take a penny."

"His father wouldn't let him take the penny," said Pridd, setting the kettle back. "His father held that a wedding cake carried for luck must be carried free, or the luck stays in the shop." He was quiet a moment. "It was a good cake. Saffron in the crumb. He let me stone the fruit."

Mrs. Gamage looked at him — at the grey in him, and the flour that never quite leaves a baker's forearms, and the sixty-three years of him — and she smiled like a girl looking through a church door.

"It was you," she said. "You've snow on you still, I declare."

By eight o'clock the shop held nine souls and the stove had been fed to roaring, and Barney had been three times down the cellar for coals, taking a candle and coming back each time a little slower, because a cellar you have raced up and down four years of mornings becomes, on the last night, a place a boy says goodbye to in instalments.

The nine were these: Kit, thawed now, and installed by the stove with the heel of a quartern loaf and the entire, devout attention of a person eating; the Alderman, filling the best chair and beginning, under the influence of heat, to give off steam and opinions in about equal volume; Huggins the coachman, who would not sit down, but stood holding his tea like the reins of it, respectfully, near the door; Mrs. Gamage; a young clergyman, the Reverend Decimus Snell, curate, bound for his first parish in Kent by a coach that assuredly was not running, who had knocked in a flurry of apology and asked, could he just consult somebody's clock — and had been inside and holding buttered toast before he could finish constructing his sentence, and looked as though nothing of the kind had ever happened to him before, and kept glancing about himself like a man taking notes for a sermon he did not yet know he would give; a dressmaker's apprentice, Sally Sprat, aged fifteen, sent out at five o'clock — on such an evening! the room said it as one — with a banded box containing a ball-dress for a house in Bow Lane whose knocker nobody could find in the drifts, and who had cried a little with the cold when she first came in, and was now quite recovered and holding the box on her knees like a lapdog; a one-armed old soldier, Sergeant Culpepper, late of the 45th, who had said, "By your leave, master, I'll stand at the warm end," and taken up a position by the bakehouse door as if the stove were regimental colours; and — arrived within a minute of one another, off the same drift, shaking the same weather from their shoulders — a large, mild, sad-eyed foreign gentleman in a comforter, who bowed and named himself Tobias Finck, sugar-baker, of Whitechapel; and a small pen-worn person in black, of the sort the City breeds by the gross and never looks at twice, who gave his name in a voice like a dry nib as Nib — "Elias Nib, sir, of Gudgeon & Sharp's — that is — of Old Jewry — a law-stationer's writer, sir, nothing more" — and then appeared to wish he had said none of it, and sat down in the darkest corner available, and looked at the floor.

Pridd stood behind his own counter and surveyed his shop — his sold shop, his shop full of firelight and steam and other people's evenings — with an expression Barney could not read, though the boy tried.

"Mr. Pridd," Barney whispered, on his way past with the coal, "it's a good job we baked, after all."

"It is always a good job we baked," said Pridd. "Mind your sparks."

And the snow came down. It came down as if it had heard the shop was closing and meant to buy everything left; it packed Pannier Court to the second window-boards; it silenced Cheapside from the Poultry to St. Paul's; it lay on the dome of the cathedral itself like icing let down from heaven on a string. And it drove before it, up the middle of a Bread Street that no longer had any edges, the last three guests: a lady in a plaid travelling-cloak over what appeared, at the hem, to be the costume of a fairy queen; a young woman carrying a carpet-bag, walking not like the lost but like the arriving, who stopped at the window and set her bare hand flat against the glass; and, some distance behind her, picking his way with a stick and a lantern gone out, a spare, upright, grey gentleman in a great-coat of the first quality, who looked up at the sign over the door for a long moment — PRIDD'S. ESTABLISHED 1762 — with the snow settling on his shoulders, in the attitude less of a man seeking shelter than of a man verifying an entry in a ledger.

The chime went. The door opened. The cold came in, and the last of them with it.

"Room by the stove," called Pridd, over the general shuffle. "Barney, the kettle. Ladies first, and the gentleman shut the door, if you'd be so kind — the weather's had my flour, it shan't have my warmth too."

The grey gentleman shut the door. Asked his name — the Alderman did the asking; the Alderman always did the asking — he paused for the smallest slice of a second, no longer than it takes a clerk to turn a page.

"Cork," he said.

# Chapter Three — Twelve by Candlelight

It was Kit who did the counting. He did it from the top of the flour bin, where he had been perched out of the way of feet, going round the room with one grubby finger, lips moving, twice over to be sure, and announcing the result with the gravity of a returning officer.

"Twelve," he said. "There's twelve of us, not counting Mr. Pridd nor Barney, on account of it's their house. Twelve strangers."

"Not strangers now," said Mrs. Gamage comfortably. "You can't be strangers past nine o'clock. It isn't done."

"Twelve," repeated the Reverend Mr. Snell, half to himself, in the tone of a man to whom the number suggested several improving parallels, all of which, being shy, he decided to keep. But he took out a little memorandum-book, and wrote in it, and blushed when he saw Sally watching, and put it away.

The lady in the travelling-cloak had proved to be Mrs. Fan Tarleton, of the Theatre Royal, Drury Lane — "of the pantomime, my dears, this fortnight past and five weeks yet to come, if the snow spares us" — driven into the City that afternoon to a Cheapside dressmaker about repairs to the fairy queen's train, in which garment's lower portions she was even now obliged to appear, the dressmaker having kept the skirt. She had a laugh that took the whole room in, a way of standing that made the counter a proscenium, and a flask of something restorative in her muff which she offered round with perfect impartiality, beginning with Mrs. Gamage, who took some.

The young woman with the carpet-bag gave her name quietly: Comfort Pell, of Rochester. She said very little else. She had chosen, of all the seats in the shop, the plain stool at the counter's end nearest the bakehouse door, where the old warmth of the ovens still breathed through; and she sat there with her bag at her feet and her hands folded, and watched the room — not unkindly, not shyly either; the way, though nobody present could have named it, a baker watches an oven through the flue-glass. When Barney staggered past with the coal-scuttle, she rose without a word, took its other handle, and shared the weight to the stove as one who had carried scuttles all her life; and when the tea went round again she somehow had the cutting of the bread and butter, and the slices came off her knife so even you could have sold them by weight.

"You've a trade, Miss Pell," said Pridd, watching the knife. It was not a question.

"My father was a baker," she said. "Noah Pell, of Rochester. The Golden Sheaf, by the bridge." A small pause, exactly the length of a grave. "Until Michaelmas."

"Ah," said Pridd. And then, because bakers say things to one another in loaves and silences that other trades say in letters of condolence: "The Golden Sheaf. I knew the sign. There was a twist they made there, a saffron twist, came up to the Maidstone show one year and took the ribbon from better men."

"From bigger men," said Comfort Pell, and the ghost of her father went round the ring of firelight, bowing.

Herr Finck, meanwhile, had gravitated to the window as a compass needle gravitates north, and stood before the great cake with his hands clasped behind him, in the unmistakable attitude — Mrs. Tarleton recognised it at once, professionally — of an artist in front of somebody else's masterpiece: one part homage, one part inventory. He was heard to murmur to himself in two languages. At last he turned.

"Sir," he said to Pridd, with a small bow, "the swan. The swan with the green ribbon. That is a Kummel mould, or I am a pastrycook." He said *pastrycook* the way a frigate captain might say *ferryman*.

"It is," said Pridd, surprised. "Old Herr Kummel of Goodman's Fields supplied my father thirty years, and me after him, till he died. The swan, the Grenadier, and the crown — the gilt crown's his too; the wreath under it. I've kept his moulds in use every Twelfth Night since. There's no better work in London, though I've had to do the casting myself these last years, and he'd box my ears for my seams."

"He boxed mine for the same," said Tobias Finck. "I was his apprentice, sir. I buried him. I have his other moulds in Whitechapel, and his lease, and his debts, and his name over my door in letters I cannot yet make myself paint out." He looked back at the cake, and his sad eyes shone in the lamplight. "I came tonight through your City on the way from a customer who did not pay, in weather that wished me dead, and I stop at a lit window, and there is my master's swan, swimming. Sir, I will shake your hand."

They shook hands, the baker and the sugar-baker, solemnly, across the counter, as across a border; and nobody in the shop understood precisely what had been transacted, except possibly Comfort Pell, but everybody felt the warmer for it.

It was the Alderman — give the Alderman his due; the night would owe him this much — who said the necessary thing.

The clocks had gone nine. The tea was three rounds old; the bread and butter had fallen back before organised appetite like infantry before cavalry; Kit had been fed twice and was working on diplomatic relations toward a third; and the stove, mighty as it was, had begun to lose ground against fourteen souls' worth of thawing boots and the draught under the shop door. Mrs. Gamage had her shawl up round her ears. The snow outside had reached the first window-boards and was auditing the second.

"Pridd," said Alderman Pettifer, "this won't answer. We're fourteen in a shop built for passing trade, half of us wet through, and the fire's fighting the door. You've a bakehouse back there with an oven in it drew its fire only this afternoon, if my nose is any judge, and a bakehouse oven holds its warmth the way the Bank holds bullion. I've sat on committees, sir, that reasoned worse than that. I move we adjourn."

For a moment Josiah Pridd stood quite still. The bakehouse was his own place as no other spot on earth was — the room he had been born over, the room his father had died over, the room whose ovens he had drawn that afternoon with his own hands, closing the iron door on forty years of mornings — and to open it now to a room full of strangers was to let the world walk on a grave not one day old. Barney, who understood perhaps a third of this and felt all of it, held his breath.

Then Pridd looked round them: at Kit's blue lips gone pink; at Mrs. Gamage small in her shawl; at Sally hugging her band-box; at Miss Pell sitting where the oven's breath came through the door, as near the warmth of the trade as the shop would let her.

"My father," he said, "held that a bakehouse is like a pulpit: nobody idle in it, and nobody cold near it. Barney — lamps. Sergeant, if you'd bring the coal. Alderman, your committee may carry its own chairs. Ladies and gentlemen —" and he opened the bakehouse door, and the deep, dry, cliff-face warmth of the great oven rolled out over them all like a benediction — "walk in. Mind the trough. Welcome to the back of the shop."

They filed through, twelve strangers and a boy and an Alderman's chair, into the long brick room with its scoured tables and its hanging peels and its oven like a sleeping whale; and the lamplight ran along the old wood, and the warmth folded them in, and Mrs. Tarleton, entering last but one, stopped on the threshold and said, in a voice that filled the room to its corners without once being loud, "Oh, my dears. It's better than the theatre."

Last of all came the grey gentleman who had given his name as Cork. He paused in the doorway; he looked down the bakehouse — at the oven, at the peels, at the flour board with its chalked date and its empty order column, at all of it — with that same air of a man verifying a ledger; and something crossed his face that no one saw except Elias Nib, who had been all evening in his corner trying not to look at him, and looking at him, and trying not to, like a man with a sore tooth and a tongue.

Mr. Nib turned quite pale, and found a seat in the new room as far from the gentleman as its geography allowed.

# Chapter Four — The Cake in the Window

It will have been observed by persons of experience that a room containing fourteen people, a warm oven, and no possible means of departure before morning will, by ten o'clock, arrive at one of two conditions: quarrelling, or telling stories. Which of the two it shall be is generally decided by whether anybody feeds it.

Pridd fed it. He went round the bakehouse shelves by lamplight — the last shelves of a shop sold over his head — and he set out on the great scoured table everything the trade had left him: a basket of Twelfth-buns, spiced and sugared, baked two days since for a custom that the weather had now cancelled; a crock of butter; a jar of honey; the end of a Stilton cheese sent at Christmas by his daughter Susan's husband, the corn factor at Ipswich; a string of figs; a paper of almonds; and, going down himself to the cellar and coming up with cobwebs on his shoulders and a look Barney had never seen on him, two bottles of a cordial his wife had made — elder and spice — the autumn before she died, which he had been keeping, he discovered as his hand closed on them in the dark, for absolutely nothing at all.

Sergeant Culpepper mulled ale at the stove-plate with his one hand and a soldier's patience, grating in nutmeg from Mrs. Gamage's own pocket-grater — she was of a generation that no more went abroad without a nutmeg than without a prayer-book — and dropping in roasted apples till the pot wore a lamb's-wool collar and the whole room smelled like the inside of a carol. Sally handed cups. Kit was made lamp-trimmer and took the office seriously. Mrs. Tarleton, discovering the bakehouse's acoustics, gave them the fairy queen's speech from the pantomime with a peel for a sceptre, and then, because Mrs. Gamage asked, something older and softer that had been sung when Mrs. Gamage was married, and the old lady kept time on the table-edge with two fingers, forty-seven years away.

And the talk, as it will around a scoured table when the wind has the streets, came home by and by to the thing standing lit in the window at the front of the shop, whiter than the weather: the cake.

"What I say," said the Alderman, who had dined, but for whom dinner was less an event than a condition, "is that it is a monstrous fine cake, Pridd, the finest since the year of the Coronation cake, which I judged, sir, at this very window, in my capacity — and I ask, as I have asked in the Court of Common Council on questions of graver public moment but never, I confess, of keener interest: *what is to be done with it?*"

"It's ordered by nobody," said Pridd, "and owed to nobody. It stands where it stands because it's Twelfth Night, and this house puts a cake in that window on Twelfth Night. Tomorrow —" he stopped; and the room heard the sentence he did not finish, which was: *tomorrow there is no this house.*

"Begging pardon," said Kit, from among the lamps, in the ringing treble of a person who has not yet learned that some questions are not asked, "but ain't anyone going to EAT it?"

There was a silence.

"Kit," said Sally, scandalised, "you can't eat a cake like that. It's — it's like eating the Lord Mayor's Show."

"You can, though," said Kit doggedly. "That's what it's FOR. I've watched that window ever year since I can remember anything, and I always says to myself, that's the realest thing in London, that is, on account of grand as it looks, it's dinner underneath. It ain't a monument. It's dinner in its Sunday best. And it's got one night left same as the shop has, and seems to me —" his courage ran out all at once, as boys' courage does, with the whole room looking at him — "seems to me it oughtn't to be wasted, is all," he finished, very small, and became extremely busy with a lamp-wick.

"The child," said Mrs. Tarleton, "has delivered the prologue. I could not have placed it better myself."

But Pridd had turned away, to the oven, and put his broad hand flat against the warm brick of it, as a man puts his hand on a horse that is being talked about; and when he spoke it was to the oven as much as the room.

"You'll think it strange in a tradesman," he said, "to bake a thing and grudge the eating of it. I don't grudge it. Only — while that cake stands in the window, this is Pridd's, and it's Twelfth Night, and nothing's ended yet. The minute it's cut —" his hand moved a little on the brick — "the minute it's cut, it's the last cake, and eaten, and swept up, and the shop's a corner property in Bread Street ward, vacant possession at Lady Day. A man wants one more night where nothing's ended yet. That's the whole of it. It's not sense. I never said it was sense."

Nobody answered him directly, because there was nothing in it to answer; it was as watertight as grief ever is. It was Comfort Pell who spoke at last, and she did not answer him either. She had gone, while the others watched Pridd, to the doorway between bakehouse and shop, and was looking down the length of the counter at the back of the great cake where it stood against the whitened glass — the unseen side, the baker's side, the side with the little door in the third tier's ornament where the trials of the icing had been made.

"Mr. Pridd," she said. "Who is it for?"

"I told you. There was no order. It's for nobody."

"No," said Comfort Pell, turning round, "that's who was ordered to pay for it. I asked who it's *for*." She came back into the lamplight, and her voice was still quiet, but it had a baker's-daughter certainty in it, learned at a shop door on winter mornings, that held the room like the warm brick held its heat. "My father made a great cake every Twelfth Night at the Golden Sheaf, in a town where half the trade thought a fancy window was above a bridge-end bakery, and every year the same men asked him what it cost and what it fetched, and every year he told them it fetched nothing and cost a fortnight, and made it again. I asked him why, the year I was tall enough to comb the icing. He said: *Comfort, three hundred and sixty-four days a year, this shop feeds the town its bread. One day a year, it feeds them something they'll stand in the snow to look at. The cake's not for the customers. The cake is the shop, saying what it thinks bread is.*" She looked at the window, and back at Pridd. "Your shop's been saying it forty-six years, and it's said it once more with the fire drawn and the papers signed, and I walked from the Borough through this snow tonight because I heard, in a bakehouse where I was being told no for the eleventh time, that Pridd's of Pannier Court had its cake in the window though the house was sold — and I wanted to stand in front of one shop in London that talked that way, once, before I went home again." She stopped; she had surprised the colour into her own cheeks; but she did not sit down. "You asked what trade I'm of, and I told you my father's. I'm of the same. Eleven London houses this fortnight have told me a bakehouse is no place for a woman to be master in, and I don't notice the trade's opinion of me keeps the snow off. So I'll say the rest. A cake in a window is the shop speaking. A cake on the table is the shop *feeding people*. And the second is the honester sentence, and you know it, Mr. Pridd, and your father knew it, and mine — and if this house has one night left, I'd sooner it ended feeding fourteen people snowed out of their lives than standing grand and hungry in a window with nothing ended yet. Nothing ended is nothing finished. There's a difference between keeping a thing and refusing to give it."

The lamp-flames stood up straight in the still air. Barney was looking from her to his master with his mouth open. Old Mrs. Gamage said, "Amen," placidly, as at church.

Josiah Pridd looked at the last apprentice of the Golden Sheaf for a long moment, across his scoured table, across the whole sold kingdom of his bakehouse; and those nearest him said afterwards that what came into his face was not surrender, and not grief either, but something that had been out of it since three o'clock that afternoon, in a lawyer's office in Old Jewry: interest.

"Barney," he said. "Fetch the bean."

# Chapter Five — The Rule of the Bean

The bean lived in a twist of paper in the spice drawer, as it had lived every January since the spice drawer was built, and Barney brought it to the table as if it were the Coronation regalia, which, in the only kingdom any of them would ever be offered, it was.

It was a dried bean, of the broad sort; brown, wrinkled, entirely unremarkable; the last of a line of beans stretching back past Obadiah Pridd into a history no one had kept. Pridd took it and stood it on end on the table, and the whole company gathered round it in the lamplight as round something that might go off.

"Now," said Pridd. "If it's to be done, it's to be done properly, by the whole custom, as this house has always done it — for them that only know the cake from the window side, hear the rules. In the crumb of every true Twelfth cake there is hid one bean, and one pea. The cake is cut and handed round, every soul present to a slice, no soul refusing. Whoever finds the bean is king of the feast. Whoever finds the pea is queen. And from the finding until the last candle goes out, the king is king and the queen is queen — apprentice or alderman, no appeals. The king's word runs in this room like the Queen's in her three kingdoms, God save her. Rank goes in the pot. Age goes in the pot. The king may command any man to sing, any man to answer, any man to hold his tongue; and the meanest soul in the room, if the bean comes to them, shall be served first, sat highest, and obeyed. That's the whole law, and there's no court above it till the candles fail."

"It's not canonical," murmured the Reverend Mr. Snell, with his eyes shining, "but I believe it may be sound."

"It is the world turned upside down, sir," said Mrs. Tarleton. "One night in the year the world agrees to turn upside down, and this is the night, and quite right too. In the theatre we keep the arrangement all year round, and I recommend it."

"And mark this," said Pridd, and his eye went, not by accident, from Kit on his flour bin to the Alderman in his chair, "there's no telling where the bean goes. My father put it in with a blindfold on, so help me — took his oath every year he couldn't say within a tier. The bean turns up where it likes. It has crowned a sweep before now in this very house, in my grandfather's day, and the sweep sat where the Alderman's sitting, and was called Majesty till two in the morning by a table that included, I have heard, a Baronet." He picked the bean up. "That's the custom. Is it the company's pleasure?"

It was the company's pleasure. It was the company's pleasure so loudly that the pans rang.

"Then," said Pridd, "there's one office to fill and one thing wanting. The cake was made blind — bean and pea went in with the batter, this year as every year, and where they lie no man living knows, myself least of all. But the handing round must be blind too, that's the fairness of it — and by custom the youngest goes under the table, and as each slice is cut, the crier under the table calls out who it's for, sight unseen. Kit. You're youngest."

"I'm under the table," said Kit instantly, and was.

"And the thing wanting?" asked Mrs. Gamage.

"Why, the cake," said Pridd, and took up the two best lamps, and looked at his twelve snowbound guests with the ghost of the shopman's bow he had made across that counter for forty years. "It's in the window. Sergeant, Herr Finck, Mr. Huggins — it weighs what a christened child weighs, and the board's as wide as a door. Gently through, and against the drift under the sill. Ladies and gentlemen: Pridd's is open for business."

⁂

They went and fetched the cake, and it came in in procession, and no procession of the Lord Mayor's ever went better attended.

The Sergeant and Herr Finck bore the board between them, one-handed and two-handed, with Huggins walking backward before them calling the turns as if backing a pair through Temple Bar — "easy — easy — pillar on your left — well over, whoa" — and Barney went ahead with a lamp, and Mrs. Tarleton behind with the other held high, singing the coronation anthem from the pantomime, and the rest fell in behind Mrs. Tarleton because it was not in human nature not to. The great cake left the window where forty-six of its ancestors had stood, crossed its own dark shop past its own counter, and came in under the bakehouse lintel with two inches to spare on either hand, white and glittering, the gilt crown swaying at its summit; and as it passed the oven, Josiah Pridd — nobody saw it but Comfort Pell, and she never told anyone but her own apprentices, years after, at this same season, in this same room — touched the warm brick once, lightly, with the flat of his hand, the way you touch an old dog when company comes in, to say: *it's all right; it's friends.*

They set it down on the scoured table, in the middle of the lamplight, and for a moment the whole fourteen of them stood round it and nobody said anything at all.

From the window it had been grand. Here, close to, with the lamps on it, you could see what the grandness was made of: the comb-marks in the icing, drawn by a steady hand at the end of a sold day; the sugar figures with their tiny painted faces — the milkmaid's smile put on with a brush of three hairs; the swan's green ribbon; the fiddler mid-jig; the little sugar baker with his caraway loaf, standing at the foot of the whole three tiers as if he had built them. It was eleven guineas of skill and three days of a breaking heart, and it was, precisely as Kit had said, dinner in its Sunday best.

"Herr Finck," said Pridd. "The crown."

The sugar-baker lifted the gilt crown down from the summit — his dead master's crown, cast in his dead master's mould — straightened one point of it with a fingertip, and set it on the table, ready. Pridd took up the long knife.

"Majesty's under there somewhere," he said. "Let's have him out."

# Chapter Six — The Cutting

There is no suspense at the Old Bailey, none at the gaming table, none in the last furlong at Epsom, to compare with fourteen people watching a Twelfth cake being cut when nobody in the room knows where the bean is.

Pridd cut. The iced wall of the third tier came away before the knife with a crunch like frost underfoot, and the dark crumb underneath breathed out its cargo — fruit and brandy and forty-six Januaries — into the warm air, and Kit's voice came up hollow from under the table:

"Who's it for?"

"Call it, crier."

"Mrs. Gamage!" called Kit; and the first slice of the last cake of Pridd's went, on the best plate, to the oldest guest, which everyone present felt to be an excellent omen for the constitution of the coming reign.

"Who's this for?" — "The Sergeant!" — "And this?" — "Sally!" — "And this?" — "The reverend gentleman!" So it went round, blind and merry, the crier under the table calling names in an order known only to Providence and a nine-year-old: Mrs. Tarleton; Huggins; Herr Finck ("a thin slice, crier, I am judging the crumb" — "a THICK 'un for the sugar gentleman!"); the Alderman; Mr. Nib, who took his plate in both hands and looked as if he might cry; Comfort Pell; Barney — "the 'prentice next, on account of he stoned the fruit!" — and Pridd himself, commanded to a slice by a unanimous table and cutting it with a bow; and Kit's own slice passed down to him under the table, where he remained, on the constitutional principle that the crier's term ran till the finding; and last of all — "the grey gentleman by the door!" — Mr. Cork.

Mr. Cork had taken no part in the procession and little in the general joy. He had stood a pace outside the lamplight all this while with his hands behind him, watching — watching the cake, the room, the faces round the table, and most of all Pridd, with an attention there was no name for on this side of the ledger. Called by the crier, he hesitated — the second time that evening the room had seen him hesitate over a small thing, though only Mr. Nib, pale in his corner, seemed to find the hesitations interesting. Then he stepped in, and took the plate that was handed him, and said, "I thank you," with the accent of a man thanking a Court for its judgment.

"All served," reported the Alderman, who had constituted himself teller. "Fourteen slices, no soul refusing. Crier, you may—"

"Not yet," said fourteen-year-old constitutional scholarship from under the table. "Everyone EATS. That's the finding."

They ate. Reader, wherever this finds you, and in whatever January: they ate the last great cake of Pridd's of Pannier Court to the sound of the snow ticking on the shutters and the oven breathing behind them, and it was — the point was settled at once, unanimously, across every rank and calling at that table — the best Twelfth cake any of them had eaten in their lives. It was better than the Coronation cake. The Alderman said so, officially. The crumb was black with fruit and light as a psalm; the icing broke like the first step on new snow; the almond paste underneath was bitter-sweet, which Mrs. Tarleton observed was true of all the best things, last nights included —

"Oh!" said Mrs. Gamage.

The room stopped chewing.

But it was the pea. She held it up, between finger and thumb, delicately, like a pearl out of an oyster: the pea, the queen's pea, found in the first slice cut; and the bakehouse rose at her, cheering, while she sat pink and upright and seventy-four years old with her hand at her cap; and Mrs. Tarleton swept her a court curtsey that used every inch of the fairy queen's surviving train, and Herr Finck was discovered to be fashioning her a sceptre out of a peel-handle and a sugar bay-leaf, and Sally, at a whispered word from Comfort, was already twisting a paper coronet; for whatever else the night held, the queen at least the company had got right, and knew it, and the Sergeant proposed her health, "Queen Gamage — and the kingdom's luck to her," and it went round with a will.

"But the bean," said Kit sternly, from below. "The bean ain't found. Eat SLOWER."

They ate slower. Slice by slice the plates emptied; fork by fork the field narrowed; the Reverend Mr. Snell was seen to go through his crumbs with the thoroughness of a man who has decided that, doctrine or no doctrine, he would not in the least mind being king; the Alderman finished entirely, mourned publicly, and was consoled by the queen; Comfort Pell reached the end of her slice with steady hands and a face nobody could read, and folded her hands again; Pridd finished his own and stood — the room understood suddenly that he had been holding his breath over every plate, that some part of the man had wanted the bean the way a child wants it, one last crown under his own roof — and said, a little heavily, "Well. It goes where it likes, I told you—"

"Your pardon," said Mr. Cork.

He said it quietly, but the room turned as one head. The grey gentleman stood by the table with his plate in one hand, and in the other, held up to the lamplight between two precise fingers, wiped clean and unmistakable: the bean.

"I believe," said Mr. Cork, in a level voice, into the enormous silence, "that I must appeal."

"NO APPEALS!" roared the table, the Alderman loudest of all; and under it, unseen, the crier turned three somersaults against the trough; and Herr Finck was already coming round the table with the gilt crown in his two hands like a whole procession of Archbishops; and that is how, at twenty-one minutes before midnight on the fifth of January, 1847, in a bakehouse with its fire drawn and its papers signed, the bean came to the one man in fourteen who wanted no crown, no feast, no notice, and no questions — which is to say, exactly as the feast's whole history could have foretold, into the most inconvenient slice in London.

# Chapter Seven — The King of the Cold Ovens

They crowned him by the oven, because the queen said the throne should be the warm end, and the queen's word on matters of comfort was already law.

He stood very stiff under the gilt paper, a spare grey careful man with a crown askew on his grey head, looking as if he had been convicted of something; and the company, flushed with cake and lamb's-wool and the general rightness of the night, waited for their king with shining faces; and anyone but the fourteen of them could have told you it must all now go wrong, because of every soul the snow had herded down Bread Street, this was the one with winter in him.

"I am no hand at revels," said the king at last. "You'd have done better with the boy under the table."

"The bean don't make mistakes," said Kit, emerging. "That's the whole point of a bean."

"The finding's the crowning," agreed Pridd. "This house has never once known the bean to fall wrong, Mr. Cork, in forty-six years — though I'll grant it has taken till morning, some years, to see the sense of it." He said it lightly; but he looked at his guest as he said it, steadily, the way he looked at a proof of dough that was behaving oddly, and Mr. Cork looked back at him, and something passed between the two grey heads that neither could have written out.

"Very well," said the king. "If I must govern, I'll govern as I—" he stopped himself; the room did not know what word had been coming, but Mr. Nib, in his corner, seemed to; "—as the feast requires. What are my powers?"

"Anything, Majesty," said Mrs. Tarleton, "that keeps the room warm and nobody cruel. Command us. Songs, forfeits, questions, stories. The old kings," she added, with the guile of a woman who had played more thrones than most monarchs sit on, "always began with the stories. Round the table. Everyone pays the feast a story, and the king pays last."

The king considered his kingdom: twelve strangers, a baker, a boy, a warm oven, four candles now to eke the lamps, and the snow at the shutters, ticking.

"Then that is my decree," he said. "Every subject at this table shall state — shall tell —" and here the careful voice picked its way, like a man crossing ice, "—what the snow interrupted. What you were bound for, when the weather brought you here instead. Truthfully told. That's the forfeit and the fee." He paused, and added, dry as sand, "Reserving my own account till last, by Madam's rules."

It was, the table agreed afterwards, a strange first decree, and none of them could have said quite why it worked; except that perhaps there is no faster way through fourteen sets of company manners than to ask everyone, at midnight, in the middle of a snowstorm, where they were really going.

⁂

Huggins was bound home to Lambeth, where his wife would have long since stopped supposing him frozen and started supposing him at the Swan, "and the bitter part, Majesty, is that for once I am at the Swan — leastways Duchess is — and I shall never make her believe it, not if the whole company signs a paper." The Sergeant was bound nowhere: he said it plainly, without bid for pity, a man whose lodging went by the week and whose week had run out that morning; he had been walking to keep warm until somewhere opened, "and this establishment, Majesty, opened." (The queen here decreed him the chair nearest her own, and the matter of the Sergeant's lodging was observed by the table to pass into the queen's particular notice, where, it may be said at once, it did not remain unattended to.) Sally had been bound for Bow Lane with the ball-dress, and her terror — out it came, at midnight, to a room of strangers, as it had not come out to anyone in her fortnight of dreading it — was that the house would say the delay was her doing and her mistress would turn her off; whereupon the Alderman took a card from his case and said that if any householder in Bow Lane wished to dispute the evidence of an Alderman of the ward as to the state of the streets on the night of January the fifth, they were welcome to call at the Guildhall and try; and Sally held the card all the rest of the night in her glove, like a talisman, which as near as anything that evening made the Alderman worth his chain.

The Reverend Mr. Snell was bound for Kent, a parish called Underhanger, his first; he confessed — the cordial may have assisted; the room had reached the confiding hour — that he had been dreading it beyond measure, being no preacher, and that his trunk held forty-one attempts at a first sermon, on subjects he now saw were all of them alibis; "though I begin to think," he said, looking round the bakehouse, at the crowned clerk and the pea-queen and the cabman's empty cup, "that the sermon may have been trying to reach *me*, and required only sufficient snow." Mrs. Tarleton was bound for Drury Lane and half-past-six tomorrow, snow or none — "the pantomime, Majesty, has never once in history been cancelled; the audience arrives on the ice like the Russian court; if I am not through this drift by four tomorrow I shall be replaced by Miss Fanshawe, in MY train, and the thought will keep me marvellous company till the thaw." Herr Finck had been bound for Whitechapel from a customer who had taken delivery of two gross of sugar swans for a Twelfth-night rout in Portman Square and then gone out of town leaving no instructions and no payment; he told it mildly, shrugging, an artist's account of the world's arithmetic; "and then I am standing in your Bread Street, Majesty, with the snow going down my neck, thinking: Tobias, the trade is dead, the master is dead, the swans are not paid for — and I look up, and there in a lit window is the master's swan, swimming. A man cannot be bound anywhere, after that. He is arrived."

Kit, asked where the snow had been taking him, said, "Nowhere. I sleeps where it's dry," with the simplicity of a fact long past feeling; and the bakehouse went quiet for a moment, and the king looked at the boy with an attention he had given nothing else that night, and wrote something — the whole table saw him do it, king or no king — in a small black memorandum-book he took from his waistcoat, and put away again without one word of explanation.

And Comfort Pell told her story last but one — the eleventh; the king's made twelve. She told it as she did everything, plainly and without discount: the Golden Sheaf; her father dead at Michaelmas and the lease sold up; eleven London bakehouses in fourteen days, and the same sentence in eleven mouths, some kind, some not: *we don't take women to the ovens.* Her money running out at a lodging in the Borough; her mother's sister at Norwich willing to take her in, to sew. "I was bound for the Norwich coach office, Majesty, in the morning, to book the Friday outside seat. That's what the snow interrupted. And I came the long way round, by Bread Street, to see this window before I gave it up — because a journeyman through Rochester in November told us Pridd's of Pannier Court was sold, and the master was baking his great cake regardless, and my father was three weeks dying and made me swear —" for the first time the plain voice stumbled, caught itself, went on level — "made me swear that if ever I was by Cheapside at Twelfthtide I'd see the last of the great windows while there was one to see. He held there were four bakers in England worth the flour they spoiled, and he'd only ever met the other three." She folded her hands. "That's my account, and the fee's paid."

In the silence, Josiah Pridd, who had not moved through the whole of it, said, "What was the fourth name, Miss Pell?"

"You know the name," said Comfort Pell. "You've had his twist beaten by better men, I think you said. Bigger, I said." And she looked at him with her father's whole unbeaten pride in her face, and the table laughed and ached at once, and Mrs. Tarleton blew her an actual kiss, and the last candle but three stood up straight; and the king of the feast, alone of the company, did not laugh: he sat under his gilt crown looking from Comfort Pell to Josiah Pridd and down the warm brick length of the bakehouse — verifying, verifying — like a man finding, at the foot of a column of figures he had added a hundred times, a number that would not come out the same twice.

"Majesty," said the queen. "Eleven paid. Yours is owing."

"Mine is owing," agreed the king slowly, "and Madam shall have it. But I find —" he set down his cup; and Elias Nib, in his corner, went the colour of his own paper — "I find I must open my account further back than the snow. It will not balance else."

# Chapter Eight — The Queen's Command

But it was not to be Mr. Nib whose conscience broke the evening open, after all; the poor pen-worn gentleman had been trembling on the brink of speech for an hour, rehearsing it — the company later agreed he had been three times within a syllable — and history was swifter.

The king rose. He took off the gilt crown — "By your leave; a man should give his account bareheaded" — and set it on the table by the wreck of the great cake; and standing there in the lamplight, grey and spare and precise, he gave the feast its twelfth story.

"My name is not Cork," he said. "It is Crail. Onesiphorus Crail, of Crail and Mowl, of Leadenhall Street. This afternoon, at three o'clock, in Old Jewry, my firm completed the purchase of a corner property in Bread Street ward: the house, shop, bakehouse, and goodwill of Pridd's, of Pannier Court. The snow interrupted me on my way to view it. I am the man who bought this bakery, and I have sat at its last table, and eaten its last cake, and — by the operation of a dried bean — I am its king. You will allow," said Mr. Onesiphorus Crail, into a silence like the middle of the Thames, "that the feast has a sense of humour."


Nobody allowed anything. The warmth went out of the room as if a door had been opened; and no door had been opened; that was the terrible economy of it. Sally's hand went to her mouth. Huggins put down his cup with the care of a man in a stranger's parlour. Barney had gone white to the ears, and then red, and stood gripping the peel-rack as if the peels might be wanted. And Josiah Pridd stood at the head of his own table with the light gone out of his face — not anger; the company could have borne anger; it was the shopman's look, the papers-and-lawyers look, the three-o'clock look, come back into a face the evening had spent five hours warming.

"You might have said," said Pridd at last, evenly, "before you ate at my table."

"I might," said Crail. "I ate at your table because your boy hauled me out of a drift by the arm and your house asked my name at the door and nothing else. I have taken bread tonight from a man whose bread I never paid for. You may weigh that as you please; I have been weighing it since seven o'clock." He stood very straight. "I'll say the rest plainly, for it will be said in this room tonight by somebody, and it had better be said by me. I did not come here to gloat, nor to spy; I did not know, when the snow put me through that door, that your fourteenth chair would crown me; and if any soul present thinks I contrived to draw the bean, I invite them to consider whether a man would conspire to be made king of the one room in London where his face is a confession." A wintry flicker, almost humour, almost. "The purchase is honest. The price was fair; Mr. Pridd will say so himself, for he drove it up. The sale papers he signed at three o'clock will hold in any court in England. Crail and Mowl wants the corner for offices; the trade here doesn't answer, hasn't answered these five years; the goodwill is worth more broken than standing. That is the account, and every figure in it is true." He stopped, and looked down the table at the ruin of the great cake, and his voice, for the first time that evening, missed its footing. "I had the figures by heart before tonight. What I did not have—"

"The FIGURES!" cried the Alderman, arriving at eruption. "Sir, I have sat across a Guildhall table from your firm, and I know your figures, and I tell you to your crowned head, sir, there are things in this ward older than your ledger—"

"Alderman," said the queen.

She did not say it loudly. But she was the queen; and she was, moreover, Mrs. Gamage, who had buried Samuel and two sons and kept Twelfth Night all the same for forty-seven years; and the Alderman, who was a blusterer but not a fool, sat down.

"Now," said Queen Gamage, and her small upright person gathered the whole guttering room to itself as a banked fire gathers a kitchen. "I've been queen one evening of my life, and I'll spend it as I see fit. Nobody leaves — there's weather on that point stronger than I am. And nobody's un-kinged, neither. The bean fell where it fell. If it's only kings we like that the crowning does us credit —" she looked round them all, mild as milk and immovable as the Monument — "then it's no custom at all, only flattery with currants in it. He's king till the candles fail. And king or no king," she went on, turning to Crail, folding her hands, "you sat down at this feast, sir, and the feast has rules. You've given us the figures. But your fee was the same as all the world's tonight: *truthfully told, what you were bound for.* And you were not bound for a ledger, Mr. Crail, at nine o'clock at night, on foot, in the worst snow since the old King, with your lantern out. You were bound *here.* And you've not yet said why."

The king looked at his queen for a long moment.

"No," he admitted. "I have not."

It was then that Mr. Elias Nib — who had been rising by fractions from his corner chair all through the queen's speech, like dough coming over the lip of a pan — went off at last, with a small explosive despair that startled Kit off the flour bin:

"He doesn't KNOW why! Your Majesty — Mr. Pridd — ladies and gentlemen — I beg — I must — I engrossed those papers! I, Elias Nib! Every line of that conveyance is in my hand, and a fair hand, though I say it — and I knew the gentleman the moment he crossed the shop, for I stood at the copying-desk not six feet off when he signed, at three o'clock, and never has a purchase-deed been signed so — so ANGRILY, in the whole history of Gudgeon and Sharp's! Gentlemen don't sign so! Not purchases! A man signs a purchase like his dinner, and he signed it like PHYSIC — and then, your Majesty, and then — I saw what none of you saw, for none of you was walking down Old Jewry at seven o'clock with a quire of fresh copies under his arm — I saw the purchaser of the corner property standing in Bread Street in a snowstorm, sir, in front of the very window he has bought the extinguishing of, looking at a Twelfth cake — LOOKING at it, in weather no dog would stand in — and I said to myself, Elias, there is a thing here that is not in the deed. And I followed him in. That's my true fee, and I'm sorry it's late, and my account at least is balanced, whatever his is!" — and Mr. Nib sat down as suddenly as he had risen, mopped his forehead with a piece of blotting-paper he appeared to keep for the purpose, and astonished himself by bursting into tears; whereat Sally, nearest, patted him on the shoulder, and the Sergeant wordlessly passed him the mulled ale; and the bakehouse, which ten minutes earlier had gone as cold as the shop, found to its own surprise that it was warm again — for there is nothing like the sight of the smallest man in the room spending his whole year's courage at one go to remind a company what the fire is for.

"Well, Majesty," said Josiah Pridd. And that he said *Majesty*, and said it without irony, the whole table heard, and the king heard it most of all. "The queen's asked her question. The clerk's laid the evidence. It's a poor king that's the only man in the room behind on his fees. What were you bound here for, tonight, on foot, alone, with the deed already signed and the corner already yours?"

"For the window," said Kit, from the flour bin, into the pause; not cheeky; the way a child says the answer because it is simply visible from where they sit. "Same as me."

The king of the feast looked at the crossing-sweeper for a moment that went on past all polite length; and then Mr. Onesiphorus Crail sat slowly down at the foot of the wrecked cake, under the lamp, and said:

"Yes. The same as you. Very well. The queen commands, and the fee is owing, and I'll pay it entire — but I open my account, as I said, further back than the snow. It will not balance else." He laid his hands flat on the scoured table, one on each side of nothing, like a man at a desk with no papers left to shuffle for the first time in forty years. "I must open it in the year 1801, in this street, at that window, on the coldest Twelfth Night of the century; when I was eleven years old; and hungrier, I believe, than anybody now under this roof has ever had the misfortune to be."

# Chapter Nine — The Account, Further Back

"My father was a corn-chandler's clerk in Honey Lane," said the king, "and he died in the October of 1800, of a low fever, owing eleven pounds; and my mother had died so long before that I had to be told which grave. I was taken in — that was the word used — by an uncle in the same lane, a careful man; I have been forty years grateful to him and I have never liked him; he fed me on the principle that a boy kept just hungry is a boy kept diligent. I ran his errands. I was quick at figures — hunger is a great tutor in arithmetic; you would be astonished what a boy can reckon in his head when the sum is his own dinner — and I minded, most of that winter, being cold, being small, and being nobody's.

"On the fifth of January, 1801, I was sent at dusk with a message to a warehouse by Queenhithe, and I came back the long way, because the long way came up Bread Street, and there was a rumour in Bread Street — boys' rumour, the swiftest post in Europe — that the baker at the corner of Pannier Court had gone mad, or come into a fortune, or both, and had built a cake in his window the size of St. Paul's. And there it was. The first great cake of Pridd's, in the first year of the century, with the lamps on it and the frost-ferns on the glass and half the boys in the ward six deep at the window, and I stood at the back of them in my father's cut-down coat, and I stared with the rest.

"I want to be accurate," said Mr. Crail, in the tone of a man who had never in his life wanted anything else, while the bakehouse sat around him still as a painting. "It is easy, at such a distance, to gild. I did not stand there having noble thoughts about beauty. I stood there in a rage. I looked at that cake — at the swans and the crowns and the almond wall of it, at more sugar in one window than I had tasted in my whole body since my father died — and I hated it, as only a starving child in front of plenty can hate; and I hated the boys who laughed at it because their laughing was easy and mine would not come; and I settled it with myself, at that window, on that night — I remember the resolution arriving; it arrived like a lamplighter; click, and something in me that has never gone out since — I settled it that I should never be hungry again, and that since the world was evidently a shop window with the likes of me on the pavement side of it, I would get to the other side of the glass by figures, and stay there, and let who would stand in the snow.

"You are waiting," he said, glancing round them, dry, "for the sugar-plum. Every tale told at this hour has one. Very well; it is coming; I have carried it forty-six years and never once spent it, and it is this. While I stood hating that window with my whole soul, the shop door opened, and a young man came out in an apron — seventeen, eighteen; flour to the elbows; I thought him a man grown and enviable beyond kings — and he had in his hand a sugar figure, a Grenadier, that had come out of its mould with a bad seam and a bent musket and could not go on the cake. And all those boys at the glass set up a clamour, *me, sir, me* — and the young man looked over their heads, straight at the back row, at the one boy who had not asked; and he tossed it over the lot of them, into my two hands; and he said — I give you his very words; I have had them by heart since before Trafalgar — he said, *'There. Now stop looking like that at my father's cake; it's Twelfth Night.'* And went in, and shut the door, and I make no doubt forgot the whole transaction before the latch fell."

Very slowly, Josiah Pridd sat down.

"I ate the musket in Honey Lane," said Mr. Crail, "and the rest I could not make myself eat; I kept it in my box, and I had it still, wrapped in my father's handkerchief, when I was articled to a shipping-clerk at fourteen; and I had it at twenty, when I kept another man's books in a cellar in Crutched Friars and taught myself conveyancing by candle-ends; and somewhere between thirty and now — I moved offices; I moved houses; I acquired partners, and a carriage, and seven clerks, and the freehold of the cellar I starved in, which I bought, gentlemen, I will confess it to you since tonight is apparently the night for it, purely in order to stand in it once with the deed in my pocket; and somewhere in all that removing, the Grenadier was lost. I did not notice the loss for some years. Then I minded it more than I have ever minded anything I could mention aloud at the Exchange.

"The rest is short," he said. "You will have guessed it; accounts one has guessed must still be rendered; that is what audit means. The corner of Pannier Court came to my firm's notice in the ordinary way of business, through the ward improvements — a sound frontage, a failing trade, an old man without successors. I saw the name on the papers. I told myself the name was a coincidence of no commercial significance, which was true, and I bought it, which was sound, and I signed the conveyance yesterday-was-it-or-this-afternoon —" for the first time the precise voice lost a figure, and let it lie — "angrily, your clerk says, and your clerk keeps a fair ledger. And at seven o'clock this evening, instead of dining, I found that I had walked, in the worst weather of my lifetime, to Bread Street; to see whether the window I had bought the extinguishing of would be lit; because if it was dark, you understand, then it was only a corner property after all, and the account was closed and clean.

"It was not dark," said Onesiphorus Crail. "It was lit, in a dead shop, in a snowstorm, for nobody — for the pure stubborn liturgy of the thing — and there was a cake in it the equal of 1801, and one small figure at the foot of it: a sugar baker, holding a loaf. And I stood on the pavement side of the glass, where I began; and I could not for my life recall what it was I had won by crossing it; and a boy with a broom went by me through the drifts as if he knew where warmth was, and I followed him. That is my fee, Madam, truthfully told: I was bound for this window, to see it dark. It declined. The rest the bean has done, which — I am informed —" and he looked at Kit, and the gilt crown, and the wreck of the cake, and his mouth achieved the whole of a smile for the first time in the company's experience of it — "makes no mistakes."

The candles had burned an inch in the telling; nobody had trimmed them; Kit, lamp-trimmer, had forgotten his office entire, and no one reproached him. It was Pridd who broke the hush, and his voice had sixty-three years in it, and all of them kind.

"A bad seam and a bent musket," he said. "I never knew a Kummel mould to throw a clean Grenadier in a hard frost. The sugar cools too quick at the musket; you must warm the mould first at the flue, and my father never would; he held that hurry was worse than waste." He looked at Crail across the table — across forty-six years, and eleven guineas of cake, and a signed conveyance — and shook his head slowly, wondering, unarmed. "I don't remember you. I'd be lying if I said otherwise, and this table's under oath tonight. I threw seconds to window-boys forty Twelfth Nights running; it was the shop's way; my father said the seconds paid the best interest in the trade. I don't remember one boy from another." He turned his hand over on the table, palm up, flour-grained, empty. "But it was never charity, Mr. Crail, if that's the debt that's chafed you all these years. You were owed. Every child at that glass was owed; that's what the window was FOR; that's what my father built it to say, in the only language he had that carried through glass. *This is yours too. Stand there as long as you like. It's Twelfth Night.*" He was quiet a moment. "You paid for the cake this afternoon, sir, papers and all. You're the one man in London the window was ever actually settled on. And you came through a snowstorm to see it, and you're king at its cutting."

"Yes," said Crail. "You perceive my difficulty. I have spent since seven o'clock discovering that I have bought the one property in England I cannot afford." He rose; he took up the gilt crown from the table where he had set it; he turned it in his hands, and the lamplight ran round the paper points of it. "Majesty is instructive," he said. "I recommend it to the Court of Aldermen. A king, I find, cannot hide behind his firm; there is no 'Crail and Mowl' at this table; there is one man in a paper crown who ate the bread. Madam Queen, my fee is paid. The night is yours till the candles fail — and the king," he set the crown back on his grey head, deliberately, like a man re-entering harness, "the king has accounts to think over. Sergeant, I observe the pot is dry, and I am given to understand my word is law: mull more."

# Chapter Ten — The Oven Is Not Cold

It was some while past two when Comfort Pell went and opened the oven door.

The feast had banked itself down by then, the way good fires and good evenings do: Mrs. Tarleton had sung the low song twice more by particular request of the throne; Sally slept against Mrs. Gamage's shoulder with the Alderman's card in her glove; the Alderman himself dozed upright with the majesty of a public monument; Kit lay curled on the warm flags by the oven with Pridd's coat over him, prizefighter's dreams chasing across his face; and the talk had gone quiet and gone deep, the way it does in the smallest hours, among the five or six still waking. Nobody saw Comfort go to the oven — nobody but Pridd, who saw everything in that room without looking, as he had for fifty years.

She swung the iron door and put her bare hand into the dark of it, flat, held off the brick, reading; and she stood that way a long moment, like a woman with her hand on a sleeping child's forehead; and then she said, quietly, but not only to herself:

"This oven isn't cold."

"It's drawn," said Barney, loyal, half-asleep on the sacks. "We drew it at four, before the snow. I raked it out myself."

"You drew the fire," said Comfort. "You didn't draw the heat. A brick soul like this — fired every day for eighty years, never once let go all the way down — it doesn't go cold because one afternoon's papers say so. It holds. My father's oven at the Sheaf held three days; this one's twice the mass of it." She turned round, and there was flour-dust on her palm, and her eyes had the flue-glass light in them. "Mr. Pridd, this oven is at falling bread-heat this minute, or within a faggot of it. It isn't cold. It won't be cold till Thursday." She let that stand in the warm dark, one beat, two. "And there are five sacks of sound flour in that bay Barney's sleeping on, and a jar of barm in the cellar I'd stake my apron is still lively, and fourteen people snowed into a bakehouse, and a street outside that will wake up hungry with the bakers of half the ward buried to the lintels. And I am so tired, sir," said Comfort Pell, in the very voice her father had used about the fancy window, the plain sentence with the whole vocation standing behind it, "of being told what's finished that isn't."

Pridd was silent for a moment. Then: "The sponge should have been set at nine," he said, but he said it the way a man raises the last difficulty he wants raised.

"For a full batch of the fine bread, yes. For a straight dough of household loaves, with lively barm and a warm room —" she looked at him, and permitted herself, at quarter past two in the morning of the last day, the first grin the company had seen from her, and it took ten years and the whole journey to Norwich off her face — "we'd have bread by seven. You know we would. Your window never once went dark on Twelfth Night, Mr. Pridd. Well — it's Twelfth *Day* coming, sir; Epiphany in the morning; and I never yet heard that the feast of the kings was kept by locking up the loaves."

Josiah Pridd looked at his drawn, warm, sold oven; and at the last apprentice of the Golden Sheaf standing in front of it with her sleeves already going up; and what he felt, those who watched him said afterwards, could be seen arriving in his face like morning coming up a street. Not the sadness leaving — the sadness had come to stay, and he knew it, and it belonged; you do not bake forty years in a room and walk out of it whole. But beside the sadness, room being made; the trade waking up in him one more time, unkillable, at the smell of a reason.

"Barney," he said, and his voice had the morning-voice in it, the four-o'clock bell in it, that Barney had thought drawn out with the fire; and the boy came off the sacks like a partridge. "Off the flour, boy, we're wanted. Miss Pell — Mistress Pell, I should say; you'll take the dough-end, and I'll mind the oven, and we'll see whose father taught whose trade. Sergeant, more coals to the copper, we'll want the water blood-warm. And someone wake the Alderman," said Josiah Pridd, master of Pridd's for one morning more, rolling his sleeves, "for I've waited thirty years to see a member of the Court of Common Council knead."

They baked. Reader, they all baked; and if you would know what the feast of the Epiphany was for, before the world mislaid the receipt, it was something very like what the snow saw when it pressed its face to the bakehouse skylight of Pridd's between three and seven on the morning of the sixth of January, 1847.

The queen would not be excused, and stood at the table-end with her rings off, blocking dough into loaf-shapes with hands that remembered a farm kitchen sixty years gone. The Alderman kneaded — kneaded well, to the unconcealed suspicion of all present, and confessed, sweating, chain laid by, that he had been prenticed to a Pudding Lane biscuit-baker two years before his uncle's money found him, "and I'll thank the company to let that go no further than the ward." Mrs. Tarleton floured loaves and the greater part of herself and declaimed the pantomime from the top of the flour bin. Huggins turned the crank of the dough-break as one born to shafts. Herr Finck, exalted past speech at the touch of warm sugar-work — for Pridd would have a Twelfth-Day something for the window, "the window's not dark till I say, and I don't say" — was casting the last of the almond paste in his dead master's swan mould, seams clean as scripture. The Reverend Mr. Snell had his coat off and his bands in his pocket and proved, on the second tray, a natural hand at moulding cottage loaves, and stood there joyful and floured to the eyebrows, composing aloud, between trays, without noticing he was doing it, the first sermon of the forty-two that had ever once looked like reaching Underhanger: on the loaves; and the feeding of the multitude; and how the miracle, look at it squarely, had required somebody's five loaves to begin with, brought and handed over; some boy's whole larder, given up to a crowd, on the mere unsecured suspicion that the world turned that way if you turned it. Mr. Nib, in paper cuffs of his own manufacture, was inscribing, at the king's dictation, in a fair engrossing hand, a document of which more hereafter. Sally slept on, under Pridd's coat now, and was let sleep — "she's fifteen," said the queen, "and the age for it" — and Kit ran everywhere, floured, ecstatic, officeless and indispensable, the sixth of January arriving in him like his own coronation.

And Onesiphorus Crail stood at the scoured table with his cuffs turned back and his crown on, kneading household bread; awkwardly; earnestly; a man of eight-and-fifty learning, at the far side of the glass at last, what the hands were for; and once, when the dough came alive under his palms the way it does, that first shocking time, like something consenting to be helped — those nearest heard the king of the feast laugh: two syllables, rusty, astonished, the laugh of a boy at a window; and Pridd, at the oven, hearing it, smiled into the brick and said nothing at all.

At six, the smell went out of the flue and over the buried street: the oldest good news in the world. At half past, they heard the first shovel. At seven, Pridd swung the door and drew the first tray of the last batch of Pridd's — high, hollow-knocking, brown as October — and the bakehouse cheered; and at ten minutes past, there came a knocking at the shop door, and Barney went; and it was a chimney-sweep's boy and two watermen's children and an old clerk from Distaff Lane, standing in the grey snow-light with their breath smoking, drawn out of doors down the whole length of the street by their noses, wearing the exact face — every one of them, the old clerk too — the face Kit had worn at the window at seven the night before.

"Well?" said Josiah Pridd, tying on a clean apron, master in his shop, the morning bell of Bread Street ward rung for the last time and answered. "It's Twelfth Day, friends, and the oven's not cold till Thursday. Come in off my step."

# Chapter Eleven — The King's One Law

The candles failed at eight; and by the custom of the house, that ended the reign; but before the last of them went, the king used it.

He had Mr. Nib's document in his hand — much engrossing had gone into it between dictation and dawn, and Mr. Nib stood by it now, cuffed in paper, prouder than at any deed in the strong-rooms of Gudgeon & Sharp — and the king rapped the table with the peel-handle sceptre the queen had lent him for the purpose, and the bakehouse and half the shop came in to hear: fourteen souls of the feast, and past them, wedged in the doorway, a fair sample of waking Bread Street with its arms full of warm loaves.

"By the rule of this house," said the king, "I am told there is a custom above the custom: that the king's last word, spoken before the last candle, holds good — not till morning; the year round. Mr. Pridd tells me his father maintained it sixty years, and I have examined Mr. Pridd's father's ledger of precedents —" the gilt crown inclined gravely toward Pridd — "namely, Mr. Pridd, and find the authority sound. Very well. I have signed one instrument this week in anger, and I am told my hand on it will hold in any court in England. Now attend to what the same hand does in its right mind, by candle-light, in a paper crown; and let the company witness which of the two signatures the man himself would be tried by.

"Item," said the king, and Mr. Nib followed the words down the page with a paper cuff, glowing. "The purchase of the corner of Pannier Court stands. I am not Father Christmas, whatever the season; I will not play at tearing up parchment; the money is paid, the firm is bound, and the frontage of Bread Street will have its offices — in the WAREHOUSE floors, above and behind, which want nothing but windows and clerks. Crail and Mowl acquires thereby a freehold with its offices upstairs and its heart on the ground floor, and my partner may make of it what he can; I shall enjoy watching him try.

"Item: the shop, bakehouse, ovens, fixtures, and goodwill of Pridd's are by this instrument let, from Lady Day next, on a repairing lease of one-and-twenty years, at a rent Mr. Mowl will privately consider lunacy and publicly call philanthropy, to the tenant herein named: to say, MISTRESS COMFORT PELL, baker, late of the Golden Sheaf, Rochester —" the room went up like a batch coming out; the king waited, grave as a church, till it settled — "whose covenants, mark them, are three. That the articles of the apprentice Barnabas Trott be assigned to her and served out to journeyman under her mastership. That the style of the house continue PRIDD'S, the name of PELL to stand beneath in such lettering as her pride and her signpainter may agree upon — I am advised by the trade," said the king dryly, over the noise, "that the name is worth more than the ovens, and the trade is correct. And that on every fifth of January while the lease runs, there stand in that window, lit, a Twelfth cake of the great pattern, at the cost of the house; whereof the first slice — I am particular about the first slice; it is the only rent that interests me — shall be carried round to the counting-house of Crail and Mowl, upstairs, and eaten by the partners on the premises; that the firm may be reminded annually, in the only ledger that has ever once corrected me, of what the ground floor is FOR.

"Item: Mr. Pridd." The king came round the table; the two grey men faced each other by the warm brick. "I cannot make you stay, and I have watched you since two o'clock and know better than to try; you're wanted in Ipswich, sir, by a daughter, and you've earned your chimney-corner, and the trade has had its pound of you. The instrument names you consulting master to the house of Pridd's — duties, one fortnight at Twelfthtide yearly, to oversee the great cake and quarrel with Mistress Pell about the fruit; emolument, your bed, your board, and the first hot roll of Epiphany morning. My clerk required a consideration to make it binding," and here the king's mouth flickered, "so the instrument recites: *in consideration of one sugar Grenadier, received by the lessor's firm in the year 1801, value beyond computation, receipt whereof is hereby acknowledged in full.*

"Item: the boy Kit, crossing-sweeper, of no fixed crossing" — Kit stood up very straight, white with flour and expectation — "is wanted at Leadenhall Street, Monday sennight, eight o'clock sharp, boots and all, to be entered junior office-boy to Crail and Mowl at four shillings the week, two of which he will oblige me by eating; a boy who can count fourteen strangers twice over in a strange room and call fourteen slices blind and true from under a table has the makings of a confidential clerk, and I have been looking for him, without knowing it, for some years. Sergeant Culpepper is entered watchman of the Pannier Court premises from Lady Day, lodge, coals, and uniform found — the firm has wanted a watchman, and I never yet heard of a better character in one than that he stands at the warm end and mulls for the company. Mrs. Sprat's — Miss Sally's — situation will survive her mistress's temper; the Alderman's card is good artillery, but the deposition of the ward's OTHER magistrate," the crown inclined, awfully, a quarter of an inch, "in an engrossed instrument, is better, and Mr. Nib has prepared one. And Herr Finck of Whitechapel is appointed sugar-worker to the house of Pridd's for the term of the lease, the great cake's figures to come out of the Kummel moulds while he lives to warm them at the flue — the seconds and mis-strikes, by covenant, to be thrown to the children at the window, that being, I am instructed," said Onesiphorus Crail, "the interest that pays best in the trade.

"That is my law," said the king, into a stillness with a great many bright eyes in it. "One night a year the world turns upside down. I have looked at it carefully from the new position, and I find that a number of items previously believed to be resting on the floor were in fact hanging from the ceiling. The instrument is drawn; the candle is going; Mistress Pell, will you sign?"

Comfort Pell came forward; and if her hand shook, receiving in one paper her mastership, her bakehouse, her apprentice, and her window, no one saw it, because it did not. She read it — every line, like her father's daughter, while the king held the candle and the whole street waited, and Mr. Nib died several deaths over his own commas — and she took the pen.

"One amendment," she said. "The first slice goes upstairs to the partners, agreed, and carried by the apprentice, agreed. But the second slice of every great cake this house cuts —" she looked at Pridd, and then at the doorway, where the sweep's boy stood with his loaf, watching the quality of people's luck the way such boys do — "the second slice goes out the shop door, to whoever's at the glass. Put it in the covenants or I bake it into them myself, sir; my landlord may as well learn his tenant now."

"Engross it," said the king, with enormous satisfaction, to Mr. Nib. "Second slice to the window. Sound conveyancing; the consideration moves the way it always did in these premises: outward."

She signed *Comfort Pell* in a strong round bakehouse hand; and Josiah Pridd signed under her, *witness, Josiah Pridd*, the second signature of his week and the whole heart of him in this one; and the last candle of the feast of the Epiphany, in the year 1847, stood up straight, put its light once round the room — over the crown and the crumbs, the queen and the sweep's boy, the flour on the Alderman, the tear-tracks on Mr. Nib, the two bakers, old and new, bent over one page — and went out, honourably, like a sentry relieved; and Twelfth Night was over, and the reign was done, and outside the window the sixth of January stood waiting in the snow with the sun coming up.

# Chapter Twelve — Twelfth Morning

London dug itself out slowly, in a light like the inside of a pearl; and by nine the feast was scattering, as feasts must, into it.

They went one by one and two by two, out of the warm bread-smelling dark into the staggering white morning, and Pannier Court heard the goodbyes: Huggins first, to Duchess and the Swan yard and a wife in Lambeth who — the company had subscribed a signed paper to this effect, Mrs. Tarleton's flourish taking up most of the bottom — was requested to believe every word her husband should tell her, "this once, the weather standing surety." Then Mrs. Tarleton herself, bound for Drury Lane afoot and undaunted, the fairy queen's ruined train pinned up with two of Sally's pins and worn over the snow like a banner over a battlefield, pausing at the door for her exit line — "My dears, I have played command performances, and I have played royalty, but I never till last night played to any" — and she was gone up Bread Street on Sergeant Culpepper's one proffered arm, the pair of them navigating the drifts like a frigate under tow, for the Sergeant would see her to the stage door before ever he inspected his new lodge, "a garrison, ma'am, being nothing without its colours."

The Reverend Mr. Snell went at half past nine, when word came up from the Bell that a mail would attempt the Kent road at noon. He shook hands all round, twice over, in the manner of the very happy; and at the door he stopped, and came back, and said to Comfort Pell, stumbling not at all: "The loaves and the fishes, Miss — Mistress Pell. It was the boy's loaves, you know; that's the part they leave out of the window. Somebody's whole larder, handed over first, on trust. I couldn't preach it before. I hadn't seen it done." And to Underhanger he went, floured still, faintly, about the cuffs — and preached it on the Sunday, to a small frozen congregation who did not know why the new curate glowed; and preached it, they say in that country, every Epiphany after, for upward of forty years, and never once got through it entire without stopping a moment at the loaves.

Mr. Nib went to Old Jewry with the instrument buttoned into his breast like a despatch, and a slice of Twelfth cake in paper for the cold clerk with the red knuckles; and it was remarked in the copying-room of Gudgeon & Sharp, from that quarter-day forward, that little Nib sat straighter, and was heard, on suitable anniversaries, to allude to certain engrossments of a confidential nature executed for parties he was not at liberty to name, in premises he would only describe as warm. Sally went to Bow Lane under the Alderman's own escort, band-box, deposition, and all — the ball-dress was delivered, the mistress read two lines of Mr. Nib's engrossing and became civility itself — and the Alderman rolled on from Bow Lane to the Guildhall, where he was prevented by no power on earth from telling the entire Court of Common Council about the night, twice, with the kneading left in; and Bread Street ward found, at the spring paving-committee, that its Alderman had developed a costive obstinacy on the subject of small tradesmen's frontages that outlasted his chain, his turtle, and very nearly his term.

Queen Gamage went home to Aldersgate at ten, by chair, the sweep's boy and Kit breaking trail and Barney carrying, by order of the new mistress of the house, one household loaf, one bag of Twelfth-buns, and the sugar swan with the green ribbon neck, as tribute from the kingdom she had governed one night and improved permanently; she kissed Pridd at the door, seventy-four years old and pink as a girl, and said, "Twice, now, this shop has sent me home through snow with cake. I shall expect the third, Josiah; I intend to be a great age." And Herr Finck walked away to Whitechapel through the shining streets with his dead master's crown mould under his arm — it went with the office, Pridd insisted; the moulds belonged with the window they were cast for — and his sad eyes entirely bright; and he painted out nothing over his door after all, but added under the old name, in small gold letters that first week of the year: *and successor*.

And at eleven o'clock, when the shop stood almost quiet again — Barney asleep on the sacks at last, in his articles' last fortnight of Pridd's and first of Pell's; Comfort at the counter with a stub of pencil, listing, already listing, barm and sacks and a signpainter; Kit by the stove, polishing, for practice, the boots he would present at Leadenhall Street — Onesiphorus Crail put on his great-coat and came to say what he had stayed last to say, and found there was little of it, which is the way with the real things.

"Lady Day," he said. "My men will be civil, and the dust will be terrible, and I shall be upstairs by Michaelmas, counting. You'll have complaints of me, Mistress Pell; I file them Thursdays."

"You'll have the smell of proving bread through your counting-house floor six mornings a week, sir," said Comfort Pell, not looking up from her list, "and no appeal. I'd call the account square."

Crail smiled — the second time in twelve hours; the clerks of Leadenhall Street would have subscribed a testimonial to see it — and at the door, in the snow-light, with the paper crown folded small and flat in his inside pocket, where it was found, it may as well be told now, every winter after by his laundress and never once removed, he stopped, and looked back at the window.

The great cake was gone from it: eaten, scattered, carried off in paper through a dozen white streets, gone the way of all good bread, which is to say into people. In its place, on the gilt board, the morning sun coming up Bread Street struck one small thing standing: Herr Finck's new-cast sugar swan — and beside it, set there by Barney before he slept, the little sugar baker with his caraway loaf, saved whole from the cutting by the vote of the entire feast, master of a window that was, the ward would have you know, not dark, and not for lease, and not finished.

"Twelfth Night," said Mr. Crail, to nobody in particular, in the tone of a man entering a standing order in a private ledger, "falls annually. I regard that as the soundest clause in the calendar."

Then he pulled his hat down and walked away up Bread Street through the snow to see his partner's face; and Josiah Pridd, watching him go from the doorway of the shop his grandfather lit and his successor would keep lit, wiped his hands on his apron in the ancient sacrament of the trade, and observed to the new year, mildly, that the calendar was generous, if you kept an eye on it — there was a feast of one sort or another lying about in nearly every month, if a house cared to look, and a house that had kept its window lit through the worst of them could keep it lit through the rest.

But that is the next night's cake, and this one is eaten; and the bean, as everyone at that table would tell you to the end of their days — the clerk and the queen, the sweeper and the Alderman, the king who bought the shop and the mistress who saved it — the bean makes no mistakes.
