# THE WIRE GARDEN

*A novella of the Dodendraad*

> Castelré, 1915. Lena Cuypers keeps twelve hives on a meadow that runs down to the border stream, in a hamlet so small the war has to be listened for. Her husband lies buried half an hour's walk away, in Belgium; his people live a field beyond him, in Zondereigen. In July the occupiers begin driving poles along the far bank — a fence with death strung through the middle of it — and the bees, who know nothing about borders, go over it every fair morning and come home heavy. What else learns to cross is the matter of this book.

---

**Content note:** deaths on the wartime electric fence, some witnessed; wartime hunger across a border; no battlefield violence — a quiet book about a loud border.

# Chapter One — Poles and Skeps

The bees knew nothing about the border, and Lena Cuypers had given twenty years to learning it from them, which she considered a fair rate for the lesson.

The border was the stream. It came down out of the heath above Zondereigen no wider than a man could step across in his church shoes, ran the bottom of her meadow between banks of meadowsweet and forget-me-not, and carried on its back, by some arrangement of kings long dead, the difference between two kingdoms; and in twenty years neither kingdom had sent anyone to look at it. Cows drank from both banks and were not asked for papers. Castelré itself — a dozen roofs, a chapel too small for a funeral, a hamlet Dutch by paper and Belgian by habit — went to mass in Belgium, milled its rye in Belgium, and buried its dead, when the dead had married that way, in Belgium; and Lena's dead had married that way.

Emiel Peeters had come across the stream in 1895 with a swarm in a sack, having followed it farther than a sensible man follows a swarm, and had stayed, in the manner of swarms, because the place suited him. He was the second son of a Zondereigen farm and brought no land, which her father held against him, and an entire education in bees, which her father priced at nothing and which turned out to be the whole inheritance. Eighteen years she had kept the hives with him. In the nineteenth winter the lung fever took him in eleven days, quietly, the way he did things, and they carried him back over the stream and put him in among the Peeterses at Zondereigen, under a stone she had paid for and on which the parish spelled his name right at the second attempt. That was February 1913. On fine Sundays she walked to the grave — forty minutes, the plank bridge, the sandy lane past the Peeters yard — and drank her coffee afterwards in Marthe's kitchen, and let Toon, her husband's brother's boy, walk her back as far as the stream, he being at fifteen of the age that must be walking somewhere.

The hives stood in a row on a scoured plank bench under the lime, facing south toward the water: twelve stocks. Nine were *korven* — skeps, coiled rye straw sewn with split bramble, the shape of hives in church windows — and three were the wooden frame hives Emiel had built from a Flemish pamphlet in the winter of 'nine, with movable combs and a honey chamber, which the hamlet had regarded as it regarded all pamphlets. She kept both kinds and let them argue. The skeps wintered hard and swarmed like weather; the *kasten* gave more honey and asked more judgment; and a widow with a meadow, a cow, and twelve stocks of bees does not want for judgment to practice.

July was the fat of the year. The lime came out over the bench and stood in its own sound for a fortnight; the clover was in; on the sandy fields toward Baarle the buckwheat bloomed, which smells of nothing much by day and by evening fills the lanes like bread baking. On still evenings the whole row of hives fanned together, driving the water off the day's nectar, and the smell of the flow stood over the bench like a second dusk. She took the first honey that month, cutting comb from the skeps' crowns, her wrists stung and left unrubbed — she had stopped counting stings in 'ninety-six, the year she learned they were not a wage but a punctuation — and the year, the twentieth year, was going the way of the nineteen before it.

The war was eleven months old that July and lived in the newspaper. It had come near once — in the autumn of 'fourteen the guns at Antwerp had been a weather along the southern sky, felt in the windowpanes more than heard, and the roads through Baarle had filled for weeks with Belgium walking north, barrows and priests and prams, a million of them into the country that autumn, the paper said — and then it had gone away and become lists. The Netherlands was neutral. Emiel's country was occupied and her own was merely crowded, and between the two conditions ran her stream.

⁂

The poles came up the far bank on a Tuesday in the middle of July, on requisitioned farm carts, with an escort of grey soldiers no younger than herself.

She was at the bench with the smoker lit and watched between her own hives as the work went by along the Belgian side of the stream: workmen digging — Belgians, pressed or paid, keeping their eyes on their spades — and behind them the cart with the poles, pine, barked, a forearm thick and two metres proud of the ground once set; and behind that a second cart that interested her more, loaded with small white porcelain knobs in straw, like door-handles for a house nobody meant to build. The soldiers slung their rifles on their backs like tools they had stopped noticing. A sergeant of some kind walked the line with a drawing in his hand, placing men the way you place hives, with long looks and small movements.

Toon came over the plank bridge at noon, too fast, being sixteen now and the owner of all news. They were building it the whole length of the border, he said; it was at Zondereigen already, it had been at Achel for weeks; three fences, not one — a low barbed one, then the tall one, then a low barbed one again — and the tall one in the middle was to carry the current. Two thousand volts, he said, twice, the number being most of what he had carried across. There was a proclamation on the church door at Zondereigen with von Bissing's name at the foot of it: whoever touched the wire had only himself to blame.

"Who is it against," Lena said. It was not entirely a question.

"Us," Toon said, cheerfully, meaning every person he knew, meaning nothing yet.

The tall middle fence went up in the last week of July. From her bench she watched them string it: wire after wire on the white porcelain, a hand-and-a-half apart, the lowest no higher off the ground than a boot-top, so that when it was done — she counted eight wires on her stretch — it stood like a music staff ruled across the meadow's foot from horizon to horizon, waiting for its notes. Four hundred metres east, where the lane from the bridge bent away, they built a wooden hut, low, tarred, with cables entering it under a little roof of its own: the *schakelhuis*, Toon reported, the switch-house, where the current could be given to the wires or taken from them, section by section. The last Sunday of the month she walked to the grave as usual, past the working parties, and stood longer than usual at the stone, and drank her coffee in Marthe's kitchen with the talk going quiet whenever the window brightened; and she came home not knowing to make anything more of the walk than she made of it, a woman who has buried her husband once not expecting to be parted from him a second time.

On the first evening of August, at dusk, a lantern moved from the schakelhuis along the fence and back again, and the soldiers on the far bank stood away from the wires all together, without hurry, like men in church at a familiar point of the service. Lena was at the bench with the syrup pail. She did not see anything happen. What happened was under the evening instead: a sound arriving, a pressure along the grass such as a skep gives against the palm on a strong flow — but lower, and even, without the grain of wings in it, a hum with no bees inside.

The hives fanned above it in their own key. She stood between the two sounds with the pail in her hand until it was dark, and could not, that first evening, have said what she was listening for; and the bees went over the new thing every fair morning from then on, at four metres, at no cost, and came home heavy, as though nothing whatever had been done at the bottom of the meadow — which was, she came to think, the exact size of what a border was worth, and the exact price of having a body that must keep to the ground.

# Chapter Two — The Hum

By September the border had learned its new manners, and they were the manners of a hospital ward: you could visit, at set places, at set hours, and you could not touch, and somebody was always dying somewhere down the corridor.

The set places made themselves. Where a lane met the wire on both sides, people gathered on Sunday afternoons — Dutch on the near bank, Belgians beyond the third fence — and shouted their lives across the ten metres and the three fences and the mown dead strip between: births were shouted, and prices; banns were shouted; a death went across in a woman's flat raised voice and was taken off hats to on the other side. You could not hand a christening cake across, or an apple, or a hand. The guards let it run for a while, moved everyone on when the crowd thickened or an officer was due, and let it gather again the next week, this being the kind of order that can be kept but not won. Lena went the first Sundays, for Marthe's sake, and heard her own affairs — the cow, the honey take, the weather over the graves — go up and down the air between them like laundry on a line, and came home each time more tired than threshing ever made her. Shouted things came out simpler than they went in. You could shout that you were well. You could not shout what you meant.

The passes were the second lesson. There had been *Passierscheine* in the spring — Marthe had come over twice with a day-pass for Baarle market, her papers stamped at the crossing post, her basket weighed there and back — but since January no pass at all was given to a man between fifteen and fifty-five, and by autumn the women's passes were drying up as well, refused without reasons in the way that is itself a reason. The last time Marthe stood in Lena's kitchen was the second Tuesday of September. She looked at the row of hives from the window a long time. "Emiel is better off than any of us," she said, in the voice she kept for facts. "He crossed while it was cheap." Then she walked back over the plank bridge with her stamped paper, through the gate in the wire where the guards ran their hands along the basket seams, and the gate was wire too, and shut behind her on a wooden rattle.

October made the third lesson.

Lena woke past midnight to the dogs — hers first, then the Belgian dogs answering along the line like a rumor — and stood at the small window of the bedroom without a lamp. The night was moonless and dry. Down at the meadow's foot, where nothing had a right to be bright, something went bright: twice, a flat blue-white slap of light with no shape to it, the light a wet hand makes on a lamp socket, and between the two a crack no louder than a green branch breaking over a knee. Then nothing. The hum went on under the dark exactly as before, no louder, no softer, and that was the part her body took note of and kept: that the wire sounded no different for having been touched.

She did not go down in the dark, and would not afterwards pretend to herself that this had been composure; there was simply nothing in her legs. At first light she went down with the pail as always, because the bench had to be done, and made herself look. A shape lay against the middle fence a hundred metres east, between her stretch and the *schakelhuis*, half kneeling in the second fence's wires like a man looking for something small he had dropped. Grey soldiers stood about it at a distance, smoking, not looking at it, in the attitude of men waiting for a procedure.

The procedure came at eight. A Feldwebel — an older man, heavy in the shoulders, who walked the dead strip the way a farmer walks his own headland — sent one soldier down the line to the switch-house; a long ten minutes later the man came back and said his word; and the Feldwebel put the back of his bare hand against the middle wire, high up, unhurried, in the manner of a man who had been ordered to be the proof and had decided to be a calm one. Then they took the body off the wires. It came away wrong — stiff where it should swing, the way a frosted sheet comes off the line — and they laid it on a handcart and covered it with a tent-square, and one of the young guards, a boy with his collar too big for him, turned on the knot of Belgians who had gathered along the lane hedge and drove them back with the flat of his rifle up, shouting, his voice going through registers; and the Feldwebel said one word to him, not loudly, in a flat Westphalian sort of German, and the boy stopped as though unplugged, and the people were let stand. They stood. The cart went off toward Zondereigen with two soldiers walking behind it out of step, and the older man remained a moment, looking down the fence with his hands behind his back; and Lena, across the stream on her own ground with the pail still in her hand, was near enough to see him do what she had been doing every morning for a month: he listened to it. The guards along the stretch called him Brenner; that much the wind brought over.

The dead man was nobody's. That was established by Sunday with the strange speed of border news: not a Zondereigen man, not from Minderhout or Baarle-Hertog, no name in his coat — a young man out of the deep occupied country, going north to the neutral ground by night, with a tin of tobacco on him and no rubber and no sense, said the *passeurs*' friends, and said it gently. Castelré counted him all the same. He had died within sound of the chapel bell, and the hamlet held that this constituted a claim; the women went to the mass that was said for him at Minderhout the way you go to a cousin's funeral. He was the first of Castelré's counting. The counting went on for three years, and never in that time did Lena hear anyone try to say what the wire had taken along its whole length, the numbers that went about being city numbers, newspaper numbers, too round to lift; the village counted what it could see from its own gates, as villages always had, and kept its dead the size of days. By then the wire had its name on both banks — the *dodendraad*, the death-wire — and both banks used it the way you use a neighbour's surname, without thinking what it said.

She thought about the tobacco tin more than she meant to. A man carries tobacco because he means to smoke on the far side; it is the future in a pocket. All that autumn, milking, rendering wax, sewing the skeps' winter hackles on, she kept arriving at the same flat place: the war, here, was a thing you attended. It stood at the bottom of the meadow like a neighbor's fire seen across ditches — you went down with your buckets, you stood on your own ground, you watched, and the smoke went over your roof and dirtied nothing but the washing. The Netherlands was neutral. The house was not burning. She had a dry bed, white bread, a whole roof, a live nephew; on the far bank they had the proclamations and the passes and the fire itself. All the border had been given to share alike was the hum — that, both banks had all night, every night, under everything; and some nights, waking to it, she could not have said which country she heard it in, and lay listening in the dark of the one that was supposed to be at peace.

# Chapter Three — Paper Kites

The first letter crossed the wire on the last Sunday of November, on a south wind, at the end of a length of waxed shoemaker's thread, under a kite the size of a dinner plate.

She had seen kites over the far bank three Sundays running before that and thought nothing of them — boys' work, oiled paper and split cane, the toys of a country whose boys had been left nothing else to fly — until the Sunday one of them rose from behind the Peeters hedge itself, stood shivering over the three fences at the height of the poplars, leaned, and came down in her meadow like a shot bird, thirty paces up from the bench. Toon's work; she knew it before she reached it, from the sewing. The thread had been cut on the far side the moment the kite stalled. Under the cane cross, folded flat and sewn — sewn, Marthe's needle, waxed thread, six stitches to the inch as though the wind graded seams — a letter.

She read it standing in the cold with her back to the wire, which she was aware even then was a manner of hiding a thing in plain sight of it.

It was not, in its opening, anything the guards could have objected to: rain, the cow, the price of nothing at a market nobody could reach. Then, in the lower half, in the smaller hand Marthe used when she came to the matter: *Gust's people are in Tilburg since Antwerp — the Wouwstraat, or near it, the schoolhouse took them in '14 — we have had one card in a year. His mother is with us and is old. If a letter were posted INSIDE your country it would go. You see what I am asking. Burn this half.*

Lena stood a while. Then she went in, and did not burn anything, and wrote two letters at the kitchen table with the lamp lit at three in the afternoon: one to the address in Tilburg, in her own name, a Dutch widow forwarding family news, which was every word of it true; and one to Marthe, for whatever wind went south.

The wind went south four days later, and taught her the other half of the trade. A kite will not fly back the way it came; what went from her side went the older way, the way the *passeurs*' friends had used since the first week of the wire: paper folded hard around a heavy bolt-nut, the packet wrapped in oilcloth, thrown. Ten metres of fences is nothing to a farm arm; the art was not the distance but the address — a throw too short lay in the dead strip between the fences, in the mown grass of the *niemandsland*, where no one could fetch it and the guards raked at their leisure, and a name on such a paper was a summons. So there was no name on hers. There was a berry basket left as if forgotten on the Peeters side of the lane hedge, and a Tuesday, and a dusk; she threw from the alders on her own bank with her heart going like a swarm in a sack, absurdly, a forty-four-year-old woman throwing ironmongery in her own meadow; and the packet went over the three fences with a yard to spare and lay in Belgium in the twilight looking like nothing, and before full dark it was not there any more.

The answer from Tilburg came inside the week, five pages, the old mother's dictation down the margins. She sewed it — Marthe's stitch, since that was now the house style — around the nut, in oilcloth, and threw it on the Friday. And that, with no one ever agreeing to anything, was the founding of the post office.

By Christmas she carried five families' letters. It grew the way such things grow, sideways, by kitchens: a Zondereigen woman whose sons were labourers in Rotterdam; the miller's people at Minderhout; a girl in service in Breda writing to her father. Northbound came under kites; southbound went around the bolt-nuts, Tuesdays and Fridays, dusk, from the alders. What waited in the house between winds she kept where a mind that knew hives would keep it: under the flat zinc-lined roof of the middle frame hive, in the dead air over the crown-board, where paper stays dry as a sermon and no man who has ever been stung puts his hand by choice. She topped the roof-space up and cleared it by lamplight, in the cold, when the bees had no opinion; and she told no one at all, which is the only size of post office that lives.

⁂

The marechaussee came on the second of January, on a tall bay, in the blue-black winter cloak of the corps, and asked at the door for nothing more than coffee.

Wachtmeester Van Dijk, of the Baarle-Nassau brigade; she knew his face from markets. He drank the coffee standing, cap under his arm, and admired the room — the way policemen admire, wall by wall — and talked about the border the way you talk about weather to a farmer: generally, and meaning it. Salt was going south, he said. Gin. Quinine, lately, which brought a different class of person into the lanes. A horse had been walked over frozen flood-water into Belgium at Ulicoten, shod backwards, an old trick, he said, with a certain respect for it. The fence had made every gram going south worth triple, and Her Majesty's government, being neutral, was obliged to care about exports with an exactness the smugglers found flattering.

"And letters," he said, at the door, into his cap as he put it on. "There is a great post in letters now, they tell me. Letters I have no orders about, mevrouw, so long as letters is what they are. Paper carries nothing but what is written on it." He gathered the reins and looked, for the first time and quite frankly, down her white meadow at the fence, and then at the row of hives standing in their winter straw. "A tidy stand," he said. "My father kept bees. There is no better-guarded furniture in the world than a bee-hive. Even a customs man will take a great deal on trust from a bee-hive. Good day."

She watched him up the lane a long time. Not a threat; she turned it every way that evening and it would not lie as a threat; a boundary, rather, drawn by a man whose trade was boundaries, and drawn — she came to be sure of this later, in the worst month, when it mattered — half an inch outside where he guessed her to be standing, on purpose, so that she should see exactly how much room she had and exactly whose gift the room was.

That night she wrote the rule out plainly in the Friday packet, for Marthe, and for whoever stood behind Marthe in the kitchens of Zondereigen, because a rule not sent is only a mood: *Paper I will carry, all winter, both ways, and gladly. Nothing that eats, nothing that pays, nothing that breathes. Do not ask me the third thing twice.*

She read it over once and sat looking at the last sentence, mildly surprised at her own hand, the way one is by a frost-flower on a known window; nobody had asked her the third thing yet. Then she sewed the packet shut. Wax the thread, six stitches to the inch; the house style. On the wire the winter rain hung in droplets the length of four hundred metres, each one carrying the same grey daylight, and the hum went on under the sleet as under everything; and in the roof of the middle hive the letters lay over the wintering bees, warm side down, the two soft freights of the meadow wintering together, and what she felt about the year to come was not what a sensible woman would have chosen to feel, and she went to bed without naming it.

# Chapter Four — The Passeur

The third thing came and asked in the last week of January, in a hard frost, and it was sitting on her chopping block in the dark of the outhouse when she came in with the lantern at six in the morning.

"Mind the light," it said, mildly, and moved out of the doorway's line of sight, and that was her introduction to Staf: a smallish man past forty in a Dutch farm coat over Belgian trousers, with mud to the waist and frost on him, holding her wax-rendering kettle in both arms the way you hold a stove, having plainly been holding it most of the night. He had crossed near the bend after the moon set, he said, as though naming a road he had taken; the frost was good for it, wet grass being a thing his trade had opinions about. He knew her name, her husband's name, the stone at Zondereigen, and the Tuesday-and-Friday habits of her south wind, none of which she had told anybody in Belgium except by post; and he laid all this out quietly and without threat in it, in the border Dutch that belongs to neither bank, while the lantern steadied — a man showing his papers, was what it was; in his trade references could not be written down, so you carried what you knew, and what you knew said who vouched for you.

She gave him coffee in the kitchen because it was that or watch him shake, and he drank it with both hands and talked his trade with the frankness of a man who has decided you are worth frankness, which she understood to be itself a tool, and let him use it.

He was a *passeur* — a taker-across; he worked the Zondereigen stretch and two others, and he did not vault the wire or throw dogs over it or any of the things in the songs. The trade had tools. There was the barrel: a wineseller's cask with both heads knocked out, pushed slowly between the middle wires until it held them apart like a yawn, and a man went through it as through a culvert, touching only wood. There were the gloves, cut and sewn from bicycle inner tubes, two layers, with which — on a dry night, with prayer — a man could part the wires and hold them for another; rubber had crossed from luxury to sacrament in one winter, and the guards searched for it now the way they searched for letters. There were the double ladders, two hayloft ladders lashed at the head into a free-standing steeple that straddled the whole middle fence and touched no wire at all, for crossings where speed mattered less than a pregnant woman or a man of sixty. And there was the thing he showed her because his trade, like hers, had its pride: from under his shirt, warmed all night against his chest, a bundle of oak slats and hinges that opened in his hands, with four soft clicks, into a frame — the *passeursraam*, the taker's window, a collapsible casement the size of a hearth screen, its slats grooved to seat wires. Set into the fence, it held four of the eight wires sprung apart, and the current went round its dry oak margins peaceably while a man passed through the middle of the death itself; folded, it went down a trouser leg and became a limp. He handed it across the kitchen table for her to hold. It weighed what a well-made thing weighs. She thought, and was not proud of thinking it, that Emiel would have taken it to the window and turned it over for an hour.

"You'll want something," she said.

He wanted her meadow. He laid it out the way you lay out a field's worth: her stretch lay midway between posts, the *schakelhuis* light showed a patrol two hundred metres off down the line, the alders held the bank, the stream ran loud enough in thaw to eat small sounds, and her barn stood a hundred paces from the water with its back door away from the lane — a receiving ground, he said; nothing on her side of the law at all, as the law stood; men who had already crossed, waiting out the last dark hour under a Dutch roof, gone by full light. Two, three a month. He named a sum per head, in guilders, without apology, and she understood that most of the doors he asked this at, the sum was the whole conversation.

"No," Lena said.

He didn't argue; he tilted his head, which meant she should go on, and she found she owed the going-on to herself as much as to him. Not the danger, she said, or not first. The letters. Twenty families' post came through her meadow because her meadow was the most boring four hundred metres on the border; because the wachtmeester drank her coffee once a winter and rode away easy; because the grey men opposite had watched her carry syrup pails past their wire for two seasons and stopped seeing her. Boring was the whole of her capital. One man found sleeping in her barn would spend it in a morning — and the letters were not hers to gamble; they belonged to the kitchens on both banks that had nothing else crossing. "You'd put a road through the garden," she said. "It's a good garden because there's no road."

Staf looked at her for a while, the way a man prices a field he is not going to get. "And if one comes to your water that I haven't sent," he said. "It happens. They come north on their own legs with a map drawn in a café and they end on the wires like washing. If one comes over live, on your ground, at your door."

"Then he's a man at my door," she said, "and I've never turned one of those away. That's not trade. Trade you do on purpose."

He nodded slowly, the distinction being evidently one his own ledger kept as well, and stood, and would not take bread for the road because bread found on a man near the wire wanted explaining; the trade ate before it crossed, he said. At the outhouse door he paused and settled his coat, and she saw him become, between one breath and the next, a Dutch farmhand mooching home from an early milking, shoulders altered, gait altered, twenty years added and no limp at all; the frame had stayed on her kitchen table.

She went in and looked at it. Folded, oak, waxed at the joints, no bigger than a seed tray. She carried it to the barn and up to the loft and put it under the seed trays proper, where a thing of slats and hinges is furniture among furniture; and she was aware, doing it, of what she was doing, which was not keeping a souvenir. A borrowed tool obliges. He had left it the way you leave a bucket at a well you mean to come back to; and she had shelved it where it would be dry, which any well knows is an answer.

# Chapter Five — Fifteen Grams

In March the letters changed weight without changing thickness, and it took her most of the month to name what had gone out of them, which was food.

Not the word; the word came in everywhere. What went out was the rest — the cow, the banns, the parish comedy — until a letter was bread from margin to margin: the ration bread that came from the ships and was never quite enough, grey and wet-hearted, said Marthe, half of it something that was not wheat; the queue at Zondereigen's depot that formed at four in the morning for what opened at eight; the pig given up to the requisition lists, the hens counted and recounted by men with satchels, the seed potatoes eaten in February with next year's mouth. The official schedule that spring allowed the occupied country, per head and per day, an amount of sugar that Marthe set down once without comment, in the middle of a sentence, the way you set down a stone too heavy to carry further: *fifteen grams*. Lena weighed it out on the kitchen scale to see it — a heaped spoon, a wet thumbprint of sweetness — and then put the sugar back in the tin, and found she was angry at the tin, and went out and did the bench over twice.

Because her side of the stream, that same March, was merely inconvenienced. Dutch sugar was dearer and the white bread greyer and the coffee half chicory, and Baarle market grumbled the way a well-fed man grumbles; and forty metres south of her hives the same spring came up through a country that was being eaten hollow from inside its own fence. That was the arithmetic of the border now, and her trade sat squarely on the wrong side of every line of it: she was a maker of sugar. Thirty, forty kilos of honey would come off the meadow by September, and every kilo of it could be sold in Baarle in an afternoon at prices that embarrassed her — honey had gone from Sunday food to currency while she wintered the bees — and not one spoonful of it could lawfully or physically cross the fifteen metres to the kitchens that wrote to her about fifteen grams. Paper crossed. Paper was her own rule, sworn to Van Dijk's half-inch of room and to twenty families' post: nothing that eats. She had drawn the line to keep the letters safe, and the line had held, and now she lived on top of it like a woman keeping shop on a bridge, selling sweetness north because south was not allowed; and there were market Tuesdays that spring when the weight of coin in her apron came home feeling like something she had got away with.

⁂

Toon's letter came under a kite in April, the first warm week, and it was not sewn in Marthe's stitch; he had sewn it himself, badly, which told her to read it standing still.

He was seventeen that month. The letter was five lines and had been carried in his head so long it had gone smooth like the proclamation prose it grew up under: he was of no use to his mother's table, one mouth was a gift to it; men of Zondereigen younger than he was had gone over and were in England now, in the Belgian army's depots, he had this on good authority; he knew the men who knew the way; he asked his aunt — and here the schoolboy hand pressed through — to say nothing to his mother until he was across, and to think well of him.

She wrote the answer the same evening and threw it south on the Friday wind, the bolt-nut heavier than it had ever weighed. She did not write about the wires, or about rubber, or about the boy in the second fence in October who had also known men who knew the way. She wrote: *Your mother has one son, and the fence has been fed enough of everyone. Seventeen carries no country anywhere. If you must be of use, be of use alive; I will send what work I can across to you in the letters. And you will tell her yourself what you were minded to do, or I will.* She read it over and heard in it, with no pleasure, her own father's voice, the voice of the bank that is not on fire; it was easy to write *stay* forty metres north of the hunger. She sent it anyway. Some letters are true and unfair together, and you sew them shut all the same.

His answer took three weeks, one line, unsewn, wrapped so carelessly around its stone that the rain had got the corner of it: *I told her myself. She says to send the work.* And through May and June the kites came weekly with columns of figures in a boy's hand — hive weights, for she had put him to keeping bees on paper, Marthe's two skeps and the miller's four at Minderhout; what else crossed in those letters, an aunt was not obliged to notice — and slowly the hand grew calm again, or the columns calmed it.

At the market they told the Achelse Kluis story all that spring, over and over, the way a village files a grievance it cannot post. West along the wire, at Achel, the Trappists' abbey lands lay against the border, and the fence had been run straight across the monks' own ground, through the plantation their fathers had drained out of the moor; and the occupation had then presented the abbey with the bill — made the monks pay for the material of the fence on their land, the wire itself, the porcelain, the death and its fittings, priced and invoiced. Baarle told it, Castelré retold it, not because it was the worst thing the wire had done — the wire had done far worse within the hearing of everyone repeating it — but because it was the wire explained, in a sentence a market could carry: you would be fenced, and you would be billed for the fencing.

The bees, who were not told any of this, made a spring of the old size. The willows gave, then the fruit, then the clover came in like a paid debt; the skeps hung their beards out in the warm evenings; and Lena went down the row with the smoker and the water pail while the meadow roared its one annual roar, and found the season had a new taste she did not ask it to lose again: the flow was the flow of every year of her life, and it ran, now, beside the other constancy, the wire's one note that kept no seasons at all; and between the two hums, all that green spring, a woman could stand with her eyes shut and hear exactly where the world had broken.

# Chapter Six — The Swarm

The copper decree came over under a kite at the start of July, in the middle of a swarming season that had already cost her sleep, and Marthe for once spent ink on the comedy of it: all Belgium was to declare its copper to the occupation — kettles, boilers, doorknobs, the brewer's vats, the very stair-rods — declared now, requisitioned in due course, paid for in paper promises. *They are counting our pans*, she wrote. *Toon asks will they come and count their own fence.* For the fence hummed on copper: some three hundred kilometres of wire with the current in it, strung through the grass at the meadow's foot, while men with satchels went kitchen to kitchen weighing kettles. She put the letter under the hive roof with the others, and thought that the occupation had at least the honesty of a magpie: it knew what shone, and it knew where its own nest was.

The swarm went the same week, and it was her own fault, which made everything after it her business.

It was the strongest skep on the bench, the big nine-year lineage from Emiel's mother-stock, and she had known it was crowded — had heard the queens piping in it two evenings running — and the morning she meant to set the catching-cloth it went at ten o'clock, on the first still heat, a *prime* swarm, the old queen and half the city with her. It rose off the bench in the shape of a loose bell, stood over the meadow deciding, and then went south down the slope of the warm air, over the stream, over the first low fence, and settled in a beard the size of a churn on the middle fence itself: on the iron bracket of a pole, against the white porcelain, in the mown strip of the *niemandsland* where no living thing with feet had stood since 1915.

They did not ground themselves; bees hanging on a dry bracket touch nothing that costs. But there they hung, twenty thousand of Emiel's line and a nine-year queen, in the one strip of ground on earth that no one could enter, six wires of current breathing under them; and by noon guards on the far lane were looking at it through field-glasses, and the young guard — the same boy of the October cart, a year older and no wider — was for hosing it off the bracket with water, which against two thousand volts is a thought a man is lucky to have only once. It was Brenner who put a stop to that. The Feldwebel came down the strip, looked at the swarm the way she looked at swarms, from below, against the light, judging the hang of it; and then he turned and looked across the wires and the stream, directly at her, standing among her hives, and lifted a hand — not a wave; the raising of a hand that means *stay there* — and sent the boy up the line to the *schakelhuis* at a trot.

What followed, followed because the fence had housekeeping. Wednesday, mid-morning, her section died on a schedule — she had watched it for a year without knowing she knew it: the crew that walked the wires, the grass hooked out from under the lowest strand, the Feldwebel's bare hand laid on the middle wire at the start of it, the proof once ordered and now merely kept, like an old man's habit of touching the doorpost. The swarm hung its second night on the bracket, and scouts were already dancing on its shoulder for some hollow tree in Belgium; and on the Wednesday, at the hour of the dead window, Brenner came down the strip carrying, by its heels, a straw skep.

"Frau Cuypers," he said, across the low outer fence and the stream — her name in his mouth — "sagen Sie mir, was zu tun ist. Tell me what is to do. I have bees at home." He added, in the way of a man mentioning a wife: "Vierzehn Völker. Fourteen stocks. Heath bees, in Westphalia. My brother minds them." A small pause, the length of exactly one truth. "Badly."

She crossed the stream. One long step off her own bank, her first step into Belgium since the last Sunday of July 1915 — the guards on the lane watched her stand on it and nobody wrote anything down — and from there, over the low barbed fence, she talked a German soldier through the taking of a swarm: the *korf* held under, so, its mouth across the hang of the beard; the one sharp knock on the bracket, one, not two, all of them or none; then down on the grass on the spread neck-cloth, mouth propped on a stone the width of two fingers, and wait, and do not brush at what crawls on you, and watch the mouth of the skep.

He did it all the way a man does who has done it forty times in another country: the knock clean, the beard falling into the straw with the sound of a wet rope dropped, the skep grounded and propped in one motion. Then he stood in the mown strip with bees walking on his grey wool and his bare hands hanging, watching the skep's mouth; and when the fanning began — the ranked bees at the entrance, wings driving the queen's scent backward like little chimneys, the sign the house is bought and the queen is in — he said, "Sie stehen und fächeln. They stand and fan," in the voice men keep for the second-best day of their lives. Emiel used to say the fanning was the best moment of the trade, the moment the argument ends.

At noon he handed the skep back over the low wire, over the stream, into her lifted hands, heavier than a full pail — "Schwer," he said. "Heavy. Good queen" — and that was the whole of the ceremony, twenty thousand bees passing from the German Empire to the Kingdom of the Netherlands without papers over two strands of barbed wire, while the boy guard stood holding Brenner's rifle in the exact attitude of a man holding a hat in church. She thanked him in Dutch. He answered in Westphalian something she did not have, and then in his schoolbook French: "Les abeilles ne savent rien. The bees know nothing. It is the best thing about them, madame."

She carried the skep up her meadow. At half past twelve, from the bench, she heard the section take the current again — no ceremony there either; the hum simply resumed mid-air, like a lamp lit in another room — and she stood with her hand flat on the warm straw of the recovered swarm and attended to what had actually happened. The fence had not relented; the fence had been dead one hour and fifty minutes by schedule and was now alive again, and it had no opinion about bees, and would kill the next man in the dark with the same evenness. The kindness had stood *inside* the wires, in grey wool, sweating, reading the fanning at the mouth of a borrowed skep. Those were two different facts and she made herself keep them separate, because the border was going to test the difference someday and a muddled woman would fail it.

And a third fact had come home with the swarm, uninvited, and sat in her now like a stone in a boot: she knew the housekeeping. Wednesday, mid-morning, the dead window; the crew's pace; the proof-hand on the wire; one hour and fifty minutes. She had not asked to learn it — she had learned it the way you learn a neighbour's stairs, by living against the wall — but learned was learned, and knowledge of that kind does not lie still in a house that keeps twenty families' letters under a hive roof. She put the new stock on the empty stand-end at dusk, and the meadow hummed in its two registers, above and below; and the one below had a hole in it now, two hours wide, that only she on all her bank knew how to find.
