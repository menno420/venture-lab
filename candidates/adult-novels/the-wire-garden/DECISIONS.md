# Decisions — The Wire Garden

Coordinator-dispatched night write-slice: concept #2 of `docs/ideas/2026-07-14-adult-title-concepts.md`, written as a complete novella by two workers in one night session. This title has **no vetting packet yet** — the manuscript precedes any vetting packet; the packet is a recorded follow-up slice owned elsewhere tonight, and nothing under `docs/publishing/` is touched by this slice. The manuscript is the whole deliverable, plus this per-title decisions file.

- **Premise fidelity** — Delivers the concept as scored (26/30): Brabant border village, 1915–1918; the Dodendraad — the lethal electric fence the occupiers of Belgium strung along the neutral Dutch border — runs at the bottom of a beekeeper's meadow; her hives stand within sound of it, and what crosses with the bees is letters, then people; the Netherlands neutral, a war watched at the fence, not lived under. **The fence itself is the antagonist — no villain-shaped villain**, mirroring the house pattern (Sweetwater's dike, right and ruinous at once): every human under the wire — German Landsturm guards, Dutch marechaussee, passeurs — is a person, none monstrous.
- **Scope/length** — One complete, standalone literary-historical novella, English, house band 15,000–16,000 words. Honest count to be re-measured from the file at the flip (house rule: counts are re-measured from the file at hand, never copied forward).
- **POV/tense** — Close third person on Lena Cuypers, past tense, single POV throughout; all other minds — Brenner, Van Dijk, Staf, Marthe, Toon — are seen strictly from outside.
- **Structure** — 12 chapters, July 1915 → 1919, ⁂ scene breaks used sparingly within chapters: One — Poles and Skeps (the meadow and the hives; the fence raised on the Belgian side; the first current test). Two — The Hum (border life rearranged; the Passierschein regime; a stranger's death on the wire, witnessed as light and sound; Brenner introduced). Three — Paper Kites (the first letter over the wire; Marthe's request; the quiet post-office under a hive roof begins; Van Dijk's first visit; letters only — the line drawn). Four — The Passeur (Staf and the crossing trade's kit; her meadow refused as a receiving point; the craft catalogued like tools of a trade). Five — Fifteen Grams (the hunger differential; honey she can trade in Baarle but not send across; Toon means to cross for England, she writes no; the Achelse Kluis story). Six — The Swarm (the copper decree against the copper wire; a prime swarm clusters on a fence pole in the dead strip; Brenner, beekeeper at home, arranges its taking during the maintenance dead-window; the current returns). Seven — the crossing (Toon comes over; what Lena knows of the guards' rhythms spent at last). Eight — a stranger dies on her stretch. Nine — she keeps the frame and helps. Ten — a wet-night failure and the guilt of it. Eleven — armistice; the current cut; the wire torn down. Twelve — coda, 1919: salvaged fence wire strung as bean trellis in her garden — the title image — and porcelain shards surfacing in the plough-line for years.
- **Historical accuracy — verified spine** (checked this session against the standard record via web search): built spring 1915 by the German occupation authority in Belgium (Generalgouvernement Belgien under von Bissing), live from about 24 July 1915, with some stretches (Hamont-Achel) only closed in 1916; official name *Grenzhochspannungshindernis*; roughly 2,000 volts AC; route from Vaals to the Scheldt/Zwin near Knokke; **length is disputed across sources — the prose therefore says only "some three hundred kilometres", never one precise figure**; three-fence structure — two low barbed outer fences flanking a lethal middle fence of five to ten wires on porcelain insulators, lowest wire near the ground — with wooden *schakelhuizen* (switch-houses) every 500–2,000 m used to switch sections dead for maintenance and corpse removal; guards mostly older or invalided Landsturm men, patrols at irregular hours; **death toll contested — scholarly count ~300–500 (Vanneste) against popular counts of 2,000–3,000, with 23 dead counted at Baarle-Nassau; the prose never gives a total, only what the village itself counts**; some 20,000–25,000 successful crossings; documented crossing kit — barrels pushed between the wires, rubber gloves cut from bicycle inner tubes, double ladders, the collapsible wooden *passeursraam* frame, blankets, bottles, pole-vaulting, paper kites and weighted paper for letters; 300-mark fine for unauthorized presence in the border zone, curfews, threats of fines and confiscation against families of the escaped; *Passierschein* restrictions (none issued to men 15–55 from January 1915); about a million Belgian refugees entering the Netherlands in 1914; occupied-Belgium requisitions used at newspaper precision — copper declaration decree July 1916, copper/nickel/pewter/bicycles March 1917, clothing/mattresses/nut harvest July 1917, remaining copper and wool December 1917; the 1916 official ration including 15 g of sugar a day; the wire run across the Achelse Kluis Trappist abbey's own grounds with the monks made to pay for the material of the fence on their land; armistice November 1918 — current cut, locals tearing the wire down within days and reusing it as farm fencing, porcelain shards marking the route for years after.
- **Historical accuracy — marked inventions** — All named characters are fictional (Lena Cuypers, the late Emiel Peeters, Marthe and Toon Peeters, Staf, Feldwebel Brenner, wachtmeester Van Dijk); no real person appears or speaks anywhere in the text (von Bissing acts only through paper and proclamation). **The beekeeping-at-the-wire premise is wholly invented: no source ties bees to the Dodendraad — the book's central image is a period-plausible construction, not a documented case.** No specific honey or wax requisition order is claimed anywhere; requisition pressure is kept general and documented. The dramatized crossings, letter-drops, and the swarm taken in the dead strip are directionally true to documented methods (the kit, the dead-windows, the kites) without documentary basis as specific cases. The specific Castelré meadow geography — Lena's twelve hives, the border stream, the *schakelhuis* 400 m east — is fiction laid over a real hamlet opposite the real Zondereigen stretch.
- **Sensitive-history care** — Deaths on the wire are rendered without kitsch: witnessed as light and sound, carried afterward by objects and work, never gore; the contested death toll is never flattened to one number in the text — the village counts only its own; the hunger across the border is period fact handled as witness, not spectacle. The content note declares the register.
- **Keyword discipline** — Era-register disjointness holds across the shelf: modern Amsterdam crime = The Weigh House; Golden Age 1654 = Ultramarine; WWII Hunger Winter = The Paper Orange; 1927–1932 Zuiderzee closure = The Sweetwater Sea; **this title claims the WWI 1915–1918 neutral-border / Dodendraad register**, previously unclaimed. Per the shortlist's own risk note, the forbidden-aid adjacency to The Paper Orange is managed by era AND valence: neutrality/witness at a watched border versus occupation/survival under it — different war, different decade, different search phrases by construction.
