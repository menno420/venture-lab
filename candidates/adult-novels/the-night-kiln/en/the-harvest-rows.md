# THE HARVEST ROWS

*A Cozy Fantasy Novella · The Night Kiln, Book Three*

> Everyone in Wenholt knows the rule: bring Edda a memory before moonrise, and she will fire it into clay you can hold. But on the witch's private shelf stands a dish with a crack straight through a fifty-year-old rowing race, so that the race stops forever mid-stroke — and this harvest, of all harvests, is the one it was promised for. The kiln keeps; the kiln, asked kindly, gives back. Nobody has ever asked it to mend. This autumn, at the year's own balance, somebody must.

---

**Content note:** Adult cozy fantasy. Contains grief, remembered bereavement (offpage, in the past), and a beloved character living with memory loss — all handled gently. No violence, no explicit content. A warm read for adult readers. This is the third Night Kiln novella; it stands alone.

# Chapter One — Harvest Weather

The rule in Wenholt was older than anyone who kept it, and it was this: if you wanted a memory fired, you brought it to Edda Marren before moonrise, because the kiln burned at night or not at all. You came up the witch's lane with your year in your hands; you sat in the good chair by the workshop stove with your fingers around something warm, and you told the memory out loud — properly, the whole of it — and the telling went into the witch, and bided in her; and on the firing night the fire drew it out of her, through her hands on the stoke-wood, into the waiting clay, so that ever after the memory lived in a mug or a bowl or a butter crock: a thing warm past what any cooling explained, a thing that could stand in the room with you when the rememberer no longer could. The kiln keeps. It does not take. Say it back. Four generations of Wenholt witches had taught it like a catechism, and since one short midsummer night, not long past, the catechism had grown a second line, taught to the fire and the village together: And what it keeps, asked kindly, it can give back.

Harvest weather came early that year, and meant it: mornings with a cold edge newly honed onto them, afternoons of standing gold, the Ler running low and amiable under the ferry crossing, and every field from the Askews' hill farm to the mill bend leaning all one way, heavy-headed, waiting on the blade. The oats went first, as oats do. Edda stood at the workshop window the morning the reaping started and watched the far hillside change its coat acre by acre, and felt the year turn under her the way clay turns under a steady hand: not fast, not stoppable, and dead on center.

She was sixty that autumn, and had been the witch of Wenholt twelve years — twelve years since Grams Ilsabet shelled her last peas and left her the workshop, the kiln, the catechism, and a shelf of the craft's mistakes and mercies that no customer ever saw — and she knew the shape of a witch's harvest by heart. Harvest was the trade's quiet fortnight. The gate went un-rung of an evening; the good chair stood empty and faintly affronted; the queue was out in the fields to a soul, living the sort of days it would come up the lane wanting kept in twenty years' time. What tellings arrived came late and dusty: Cobb the woodman brought one down from the hills mid-month in his usual office — spoken to him word for word on a farm doorstep, repeated in the good chair with his eyes shut and his hat wrung to death in his fists — an old shepherd's memory of scything beside his father sixty summers gone, the two blades keeping time like ringers, wanted in a cider mug before winter. The slip in Edda's lap went the pale burnt gold of stubble, and Cobb took his apple-wood out to the cart and said, as he said most years: "Valley's all one crop now, missus. You'll be busy come the corn's in."

The workshop kept its liturgies regardless of season: the kettle just short of singing, the glaze jars ranked in Ilsabet's painted hand, the telling shelf at the height of Edda's heart with its nine bowls empty and patient. On the mantel, squared up like a class at inspection, stood the summer's letters from Stonebeck — one a month since June, in a careful hand that built its letters like courses of brick, from a schoolmistress who had come to Wenholt in the spring with a box and a question and gone home with a morning. The jar stands where the early sun gets in, the last one said. On cold mornings I lift the little ones up to it one at a time, and there is no lesson in the day so easy to teach. There were three lines at the foot of it in three other hands, each more heroically upright than the last, because Mercy Hale's pupils had discovered that a letter to the witch of Wenholt got answered, and no schoolmistress alive could have stopped them.

And in the workshop's darkest corner, behind the glaze jars, the private shelf kept its own small register: Grams Ilsabet's first pot, thumb-marked and honest; the two pieces of a broken jar-lid, edges ground smooth by a mason who wasted nothing, laid slightly open, like a door ajar; and a dish.

Dusting that shelf was Perrin Loft's office, the prohibition on his touching it having died quietly of obsolescence, the way Wenholt's rules mostly died. Perrin at seventeen was still made of elbows but no longer only of elbows; he could center four pounds of clay without chasing it, had taken one telling in his life and carried it true, and dusted the private shelf the way other people visit family graves — regularly, gently, and with conversation. So it was no surprise, on the evening the first oat-field came down, to find him stopped in front of the dish, duster forgotten in his hand, his sideways look on, listening to it with the wrong sense on purpose, the way she'd taught him.

It was not much to look at, the dish. Wide, shallow, the kind of dish a goose goes to table on; glazed the grey-green of the Ler under a September sky, with one thread of gold wandered through like low light on water; and straight across it, end to end, a fine ringing crack — hairline at the rims, a whisker's width at the heart — so that anyone with hands to feel it knew, inside a held minute, what the private shelf knew: that the dish had a rowing race in it, and that the race ran only half its course and stopped forever mid-stroke.

"Mistress," Perrin said, not turning around. "The oats came down at Askews' today. First field of the year."

"I saw it off the window. You'll be wanting supper early, if you're planning to say whatever it is you've been three days loading."

"You told me at summer's end," he said, "when I asked whether a fire could ever be asked to finish a race — you said, wash the dust off, that's a harvest story." He turned around then, the duster in one hand and the other flat on his breastbone, over the pocket where a carry once sat, which he likely did not know he did. "It's harvest, mistress. I've washed the dust off."

Edda looked at him a long moment. Then she swung the kettle over the heat, because no version of this evening began without tea, and while it climbed she lit the second lamp, carried it to the corner, and — for the first time in longer than she cared to count — took the dish down off the shelf.

"Hold it with me," she said. "Both hands. You've listened to it sideways three years; you may as well hear it straight through once, and know why I've never let you."

They held it between them, four hands on the grey-green glaze, and the dish did what it had done every time for twelve years, which was everything it could.

The race ran. Not visibly — nothing so cheap — but the way kept things ran: cold bright water-morning, and the particular carrying hush of a crowd along two banks; the knock and feather of oars finding their swing; gulls up off the flats; spray, breath, the green shoulder of the river leaning into its own current at the mill bend; two boats level, nothing between them but noise; the stroke rising, rising, the whole morning gathering itself the way a laugh gathers in a chest — and a girl's laugh beginning, actually beginning, bright and helpless over the water —

— and stopping. All of it. Mid-stroke, between the catch and the pull, the way a dance stops when a door slams; and after it nothing, not even quiet — the held way a keyhole holds after the house is gone.

Perrin set his end of the dish down on the bench as gently as he had once set down a honey-glazed jar, and moved his hands away from it, and looked at them.

"It doesn't even end," he said. "Things end. That doesn't end, it —" He hunted for it, elbows and all, and found it, because the craft kept arriving in him at inconvenient hours: "It's mid-stroke. Forever. A held breath nobody's ever let out. Mistress, whose is it? Twelve years I've dusted this shelf and you've never once said, and it's the only pot on it you go quiet past."

The kettle arrived at its note. Edda let it sing a moment before she lifted it, which was not like her; and she poured, and put a warm cup into his hands, and kept her own around her crooked one until the peas-and-summer smell of it steadied her, which was what the cup was for.

"Sit down, boy," she said. "No — not the good chair. The good chair's for tellers, and tonight I'm the teller, and some tellings want giving standing, the way you'd stand in a moot to be sentenced." She turned her cup a quarter-turn, caught herself winding, and let herself wind; she had earned no better. "That dish is the only pot on that shelf I have never told you the truth about. Nor anyone living, but one — and she's forgotten it, which is half the truth and the worse half. Pots don't lie, Perrin. Their keepers do, by keeping quiet, one harvest at a time, for twelve of them."

"You," said Perrin. Terrible directness; she'd taken him on for it; there were evenings she remembered why, and this would have to be one. "It's yours. The mistake."

"Mine as the frost let it be, and mine as shame kept it after, and the whole of it you'll have tomorrow, by daylight, out of the ledger where I wrote it down the week it happened — a confession without its paper is just a mood, and you're old enough now to hold the page. Tonight you'll have the shape." She looked at the dish, lying in the lamplight with its crack turned up, and gave him the shape the way you hand over something with an edge. "Fifty years ago this Harvest Home, two boats rowed from the ferry steps to the mill bend, and everyone in this valley over sixty was on the banks, and the whole parish will tell you to this day how that race ended. Twelve years ago somebody who was in one of those boats told the race into my keeping, into that dish, for a gift — a secret, banked long ahead, against a harvest still years off coming. And I fired it, and the frost got into my cooling, and the ware dunted, and the crack you just heard fell where it fell. And the harvest it was banked against, Perrin —" she drank, because her mouth had gone dry, and the tea was there, and the tea was the point "— is this one. It's come due, boy. The gift, the debt, and the truth, all three."

The boy was quiet for a long time, for Perrin. Out the window the moon was coming up over the ridge, patient as a customer, over a valley half gold and half stubble; and down the garden the kiln stood in its open shed against the first stars, warm at the heart-bricks as it was always warm, keeping all it bore and bearing all it kept.

"Then we're mending it," said Perrin Loft — not a question, and the we not a question either.

"Drink your tea," said Edda, which was not an answer; and put her hand flat on the old dish, over the crack, the way she'd kept her hand on ailing things all her life; which was.

# Chapter Two — The Short Entry

The ledgers of the Wenholt witches forgave nothing, which was their whole use; a witch in want of forgiving had to go and earn it somewhere off the page.

There were nine of them, in the elm chest under the telling shelf, one hand giving way to the next mid-book the way one ferryman hands the sweep to another mid-river; and the ninth was Edda's, twelve years of the parish's commissions in a square workshop hand that had grown squarer as the years taught it what ink was for. She had it out on the bench before Perrin had his apron on, the next morning, open to a page she had not needed to hunt for. You do not hunt for the stair that creaks. You know it in the dark, in your stockings, at every age you will ever be.

"Read it," she said. "Aloud. It was written to be read by whichever witch came after me, and it's the one entry in that book I wrote hoping she'd be cleverer than I was. As it falls out, he will need to be cleverer by the equinox, so read."

The entry was seven lines long, in a hand Perrin visibly did not know for hers — younger, tighter, the letters leaning on each other like new lambs — and he read it the way she'd taught him to handle bisqueware, as if his voice might put a thumb through it:

*Taken, the 9th September, a keeping: a rowing race, told entire, into a dish. For G., of the ferry; her gift, banked against a harvest to come; no fee till claimed.*

*Fired the 12th. Frost came hard on the first night of the cooling, early even for that black autumn. My clamming was green and my patience greener.*

*The ware dunted. One crack, clean through, and it rang like a bell when it went. I was in the garden and heard it and knew.*

*What lies past the crack is gone out of the ware. Half the keeping runs. Half does not, and will not.*

*Owner told the whole of it by me, plainly, the 15th.*

*She said: keep it, dear, till it's mended. She has not asked after it since, and I do not think she will ask again.*

*Kept, at the owner's word. Not sold, not shown, not spoken of. — E.M., her first autumn alone.*

The workshop was quiet a moment, the good quiet of a room letting a thing land. Then Perrin, who had been raised properly and ruined slightly by three years of her company, said: "G., of the ferry," and looked up, and all the elbows went out of him at once. "Griet. It's Griet's race. The Harvest Rows — the one the whole valley tells — that's the race in the dish. Mistress, that's the race *Wim won*."

"That is the race the village says Wim won," said Edda, "which is a different pot, and you'll learn to tell them apart or you'll be a poor witch and a worse gossip. Sit down and I'll give you what the ledger was too ashamed to hold. It's short. Shame writes short."

She told it to the wedging bench, mostly, and to the window, because twelve years had not made it tellable to a face.

"It was my first autumn alone. Ilsabet was new in the churchyard, and every telling that came into me that season I carried the way you carried your first, boy: like a bird's egg the size of my head, certain the whole time that the real witch would be back any day to take it off me. And in the middle of that, on a clear September morning, up my lane came Griet of the ferry — alone, mind. She'd rowed herself over. Miller's daughter; she could row before she could plait; folk forget that about Griet, the tide's taken so much else. And she sat in the good chair and turned her cup and said she'd a thing to bank with me, and it was to be a secret, and the secret had to keep better than she could."

"She knew," Perrin said quietly. "Already. Twelve years ago."

"She knew first. That's the thing about the going-out, Perrin, and you'll want it someday so keep it: the one going out the door feels the draught before anyone in the room does. There was nothing to see for years after — you'd have called her sharp as a pin, and mostly she was. But she sat in my chair with her hands round the warm and said — I'll give it you as near her words as twelve years leaves me — *there's a thing I mean to give my Wim at our fiftieth harvest, and I'll not trust my own keeping of it so far as that. It's begun to turn on me, dear. I feel it of a morning, like a tide deciding. So you'll bank it, and when the fiftieth comes round, whatever's left of me, the dish will mind it for both of us.* And then she told me the race."

Edda turned her cup a quarter-turn and did not catch herself.

"I'll not tell you what she told me. Partly that's the chair's rule and partly it's the plain truth: I couldn't. A witch is a channel and not a cistern; what goes through me goes, and what I could give you this morning is the weight of it and the colour of it and not three words of the words. The weight was — considerable. Fifty years of marriage rowed into one morning; you felt the whole of the rest of it leaning on that race the way a barrel leans on its keystone. And the colour —" she nodded at the dish, at the thread wandering through the grey-green "— the slip went river-green with one thread of gold through it, and I have never had that colour off any telling since, and I put it under a clear glaze so it would show, because I was young in the trade and proud of it, God help me, proud as a baker with a crust."

"And then the frost."

"And then the frost. I fired it three nights on, a small load, a good fire — nothing wrong with the fire; the fire did all it was asked. It was the cooling. Frost came down that night like a debt-collector, first hard frost of the year and weeks early, and my clamming had a green witch's haste in it, and somewhere before dawn the cold got its finger through and touched the ware while the ware was still speaking." She had come to the bench now; she put one finger, very lightly, on the crack, at the heart of the dish where it ran widest. "Dunting, we call it, and I'd had the word off Ilsabet a hundred times and learned it that night the way you learn words: alone, in a garden, in the dark, hearing a bell ring once inside your own kiln and knowing. A dunt is a small death, Perrin. The crack doesn't cut the keeping the way a knife cuts bread, both halves lying there beside each other. What lies past a dunt goes *out*. It went out of that dish the moment the ring went round it, and what stayed is what you held last night: half a race, stopped mid-stroke, forever, at —" and here twelve years of not saying it made the saying slow "— at her laugh. The crack falls at her laugh. Of all the places in a race a frost could choose."

Perrin looked at the dish for a while. When he spoke it was carefully, the way you walk on a mended leg. "The fifteenth. *Owner told the whole of it by me.* You told her."

"I sent for her and told her plain, because Ilsabet held that a witch who softens her own failures is spending the craft's honesty on her own comfort. Griet came, and held the dish, and felt it stop — I watched her feel it stop, boy; I stood where you're standing and watched fifty years of her stop mid-stroke in her two hands — and she was quiet a long time. And then she patted my hand. Mine. As if I'd been the one holding the cracked thing. And she said: *keep it, dear, till it's mended.* No blame in it, no question but that mending was a thing days did. And she went home across her river, and the tide went on deciding, and by the harvest after, when I'd steeled myself to raise it — I did raise it; give me that much — she looked at me kindly the way she looks now, and asked was it a dish of her mother's, for she didn't mind it. She never asked again. Wim was never told. It wasn't mine to tell; a telling's private as a grave, and the gift was hers, and to spend her secret to ease my conscience — no. That's the clean reason. The other reason is that confession wants a mend to offer, and I had none, and you may write on my stone that I looked for one harder some years than others."

The kettle, unbidden, had been climbing all this while, and found its note, and Edda went to it out of pure liturgy; and Perrin did the sum a seventeen-year-old does faster than a witch who has been avoiding it for a decade.

"Fifty years this Harvest Home," he said. "You said it last night. Their fiftieth harvest, mistress — it's *this* one. The one she banked it against. The dish comes due in a month." And then, with the terrible directness, straight as a strike-stick: "Then it's owed, mistress. Twelve years' interest."

"Twelve years' interest," Edda agreed, "on a debt where the coin is half a race, and the half I owe is the half that's gone out of the ware entire. So attend, because here is the whole arithmetic of the thing, and it beat me for twelve years and I want better heads than mine on it now — which is you, and a mason, and whoever else this village decides it is, the village generally deciding it's everyone." She held up one finger. "In the dish: the race to her laugh, and not one stroke past it, fired and fast and true as far as it runs. In me: nothing; the channel doesn't keep." A second finger. "In Griet: whatever the tide has left, which is not nothing, mind — the hands remember longest — but is not a telling, and no fire of mine or anyone's will ever go looking in her for it; that border you know, and this parish knows, and I'll say it again at every gate where it wants saying." A third finger, held longest. "Which leaves one place in the living world where the finish of the Harvest Rows still runs entire, morning and laugh and post and all. One old man, who rowed it, and has pulled a ferry-sweep across that same water every working day of the fifty years since."

"Wim," said Perrin. "Wim's got the finish."

"Wim has the finish, and Wim doesn't know his wife banked the race, and Wim doesn't know a dish exists, and Wim has been let believe for twelve years that the witch of Wenholt keeps nothing of his but a porridge bowl's snow and three years of heron-bowls. So the first mend, boy, is not clay at all." She took her shawl off its hook, and the ledger stayed open on the bench behind them, the short entry face-up in the morning light, twelve years old and waiting, like everything else in this story, for its answering line. "Wash your hands and mind the shop. I'm for the ferry."

# Chapter Three — What the Frost Took

News that has waited twelve years does not ripen; it only sits, and the sitting is on the one who kept it.

Edda went down to the crossing in the middle of the morning, through a village up to its elbows in harvest — carts on Bake Lane, stubble-dust in the sunlight, Maud Brock's ovens going like a foundry against the noon bait — and the walk was not long enough by half, because she had spent it the way she had spent a fair part of twelve years, rehearsing sentences and finding them all either too soft to be honest or too hard to be kind. Ilsabet had held, usually while shelling peas, that a truth worth telling walked better without its dressing. Ilsabet had never had to carry this particular truth down this particular lane, but then, that was what the dead were for: advice with no luggage.

The ferry-house sat above the landing with its door open to the morning, and Griet was in it.

She was at the door before Edda's boot found the bottom step, small and soft-wrapped and pleased, her white hair done neat as it was always done — Wim's work now, and nobody in the parish had ever once let on they knew it — and she said, warm as the doorstep flags: "There you are, dear. You're early for the fair. The boats won't go till Wim's had his dinner."

The fair at Harrowgate was two months gone. The kettle behind her, on the hook over the fire, was on and just short of singing, and there were two cups set out on the table as though the morning had told her to expect somebody, and perhaps it had. Griet had lost the day — the day slipped its mooring most mornings now — but she had not lost the kettle. The kettle was kept somewhere lower than days, down in the hands, where the tide didn't reach.

"I'll wait for the boats, then," said Edda, "and gladly, if there's tea in it," and sat where she was put, and drank what she was given, and let herself be told about the herons, which were the same herons and always would be; and if her eye went once to the shelf by the window — where a porridge bowl the grey-blue of dawn snow stood in the morning light, first thing a waking eye would find, the lamp in the window of a house whose doors were going — she did not let her face go with it.

Wim came up from the water with his oilskin over his shoulder, took in the visitor, the teapot, and the witch's face, in that order, and said: "Griet, love, the witch and I have a crossing to make." And to Edda, no question in it: "Talk's wanted, I take it. Talk wants water. It's the one place in this parish it can't be overheard except by them as keep secrets professionally." The dryness was for his wife's benefit, and his wife laughed, and put her shawl on with the sure hands of a woman who had put a shawl on for the water ten thousand times, and came with them down to the boat as a matter of course; and neither the ferryman nor the witch said otherwise, because there was no otherwise. Where the boat went, Griet went. That was the ferry now.

He pulled them out into the stream with the long unhurried strokes of a man who had worn his own groove in the river, and for a while nobody said anything, and the water said what it always said. Griet sat in the stern with her face to the sun. And presently — Edda watched it begin, and felt the back of her neck go cold in the warm morning — Griet's hand began to beat time against her knee. Not wandering; not fidget. Time. Stroke for stroke with the oars, catch and pull, a little ahead of each catch the way a coxswain's call runs a little ahead of the blades; an old woman keeping a stroke-count her own morning could not have told you the name of, her eyes half-shut, pleasure on her face, sixty years down in the hands where the tide didn't reach.

Wim rowed, and watched his wife's hand, and said nothing with his whole back.

Midstream, Edda told him.

She told it the way the ledger told it, plain and in order, no dressing: the September morning twelve years back; his wife at her gate, come rowing over alone with a secret; what the secret was, and what harvest it was banked against, and why Griet had trusted a dish to keep it sooner than herself; the firing; the frost; the ring in the dark; the crack, and where the crack fell; *keep it, dear, till it's mended*; and the twelve years on a shelf that no confession had come off of, and whose fault that was, and it was not the frost's. She did not soften her own part by a thread. She had promised herself that on the lane, and it was the one sentence of all her rehearsed ones that survived the water.

Wim stopped rowing somewhere in the middle of it. Not suddenly; the strokes just widened out and went, the way a tune goes when the whistler starts listening to something. The boat came round slow with the current, the mill bend turning green and easy off the bow, and the ferryman of Wenholt sat with his two hands still on the oar-looms, holding them the way a man holds a gate he has just been told the news at, and looked at nothing, out over the water he had crossed all his life. His stillness was the stillness of the heron tribe: total, unhurried, and not to be knocked on. In the stern his wife's hand had gone on beating time a few strokes after the oars stopped, and slowed, and settled; and she watched the flats contentedly, out of whatever morning she was in.

"Fifty years she kept a surprise," Wim said at last. His voice was dry as a pulled-up boat; his eyes were not, and he did not trouble to pretend otherwise, being past the age where a man's face is anyone's business but his own. "Fifty years. In this parish. Where a sneeze at the ferry's news on Bake Lane by dinner." He shook his head slowly, and the wonder in it was real, and so was the grief under the wonder, handled the way rivermen handle everything, with the wet side kept to the water. "Frost's the only thing in this valley ever got ahead of her. I could have told it so. It come for her twice, and both times early."

"Wim. I should have come to you twelve years ago. The secret was hers, and I'll stand on that half of it, but the other half was shame, and shame's poor cargo; it does nothing all voyage and rots what it's packed with. I'm sorry. Sorrier than the word's worth."

"You're the second-sorriest soul in this boat, then," said Wim, "for I've heard what she paid for that dish, and it wasn't your fee." He looked at Griet's hand on her knee, quiet now, and something moved over his face like light off water onto a wall. "She felt it going, and the first thing she did about it — the *first* thing, before doctors, before me — was row across to bank a gift twelve years out. That's Griet entire. That's her to the keel." He took up the oars again, squared the boat to the current, and did not pull yet. "Well, witch. You didn't row out here to be forgiven, or I hope you didn't, for I've no use for the job. What's wanted?"

So she told him the rest: what a dunt kills; what the dish still held, and to the syllable where it stopped; and the one place in the living world where the finish of the Harvest Rows still ran entire. And here is where, in twelve years of imagining this conversation, Edda had always imagined wrong. She had imagined telling an old man that his wife's gift was broken. She had not imagined asking him for the finish of a race, and watching a fifty-year-old amusement come up through the grief like a spring through a field.

"The finish," said Wim. "Aye. I've got the finish." And nothing else. The amusement sat in the seams of his face and declined to explain itself.

"The whole parish has the finish, if you ask it," Edda said, probing exactly as far as manners went. "They'll give you the year you won the Rows down to the length of the winning."

"They will," Wim agreed, comfortably, and rowed, and it was precisely the comfort a man shows when he has decided a subject is closed; and Edda, who had spent forty years listening to what people did not say, heard the shape of something under the water of it, the way you feel a boat pass over a deep place — and minded it later, and said nothing then.

"When the craft's ready — if it can be made ready; I'll not promise a mend I've not proven — it'll want the race told. Told entire, Wim, in the good chair, start to post, by the one who holds it. Not now; the telling can't sit carried for weeks, and there's craft to build first, and a fire that doesn't exist yet. But I'd have your yes before I set a single soul to work, because without the finish there's no mend, only a tidier crack."

"Then you've my yes," said Wim, "and two conditions on it, one now and one when I've thought." He feathered the oars and let the boat run a moment. "The one now: you'll get the race at the proper hour and not a stroke of it sooner. Don't ask me how it ended, witch — not you, not the boy, not Odile Vance with her almanac face. Fifty years that finish has waited on its own harvest; it can wait out a fortnight more. A finish belongs at the end of its race. Say it now, it's only news." He pulled, one long stroke, and the bow came round for home. "And the other condition I'll name when I've thought. A ferryman prices a crossing after he's seen the load."

They came back up the river with the sun high, and Griet's hand took up its time-beating again the moment the stroke settled, catch and pull, catch and pull; and Wim watched it the whole way home, rowing to his wife's count without letting her see him do it, which was, Edda thought, as exact a picture of the man's whole marriage as the parish was ever going to be shown.

At the landing he handed his wife up onto the stage, courtly as a young man, and she went up the path to her open door and her kettle. Wim stood a moment with the painter in his hand.

"She beats time," he said. "Every crossing, this last year or so. Doesn't know she does it. First I thought it was the boat's rhythm got into her — it does that, boats. Then I minded it's a stroke she's beating, and not my stroke neither. Mine's longer. That's a racing stroke, witch, short and proud, and she's had nothing to hang it on these dozen years, and the hands keep it anyway." He looked up the path after her, and the October that was coming stood in his face a moment alongside the fifty Septembers that had been. "The hands are the last out the door. Whatever your fire can do or can't — the hands'll know that dish when they hold it. Mind you get it to them mended."

# Chapter Four — The Third Temper

Councils of war are held over maps; councils of craft, in Wenholt, were held over a teapot, and were the more binding of the two.

This one convened at the workshop bench the following evening: the witch; the apprentice; the mason, sent for by a note that said only *bring your eyes and your father's level*; and, in the doorway, Nell Rooke, who had walked over from the clay-pit cottage carrying the supper Lena refused to believe Edda would otherwise eat, and who showed no sign whatever of walking back. Ansel Rooke had been the kiln's own mason and the parish's for a year now, a big quiet man whom the village had rebuilt as thoroughly as he had rebuilt the kiln; and he stood at the bench with the lamplight on his lime-bitten knuckles and held the dish the way he held everything, as though it might be listening, and felt the race run and stop mid-stroke, and set it down, and was silent for the length of time it took the kettle to climb halfway to its note.

"Well," he said at last. "There's my evening's education. I've handled dunted ware all my working life — a cracked quarry tile's no rarity, nor a rung brick. I never once had one stop a *story* in my hands." He turned the dish gently, rim over rim, reading the crack with one thumb the way other men read a plumb-line. "The crack itself, now. That's twelve years old and finished. Clean, one line, no spider to it, no spring in the halves — a crack that's done travelling and taken up residence. As breaks go, mistress, it's an honest one. But you're not asking me about the break. You're asking me about the break *in a fire*, and that's another country. Heat wakes cracks. It's near enough what a dunt is — heat's grudge, collected late. Put this dish through a second firing the ordinary way and I'd not give you odds on it; the crack drinks the heat faster than the body does, the two halves breathe out of step, and somewhere around the first cone your one honest crack is three, and then it's shards, and whatever half a race is still in it —"

"— dies," said Edda. "For good. There's no third telling of this race, Ansel; the teller's telling days are done, and the finish lives in one head aged seventy. If the crack runs in the fire, the Harvest Rows stop mid-stroke forever, and I'll have spent Griet's gift to buy my own conscience a headstone. That's the stake, and everyone at this bench will know it plainly or work on something else." She said it to the room, but it was Nell's supper-basket she said it over, because Nell had come all the way in by then and taken the stool nobody had offered her, in the manner of her mother arranging an invoice.

"So don't fire it the ordinary way," said Nell. "You've two extraordinary ways in the family ledger already, by all accounts. Which one mends?"

"Neither," said Edda, "and there's the wall I've stood twelve years in front of, so come stand at it with me; it's roomier than it looks." She poured, and handed cups down the bench, and while they warmed their hands she laid the craft out flat, the way she'd have laid it for Ilsabet to check her working. "The fire has tempers, and we know three. The daily one: the keeping fire, fed and eased by feel, that draws a telling out of the witch and settles it in the clay. And two others, one at each end of the year, one at each end of the fire's nature. Starved —" she did not point at the shelf, and everyone's eyes went to it anyway, to the two smooth-ground pieces of a lid lying slightly open "— a reduction fire, choked down hungry in the black of a midwinter moon-dark, that reaches into the ware and *takes*: done once in four generations, and the once was mercy, and the jar it filled sits empty in Stonebeck sunshine now, so I'll hear no dark talk of it. And fed: an oxidation fire run generous, wide-breathed, at midsummer full moon, that opens its hand and *gives back* — taught to this kiln this very midsummer, in front of most of this parish. Takes; gives back. Pull; pour. And a mend, children, is neither. If I run a hungry fire at that dish, it will pull — and what it pulls on is a keeping held in cracked ware by twelve years of habit, and it will pull the race out through the crack like a nail out of wet plaster. And if I run a giving fire, it pours — and what it pours is whatever fresh telling rides to that fire in the carrier, and a fresh telling is *loud*, Ansel, it's new-cut, it has a living voice behind it; pour it into that dish over the old half and it will speak over Griet's telling the way a young choir drowns an old bell. Either temper, one half of the race eats the other. And the race is only the race if both halves of it are true."

"Then what does the fire do," said Nell Rooke, slowly, with her chin on her fists and her mother's eyes gone narrow, "when it neither pulls nor pours?"

The kettle found its note. Nobody moved to lift it.

"Say that again," said Edda.

"You've a fire that takes and a fire that gives. Every trade I ever watched has three of everything — my mother's books have owed, owing, and *settled*; Uncle Anvil's walls have lean-to, lean-from, and *plumb*. So: what's the fire's plumb? What does it do held dead level, neither hungry nor generous, all night — not asked for anything at all?"

"It listens," said Edda Marren; and heard, as she said it, the click of a thing arriving true, like a keystone going home under the tap of a level; and had to set her cup down, because her hands had gone stupid with the size of it. "Twelve years I've asked what fire mends, and the answer's been sitting in the middle of the question. None of them mends. Mending's not a doing, it's a *refraining* — it's two tellings meeting as equals with neither given the whip hand, the old half in the ware and the new half in the carrier, and a fire between them that pulls neither way, favors neither, a fire held plumb from sundown to sunrise so the two halves have the whole night to find each other across the crack and *knit*. Not the fire's mend at all. The fire just holds the room steady while the race mends itself. The third temper. Level."

"A fire run level all night," said Ansel, in the voice of a man pricing a cathedral, "is a fire run against its own nature the whole of it. Fires don't sit plumb; that's the first thing a fire wants you to know about it. It runs rich or it runs lean, it's always leaning one way or falling the other — you of all people teach that, mistress; feed it easy, starve it deep. To hold one dead level, dark to dawn —" He stopped. Being Ansel, he was already building it. "It'd be like rowing a straight course across a current. You'd never once *be* level. You'd be correcting, stroke by stroke, all night, two ways at once."

"Two stokers," said Perrin suddenly, from the wedging bench. "Pulling even. One feeding against lean, one easing against rich, turn and turn about — and somebody watching the flame who isn't stoking at all, calling which."

"You're crewing a pairs-boat, Uncle Anvil," said Nell, into her teacup, dry as her mother's ink. "Two oars and a cox. For a *race*. I'd say somebody's fire has a sense of occasion."

And there it sat, in the lamplight, grinning at all four of them: the shape of the thing, arrived whole the way true shapes did. Edda gave it the silence it had earned, one full lap of the workshop, before she put the practical teeth back in.

"When. A level fire wants the year's leave, same as the other two tempers had it. Starved ran at midwinter's black; fed ran at midsummer's full. The fire's plumb runs at the balance — the autumn equinox, day and night in equal halves, the harvest moon to light the crew. And before anyone reaches for the almanac —" she had reached for it herself that morning, and found what some part of her had known since the ledger page "— the equinox this year falls in Harvest Home week. To the week, the fiftieth. I've stopped calling that luck. The year's been keeping this appointment longer than we have."

"Then hear the mason's terms, for the ware's side of it," said Ansel, and set his father's level on the bench between them, the worn oak and brass of it, the way another man would have put down his hand of cards. "First: that dish doesn't stand in any fire, cracked as she is, on her own two feet — she rides. A cradle-saggar, purpose-built: river clay, thick-walled, bedded soft, made to carry a broken thing through a fire the way you'd carry a hurt man over a ford. I'll build it. Second: a mend wants makings. You can't knit across nothing — a crack's a gap, however fine, and the knit needs a seam packed into it before ever the fire sees it: ground glaze of the dish's own kind — you'll have wasters of that batch in the shard-pile, knowing you — and sand of the Ler, for the body. And a flux to carry the melt, which wants ash, and there's where I stop, because ash *remembers*. Every ash is its wood; that's half your glaze-craft, you've taught me. Apple ash for laughs. So what wood does a man burn for the seam of a fifty-year race? Not any stick that's handy. That answer's not at this bench tonight, and I'll not let us invent it just to feel finished. Some answers you wait for."

"And third," said Edda, "since you're too polite to say it: you can't run a fire level without a level. That one rides the arch, the whole night, where the crew can see it. Witness and instrument both." She looked round the bench — the mason, the girl, the boy, the teapot; as war-councils went, Wenholt's ran small and drank more — and named the last brick plainly. "And nothing of this touches Griet's dish until the temper's proven. A first-ever fire gets proven on lesser freight; that law's older than I am and it held good last year and it holds now. Somewhere in this parish is a crack with living owners and a smaller grief in it, and before the equinox we'll have found it and asked it to go first. The village will see to that part."

"The village," said Nell, standing, gathering her mother's basket, entirely unabashed, "has been listening under the window this half hour, in the form of me, and will have it all over both banks by kettle-time tomorrow. You knew that when you let me stop in the doorway." She kissed the top of the witch's head, which nobody in the parish but Nell Rooke would have dared, and went out into the dark; and the moon, rising past the gate as she went, was a fattening harvest moon, patient as a customer, with three weeks' custom yet to come.

# Chapter Five — Boats and Opinions

By noon of the second day the whole village knew, in the way of villages — everything, immediately, and mostly wrong — and this time the mostly-wrong had a fifty-year head start on the news.

The versions, as collected by Hessa across the Copper Kettle's counter and delivered to Edda in her operating whisper, ran roughly so: that the witch had broken a dish of Griet's and must mend it before the frost or forfeit her craft (half true, if you squinted); that the dish held the famous race, the one old Wim won the year he got his Griet (the parish said this part with total confidence, which Edda noted and filed); that the mending wanted a fire never yet fired, on the equinox, with a spirit level in it somewhere (near enough); and — this version quietly, hopefully, from more than one mouth, and it was the one that mattered — that the witch had found a fire at last that would give Griet her remembering back.

That one Edda killed the same afternoon, publicly, on her feet in the middle of the Copper Kettle, in the voice she kept for borders.

"Hear it plain, and carry it plain, because I'll know whose version I meet coming back up the lane. What's going out in Griet is no fire's doing, and no fire of mine or any witch's will ever be pointed at it. What shuts those doors, nothing opens — I'd have burned the workshop years ago to warm one hinge of them else, and so I told the schoolmistress on the water last summer, and so I tell you now. What the dish can be, if the craft holds, is what the porridge bowl is of a morning: a thing in the room. A lamp in a window. It is not the house, and anyone who goes about this parish selling it as the house will answer to me at my gate." She picked her basket back up. "The tea's very good, Hessa. I'll have the other half of it sitting down."

The village took the correction the way Wenholt took most corrections — gratefully, once it had checked the corrector's face to see how much hope it was still permitted — and having been told what the mend was not, threw itself with entire good cheer into deciding what the mend required. And what the mend required, the parish concluded inside a week, in the way it had once concluded that a cracked kiln required the whole village's bricks, was obvious: if the witch's fire was to finish the Harvest Rows, then Wenholt was going to row them.

The old race had lapsed a generation back — a wet year, then a lean year, then a custom nobody quite remembered dropping — but it had never gone out of the valley's blood, because the lore of its last great running was told at every harvest supper: how young Wim of the ferry and Dob Askew had pulled the course of their lives, ferry steps to the mill bend, and taken the post by daylight, and how Wim had worn the Harvest Crown — the last-sheaf plait, that the winner wears to the dance and by right of it chooses their partner for the first reel — and chosen his Griet out of the whole bank of watchers, and married her by Wintermark. Half the parish told it that way, and the other half told it that way louder. Edda, standing in the moot-hall listening to men younger than the race describe its finish in loving detail, held her tongue with both hands. She knew exactly one fact the tellers didn't: that a dish on her shelf ran out of race two lengths from the post; and one fact more, which was the shape of something under the water of an old man's comfortable silence; and neither fact was hers to spend.

Odile Vance, it turned out, was the loudest of the accurate rememberers, which was a sentence Edda had never expected to think.

"I was six," the reeve said at the moot, dry as a good sherry, in answer to no motion whatever, "and my father held me up on his shoulders, and I remember it perfectly: Wim of the ferry won by a clear length, and my father lost a shilling on the miller's girls and swore in front of the vicar. I will thank the parish not to embroider a race I personally witnessed." Which settled it, as far as the moot-hall was concerned, the reeve having been right about drainage for twenty years; and Edda looked at Odile's certain face, and thought about what six years old does to a finish two banks away, and said nothing at all, with some effort.

The moot itself went the way Wenholt moots went when the village had already decided: fast, and then slowly over details. The Harvest Rows would be rowed again this Harvest Home, the fiftieth of the lore; the wardens would pay for parish oak, Odile attaching her conditions and making herself useful in the same breath, as was her gift; Cobb would cart the timber down, and had his hat off about it before the vote finished, cartage of this kind being to Cobb a graver honor than logs. The two old pairs-boats still hung in the tithe-barn rafters, where the lapsed custom had put them up a generation since — dry as biscuits, sound in the keel, sorry in the strakes — and Ansel Rooke and Tom Chandler were charged with making them true again, an alliance of the parish's two most careful men that Wenholt looked forward to eavesdropping on immoderately, Tom Chandler never in living memory having finished a sentence and Ansel Rooke never having started one he didn't mean to finish.

Crews were the noisy part. The Askew boys claimed the first boat before the motion was fairly read, on grounds of blood: their great-uncle Dob had pulled bow in the great race itself — "in the boat that *won*, mind" — and the family had therefore, they explained over each other, a standing to defend, an inheritance of form, and a rowing style passed down father to son. ("Falling in," said someone at the back, "is also passed down father to son," and the moot had to be gavelled.) The second boat went quieter. Nell Rooke stood, said that the clay-pit cottage would answer for one oar of it; and, asked about the other, looked across the hall at the witch's apprentice with her mother's eyes and said, "Loft rows Sundays for the ferry when Wim's hip is wrong. He keeps a count and he does what the count says. He'll do." Perrin, seventeen, went the color of a salmon brick, kept — Edda observed with pride — both his feet and his silence, and was voted an oarsman of Wenholt by acclamation between two heartbeats; and if his mistress foresaw certain complications in an apprentice rowing a race whose finish would shortly be riding under his shirt, she folded the thought away for its proper chapter and let the boy have his evening.

Wim attended the whole moot from the bench by the door and said one thing only. Asked, half-reverently, by the elder Askew boy, whether he'd judge the finish himself, seeing as he was the last man living who'd rowed the old course — the old reeve who called his race being fifty years in the churchyard — the ferryman turned his cap over twice and said: "No. I'll be otherwise engaged; I'm teaching a dish to mind its manners. Let the new reeve call it. She remembers it perfectly." Which the moot took for gallantry, and Odile took with a gracious nod; and only Edda, watching the seams of the old face, saw the amusement come up through it again, like a spring through a field, and go down before it was anybody's business.

Maud Brock held out longest, as was her office. The baker had been fifteen years Edda's licensed skeptic, and she worked the position honestly: mending, she announced to her bread-queue, was throwing good fire after cracked clay; a pot that had spoken once didn't speak twice; and the witch would do better firing the village's own commissions than teasing a poor dead dish through a poor dead argument — "and you may tell her I said so, since she's standing in my queue." Edda paid for her loaf and told her they were not square, which was liturgy, and went her way; and the argument was settled two mornings later, not by the witch at all, but by the Copper Kettle, where Maud stopped for her market tea and was served it, without one word of comment, in a two-handled cup glazed the blue of a kept April — a wedding cup thirty years unclaimed, opened by the summer's proving fire and standing in common service since, doing Tuesdays. Maud Brock drank her tea, and turned the cup a quarter-turn, and looked at it a long moment with her floury chin set; and put her penny down; and said, on her way out, to the room in general and nobody in particular: "Well. That one came back to work, I'll grant." Which was, from Maud, capitulation with full honors, and Hessa polished the counter and let it stand.

The letter from Stonebeck came at the week's end, in the careful hand, and was read aloud at the workshop over supper. *We are told, by the carrier, who had it from your Cobb, that Wenholt is mending a race and rowing one, in that order. The children have voted, over my one abstention, that a village which can be visited by a morning can be visited by us. I will bring the three whose people can spare them at harvest — they have held a morning, and they ought to see what a harvest is worth keeping. We will come by the lime-cart, boots over the tailboard. P.S. — the little ones wish it known that they have been practicing their rowing on the school bench, which now requires mending also. You see the kind of thing your parish exports.*

Perrin laughed so hard he had to put the letter down. Down the garden, in the long evening, the two old boats stood upside-down on trestles in the kiln-shed's lee, smelling of fresh oak and pitch, with a chimney-builder and a mason working one to each hull in a companionable quiet broken only by Tom Chandler starting sentences; and Ansel Rooke's palm, now and again, went flat along a strake the way it went along warm brick, feeling for what the wood remembered; and the harvest moon came up one night fatter over a village that had, without quite votes enough to make it official, already begun to enjoy itself.

# Chapter Six — The Race Told

Of all the things a witch must learn to take, the hardest is the one she must stand aside from.

Edda laid that lesson on the wedging bench two evenings before the telling, between the boy and his supper, where the large ones went.

"The equinox fire is mine to steer, and a level fire wants a level hand, so attend. A carrying witch *leans*. You know it; you've done it; a telling under the breastbone is a weight on one side of you, however kindly it sits, and all night at the stoke-mouth I'd be trimming against it without ever knowing which of my judgments was mine and which was the load's. Any other firing, no matter — the fire forgives a lean, it's leaning itself half the time. This fire is the one fire in the craft that cannot afford it. I go to the equinox empty, Perrin. Swept and dressed and nothing in the house. Which leaves the race —" she watched it arrive in his face, the way she'd once watched a bowl of black slip arrive there "— to you. Wim's telling. Taken by you, carried by you, dark to dawn through a fire no witch has ever run. Your second carry, boy. The craft's knocked twice now. It won't always knock."

Perrin was quiet a long moment, his hand flat below his collarbone, on the pocket where a Stonebeck clerk had lived for five days a season ago.

"The first one you gave me because I threw the vessel," he said. "This one there's no vessel. There's a dish another hand threw, and a crack, and a seam that doesn't exist yet, and a fire nobody's run. You're giving me the carry because it *must* be me, which isn't giving at all. It's needing." He looked up. "That's the trade grown up, isn't it. That's what it's like the whole way."

"That," said Edda, "is the truest thing said at this bench all year, and if you ever tell Nell Rooke I said so I'll fire you into a doorstop. Eat your supper. He comes at moonrise, two days hence."

Wim came at moonrise exactly, which from Wenholt folk was a statement — comfortably early was the custom — and Edda understood it the moment she saw him under the gate-lantern: he had come to the biggest telling of his life the way he'd have come to a crossing in weather, on the tide's terms, not a minute given away to nerves. He stamped his boots against the first real cold of the autumn and looked past her to where Perrin stood at the workshop door.

"So it's the boy takes it," he said. Not a question; the village had carried the craft's reasoning to both banks days ago, mostly right for once. He considered the apprentice a long, riverman's moment — seventeen, scrubbed, terrified in good order, standing like a man who intends to be stood by — and something in the old face settled, as if a sounding had come back deep enough. "Aye. He rows Sundays. He does what the count says, and he listens with his back. He'll do." And then, going in past him, with the dryness the parish would be quoting for a generation: "Mind, I'd have asked for references, but the last fellow you carried was a bookkeeper, I'm told, and they say he kept. That's reference enough for the ferry."

The workshop had been made ready the way it was always made ready, which is to say nothing had changed and everything had: the stove built up; the kettle just short of singing; one lamp turned low; and on the telling shelf, at the height of the witch's heart, eight bowls standing empty and a ninth waiting on the apprentice's knees. Edda kept the stove and the door and nothing else — teapot, threshold, and witness — and one thing more, agreed between all three at the gate: of the words said in the chair tonight she would keep none past the evening, and of the race's finish she would hear nothing at all. "I steer that fire blind and level," she'd said. "The less I know of the cargo, the truer I'll row." Wim had nodded once, as at a sensible bill of lading.

He took his tea black and his time considerable, turning his cup a quarter-turn and another, winding fifty years up out of the well; and Perrin settled the bowl of slip in his lap — right, first go — and went still in the way she'd taught him, sideways-listening; and the moon came up into the top of the window; and the ferryman of Wenholt began, the way the born tellers always began, in the middle of the weather.

"Harvest morning, and cold off the water first thing, and the sky rinsed — the kind of morning the valley gets three of a year and remembers as the whole autumn. Both banks lined from the ferry steps to the mill bend, the whole parish and half of Harrowgate's barges besides, and the old reeve up the far end at the post with his flag, and two boats at the steps. Ours, and theirs." The cup turned. "Dob Askew pulled my bow. Built like a gatepost, rowed like one too till you gave him a count to hate, and then he'd lay onto it like a man killing wasps — I never rowed with a better bow nor a louder one. And in the other boat the miller's daughters, Griet and her sister Bett, and the bank laughing at the pairing, two girls against the ferry — the bank laughed all morning." He looked into the fire. "The bank hadn't watched them bring sacks down the mill-race in spate. I had."

And the telling went down into the boat, and stayed there, which was the wonder of it; Edda had taken a lifetime's races and weddings and mornings told from the bank of themselves, and here was an old man handing a boy the *inside* of a thing, stroke by stroke, so that the workshop's lamplight seemed to move like water-light. The start, called off the reeve's dropped flag, and the first ten strokes all spray and iron, Dob's count in his ear like a hammer on a shoe; the shudder a boat gives when it decides to run; the mill bend's current coming green-shouldered off the bow, and the line you took against it if your father had taught you the river, and the line the miller's girls took, which was better, because their father had taught them the *mill*. The crowd's roar going down the bank like one long vowel. Bett's blade throwing light. And Griet — Griet level with him half the course, one boat's width of brown water between her oar and his, pulling her short proud stroke like the mill-race itself, her sleeves rolled, her plait coming down, close enough that between one catch and the next he could hear her breathing — "and I'd tell you tonight what I'd have told you that morning: there was never a thing on that river to watch like Griet rowing angry and happy in the same body, and the post coming up, and two lengths left in the race, dead level, the whole valley on its toes —"

The cup had stopped turning.

"— and she laughed."

The stove ticked. In the bowl on the apprentice's knees the slip had been changing this good while, past grey, past any spring green, into the deep river-green of the Ler under a rinsed autumn sky; and at the laugh, through the green, there rose one fine thread of gold, wandering, like low sun on water; and Edda Marren, standing at her stove with a teapot in her hands, saw twelve years close up like a book, and set the pot down before it told anyone her grip had gone.

What came after the laugh, no ear in the room could keep. Wim's voice dropped below the kettle's held note and stayed there — not whispering; deeper than whispering; the way the deep of a river goes on under its own surface, moving, entire, making no sound the air is allowed — and the rest of the race passed out of him wordless, the finish and all, down whatever channel runs between a teller and the one carrying. Three breaths, maybe four. Perrin's face changed once, in a way the stove-light caught and let go, and no one asked; and the slip in the bowl went still, green and gold, done.

"There," said Wim, into the quiet, and his voice was his own size again. He sat looking at his hands the way tellers did after, finding them lighter and not knowing where to put the difference. "Fifty years I've had it to myself. That's not the same as keeping it, mind. A thing you can't hand anyone — you don't keep that. It keeps you." He looked over at the boy who held his race, and the amusement came up through the old face and stayed, this once, long enough to be seen. "Lighter. Not emptier. There's a distinction your trade ought to put on a sign."

"Two on the rope," said Perrin. His voice came out half a size strange; a fifty-year stroke was bedding into him even as he sat, and his eyes were shining and slightly appalled, which was correct. "It's warm," he added, to his mistress, helpless. "Nobody ever says the warm."

"You said that the first time," said Edda.

"It's truer this time."

At the gate, with the moon high and the cold settling and the whole valley smelling of cut corn, Wim named his fee.

"Three things, witch, and none of them coin. First: when your fire's done its work, the first hands that dish goes into are Griet's. Not mine, not yours, not the reeve's for the dignity of the parish. Hers, warm from the wool, before another soul has held it mended. Second: after — it doesn't come back to your shelf, triumph or relic. It lives at the ferry-house, on the shelf by the window, next to the porridge bowl, where things that mind mornings live. And third." He set his cap on, and his voice did not change at all, which was how she knew what the third had cost to compose. "The telling-back. When your fire's given the race to the world again, it's told the way I hear the Stonebeck morning was told — to the room, entire, in front of the parish that's been telling it wrong for fifty years. At Harvest Home, in the moot-hall, with my Griet in the best chair in the house. She'll likely not know the day. She'll know the chair's warm and the room's kind and the story's hers — the hands'll know. That's my price. I've seen the load now. It's fair."

"Done," said Edda Marren, "all three, and cheap at the freight"; and the ferryman went down the lane through the moonlight, stepping like a man who has taken on ballast of the right kind; and behind her a boy stood at the telling shelf with one green-and-gold bowl among the eight empty, guarding a finish two lengths long, and down the garden the kiln stood warm at the heart-bricks, waiting to be taught its third temper.

"Not one word of it," she said to the workshop at large, going in. "Not in your sleep, not at your oar. You heard him: a finish belongs at the end of its race."

"Mistress," said Perrin, with dignity, "I'm the only soul in this parish who knows how the Harvest Rows come out, and I'm going to wedge clay until I stop feeling it wanting to tell me." Which he did; and if his hands, all that evening, kept falling into a stroke his arms had never learned — short, and proud, and a little ahead of the count — the wedging bench kept its counsel, and so did the moon.

# Chapter Seven — The Colour of the Ler

An ash remembers its wood, a glaze remembers its ground, and a mend that means to hold must be made of nothing that has forgotten where it came from.

That was the fortnight's working rule, and it sent the whole village back to the river. The cradle-saggar wanted clay, and the clay it wanted was the Ler's own — the bend below the ferry landing, the same bed the kiln was born of forty years back and the dish thrown from twelve, because a broken thing, Ansel held, rides easiest in a box that's kin to it. So on the first still morning after the moot, Wenholt went down to the water with spades and planks and its second wind, and dug.

Wim ferried diggers and grinned like a tide-mark. Maud Brock set her trestle on the bank with hot loaves and the wicked cinnamon milk, having capitulated with full honors and being resolved nobody should catch her at half measures now. Tally Weaver marshalled the puddling-gang by right of succession — the plank trough, the forty small feet, the shrieking; the Askew boys went into the pit twice and were hauled out on a three-count by their own crew, smug as men in training for greater waters. And Griet came down wrapped in three shawls to watch from a chair on the landing, on a morning that was not one of her clear ones and did not need to be, water and shouting and the smell of cut clay living lower than days; and she beat time against her knee for the puddling-gang, and called it the best dance of the year, which was no less true for being a keeping of the hands rather than the head.

Edda dug the saggar's clay herself, the first spit of it at least, a witch's superstition she had no intention of examining; and left the bank at noon, and went home to the shard-pile.

Every pottery keeps one, out behind the woodshed as a rule, and no potter looks at it if looking can be helped: the register of everything that did not come right, forty years of wasters deep, nettle-grown and honest as a churchyard. Edda went to one corner of it without hunting, the way you find the creaking stair in the dark, and turned back twelve years of weather with a trowel, and came up with three pieces of her first autumn alone: a trial tile, the better half of a slumped porringer, the shoulder of a jar kept by a green witch too poor in confidence to throw her failures far. All three glazed from one batch, the batch she had ground proud that September and never mixed again: grey-green under a clear coat, the exact colour of the Ler under a rinsed autumn sky, with a thread of gold that wandered where the thickness took it, like low sun on water.

"The dish's own kind," she said to Perrin that evening, setting the shards by the mortar. "Same batch, same burn, same stupid pride. A seam of foreign glaze would sit in that crack like a lodger; this will sit like family. Grind it fine, boy — finer than flour, fine as talk — and don't lose me a thumbnail's weight, for there's no more in the world and never will be."

Grinding fired glaze is slow work and loud, and Perrin did it in the mornings, mortar between his knees, and the pestle, Edda noticed and did not mention, went round to a stroke: short, proud, a little ahead of any count she'd have called. Into the ground glaze went washed sand of the Ler, sieved to silk, for the body of the seam. Which left the flux; which left the ash; which left the question Ansel had set on the bench a fortnight before and forbidden anyone to answer cleverly. Every ash is its wood. Apple ash for laughs. What wood does a man burn for the seam of a fifty-year race?

The answer came up the lane two evenings later, before moonrise, on a ferryman's shoulder.

It was an oar. Fifty years out of the water, grey as a heron, light as a biscuit, the leather of its button gone hard as horn — but an oar entire, made in the old pattern, and kept with evident care: not a worm-hole in it, not a split, the blade sound to the tip. Wim carried it in the way Cobb carried tellings, and stood it against the good chair, and looked at it a while before he spoke; and so did everyone else, the whole crew being there that evening, and none of them fool enough to hurry a ferryman with freight.

"You wanted wood for your seam," Wim said at last, "and it had to be told wood, your mason says — wood that was there. I've heard the parish all week offering you its gates and cradles and the beam off the tithe-barn. Well. This was there." He turned it in the lamplight, and his thumb found a place on the loom worn smoother than the rest, and rested on it, the way a thumb rests where it has rested before. "It was in my hands at the start of that race, and it wasn't in my hands at the end of it, and how that came about you'll none of you have from me; a finish belongs at the end of its race. What I'll give you is this much: somewhere off the mill bend, fifty years ago, this oar went swimming. And three days after, it come ashore on the shingle below my own landing — the Ler returning what's hers to return, in her own time, to the address she thinks proper. I was one-and-twenty. I fetched it up into the ferry-house rafters, and when my father asked why, I told him: a ferryman keeps what the river gives back. He let that stand. It's the only rule I ever made that I never once broke." He held it out — to Ansel, not to Edda, wood to the man of wood and stone. "Fifty years in my rafters over her kettle, drinking the steam of every supper of our married life. If there's a stick of wood in this valley that's told, it's this one. Burn it for the seam."

Ansel took it in his two hands and weighed it, the way he had once weighed a heart-brick, and said nothing about what he felt, but after that he handled it as if it might be listening. They burned it that same night in the yard by the kiln-shed — a small fire, a careful one, an iron pan under it to catch every ash — and nobody said anything that could be called a word while it burned, though Wim stood with his cap off, and Edda noted that too and let it be. The ash, sieved cold next morning, filled a jar smaller than a fist: pale, fine, faintly grey-blue, like dawn snow. Perrin could not think what to put on the label, and Edda took the pen and wrote it for him: *Ash: the oar. Enough.*

The saggar Ansel finished the following week: a cradle more than a box, river clay, thick-walled, shaped inside to the dish's own curve so that the broken thing would ride the fire lying down, bedded soft in clean sand, the way you'd carry a hurt man over a ford; and on the underside, where only the fire would ever read it, the mason set his mark, two rooks, back to back. And then he stood a moment, chisel in hand, with the look of a man listening to a far room; and under the rooks, small, in three strokes and a curve, he cut a boat. Nobody asked. Nell, who was there, kissed the top of his head, which nobody in the parish but Nell Rooke would have dared, and said, "Passage for one, Uncle Anvil?" and he said, "Passage home," and blew the crumbs of clay off his work, and that was the whole of the speech at the launching.

It was that same evening, walking down to the crossing with word the makings stood ready, that Edda saw the other side of the ledger.

She heard it before she reached the door: Griet's voice, high and courteous and wrong, and under it Wim's, level as slack water. Griet stood in her own kitchen with her shawl half on, asking the strange man by the fire — kindly, the way she did everything — when Wim would be home; the boat was late; Wim was never late; the man would know, perhaps, being off the water himself by the look of him. And the strange man, who had been her Wim for fifty years, said, easy as a man giving the depth of a channel sounded ten thousand times: "Soon, missus. He's crossing now. He'd want you warm for him — here," and took the porridge bowl down off its shelf, and warmed it a half-minute in his two big hands the way he warmed it every morning, and put it into hers.

Edda stood at the door and did not go in, and watched the minute come into Griet's face. Not the day; the day was gone where days went. But her hands knew the bowl, and the bowl held what it held, and somewhere below the lost day the waiting turned from fright back into what it had been all her life: the lamp in the window, the boat coming up out of the dark, the best of it. She settled into her chair with the bowl in her lap, and said, comfortable, to the stranger: "He'll be along. He always comes." And Wim said, "He does that," and put wood on her fire.

He came out to Edda on the step after, and they stood in the blue dark hearing the river run.

"You'll understand," Edda said, "why I say it again, and forgive me the saying. The dish will be that" — she nodded at the window, the lamp, the small wrapped shape by the fire — "on its good evenings. A second lamp on the same shelf. It will never once be the door, Wim. If any breath of you hopes otherwise, spend it now, on me, where it's cheap."

"I know what I bought, witch," said Wim, mild as milk. "I've kept a lamp going some years now. Nobody keeps a lamp thinking it's the sun." He looked back in at his wife, holding a winter morning in her two hands with the summer of her going down around her. "But a window with two lamps in it is a window with two lamps in it. Some nights that's the whole of the arithmetic. Go home, mistress. Your kettle's cold and mine's wanted."

Perrin was still up when she came in, at the wedging bench, not wedging: sitting with his hand flat below his collarbone and his eyes at sea, the look of a boy three days into a fifty-year carry. She made the tea, and taught him what Ilsabet had taught her, the sorting every carrying witch comes to sooner or later on a bed's edge at owl-light: name what's yours, out loud, until the guest settles. My gate. My quinces. My kiln. My wheel, my hands, my morning coming. My stroke — she watched him find it, the way he found everything, sideways and then all at once — my stroke, and not mine.

"His is shorter," Perrin said at last, sheepish, wondering. "It wants the catch sooner. All day at the wedging it's been wanting the catch sooner."

"Then give your own its name each time the wrong one reaches for the clay," said Edda, "and let the old one sit warm where it lies. It's not after your arms, boy; it's just the only thing in the house that knows the way home. Three weeks. Then the fire gives it somewhere to go."

# Chapter Eight — Load-Bearing Breaks

The hardest part of any mending is deciding what to leave broken.

Nobody teaches it as the first lesson, because as a first lesson it curdles the pupil; but it was the lesson the week demanded, and it arrived, as the large lessons mostly did now, on the end of one of Perrin's questions. The law was older than Edda and not negotiable: a first-ever fire proves itself on lesser freight. Before the equinox burn could be trusted with half a race and no second chances, the level temper had to be run once, entire, dark to dawn, on something that could be risked; and it was while the workshop was casting about for that something that Perrin looked up from the wedging bench with the pleased, dangerous expression of a boy who believes he has solved two problems with one economy.

"The lid," he said. "The morning-jar's lid. It's cracked clean, it's ours, its owner would bless the trying, and if the mend fails we've lost nothing but two pieces of a thing already broken. And if it holds —" he was up now, halfway to the private shelf, elbows going like a mill "— if it holds, mistress, we send it to Stonebeck mended. The jar made whole again. It'd be a wonder."

"Put it down," said Edda, without heat, and without looking up from her glaze-sieve; and something in the level of it stopped him with his hand an inch from the shelf. She finished the pass of the sieve, wiped her hands, and came over, and stood with him in front of the two smooth-ground pieces where they lay in their hand's-breadth of clean board, slightly open, like a door ajar; and when she spoke it was in the voice she kept for borders, which he had heard her use at gates and in inns and never before in their own workshop.

"Look at it and tell me what it is. Not what it was. What it is."

Perrin looked. He had dusted it a hundred times; he had the grace, she noted, to actually look anyway. "It's the lid off the Sorrel jar. Broken along one line when the opening fire — " he slowed; she watched the craft catch up with the cleverness, the way it did in him, sideways and then all at once " — when the opening fire finished. Ansel said. He said the door came open and the lock came off. He ground the edges smooth and said that was all the mending it wanted."

"And he was right, and he's a mason, and masons know a load-bearing member when they see one. That break, Perrin, is doing work. It stands on this shelf doing work every day of its life: it says a shut thing was opened here; it says the craft owns its history instead of tidying it; it says — to me, boy, every morning of mine, and one day to you — that a door once cost a woman her whole morning and a fire gave it back, and the proof is these two pieces lying open and no power in this workshop willing to close them. Mend that lid and what have you got? A lid. A fitted lid, for a jar in a schoolhouse window, whose whole answered prayer is that it stands open where the sun gets in. You'd be mending a door back into a lock." She took his shoulder, turned him from the shelf, and gave him the doctrine plain, because doctrine arriving on the heels of a mistake is the only doctrine that keeps: "Some breaks are load-bearing. The crack in Griet's dish is a stopped race — a held breath, a debt, a thing every hour of its twelve years has wanted finishing. The break in that lid is a finished thing already. It ended well. Learn to tell a wound from a scar, Perrin Loft, or keep your hands in your pockets round broken things; the fire can't tell them apart, so the witch must."

Perrin was quiet a while, restacking that inside himself — she could all but hear the shelves being moved — and then he said, in his own defense, mildly: "It would have been a wonder, though," and Edda said, "So is a lock, to a locksmith. Sweep the floor," and the matter passed into the law of the workshop, where it has been taught ever since one shelf-dusting boy to the next: mending is for stopped races, not for opened doors.

The proving freight found them two days later, as freight in Wenholt reliably did, by way of the village knowing everything immediately. It came down from the hill farm in the Askews' cart, wrapped in a hay-lined basket and flanked by the entire dynasty in its Sunday coats, and it was the naming bowl — the December baby's naming bowl, fired at Wintermark two years since, the whole family's telling of the name and the reasons for the name and the snow on the night of the choosing; and across its foot, since February, a crack. The baby herself, coming up two and a known force, had done it: hauled on the dresser-cloth one washing-day morning and brought the bowl down onto the flags, in the way of two-year-olds, who break what they love by loving it at their own strength. It had not shattered — Askew ware was thrown stout for good dynastic reasons — but it had cracked through, and the family had felt the naming go quiet past the crack, and had said nothing all spring, out of the particular shame of a house that breaks a witch's work; and now stood in the workshop garden in a body, shuffling, while the boys' mother said what she had come to say with the basket held out like a christening.

"We heard what the fire's learning," she said. "And we'd sooner lend our trouble than watch you risk Griet's on an untried flame. She's in it too, mind — the little one. Her gran's voice saying the name is in that bowl, and her gran's two years gone now, and the far side of the crack's gone quiet. If your fire can teach it to speak again, it can practice on us."

So it was done as the craft required, and the doing taught them the shape of everything to come. The mother sat in the good chair and told the missing half fresh — the name said over again, the reasons, the snow — and Perrin took it, his third carry, a small bright guest in alongside the fifty-year lodger, and reported the two of them under his breastbone as unlikely but civil company, like a wren nesting in a boathouse. The bowl went into a plain saggar on river sand; the crack was packed with a seam ground from a broken cup of the bowl's own batch, which the Askews produced from a drawer with suspicious readiness, hill farms throwing nothing away; and on a night two weeks short of the equinox — off the pole, out of season, with no year's leave and the moon a day past half — they bricked the door, laid the level on the arch, and asked the fire, for the first time in the history of the craft, for nothing at all.

It was the hardest firing any of the three of them ever owned to. A fire asked for nothing keeps offering things. It wanted to run rich; eased, it wanted to run lean; it leaned and yawed like a boat with no hand on the tiller, because that is what it was — all fires having been steered all their lives by somebody's wanting, and this one asked only to stand plumb in the dark and listen. Edda stoked the odd strokes and Perrin the even, small charges, turn and turn about; Ansel stood at the spy-hole and the level and called the flame's colour like soundings; and for two hours the bubble in the little glass walked its cage and would not sit. At the third hour it got away from them. The wind came round the ridge, the draw deepened, the flame off the stoke-mouth went from straw to butter, rich and getting richer, and Edda's hand was already moving to choke the air down hard — the old reflex, force against lean — when Perrin said, "No — don't fight it, ease the next three and shorten the stroke," in a voice that was not entirely seventeen; and Ansel, glancing from the flame to the boy, said, "Do as the count says"; and she did: three light charges, quick and small, the rhythm carrying what force would have spoiled, the way a crew rows down a gust rather than against it. The flame came back to straw. The bubble walked home to the middle of the glass and sat. Nobody said anything for a quarter hour, and then Ansel, from the spy-hole, in the dark, to no one: "His fire, that one."

They clammed at dawn, and cooled it the slow two days, and opened it on a grey morning with the whole Askew dynasty at the gate an hour early; and out of the saggar, warm into the cold air, came the naming bowl, whole. Where the crack had been there ran a hairline seam, thinner than the dish's would be, faint gold against the glaze, like the first line of light under a door; and when the boys' mother took it in her two hands, the naming ran entire — the name, the reasons, the snow, her own mother's voice saying it, in past the seam and out the other side without a stumble — and neither half spoke over the other, the old telling and the new lying along each other's grain like two hands folded; and she stood in the garden and wept the good kind, and the smallest Askew, author of the whole trouble, patted the bowl and said the name herself, loudly, in the wrong order, to general ruin.

It was after they had gone, cart and dynasty and all, that Edda stood alone by the kiln with her palm flat on the warm brick over the heart, where forty years of the parish lived, and tried the words. She did it half-aloud, the way she ground glazes, tasting the mix: the catechism wanted a third line now, the craft having grown one, and a line for the witches after had better be built like a course of brick, no ornament, nothing to date it. *The kiln keeps. It does not take.* Two lines old as the arch. *And what it keeps, asked kindly, it can give back.* The summer's line, hers and the boy's and a schoolmistress's. And now, over the cooling bricks, quietly, to the fire that had earned it:

"And what breaks, told together, it can mend."

She stood with it a moment, weighing it on the tongue the way she tested glaze on the wrist. Then she took her hand off the brick. "Not yet," she told the kiln. "You've done it once, to a bowl, off-season. Do it at the balance, to the dish, and I'll write you into the book."

# Chapter Nine — My Stroke and Not Mine

What the hands learn, the hands rehearse, whether or not the owner is consulted.

Perrin rowed every evening that last week, because the parish expected its boat crewed and because — though nobody framed it so aloud — an apprentice carrying a fifty-year stroke had better be given water to spend it on, the way you walk a young horse past the thing it shies at. He and Nell Rooke took *Second Thoughts* out after supper, up against the current to the mill bend and home on the slack, and the Ler in the long light was gold and easy and entirely unhelpful, because the trouble was not the river. The trouble was the catch. Twice an outing, three times, his blade went in early — a hand's-breadth short of his own reach, quick and proud, a stroke made for a lighter boat and a nearer post and a morning fifty years upstream — and the boat checked, and Nell, at bow, addressed the back of his head in her mother's bookkeeping voice.

"That one wasn't yours either. Row your own age, Loft."

"My stroke and not mine," said Perrin, to the river, and named it, and let it settle, and rowed on. That was the whole ritual, done wet: the sorting, taken out of the owl-light bedroom and put to work in a moving boat, until by the third evening he could feel the old catch coming the way you feel a sneeze, and name it before it reached his wrists, and give the water his own stroke instead — longer, seventeen, nothing to be ashamed of. What he could not do was make the lodger stop offering. "It's not trying to row," he reported to Edda, wondering, rubbing liniment into forearms that had done two men's practice. "It's remembering forward. It knows there's a race coming — the way your foot knows the stair's there. It wants to finish something."

"It will," said Edda. "Once. Mind it isn't twice. On the water you're an oarsman, not a carrier; keep the count in your ears and not in your keeping, and if Nell Rooke tells you a stroke isn't yours, believe her before you believe your own arms."

"I generally do," said Perrin, with feeling.

Once, and once only, the two strokes agreed. It was the last practice evening, on the turn for home, with the light going copper and the current dying; and for twenty strokes together the boat ran quiet as an idea, no check, no chatter off the blades, the old catch and the young reach folded into one motion that belonged to neither of them; and Nell said nothing at all until the landing, and then said, "Whatever that was, bring it Saturday," and Perrin, honest to the bone, said he couldn't promise, it hadn't been entirely his to bring.

Whatever else was quietly under way in that boat, the village had the mercy not to name it and the shamelessness to watch it nightly from the bank. Nothing was declared; nothing needed to be; there was only a girl with her mother's dry eye calling a boy's stroke back into his own body every evening, and a boy who had stopped going red at moot-halls and started keeping his blade's feather flat because somebody expected it of him; glances and competence, the parish's oldest recipe, and no business of any chronicle until the parties made it so.

Mercy Hale arrived on the Thursday, on the lime-cart, boots over the tailboard, with three Stonebeck children ranged beside her like a difficult sum brought on holiday, each clutching a bundle and a set of manners drilled to a shine that lasted fully an hour past the ford. Wenholt absorbed them the way it absorbed everything: the children were sorted into the puddling-and-errands economy by nightfall, adopted by the Askew boys as auxiliaries, fed by Maud on the ends of batch loaves "for the school," and taken solemnly, on the second morning, up the witch's lane — where the smallest one stood in front of the private shelf, looked at the two smooth pieces of the lid she had been lifted up to touch on cold mornings all her school life, and said, "It's littler than at home," which Mercy said was the most educational sentence the child had produced all year. The schoolmistress herself took her old room at the Copper Kettle, submitted to Hessa's system of subterfuges, and stood a long while at the workshop bench that evening, reading the seam-jar's label in the lamplight — *Ash: the oar. Enough.* — with her chin up, Hale and Sorrel both.

"You're asking a fire to hold a balance for a whole night," she said. "At midsummer we asked it to be generous, which is at least a temper it owns. This is asking it to be *just*. I've kept a schoolroom thirty years, mistress; justice is the hardest thing you can ask of anything with an appetite." She set the jar down. "I've brought my slate. If it's to be done, it wants minuting; the witches after will forgive your handwriting sooner than your silence."

The boats were done that same week, and named, and the naming was the last argument of the season. Ansel and Tom Chandler had made them sound as barrels — new strakes scarfed in, new pitch, the old oak sweet under it all; "she'll outlive the — " said Tom Chandler, of each in turn, leaving the survivor unspecified — and custom demanded names painted on before race day. The Askews wanted blood and history and got it: *Dob's Revenge*, in letters you could read from the far bank, for the great-uncle who had pulled bow in the great race and whose honor, the boys explained over each other, had waited fifty years for satisfaction, though pressed on the question of what precisely wanted revenging they went vague, lore being lore. And the second boat went unnamed until the evening Nell walked round it, twice, with the paint-pot, and wrote *Second Thoughts* along the bow, and answered the bank's raised eyebrow with her mother's blandest smile: "She's had a whole second life, and I row stroke-side of a man who audits every one he takes. It's a compliment," she added, to Perrin, "eventually," and Perrin, who had learned some things at two fires now, said nothing and feathered his blade, and the bank found somewhere else to look, disappointed.

The harvest came in whole that week too — the last carts up from the mill fields under a moon one night short of full, the stubble pale as bisqueware clear to the ridge — and with it, by a custom nobody had exercised in a generation and everybody proved to remember perfectly, the crown. The last sheaf was held back from the cart, and brought to the green, and plaited: the Harvest Crown, the winner's wreath, straw worked in a pattern that had a knack to it, and the knack, it turned out, lived where such things live. Tally and Mercy's children and half the puddling-gang made the first three attempts and produced objects variously described as a nest, a hat for a horse, and an accident; and then somebody thought of the person no one had thought of, because thinking of her had a grief in it, and the sheaf was carried down to the ferry-house, to the chair by the window, and put into Griet's lap.

Her hands knew it before her face did. Edda, who had come down with the evening bread and stayed, watched the old fingers find the straw and go still a moment — the way hands do at a door, deciding — and then begin, without one word from anywhere, to plait. Over, under, turn; over, under, turn; the pattern coming off her fingers the way the stroke came off them on the water, from below, from the keeping of the body, sixty crowns deep; and the children knelt round her chair and were taught it, hand over hand, by a woman who could not have told one of them the day of the week, and did not need to, the day of the week being the least of what a person knows. The crown she made was the truest made in that valley in fifty years. It went on the moot-hall door to wait for its winner, and Griet watched it carried off up the lane and said, comfortably, to the room: "Somebody'll be choosing a dance off the back of that." And nobody heard it for more than custom, then.

The night before the equinox, with the moon full and the valley lying stubble-silver under it, Edda went down to the crossing and was rowed out onto quiet water by Old Wim, because he sent word asking it, and a summons from the ferry was not a thing the witch of Wenholt had ever once refused.

They did not talk business for a while. The river ran low and kind; the herons stood their posts; the ferry-house window, when the dark came up, put its lamp out across the water the way it had for fifty years, and the two of them sat in the drifting boat looking back at it, two old debts on one thwart.

"Twelve years you carried it," Wim said at last, "and fifty I carried mine, and tomorrow night we both put down our loads inside the one kiln. There's a thrift in that." He feathered an oar, idly, keeping the boat's head to the stream with the smallest movements, a man steering out of pure habit the way other men breathe. "I'll tell you the one thing more you're owed before the fire, witch, since you've owned your half to me and this is mine: I could have told the race any harvest these fifty years. The whole parish sat at my table wanting it. I let the lore grow up crooked because the lore as it stood was — comfortable. To me, mind. Not to her. There's a debt in that I've not worked out the shape of yet, but I know where it falls due, and it falls due at the telling-back, and I mean to be stood square when it lands."

"You'll not stand alone at it," said Edda. "The whole crew of this thing has a debt in the load. That's likely why it floats."

"Aye," said Wim. He looked a last moment at the lamp in his window; then he squared the boat for home, and set his blades, and said — to the witch, to the water, to the morning coming up the valley with its race in it, in the voice of a ferryman calling a crossing that has been kept waiting long enough:

"All hands, then."
