# THE NIGHT KILN

*A Cozy Fantasy Novella*

> Everyone in Wenholt knows the rule: bring Edda a memory before moonrise, and she will fire it into clay you can hold. What they don't know is what it costs her to keep the kiln burning at night — or what happens when a quiet stranger asks her to fire a memory he wants to be rid of forever.

---

**Content note:** Adult cozy fantasy. Contains grief, remembered bereavement (offpage, in the past), and a beloved character living with memory loss — all handled gently. No violence, no explicit content. A warm read for adult readers.

---

# Chapter One — Moonrise Custom

The rule in Wenholt was older than anyone who kept it, and it was this: if you wanted a memory fired, you brought it to Edda Marren before moonrise, because the kiln burned at night or not at all.

Nobody remembered why. The scholars up in Harrowgate, on the two occasions they had bothered to ask, had been told by two different generations of kiln-witches that night air settled the glaze, which was not exactly a lie and not remotely the truth. The truth was simpler and stranger, and Edda kept it the way she kept everything else that was given to her: carefully, and without mentioning it.

On the last market-day of autumn, the queue at her gate started forming before the light had properly failed. She could see it from her workshop window while she wedged the evening's clay — a soft grey stoneware dug from the bend of the Ler below the ferry landing, weathered a full year in the pit behind the woodshed, and even so it fought her a little, the way river clay always did. She leaned into the spiral of the wedging, heel of one hand rolling the mass against the bench, folding the same body over itself until the cut face showed smooth as cheese, no bubbles, no memory of the pit. Clay held onto everything. That was the whole trade, really. You spent half your craft teaching it to forget the ground and the other half teaching it to remember something better.

Tally Brock was first at the gate, as Edda had known she would be. The baker's daughter had been circling the workshop for a week the way a swimmer circles cold water, and now she stood under the gate-lantern with flour still in her eyebrows and a shape under her shawl that could only be money she could not spare.

"Put it away," Edda said, opening the gate. "Your mother sends me bread every firing night. I've owed the Brocks since before you could knead."

"Mam says a telling's not bread."

"Your mam is right, and I'm still not taking it. Come in before the ferryman beats you to the good chair."

The workshop took people the way the kiln took pots: warmly, and on its own terms. It was one long room under old beams, and every shelf in it was full — bisqueware pale as unrisen loaves waiting on the high boards, glazed work below it, jars of raw materials ranked along the wall with their names painted on in Edda's teacher's hand, wood ash, ochre, ball clay, feldspar off the Harrowgate barges, the good red iron from the stream bank that went green as bottle glass in the fire if you starved the kiln of air at the right hour. The big waisted stove in the corner kept a kettle just short of singing at all times. Edda hung it over the heat now, because there was no version of this evening that did not begin with tea.

Tally sat in the good chair and held her cup in both hands and did not drink it, which was how Edda knew the girl had brought something heavy.

"It's Gran," Tally said at last.

"I supposed it might be."

"Not — she's well. She's well enough." Tally turned the cup a quarter-turn, the way people did, winding themselves. "It's her laugh. The proper one, the one from when I was small, when she'd get going in the bakehouse and set the whole street off. She laughs different now. Smaller. And two nights ago I was trying to tell Wilf about the old laugh and I couldn't —" the cup went another quarter-turn "— I couldn't hear it. In my own head. I had it my whole life and I went to fetch it and the shelf was bare."

The kettle began to sing. Edda let it.

"So I want it in a mug," Tally said, all in a rush now. "Her morning mug, the shape she likes, with the fat handle. So that when it's — later. When it's later, and I make her tea, I'll have it. Is that a foolish thing to want?"

"It is the least foolish thing anyone will ask me for all year," Edda said. "Drink your tea. Then you'll give me the telling, and I'll have the mug out of the fire before Wintermark."

That was the trade, and this was how it went. There was no book of it and no scale of fees, though people paid what they could in coin or eggs or split apple-wood for the firing. You sat by the stove, and you told the memory — told it properly, out loud, with your hands around something warm, while Edda sat close with a bowl of slip in her lap, the clay watered down to cream. You told it, and she listened, and the listening was the craft. What the fire fixed later was not the words. Words were only the handles of a thing. What went into the clay was the whole of it, the smell of the bakehouse and the heat off the ovens and the exact ridiculous cascade of a big woman's laugh, and it went in through Edda, the way water goes through a channel, because that was what a kiln-witch was for.

Tally told it well. Most people did, in the end; the chair and the tea and the lamplight did most of the work, and Wenholt folk had grown up knowing how. She told the winter morning when the cat got into the proofing cupboard and came out wearing a bonnet of dough, and Gran Brock had laughed until she had to sit down on the flour sacks, laughed until the whole of Bake Lane leaned out of its windows to ask what the joke was and then caught it themselves with no joke at all, a street laughing at laughter, and at the center of it that huge helpless bell of a woman ringing.

Edda listened. Her fingers moved in the slip without her watching them.

And there it was — the fine strange ache under her breastbone as the memory came across, like a swallow of tea slightly too hot. The laugh settled into her chest as if it meant to nest there. It would sit in her, warm and a little heavy, all night and all the firing, until the fire took it out of her hands and into the clay for good. That was the part the queue at the gate never saw. That was the truth about the night kiln, or the first half of it: the memory lived in the witch between the telling and the firing, and a firing night meant carrying everything at once.

"There," Edda said, when the girl had finished and the slip in the bowl had gone faintly, unmistakably golden. "That's a strong one. It'll pull through the glaze by spring — laughs always do, they don't like being shut up. Now go home before your mam decides I've eaten you."

Tally paused at the door, shawl half over her head. "Does it hurt? Carrying it?"

"It's a laugh," said Edda, which was not an answer, and smiled, which was.

Through the window she watched the girl run down the lane past the queue — Old Wim the ferryman next, stamping his boots against the first real cold of the year, and behind him the moon coming up over the ridge like a patient customer. Edda set the golden slip on the telling shelf, put the kettle back over the heat, and went to open the gate again.

# Chapter Two — The Telling Shelf

Old Wim took his tea black and his time considerable.

He was the fourth ferryman of that name to work the crossing below the village, a man made mostly of oilskin and patience, and he had come every autumn for three years now with the same commission: a bowl for his Griet. He sat in the good chair with his boots steaming gently against the stove and turned his cup, and Edda let him find his own way to it, because with Wim there was no other route.

"She had a good summer," he said eventually. "You'd not credit it, some days. Knew the boat. Knew the water. Sat in the stern coming back from the fair at Harrowgate and named me every heron on the flats, same as ever." He drank. "Other days she asks me when Wim's coming home. I say soon. She likes that. She always did like waiting for me, she used to say the waiting was the best of it, the lamp in the window and the boat coming up out of the dark." He looked into the cup. "Now it's all waiting, more or less. So."

"So," Edda agreed. "What'll it be this year?"

"First snowfall. The winter we were married. We'd the cottage on the far bank then, and the snow came in the night, early, before Souls' Eve even, and she woke me at dawn to look at it. Whole valley gone quiet. River running black through all that white, and her at the window in my coat, laughing at it. Snow before Souls' Eve, she says, we'll be snug or dead by spring, and kissed me with her nose all cold." He set the cup down. "She minds it some days and not others. I want it in the porridge bowl. Mornings are her hard time, see. If the bowl can mind it for her on the days she can't —" He stopped, and looked at his hands, riverman's hands, and said, "It's not for me. I've got it safe enough. It's so it can be in the room with her, warm, of a morning. That's all I want. Things in the room."

"That's all any of it is," Edda said quietly. "Things in the room. Tell it, then, Wim. Take your time. The moon's barely up."

He told it. The snow came into the workshop as he talked — not visibly, nothing so cheap, but the temperature of the memory, the particular held-breath hush of a first fall, the cold nose, the black river. It went through Edda like well-water and settled next to Tally's grandmother's laugh, and the two of them sat oddly companionable under her breastbone, the hush and the bell. The slip in the second bowl turned the grey-blue of dawn snow. She set it on the telling shelf beside the golden one.

The telling shelf was the oldest thing in the workshop after the kiln itself: one warped board of black oak at exactly the height of Edda's heart, which was not a coincidence, because it had been set at the height of Grams Ilsabet's heart and the two women had been of a size. Nine bowls of slip could stand on it. Grams Ilsabet had held that nine tellings was the most any witch should carry into a single night, and had made Edda repeat the reasons the way other children repeated their catechism. Because you are a channel and not a cistern. Because a memory kept too long in the keeper picks up the keeper's grief like a pot picks up smoke. Because the kiln keeps, Edda. It does not take. Say it back to me.

The kiln keeps. It does not take.

Three more came before moonrise proper. Maud Brock herself, Tally's mother, arriving floury and suspicious to make sure her daughter hadn't paid ("She didn't. You'll send bread, we're square" — "We are not square, Edda Marren, we have never once been square" — this was a liturgy fifteen years old and both women enjoyed it immensely), and staying to fire a memory of her own late husband singing to the ovens on Sunday mornings, into a butter crock, because butter had been his weakness. Then Cobb the woodman, who never told anything of his own but was paid by the sack of apple-wood to cart tellings down from the hill farms; tonight he brought a lambing memory from the Askews for a milk jug, spoken to him word-for-word on the doorstep and repeated in the good chair with his eyes shut and his hat in his fists, Cobb being of the fixed opinion that carrying folks' memories was a graver cartage than logs and priced too cheap at any price. And last, just as the moon cleared the ridge, Perrin came pelting up the lane with his boots unlaced, because Perrin has been late for moonrise since the day he was born, his mother said, why would he be prompt for any moonrise after.

Perrin Loft was sixteen, made of elbows, and the first apprentice Edda had taken in the eleven years since Grams Ilsabet died. He could not yet center two pounds of clay on the wheel without chasing it around like a cat after a beetle. He also could not walk past the telling shelf without going quiet and reverent in a way that had nothing to do with clumsiness, and that second thing was why Edda kept him, and she suspected he knew it.

"Five," he said, counting the bowls, getting his breath. "Golden one's a laugh."

"Whose laugh, if you're so clever?"

He looked at it sideways, the way she'd taught him — you never looked at a telling straight on, it made them shy — and his face went through the small effort that meant he was listening with the wrong sense on purpose. "Big laugh. Bakehouse?" Then, delighted: "That's old Gran Brock! From when she used to set the street off!"

"Wash your hands," Edda said, absurdly proud, as ever. "We're loading the kiln tonight, and if you put your thumb through the ferryman's bowl I will fire you into a doorstop."

But she let him stand another moment at the shelf first, sideways, listening to a laugh from before he was born. The lamplight found all five bowls of slip: gold, snow-blue, a warm butter-yellow, the milky green of a spring field at lambing, and Perrin's favorite, the little unasked-for russet one at the end, which was Edda's own — because a witch too proud to practice what she fired for others soon forgot the cost of it — holding nothing weightier this month than the smell of the first pippins off the orchard tree.

Nine bowls could stand on the shelf. It had been years since Edda had filled it. She thought, sometimes, that she was rationing herself without knowing what she was saving up for.

By the time the moon stood fully over the ridge, the gate was shut, the queue was gone home to its suppers, and the two of them were carrying bisqueware out under the cold sky to the brick beast at the bottom of the garden. The Night Kiln squatted in its open-sided shed like a bread oven dreaming of being a whale, and it was already warm to the hand, because Edda had lit the first small candling fire that afternoon.

It was also — though only she knew it yet — cracked.

# Chapter Three — The Long Fire

A firing was a night's work the way a birth was, which is to say: in theory eight hours, in practice everything you had.

They glazed and loaded through the early evening. The tellings went on last and went on together, that being the way of it: each bowl of slip was brushed into the waiting vessel — the laugh into Gran Brock's fat-handled mug, the snowfall into the porridge bowl, the song into the butter crock, the lambing into the jug, the pippins into a little cup Edda had thrown badly on purpose, because Perrin needed to see that crooked pots deserved memories too. Over the slip went the glaze. Edda mixed her glazes from the painted jars by lamplight, and this was the part of the craft that looked most like witchery and was most like cookery: wood ash and river clay and feldspar, sieved and slaked and stirred to the thickness of single cream, tested on the tongue of her wrist like milk for a baby.

"Ash glaze for the laugh," she said, working. "Apple ash, off Cobb's own wood. Ash glazes run and pool and go streaky, no two ever alike, and a laugh wants that; shut a laugh under a tidy glaze and it sulks. Now the snowfall — the snowfall wants the blue celadon, and celadon is nothing in the world but iron, same iron as rusts a gate red. Starve the fire of air at the right hour and the iron forgets to be rust and turns to winter glass. Reduction, that's called. Are you attending, Perrin Loft?"

"Rust forgets," said Perrin, stacking kiln shelves, "winter glass, starve the fire. What does the fire eat instead? If we starve it of air."

Every so often the boy asked the exact right question and made the whole year of him worthwhile. "It eats the glaze's breath," Edda said. "It reaches into the clay and pulls the air out of the iron itself. Fire's like folk. Feed it easy and it stays shallow; put it to a little honest hunger and it goes deep."

They bricked up the door at moonhigh, clay-mortared, leaving the spy-holes and the stoke-mouth, and then the night's real rhythm began: stoke, watch, stoke, watch, the split apple-wood going in stick by stick, flame lengthening from orange toward straw-gold, the kiln beginning its slow annual conversation with itself in creaks and ticks. Inside, on the middle shelf where she could watch them through the spy-hole, stood the little clay cones — pyrometric cones, three to a pack, wedded upright in a pat of clay, each blended to bow at its own appointed heat. No dial, no glass instrument in the workshop could tell you what the cones told you, because heat and time worked on ware together, and only a thing standing in the fire could feel what the ware felt. When the first cone bowed its head, you were coming to temperature. When the second bent to touch its tip to the shelf, you were there. The third stood sentinel against going past.

And under all of it, all night, Edda carried the tellings.

This was the second half of the truth about the night kiln, the half even Perrin only half-knew. From telling to firing, the memories lived in the witch, and the fire drew them from her the way the sun draws mist off a river — slowly, all night, through her hands on the stoke-wood, through her eye at the spy-hole. It was why the kiln burned at night: not for the settling of glazes but because the drawing-out left a witch scoured and lantern-headed, fit for nothing after but sleep, and because certain memories rose easier under moonlight than they ever would under the busy sun; her grandmother's grandmother had known that much and built the custom around it. All night Edda stood in the fire's company while it gently unpacked her: the bell of a laugh going out of her chest around the second cone, the hush of snow leaving her shoulders near dawn, the Sunday singing, the lambing, the pippins, each lifting away with a pang like a hair pulled loose — a small cost, a fair one, and hers to pay. By morning she would be empty and the clay would be full. The kiln keeps. She had said it to Grams Ilsabet a thousand times and had it said to her a thousand more. It keeps because the keeper lets go. Nine bowls, no more, and never carry a telling past its firing.

At the fourth hour, with the first cone bowing and the flame off the stoke-mouth burning clean straw-gold, the kiln coughed.

There was no other word for it. A soft double thump from the crown of the arch, a stutter in the draw, and the long steady roar changed its vowel. Edda was at the spy-hole in a moment. The fire inside stood tall and true — but above the middle shelf, along the crown, a thin bright seam glowed where no seam should be, a hairline of fiercer gold against the general furnace-color, like a crack of dawn arriving nine hours early and badly out of place.

"Perrin. The small ladder and the raddle-pot. Quick and calm."

Up on the shed's stone kerb she found it from the outside: a crack across the crown brick, fine as a cat's whisker at its ends and a full straw's width at the center, breathing heat. Old damage woken up. The arch of a kiln is a held breath — every brick leaning on every other brick, the whole curve locked tight by its keystone course, and forty years of firings had been quietly working this one loose the way forty winters work a hillside. She had seen the whisker of it in spring and mortared it and told herself what she'd told herself. Now it had opened its eye.

They finished the firing, because the ware and the tellings were in it, and a kiln-witch does not abandon five memories to a half-fire while the arch still stands; but they finished it gently, easing the last hour, and Edda took her reduction shallow and prayed to nothing in particular that the winter-glass blue would forgive her. When the second cone touched its tip to the shelf she called it done, and they clammed the kiln up — every hole bricked and mortared blind, the fire shut in with its work to cool as slowly as it had risen, because ware chilled too fast rings cracked, dunted, spoiled; even endings had to be done at the fire's own pace.

Dawn came up grey and smelt of frost. Perrin leaned on the shed post with the raddle-pot still in his hands, looking at the marked crack, too tired to be anything but honest.

"Can we fire the Wintermark load on that?"

Wintermark was six weeks off, and the Wintermark firing was the year's great one — every commission in the parish, the whole village's tellings, the load that filled all nine bowls of the shelf and every shelf of the kiln besides.

"No," said Edda. It cost her something to say it that plainly, and the boy flinched as if the kiln itself had said it. She put her hand flat on the warm brick flank of the old beast, over its cracked crown, the way she'd kept her hand on ailing things all her life. "Not on that. Go to bed, Perrin. It's a two-day cooling, and troubles look smaller across a cooling."

She was wrong about that, as it happened. By the time the kiln was cool, the trouble would have a name, and the name would be standing at her gate at moonrise, asking her to break the only rule that mattered.

# Chapter Four — The Stranger at Moonrise

He came on the third evening after the firing, at the exact edge of moonrise, which told Edda two things before he said a word: that he was a stranger, because Wenholt folk came comfortably early and stayed for tea; and that he had learned the rule anyway, learned it precisely, and had waited for the last permissible moment, the way a man waits at the door of something he is not sure he is allowed to want.

She was in the garden when the gate-bell went, unloading the cooled kiln with Perrin — that slow Christmas-morning ceremony of unbricking the door and lifting the ware out warm into the cold air. It had been a kind firing after all, despite the crack: the celadon had come through winter-glass blue, barely a shade shy of what she'd hoped, and Gran Brock's mug had streaked and pooled gold down one side exactly as a laugh ought. Perrin had carried each telling-piece to the garden table as though carrying a bird's egg the size of his head. The porridge bowl, held up to the failing light, had a hush in it you could feel through your palms.

The man at the gate was perhaps fifty, big through the shoulders and careful with it, the way big men learn to be careful in small rooms. Road-dusty boots, a good coat gone shabby, a canvas tool-bag on a strap. He had a mason's hands — she saw that at once, the flattened thumbnails and the lime-bitten knuckles, hands she knew, because the men who rebuilt the workshop chimney had had them — and he had a face like a house with the shutters up.

"You'd be the kiln-witch," he said. "Edda Marren."

"I would. And the moon's not up yet, barely, so you're in time, though the kiln's cold tonight and you'd be waiting a while for your firing regardless. Come in. There's tea."

"I'd sooner say it at the gate."

"Nobody says anything at the gate," Edda said, not unkindly. "That's near enough the first rule of the place. Come in, warm your hands, tell me your name, and then tell me your business, in that order."

He looked past her at the garden table, at the warm ware sitting in rows in the dusk, at Perrin frozen mid-carry and staring the way sixteen stares. Something crossed the shuttered face — not longing, exactly; the memory of knowing what longing was.

"Rooke," he said, coming in. "Ansel Rooke. Mason, out of Harrowgate, these days. And my business is quick to say." He did not sit in the good chair. He stood by the stove with his cap in his hands, and he said it the way a man says a thing he has rehearsed on a long road, flat and complete: "They tell me you fire memories into clay so they keep. I've one I don't want to keep. I want it fired out of me and shut in a pot, and I'll pay whatever's asked, and you can drop the pot in the Ler with my blessing, or brick it in a wall, or give it to the crows. I want rid, mistress. They say in Harrowgate the old kiln-witches could do that too. Fire it in, all the way, so the teller walks away lighter." He looked up from his cap. "Is it true?"

The kettle ticked on the stove. Out in the garden, very carefully, Perrin set down the jug he was holding.

"It's true," Edda said.

She would ask herself, later, why she hadn't softened it, hadn't said it's complicated or there are stories. But Grams Ilsabet had held that a witch who lied about the shape of her craft to make her refusals easier was spending the craft's honesty to buy her own comfort, and it had seemed to Edda that this man had been lied to by something for a long time already.

"It's true," she said again. "It's called an emptying, and it can be done, and I won't do it. Not for you, not for anyone, not at any price. I'm sorry you've come far to hear that."

He didn't argue. That was the first surprising thing about Ansel Rooke: he took the refusal like a man taking weather, a small adjustment of the shoulders. What he said was, "Why not?"

"Because the kiln keeps. It doesn't take. The night I let it take is the night it learns to." She heard her teacher's voice walk through her own and let it. "That's the rule of this kiln, Master Rooke, and it's not squeamishness and it's not price-raising. I'll tell you what I tell the two or three who come asking every decade, because there are always two or three: an emptying does exactly what you'd have it do. It takes the memory out at the root. And a memory is not a splinter, whatever it feels like at midnight. It's a root, and roots hold banks, and what a root was holding you find out in the first hard rain after it's gone." She poured the tea he hadn't asked for and put it in his hands because they were empty. "I've a jar on a shelf in there that I keep for the asking-people to hold. It was an emptying. Done long ago, done well, done out of mercy, by a better witch than me. The woman it was done for comes to mind every time, and if you want the story you may have it. But not at the gate, and not tonight, because you're road-worn and I'm firing-worn, and this is a decision that shouldn't be made by two tired people in the dark."

For a long moment he looked at her, and she watched him understand that the door had not been shut, only the draught excluded — that not tonight was not never, and was also not yes, and that she had deliberately left him nowhere to stand except patience.

"They said you'd say no," he admitted. "In Harrowgate. They said the Wenholt witch fires keepings only." He turned the cup in his hands, a quarter-turn, and Edda's heart did a small strange thing, because everyone did that in the good chair, but he was doing it standing, road-dusty, at the very start. Winding himself. "They didn't say you'd give a man tea after."

"The tea's not after anything. The tea is the point; the pots are how I pay for it. Where are you lodging?"

"Nowhere yet."

"The Copper Kettle, then; ask for Hessa, tell her I sent you, she'll feed you against my account and argue with me about it later, which she enjoys. And Master Rooke —" he stopped in the gateway, a big shuttered house of a man under the gate-lantern, the moon coming up full behind him over the ridge "— when you can't sleep tonight, and you won't, here's a thing to do with the hours that beats rehearsing your grievance against me. Walk down the garden and look at my kiln. You're a mason. You'll see it faster by moonlight; heat-cracks show at a raking angle. Come back and tell me honestly what you find, and I'll owe you a mason's fee and a proper breakfast."

He was gone a moment later, boots on the frosty lane. Perrin materialized at her elbow in the way of apprentices everywhere, smelling of warm ware and questions.

"You never let anyone see the kiln first visit," he said. "You made me wait a month."

"You were fifteen and made of thumbs."

"He wanted an emptying." The boy's voice was low and thrilled and appalled; he had grown up on Wenholt hearth-tales, and emptying was a word from the dark end of them. "You said those were just stories to frighten prentices."

"I said no such thing. I said the jar was not to be dusted by you." Edda picked up the ferryman's porridge bowl, the hush of a forty-year-old snowfall steady between her two hands, real as a heartbeat, heavier than any grief the man at her gate could be carrying and lighter than a feather, both at once, the way kept things were. "Fetch the crate straw. Wim's coming for this at first light, and I want the whole load wrapped before we sleep."

But she stood a moment in the cold with the bowl, watching the empty lane. A mason. Out of Harrowgate, these days — and days did a lot of work in that sentence. A memory he'd carry two days' road in winter to be rid of. And her kiln standing cracked at the bottom of the garden with the great firing six weeks off.

Grams Ilsabet had also held — usually while shelling peas, for maximum unanswerableness — that the world sent a witch nothing in single file.

# Chapter Five — Bread and Opinions

By noon the next day, the whole village knew, in the way of villages: everything, immediately, and mostly wrong.

Wenholt sat in its river-bend and fed on its own news like a stove on split wood, and the arrival of a stranger who had gone straight up the witch's lane at moonrise and come away fifteen minutes later without so much as a jug was wood of the very best seasoning. By the time Edda came down Bake Lane with her basket, the stranger had been variously a debt-collector, a glaze-merchant, a suitor ("at her age," added the smith's mother, who was Edda's elder by a decade), and — this from the Askew boys, with relish — a Harrowgate kiln-breaker come to shut the night kiln down for unlicensed sorcery.

The truth, Edda reflected, queuing for bread, was going to be harder to manage than any of these.

"He took the small room over my stable," Hessa reported over the counter of the Copper Kettle an hour later, having abandoned the counter's queue to sit with Edda by the fire, which was Hessa's way of running an inn and the reason her inn ran. She was a square, bright-eyed woman who cooked like a genius and gossiped like a surgeon: precisely, and only where it did good. "Paid a week. Ate two suppers, spoke maybe nine words, mended the latch on the stable door without being asked — mended it properly, mind, took the whole plate off — and sat up half the night. I heard him over the yard, walking."

"He'll have been to look at the kiln, then."

"He was." Hessa refilled Edda's cup, lowering her voice to its operating register. "Saw him come back over the yard at three, moonlit, easy as a cat. Edda. Who is he?"

"A mason with a sorrow. The sorrow's his own property and staying that way, so don't go picking the lock. The mason part —" Edda turned her cup, caught herself doing it, and set it down flat "— the mason part might be the first luck this village has had all autumn. My kiln's cracked, Hessa. Crown of the arch. It fired last week's load and it'll not fire another; I'd not trust it warm past a biscuit-heat, let alone the Wintermark load with every telling in the parish inside."

Hessa put down the pot. For a moment the inn's noise went on without either of them — the market-day clatter, the Askew boys losing at draughts, someone's dog dreaming by the settle — the whole warm machine of the village running on, unaware it had just lost its winter.

Wintermark, for Wenholt, was the pole the year turned on. Longest night, whole parish packed into the church and then the square: lanterns, chestnuts, the Wintermark loaf, and at the center of it, for four generations now, the Firing. The great kiln-load of the year, lit at dusk on the eve and opened at dawn on the morning, so that the year's tellings came out of the fire warm into the arms of the longest night itself. Every family in the parish had something in that load, most years. Wedding cups. Naming bowls. The year's griefs and the year's laughs, fired side by side and carried home through the snow. There were people in Wenholt who could take or leave the church service but had never once missed the kiln-opening.

"Can it be mended?" Hessa asked.

"Mortar and hope got it through last week. The arch wants rebuilding — the crown course at the least, and once you've the crown out of an arch that old, like as not the whole barrel wants doing. That's brick-making, that's a mason, that's weeks, and it's winter." Edda spread her hands flat on the table. "So. Before the whole village hears it crooked and panics in nineteen directions: I'll say it plain at the moot tomorrow. And you'll make sure Odile hears it from me tonight and not from the lane, because if the reeve learns her Wintermark's in danger from anyone's mouth but mine, she'll have the commissions on a cart to Harrowgate by Friday and call it prudence."

Which was, give or take a day, exactly what Odile Vance proposed at the moot.

The reeve of Wenholt was a tall, dry, exact woman who had been widowed young, elected often, and right about drainage every single time for twenty years. Edda liked her considerably, which few believed of either of them. Odile stood up in the moot-hall with the cracked-kiln news still ringing off the rafters and said what half the room was privately thinking: that Harrowgate's pottery was a fine modern works with three great kilns and no cracks in any of them; that carts could take the parish's greenware over and bring the fired ware back inside a fortnight; that sentiment fired no pots; and that the alternative on offer was to gamble the entire year's tellings — the whole parish's memories, mark you — on a forty-year-old brick beast that its own keeper had just declared unfit.

"Nobody grieves this more than Mistress Marren," Odile finished, dry as a good sherry, nodding to Edda across the hall. "And nobody's word on the crack do I trust more. That's exactly why I'd not fire on it. Harrowgate this year; rebuild in spring at leisure; the kiln keeps, as our witch says — well then, it'll keep till April."

It was a good speech and it was very nearly right, and the murmur that went round the moot-hall had agreement in it. Edda stood up into it slowly.

"Everything Odile says is true," she began, which surprised the room and pleased the reeve, "except two things she can't be expected to know, because they're kiln-craft. First: a telling won't cart. It's not the pot that holds the memory before firing; between the telling and the fire, the memory bides in the witch." The room went very quiet; most of them had half-known, and half-knowing spoken aloud always lands hard. "For your ware to fire in Harrowgate, your tellings must come out of me and into their kilnman's keeping — a stranger, in a gas-fired works, who never once met your grandmother nor heard her laugh. He may be the finest kilnman alive. What comes out of his fire will keep your memory the way a bought loaf keeps a recipe. You'd have pots, right enough. I'll not swear you'd have things in the room." Old Wim, at the back, made a low sound like a mooring-rope taking strain. "And second," Edda said, "we may not need April. I'm told — on a mason's inspection by moonlight, which is when heat-cracks show honest — that the arch can be rebuilt before Wintermark. New crown, new barrel, brick by brick, our own clay fired for the courses. Six weeks, worked hard. It can be done."

"By what mason?" said Odile. "Tom Chandler builds chimneys. He'd tell you so himself if he ever finished a sentence. There's no man in this parish has raised a kiln-arch in his life."

And from the back of the moot-hall, from the bench by the door where strangers and latecomers sat, a voice that nine words at the Copper Kettle had not prepared anyone for — a low, careful, mortar-and-stone voice, the voice of a man standing up with his cap in his hands in a room full of people he'd known two days:

"There's one."

# Chapter Six — The Mason's Hands

He had built kilns before. That was the first thing Wenholt learned about Ansel Rooke, in the moot-hall, in the ringing pause after he stood up: bottle kilns at the Harrowgate works when they'd re-barreled the old number two; a glass furnace at Aldermere; lime kilns up the valley at Stonebeck, years back, with — a hitch there, small, like a man stepping over a missing stair in the dark, that only Edda and possibly Hessa marked — with his brother, when the two of them had been in trade together. Rooke Brothers, masons. Folk on the Harrowgate barges might remember the name. It had been a while.

The second thing Wenholt learned was that he did not talk in promises. Handed the moot's sudden total attention, he did not say it would be easy or certain. He said he'd want the arch down to the springing line before he'd swear to anything; that a kiln that had fired forty years owed nobody an apology and might owe them all a surprise; that six weeks was tight and midwinter mortar was a craft of its own — lime hated frost like cats hated rain, there'd be nights the work wanted blanketing warmer than he'd blanket himself; and that he'd not lay a course until the witch and he had agreed the shape, because — and here the shuttered face opened, just a crack, just for a moment, and something looked out that was purely and unexpectedly craftsman — "a kiln's not a wall. A kiln's a made animal. The fire has habits, and the brick has to be shaped to the habits, and nobody knows this fire's habits but her. I'd be her hands, not her architect."

It was, Hessa said afterwards, the longest anyone had heard him speak, and he looked as surprised about it as the room did.

The moot voted the rebuild. Odile — right about drainage twenty years running, and no fool about rooms either — read the hall, put her Harrowgate proposal quietly in her pocket, and did the thing that made her worth electing: attached conditions and made herself useful. The parish would pay the mason's proper rate from the wardens' chest, not the witch's teapot ledger. Tom Chandler and the Askew boys would labor and learn. And if the new arch was not up, cured, and proven by a trial firing a clear week before Wintermark eve, the greenware went to Harrowgate on the first cart, no appeals, and the tellings would be spoken to the Harrowgate kilnman fresh — "second-best pots being," she said, dry as ever, straight at Edda, "in my continued opinion, better than no pots. Motion carried. Go home, it's past nine."

So it began. And Edda, who had expected to spend those weeks managing a stranger, found instead that she had acquired something between a colleague and a weather-system.

He was at the kiln by first light every day. He took the arch down the way she unloaded a firing — that same Christmas-morning care, brick by brick, each one numbered in raddle and set on planks in the order of its living, so that the old kiln's body lay dismantled across the frosty garden like a beast sleeping in pieces, still faintly, impossibly warm at the heart bricks even days cold, which he noticed, and weighed in his hand, and said nothing about, but after that he handled every brick as if it might be listening. He rejected the crown course entirely — heat-rotten, spalled to biscuit, forty years of firings had eaten them, and small blame to them — and half the upper barrel; and then he stood with Edda over the sound survivors and asked his mortar-and-stone questions, and listened to her answers with his whole stopped body, the way Cobb listened to tellings. Where does she draw hardest. Where does she run cold. Where does the flame lick and where does it lie. Show me the fire's path with your hands.

"You talk about it like it's alive," Perrin said to him one noon, arriving with the bread and unable to bear silence past its allotted span. "The fire."

"Your mistress talks about it like it's alive," said Ansel. "I price accordingly."

For the new bricks they went to the river. That was Edda's word and he took it without argument: the kiln had been born out of the Ler's own clay forty years back — Grams Ilsabet had seen it built, a young woman then, had told Edda the story fifty times — and a kiln patched with foreign brick was a dog with a wooden leg, serviceable and sorry. So on three sharp blue mornings the village turned out to dig at the clay-bend below the ferry landing, and Wenholt, which had voted the rebuild the way one votes for necessary drains, discovered it was enjoying itself. Wim ferried diggers and grinned like a tide-mark. Maud Brock set up a trestle on the bank with hot loaves and a cauldron of her wicked cinnamon milk. The Askew boys dug like terriers and were pulled dripping out of the clay-pit twice, to the vast satisfaction of everyone but their mother. Tally organized the small children into a puddling-gang, forty small feet treading water into clay in a plank trough, shrieking, while Old Griet — brought down wrapped in three shawls to watch, on one of her clear mornings — beat time for them against her knee and called it the best dance of the year.

And Ansel Rooke worked the pug and the brick-moulds, sanding, striking each mould level with one clean pass of the strike-stick, turning out brick after brick after brick onto the drying boards, wet and sharp-cornered and shining. He worked the way other men rested. But Edda, carrying tea down the line to him, saw the thing she had half-seen in the moot-hall: that somewhere between the digging children and the cinnamon milk and Griet's beaten time, the shutters of the man had come open a hand's-breadth, and what stood behind them was not darkness at all. It was a house with the furniture covered, waiting. It was a man watching a village make bricks the way a starving man watches bread proof: as though he had done this before, in some other life, with some other crew, and had loved it past all language, and had walked out of it one day and never once been back.

He took the tea. He turned the cup, a quarter-turn, winding.

"Master Rooke," Edda said. "When you're ready to tell it, the chair's there."

"I know," he said. Down the bank, Tally's puddling-gang collapsed in a shrieking heap; Griet applauded. He watched them, turning the cup. "That's the trouble with your village, mistress. It loosens a man's mortar."

"Ours," said Edda, "is the correct word for the village, and you may report to the good chair any evening before moonrise. The tea's not after anything, Ansel. The tea is the point."

Two evenings later, with frost feathering the workshop window and the kettle just short of singing, he came.
