# THE MORNING DOOR

*A Cozy Fantasy Novella · The Night Kiln, Book Two*

> Everyone in Wenholt knows the rule: bring Edda a memory before moonrise, and she will fire it into clay you can hold. But the letter from Stonebeck asks for the one thing the night kiln has never done. On the witch's shelf sits a honey-glazed jar that holds a stolen morning the way a keyhole holds after the house is gone — and the woman who chose to be rid of it is long dead, and her granddaughter is standing at the gate, asking whether what a fire once shut, another fire might ever, gently, open.

---

**Content note:** Adult cozy fantasy. Contains grief, remembered bereavement (offpage, long in the past), and a beloved character living with memory loss — all handled gently. No violence, no explicit content. A warm read for adult readers. This is the second Night Kiln novella; it stands alone.

---

# Chapter One — The Wedding Cup

The rule in Wenholt was older than anyone who kept it, and it was this: if you wanted a memory fired, you brought it to Edda Marren before moonrise, because the kiln burned at night or not at all. You sat in the good chair by the workshop stove with your hands around something warm, and you told the memory out loud — told it properly, the whole of it — and the telling went into the witch, and on the next firing night the fire drew it out of her and into the waiting clay, and after that the memory lived in a mug or a bowl or a butter crock, a thing you could hold, a thing that could be in the room with you. The kiln keeps, the witches of Wenholt had said for four generations, teaching it to their apprentices like a catechism. It does not take.

That was the trade, and in the spring after the kiln was rebuilt, the whole of it was on display at a wedding.

Tally Brock married her Wilf on the first properly warm Sunday of May, in the church and then in the bakehouse yard, which smelled of two hundred sweet buns and one nervous groom. And at the heart of the long table, where another parish might have set out silver, stood a two-handled cup that had come out of the Wintermark firing with a glaze like hay-dust in sunlight; and when the meal was done, Tally and Wilf drank from it together, both pairs of hands on it at once, which was the custom. Nobody watching could have told you precisely what was in the cup besides cider. Everybody watching knew. It was the proposing itself — the actual afternoon of it, hay-loft dust and all, told to the witch in the good chair last December by a girl who could not stop laughing, and fired into the clay for keeps — so that fifty years from now, on some anniversary with the details gone soft, two old people could put their four hands around it and be twenty again, up a ladder, saying yes.

"To the potter," called Wilf's father, who was from Harrowgate and meant it kindly.

"To the witch," the village corrected him, comfortably, in one voice, and drank.

Edda Marren sat at the low end of the table, where she always sat at weddings, near the door, the way a midwife sits at a christening: the work done, the fuss belonging to others, the leaving kept easy. She was past sixty now and had been the witch of Wenholt for eleven years and its witch's apprentice for a long lifetime before that, and she drank the toast in cider from her own cup, which she had brought in her coat pocket, because she drank from no other. It was a crooked cup. She had thrown it badly on purpose, years ago, to teach a boy that crooked pots deserved memories too, and it held one of her own: a summer evening, an old woman shelling peas, a catechism said back for the last time. When the cup was warm it smelled faintly of peas. It was warm now.

Under the same coat, folded small, against her heart the way nine bowls of slip stood on a shelf at the height of it, was the letter. It had come up the lane three days ago, and she had read it perhaps thirty times, and answered it not at all.

Her apprentice found her with it at the end of the evening, when the dancing had gone indoors and the yard was down to lanterns and mothers and crumbs. Perrin Loft was seventeen that spring, still mostly elbows but less so, and in the eleven months since he had stood at a stoke-mouth through the proving of the new kiln he had developed the disconcerting habit of noticing things sideways, which she had taught him, and of mentioning them straight out, which she had not.

"You've read it again," he said, sitting down on the bench beside her. "That's four times today. Once at the church, even."

"It was a long sermon."

"Is it from Harrowgate? Is it money?"

"It is from Stonebeck," Edda said, "and it is the opposite of money," and because he was seventeen, and because he would be the witch of Wenholt someday whatever he currently believed about becoming a master thrower, and because a secret kept from an apprentice out of tenderness curdles into a secret kept out of habit, she took it out of her coat and let him read it there, under the wedding lanterns, while down the yard Tally Brock — Tally Weaver now — held the hay-dust cup up laughing to the last of the light.

The letter was written in a careful hand. That was the first thing anyone would have said of it: not a fine hand, not a clerk's flourish, but careful, every letter built like a course of brick, the hand of someone to whom writing had been given as a serious gift and who had never once left it out in the rain. It said, after the respectful things:

*My grandmother held a jar once, in your workshop, long ago — a honey-glazed jar with a fitted lid, that I believe is there still. She held it more than once. She would go to Wenholt every few years of her traveling life, and come home quiet, and when I was a small girl I went with her the last time, though I remember the cart better than the town. On the road home she said a strange thing about a door, which I did not understand then and have spent this winter beginning to. She has been gone a long time now. My father, her son, died at midwinter, and in clearing his house I have inherited the story entire, and the grief that goes with it, which I find is also entire, though I never met the people it belongs to. Mistress Marren, I am told your fire keeps what it is given. I write to ask a different thing, and I will understand any answer, including silence. I wish to ask whether what a fire has shut, another fire might ever, gently, open.*

It was signed *Mercy Hale, of Stonebeck parish.*

Perrin read it twice. She watched him arrive, both times, at the same word, and stop on it, like a man arriving twice at the same closed gate.

"Shut," he said at last. "What a fire has *shut*. Mistress — the kiln keeps. It doesn't take. You've had me say it back a thousand times. What's this woman's grandmother got in our workshop that was ever *shut*?"

Down the yard, the lanterns were being gathered. The moon was coming up over the ridge, patient as a customer, and by the old arithmetic that meant the gate should be opened and the kettle hung; but there had been no tellings asked for tonight, the village having its hands full of wedding, and so for once the witch of Wenholt was idle at moonrise, with nothing to carry but her own.

"Walk home with me," Edda said, standing, pocketing the letter and the cup. "It's time you dusted the jar."

# Chapter Two — A Nice Jar

There was a shelf in the workshop's darkest corner that Perrin had been forbidden, in his first week as an apprentice, to touch; and being forbidden it, he had naturally learned it by heart from a respectful distance, the way you learn a fierce dog on a familiar road.

It was a plain board behind the glaze jars, and on it stood the private museum of the craft's mistakes and mercies: Grams Ilsabet's first pot, thumb-marked and honest; a wedding cup fired for a wedding that had never happened, unclaimed for thirty years and still faintly humming with a hope that had outlived its occasion; a dish that had been cooled too fast and dunted, a fine ringing crack straight through a memory of a rowing race, so that the race ran only half its course now and stopped forever mid-stroke. And at the end, alone, with a hand's width of bare board on either side as though even the other pots stood off from it, the jar.

Edda lit the lamps, hung the kettle, and took it down herself.

It was a beautiful thing; that was the first shock of it, always. People expected something gothic and were handed something domestic: a small honey-glazed jar with a fitted lid, the kind of jar you would keep peppercorns in. She set it on the bench in the lamplight and made the tea, and only when Perrin had a warm cup in his hands — because some stories you do not tell to empty hands, that being nearly the first rule of the place — did she say:

"Hold it a moment. Then I'll tell you what you're holding."

He held it. She watched his face do what every face did: attend, frown, attend harder — and find nothing. Not mystery. *Nothing.* The jar sat in his palms with the exact presence of an empty jar, which is a kind of absence no truly empty jar ever has; even an empty jar holds the room it's in. This one held the way a keyhole holds after the house is demolished. Perrin set it down very gently and moved his hands away from it and looked at them.

"You'll have heard the word *emptying*," Edda said, "in hearth-tales, at the dark end. Last autumn you heard a man stand in this workshop and ask me for one, and heard me refuse him, and saw what we built together instead, so you know the refusing is real. Tonight you'll have the story that stands behind the refusing, the whole of it, because the letter in my pocket belongs to that story and you're old enough to hold a page of the ledger. You know the first half of the craft. A keeping: the telling goes into the witch, the fire draws it out of her into the clay, and the clay holds it for whoever needs it held — kept, and warm, and present. An emptying is the same gate swung the other way. The teller tells the memory *with the wish to be rid of it*, and the fire takes it at the root — out of them, whole, forever — and shuts it in the pot. Not kept. *Shut.* The teller walks away lighter. That's no tale; it can truly be done; and this kiln has done it once in four generations, and the once is on the bench in front of you."

And she told him about Widow Sorrel.

She told it the way Grams Ilsabet had told it to her, the way one trade tells about the bridge that fell. A woman of Stonebeck parish, Annis Sorrel, whose man and two boys went down the valley to the winter fair, the year of the great flood, and the ford took all three coming home. Who lived; and who two years on came up the Wenholt lane on her knees — the last half-mile of it kneeling in the frost, because she had heard somewhere that was how you asked for the old mercies — and begged the witch of that day for an emptying. And not of the flood. She could bear the flood, she said. It was the morning of it she could not bear: the ordinary last morning, porridge and squabbling and her man singing flat and one boy's boots on the wrong feet, and all of them going out the door happy. *The flood only drowned my family,* she had said. *It's the morning that drowns me. Every day, at porridge-time. Take the morning. Leave me the flood but take the morning, or dig me in beside them; I've done choosing.*

"Grams Ilsabet said no for a year," Edda said. "And watched the year eat the woman down to a lantern with no candle in it, and at the end of it judged that the root was killing the bank faster than any rain would, and did it. Did it well; there was no witch better, first to last. And Widow Sorrel stood up from the telling lighter, and slept that night, and wintered, and lived. Married again in time — a decent man, a Stonebeck lime-burner, Hale by name — and was kind to him, I'm told, the way you'd be kind in a language you learned late. And every few years to the end of her traveling life she would come up our lane, and ask, since she was passing, to hold the jar. And she'd hold it — the last morning of her men, her man singing flat, the little boots wrong-footed — and feel what you just felt. Nothing. Polite, pleasant nothing. And she'd say — every time, Perrin, like the tide — *it's a nice jar, mistress; did I pay for it?* And put it down, and go out to her cart, easy as weather."

The kettle had found its note somewhere in the middle of all this. Edda lifted it off and let the workshop be quiet a moment, because the boy's face needed the room.

"I poured her tea once," she said then. "You'll not have that from any hearth-tale; you have it from me alone. Her last visit but one, or the last — the better part of forty years since. I was younger than you are now, first year under Ilsabet, all thumbs and reverence, and I poured tea for a straight-backed old woman off the Stonebeck carrier's cart who held a honey jar in her two hands for the space of a slow minute, the way you'd check a door that used to be somewhere, and said her line about paying, and thanked me for the tea by name. She'd asked my name. Grief never once looked less like what it is." She turned her own crooked cup a quarter-turn on the bench, caught herself winding, and stopped. "There was a little girl asleep on the cart, that visit, in among the lime-sacks. I gave her the end of a wedding bun off the tray, going out. Tonight that girl has written me a letter in a careful hand, asking whether what a fire has shut, another fire might ever — *gently* — open."

Perrin was silent for a long time, for Perrin. His eye went from the jar to the shelf and back: the humming cup, the half-run race, the honest first pot; all the craft's cautions ranked in the dark like sums a child got wrong once and never again.

"Can it?" he said at last. "Be opened?"

"I don't know," said Edda, and felt the words leave her like two teeth. "Eleven years the witch of this parish, a lifetime in this workshop, and there's my whole answer: I don't know. No witch in the ledgers ever asked the question. The emptying was the terrible thing, the once-a-generation thing; who empties wants it *shut*, and the shut was always the end of the story." She took the jar up, and set it back on its board, in its ring of clean emptiness, and stood looking at it with her hands folded the way she stood at ailing things. "But I'll tell you what's been sitting under my breastbone for three days, boy, heavier than any telling. Widow Sorrel came back. Every few years, all her traveling life, two days' road each way, an old woman on a lime-cart. To hold a jar that gave her nothing, and say a line about nothing, and go home. You've dusted every pot on that shelf tonight in your mind's eye; you tell me. Does a body keep checking a door she's glad is gone?"

The stove ticked. Out in the garden, faint through the shutters, the gate-bell hung still in the moonlight, and the kiln at the bottom of the garden kept its forty years of warmth against the spring night, and kept everything else it kept besides.

"No," said Perrin, apprentice witch of Wenholt, "she doesn't," and washed the two cups without being told, and that was the first council on the matter of the morning door, concluded past midnight, two votes to none.

# Chapter Three — The Ledger and the Lime

The ledgers of the Wenholt witches lived in an elm chest under the telling shelf, and there were nine of them, one hand giving way to another mid-book the way one ferryman gives the sweep to the next mid-river. Edda spent the better part of two days in them, which is to say two days on her knees on a cold floor with lamp-oil and her spectacles, while Perrin minded the wheel and the shop and brought her tea at the exact minutes she needed it, a knack of his she had stopped remarking on because it frightened them both pleasantly.

Ilsabet's entry, when she found it, was eleven lines long. Her teacher had written the way she had thrown: nothing wasted, nothing hurried, no ornament. The date, in the black of a winter moon-dark. The commission, set down as plainly as a butter crock's: *an emptying, for A. S. of Stonebeck, widowed of the great flood; refused one year; granted.* The craft of it, in the terse shorthand of a woman writing for successors and not for judges: the telling taken with the wish-to-be-rid; the fire *starved* — a long reduction, the air choked down, the kiln made to reach into the clay and pull, the same hungry fire that turns rust to winter glass turned instead on a woman's porridge-morning and taught to swallow it whole. The jar glazed honey, with a fitted lid, *because she asked that it look like nothing.* The firing survived. The widow slept.

And then, in the margin, in a smaller hand — the hand Ilsabet used for the things she said to nobody — three lines that Edda read aloud to Perrin that evening, and then had to get up and fill the kettle before she could speak again:

*She held it a year after, and said: it's a nice jar; a morning wants a room, and mine has none now. If ever this is to be undone, it will want a kinder fire than mine, and a room to open into. The kiln keeps. Tonight I taught it to take. May some hand after me teach it better.*

"She wanted it opened," Perrin said. His voice had gone the way it went at the telling shelf, quiet and sideways. "Grams Ilsabet. All those years you knew her — she was waiting for the asking, and it never came."

"She was waiting for the *ability*," Edda said. "Which is not the same as the asking, and we have neither yet; we have a margin and a letter. What we need next is under a different roof." She shut the ledger, and squared it, and looked at him over the spectacles. "Fetch your coat. We're going to see the mason about a fire that's kinder than Ilsabet's, and I'd rather hear his no before I write a word to Stonebeck."

The mason lived where the masons of Wenholt had always notionally lived and none actually had, in the old cottage by the clay-pit, which had stood empty a generation until the autumn a big quiet man out of Harrowgate rebuilt the kiln's arch and the village, in its unhurried way, concluded that it was keeping him. Ansel Rooke had mended the cottage roof before winter and its chimney before that, on the principle — he said — that a man who lodges above his own bad flashing deserves the drips; and in the spring his brother's widow had come out from Harrowgate to see the place, and had stayed, and now the cottage by the clay-pit held three: Ansel; Lena Rooke, who kept the books of half the traders in the parish with a rent-day smile that was becoming locally famous; and Lena's grown daughter Nell, who called him Uncle Anvil across the whole village green whenever she wanted to watch him drop something.

They found all three at supper, and were fed before they were allowed to state their business, Lena being of the settled opinion that Edda Marren did not eat unless invigilated. And then the table was cleared, and the letter went round it, and the ledger's margin was said aloud; and Edda watched the mason's face while he listened, because Ansel Rooke was Stonebeck-born, lime-bred, and had a memory like coursed stone, and she had come as much for what he knew as for what he could build.

"Hale," he said at last. "Aye. The lime-yard above the ford — Hale's yard, all my growing up. Eb Hale had it; a decent man; burned the sweetest lime in the valley and never once said a sentence you could lean a ladder on." He turned his cup a quarter-turn, that being a thing that happened to everyone in the parish eventually. "His wife was old Annis. The Quiet, we called her, we boys — no unkindness in it; she was kind as bread; but you'd catch her stopped in the yard of a morning, looking down the water, the way a house looks when the family's out. We none of us knew a thing about any first family. Stonebeck doesn't tell children the water-stories till they're grown, and by the time I was grown I was gone." The cup stopped. "Davin Hale was her son. Kept the yard's books after Eb went; wrote the fairest hand in the parish and priced a load fairer. He'd a daughter he taught his letters to at the kitchen table, and if that's your Mercy Hale, mistress, then I'll tell you the thing you've come for, and it's this: my brother and I built the kilns that yard fires to this day. Rooke Brothers' work, the both of them, the summer I was nineteen. I have stood on that woman's ground and eaten that family's bread, and whatever fire wants building for them, I'm the nearest thing Stonebeck has in this parish, and it's mine to build."

"There's more," said Lena — quietly, into her teacup, in the tone of a woman who has carried an account across to the correct column and is about to show her working. "Say the rest, Ansel. They've come for the whole of it."

The mason was silent a moment. Under the lamp his face did the thing it did rarely now, the shutters moving; but they moved open.

"The ford at Stonebeck," he said, "where the great flood took her men, the year before my father was born. The parish raised a bridge there after — because of the flood, because of that flood; a crossing that has drowned its people gets a bridge or gets abandoned, and Stonebeck doesn't abandon. My father carted lime over that bridge every working day of his life. And eleven years back it stood flood-cracked, and the parish scraped the money, and my brother Joss would not leave the mending of it to any other firm, not for a Minster — *the bridge is owed*, he said, and I've told you, mistress, in the good chair, with the whole of my heart on the floor, what I said back to him, and how it ended." He laid his two hands flat on the table, mason's hands, lime-bitten, steady. "He died mending the bridge the flood built. Her flood. I sat in your workshop last autumn and gave you the worst day of my life, and I never knew till this evening that it was the same water. So don't ask me if I'll build the box her jar rides to the fire in. Ask me what clay you want, and get out of the way of my bench."

Nobody spoke for a little. The fire in the cottage grate had the good manners to fill the silence with small sounds. Then Nell Rooke, who had been following all this with her chin on her fists and her mother's eyes, said, "What I want to know is the craft of it. Uncle. Witch. The first fire was starved, your ledger says — choked down, made hungry, so it *took*. So the fire that opens —"

"— is fed," said Edda, and heard, as she said it, the click of a thing arriving true, like a keystone going home under the tap of a level. "Fed full and kept generous, the widest breath I can give it. Fired not in the black of a winter moon-dark but at the other pole of the year entire: midsummer, moon at the full, the shortest night there is. And where the emptying was told with the wish-to-be-rid, the opening wants someone at the fire wishing it *back* — with their hands around something warm, the way all asking is done in this workshop. A kinder fire than Ilsabet's, the margin says. Not a fire that reaches in and pulls." She looked round the table at them, the mason and the book-keeper and the girl, and felt the shape of the thing stand up whole in the lamplight for the first time, and felt too, cold at the edge of it, everything she did not yet know: what such a fire would do to old sealed ware; where a sixty-year morning goes when its owner is a name on a stone; what it would be, for the one standing at the spy-hole, to be the channel it came home through. "A fire that opens its hand," she said, "and asks."

She wrote to Stonebeck the next morning, before doubt was up and dressed. The reply she sent was four lines in her own square workshop hand, and the whole middle of it was this: *I do not know if what you ask can be done. I am no longer certain it can't. Come when the roads are kind, and ask me across a teapot. I promise the tea and nothing else yet.*

She walked it down to the carrier herself, and stood a while at the foot of the lane after the cart had gone, in the spring sun, by the gate where a straight-backed old woman had climbed down stiffly from among the lime-sacks the better part of forty years ago. Then she went back up to the workshop, and took the jar down off its board, and dusted it herself, and — feeling not at all foolish, or not enough to stop — turned it to face the room.
