# THE MORNING DOOR

*A Cozy Fantasy Novella · The Night Kiln, Book Two*

> Everyone in Wenholt knows the rule: bring Edda a memory before moonrise, and she will fire it into clay you can hold. But the letter from Stonebeck asks for the one thing the night kiln has never done. On the witch's shelf sits a honey-glazed jar that holds a stolen morning the way a keyhole holds after the house is gone — and the woman who chose to be rid of it is long dead, and her granddaughter is standing at the gate, asking whether what a fire once shut, another fire might ever, gently, open.

---

**Content note:** Adult cozy fantasy. Contains grief, remembered bereavement (offpage, long in the past), and a beloved character living with memory loss — all handled gently. No violence, no explicit content. A warm read for adult readers. This is the second Night Kiln novella; it stands alone.

---

# Chapter One — The Wedding Cup

The rule in Wenholt was older than anyone who kept it, and it was this: if you wanted a memory fired, you brought it to Edda Marren before moonrise, because the kiln burned at night or not at all. You sat in the good chair by the workshop stove with your hands around something warm, and you told the memory out loud — told it properly, the whole of it — and the telling went into the witch, and on the next firing night the fire drew it out of her and into the waiting clay, and after that the memory lived in a mug or a bowl or a butter crock, a thing you could hold, a thing that could be in the room with you. The kiln keeps, the witches of Wenholt had said for four generations, teaching it to their apprentices like a catechism. It does not take.

That was the trade, and in the spring after the kiln was rebuilt, the whole of it was on display at a wedding.

Tally Brock married her Wilf on the first properly warm Sunday of May, in the church and then in the bakehouse yard, which smelled of two hundred sweet buns and one nervous groom. And at the heart of the long table, where another parish might have set out silver, stood a two-handled cup that had come out of the Wintermark firing with a glaze like hay-dust in sunlight; and when the meal was done, Tally and Wilf drank from it together, both pairs of hands on it at once, which was the custom. Nobody watching could have told you precisely what was in the cup besides cider. Everybody watching knew. It was the proposing itself — the actual afternoon of it, hay-loft dust and all, told to the witch in the good chair last December by a girl who could not stop laughing, and fired into the clay for keeps — so that fifty years from now, on some anniversary with the details gone soft, two old people could put their four hands around it and be twenty again, up a ladder, saying yes.

"To the potter," called Wilf's father, who was from Harrowgate and meant it kindly.

"To the witch," the village corrected him, comfortably, in one voice, and drank.

Edda Marren sat at the low end of the table, where she always sat at weddings, near the door, the way a midwife sits at a christening: the work done, the fuss belonging to others, the leaving kept easy. She was past sixty now and had been the witch of Wenholt for eleven years and its witch's apprentice for a long lifetime before that, and she drank the toast in cider from her own cup, which she had brought in her coat pocket, because she drank from no other. It was a crooked cup. She had thrown it badly on purpose, years ago, to teach a boy that crooked pots deserved memories too, and it held one of her own: a summer evening, an old woman shelling peas, a catechism said back for the last time. When the cup was warm it smelled faintly of peas. It was warm now.

Under the same coat, folded small, against her heart the way nine bowls of slip stood on a shelf at the height of it, was the letter. It had come up the lane three days ago, and she had read it perhaps thirty times, and answered it not at all.

Her apprentice found her with it at the end of the evening, when the dancing had gone indoors and the yard was down to lanterns and mothers and crumbs. Perrin Loft was seventeen that spring, still mostly elbows but less so, and in the eleven months since he had stood at a stoke-mouth through the proving of the new kiln he had developed the disconcerting habit of noticing things sideways, which she had taught him, and of mentioning them straight out, which she had not.

"You've read it again," he said, sitting down on the bench beside her. "That's four times today. Once at the church, even."

"It was a long sermon."

"Is it from Harrowgate? Is it money?"

"It is from Stonebeck," Edda said, "and it is the opposite of money," and because he was seventeen, and because he would be the witch of Wenholt someday whatever he currently believed about becoming a master thrower, and because a secret kept from an apprentice out of tenderness curdles into a secret kept out of habit, she took it out of her coat and let him read it there, under the wedding lanterns, while down the yard Tally Brock — Tally Weaver now — held the hay-dust cup up laughing to the last of the light.

The letter was written in a careful hand. That was the first thing anyone would have said of it: not a fine hand, not a clerk's flourish, but careful, every letter built like a course of brick, the hand of someone to whom writing had been given as a serious gift and who had never once left it out in the rain. It said, after the respectful things:

*My grandmother held a jar once, in your workshop, long ago — a honey-glazed jar with a fitted lid, that I believe is there still. She held it more than once. She would go to Wenholt every few years of her traveling life, and come home quiet, and when I was a small girl I went with her the last time, though I remember the cart better than the town. On the road home she said a strange thing about a door, which I did not understand then and have spent this winter beginning to. She has been gone a long time now. My father, her son, died at midwinter, and in clearing his house I have inherited the story entire, and the grief that goes with it, which I find is also entire, though I never met the people it belongs to. Mistress Marren, I am told your fire keeps what it is given. I write to ask a different thing, and I will understand any answer, including silence. I wish to ask whether what a fire has shut, another fire might ever, gently, open.*

It was signed *Mercy Hale, of Stonebeck parish.*

Perrin read it twice. She watched him arrive, both times, at the same word, and stop on it, like a man arriving twice at the same closed gate.

"Shut," he said at last. "What a fire has *shut*. Mistress — the kiln keeps. It doesn't take. You've had me say it back a thousand times. What's this woman's grandmother got in our workshop that was ever *shut*?"

Down the yard, the lanterns were being gathered. The moon was coming up over the ridge, patient as a customer, and by the old arithmetic that meant the gate should be opened and the kettle hung; but there had been no tellings asked for tonight, the village having its hands full of wedding, and so for once the witch of Wenholt was idle at moonrise, with nothing to carry but her own.

"Walk home with me," Edda said, standing, pocketing the letter and the cup. "It's time you dusted the jar."

# Chapter Two — A Nice Jar

There was a shelf in the workshop's darkest corner that Perrin had been forbidden, in his first week as an apprentice, to touch; and being forbidden it, he had naturally learned it by heart from a respectful distance, the way you learn a fierce dog on a familiar road.

It was a plain board behind the glaze jars, and on it stood the private museum of the craft's mistakes and mercies: Grams Ilsabet's first pot, thumb-marked and honest; a wedding cup fired for a wedding that had never happened, unclaimed for thirty years and still faintly humming with a hope that had outlived its occasion; a dish that had been cooled too fast and dunted, a fine ringing crack straight through a memory of a rowing race, so that the race ran only half its course now and stopped forever mid-stroke. And at the end, alone, with a hand's width of bare board on either side as though even the other pots stood off from it, the jar.

Edda lit the lamps, hung the kettle, and took it down herself.

It was a beautiful thing; that was the first shock of it, always. People expected something gothic and were handed something domestic: a small honey-glazed jar with a fitted lid, the kind of jar you would keep peppercorns in. She set it on the bench in the lamplight and made the tea, and only when Perrin had a warm cup in his hands — because some stories you do not tell to empty hands — did she say:

"Hold it a moment. Then I'll tell you what you're holding."

He held it. She watched his face do what every face did: attend, frown, attend harder — and find nothing. Not mystery. *Nothing.* The jar sat in his palms with the exact presence of an empty jar, which is a kind of absence no truly empty jar ever has; even an empty jar holds the room it's in. This one held the way a keyhole holds after the house is demolished. Perrin set it down very gently and moved his hands away from it and looked at them.

"You'll have heard the word *emptying*," Edda said, "in hearth-tales, at the dark end. Last autumn you heard a man stand in this workshop and ask me for one, and heard me refuse him, and saw what we built together instead, so you know the refusing is real. Tonight you'll have the story that stands behind the refusing, the whole of it, because the letter in my pocket belongs to that story and you're old enough to hold a page of the ledger. You know the keeping half of the craft. An emptying is the same gate swung the other way. The teller tells the memory *with the wish to be rid of it*, and the fire takes it at the root — out of them, whole, forever — and shuts it in the pot. Not kept. *Shut.* The teller walks away lighter. That's no tale; it can truly be done; and this kiln has done it once in four generations, and the once is on the bench in front of you."

And she told him about Widow Sorrel.

She told it the way Grams Ilsabet had told it to her, the way one trade tells about the bridge that fell. A woman of Stonebeck parish, Annis Sorrel, whose man and two boys went down the valley to the winter fair, the year of the great flood, and the ford took all three coming home. Who lived; and who two years on came up the Wenholt lane on her knees — the last half-mile of it kneeling in the frost, because she had heard somewhere that was how you asked for the old mercies — and begged the witch of that day for an emptying. And not of the flood. She could bear the flood, she said. It was the morning of it she could not bear: the ordinary last morning, porridge and squabbling and her man singing flat and one boy's boots on the wrong feet, and all of them going out the door happy. *The flood only drowned my family,* she had said. *It's the morning that drowns me. Every day, at porridge-time. Take the morning. Leave me the flood but take the morning, or dig me in beside them; I've done choosing.*

"Grams Ilsabet said no for a year," Edda said. "And watched the year eat the woman down to a lantern with no candle in it, and at the end of it judged that the root was killing the bank faster than any rain would, and did it. Did it well; there was no witch better, first to last. And Widow Sorrel stood up from the telling lighter, and slept that night, and wintered, and lived. Married again in time — a decent man, a Stonebeck lime-burner, Hale by name — and was kind to him, I'm told, the way you'd be kind in a language you learned late. And every few years to the end of her traveling life she would come up our lane, and ask, since she was passing, to hold the jar. And she'd hold it — the last morning of her men, her man singing flat, the little boots wrong-footed — and feel what you just felt. Nothing. Polite, pleasant nothing. And she'd say — every time, Perrin, like the tide — *it's a nice jar, mistress; did I pay for it?* And put it down, and go out to her cart, easy as weather."

The kettle had found its note somewhere in the middle of all this. Edda lifted it off and let the workshop be quiet a moment, because the boy's face needed the room.

"I poured her tea once," she said then. "You'll not have that from any hearth-tale; you have it from me alone. Her last visit but one, or the last — the better part of forty years since. I was younger than you are now, first year under Ilsabet, all thumbs and reverence, and I poured tea for a straight-backed old woman off the Stonebeck carrier's cart who held a honey jar in her two hands for the space of a slow minute, the way you'd check a door that used to be somewhere, and said her line about paying, and thanked me for the tea by name. She'd asked my name. Grief never once looked less like what it is." She turned her own crooked cup a quarter-turn on the bench, caught herself winding, and stopped. "There was a little girl asleep on the cart, that visit, in among the lime-sacks. I gave her the end of a wedding bun off the tray, going out. Tonight that girl has written me a letter in a careful hand, asking whether what a fire has shut, another fire might ever — *gently* — open."

Perrin was silent for a long time, for Perrin. His eye went from the jar to the shelf and back: the humming cup, the half-run race, the honest first pot; all the craft's cautions ranked in the dark like sums a child got wrong once and never again.

"Can it?" he said at last. "Be opened?"

"I don't know," said Edda, and felt the words leave her like two teeth. "Eleven years the witch of this parish, a lifetime in this workshop, and there's my whole answer: I don't know. No witch in the ledgers ever asked the question. The emptying was the terrible thing, the once-a-generation thing; who empties wants it *shut*, and the shut was always the end of the story." She took the jar up, and set it back on its board, in its ring of clean emptiness, and stood looking at it with her hands folded the way she stood at ailing things. "But I'll tell you what's been sitting under my breastbone for three days, boy, heavier than any telling. Widow Sorrel came back. Every few years, all her traveling life, two days' road each way, an old woman on a lime-cart. To hold a jar that gave her nothing, and say a line about nothing, and go home. You've dusted every pot on that shelf tonight in your mind's eye; you tell me. Does a body keep checking a door she's glad is gone?"

The stove ticked. Out in the garden, faint through the shutters, the gate-bell hung still in the moonlight, and the kiln at the bottom of the garden kept its forty years of warmth against the spring night, and kept everything else it kept besides.

"No," said Perrin, apprentice witch of Wenholt, "she doesn't," and washed the two cups without being told, and that was the first council on the matter of the morning door, concluded past midnight, two votes to none.

# Chapter Three — The Ledger and the Lime

The ledgers of the Wenholt witches lived in an elm chest under the telling shelf, and there were nine of them, one hand giving way to another mid-book the way one ferryman gives the sweep to the next mid-river. Edda spent the better part of two days in them, which is to say two days on her knees on a cold floor with lamp-oil and her spectacles, while Perrin minded the wheel and the shop and brought her tea at the exact minutes she needed it.

Ilsabet's entry, when she found it, was eleven lines long. Her teacher had written the way she had thrown: nothing wasted, nothing hurried, no ornament. The date, in the black of a winter moon-dark. The commission, set down as plainly as a butter crock's: *an emptying, for A. S. of Stonebeck, widowed of the great flood; refused one year; granted.* The craft of it, in her terse successor's shorthand: the telling taken with the wish-to-be-rid; the fire *starved* — a long reduction, the air choked down, the kiln made to reach into the clay and pull, the same hungry fire that turns rust to winter glass turned instead on a woman's porridge-morning and taught to swallow it whole. The jar glazed honey, with a fitted lid, *because she asked that it look like nothing.* The firing survived. The widow slept.

And then, in the margin, in a smaller hand — the hand Ilsabet used for the things she said to nobody — three lines that Edda read aloud to Perrin that evening, and then had to get up and fill the kettle before she could speak again:

*She held it a year after, and said: it's a nice jar; a morning wants a room, and mine has none now. If ever this is to be undone, it will want a kinder fire than mine, and a room to open into. The kiln keeps. Tonight I taught it to take. May some hand after me teach it better.*

"She wanted it opened," Perrin said. His voice had gone the way it went at the telling shelf, quiet and sideways. "Grams Ilsabet. All those years you knew her — she was waiting for the asking, and it never came."

"She was waiting for the *ability*," Edda said. "Which is not the same as the asking, and we have neither yet; we have a margin and a letter. What we need next is under a different roof." She shut the ledger, and squared it, and looked at him over the spectacles. "Fetch your coat. We're going to see the mason about a fire that's kinder than Ilsabet's, and I'd rather hear his no before I write a word to Stonebeck."

The mason lived where the masons of Wenholt had always notionally lived and none actually had, in the old cottage by the clay-pit, which had stood empty a generation until the autumn a big quiet man out of Harrowgate rebuilt the kiln's arch and the village, in its unhurried way, concluded that it was keeping him. Ansel Rooke had mended the cottage roof before winter and its chimney before that, on the principle — he said — that a man who lodges above his own bad flashing deserves the drips; and in the spring his brother's widow had come out from Harrowgate to see the place, and had stayed, and now the cottage by the clay-pit held three: Ansel; Lena Rooke, who kept the books of half the traders in the parish with a rent-day smile that was becoming locally famous; and Lena's grown daughter Nell, who called him Uncle Anvil across the whole village green whenever she wanted to watch him drop something.

They found all three at supper, and were fed before they were allowed to state their business, Lena being of the settled opinion that Edda Marren did not eat unless invigilated. And then the table was cleared, and the letter went round it, and the ledger's margin was said aloud; and Edda watched the mason's face while he listened, because Ansel Rooke was Stonebeck-born, lime-bred, and had a memory like coursed stone, and she had come as much for what he knew as for what he could build.

"Hale," he said at last. "Aye. The lime-yard above the ford — Hale's yard, all my growing up. Eb Hale had it; a decent man; burned the sweetest lime in the valley and never once said a sentence you could lean a ladder on." He turned his cup a quarter-turn, that being a thing that happened to everyone in the parish eventually. "His wife was old Annis. The Quiet, we called her, we boys — no unkindness in it; she was kind as bread; but you'd catch her stopped in the yard of a morning, looking down the water, the way a house looks when the family's out. We none of us knew a thing about any first family. Stonebeck doesn't tell children the water-stories till they're grown, and by the time I was grown I was gone." The cup stopped. "Davin Hale was her son. Kept the yard's books after Eb went; wrote the fairest hand in the parish and priced a load fairer. He'd a daughter he taught his letters to at the kitchen table, and if that's your Mercy Hale, mistress, then I'll tell you the thing you've come for, and it's this: my brother and I built the kilns that yard fires to this day. Rooke Brothers' work, the both of them, the summer I was nineteen. I have stood on that woman's ground and eaten that family's bread, and whatever fire wants building for them, I'm the nearest thing Stonebeck has in this parish, and it's mine to build."

"There's more," said Lena — quietly, into her teacup, in the tone of a woman who has carried an account across to the correct column and is about to show her working. "Say the rest, Ansel. They've come for the whole of it."

The mason was silent a moment. Under the lamp his face did the thing it did rarely now, the shutters moving; but they moved open.

"The ford at Stonebeck," he said, "where the great flood took her men, the year before my father was born. The parish raised a bridge there after — because of the flood, because of that flood; a crossing that has drowned its people gets a bridge or gets abandoned, and Stonebeck doesn't abandon. My father carted lime over that bridge every working day of his life. And eleven years back it stood flood-cracked, and the parish scraped the money, and my brother Joss would not leave the mending of it to any other firm, not for a Minster — *the bridge is owed*, he said, and you know, mistress, what I said back to him, and how it ended." He laid his two hands flat on the table, mason's hands, lime-bitten, steady. "He died mending the bridge the flood built. Her flood. I sat in your workshop last autumn and gave you the worst day of my life, and I never knew till this evening that it was the same water. So don't ask me if I'll build the box her jar rides to the fire in. Ask me what clay you want, and get out of the way of my bench."

Nobody spoke for a little. The fire in the cottage grate had the good manners to fill the silence with small sounds. Then Nell Rooke, who had been following all this with her chin on her fists and her mother's eyes, said, "What I want to know is the craft of it. Uncle. Witch. The first fire was starved, your ledger says — choked down, made hungry, so it *took*. So the fire that opens —"

"— is fed," said Edda, and heard, as she said it, the click of a thing arriving true, like a keystone going home under the tap of a level. "Fed full and kept generous, the widest breath I can give it. Fired not in the black of a winter moon-dark but at the other pole of the year entire: midsummer, moon at the full, the shortest night there is. And where the emptying was told with the wish-to-be-rid, the opening wants someone at the fire wishing it *back* — with their hands around something warm, the way all asking is done in this workshop. A kinder fire than Ilsabet's, the margin says. Not a fire that reaches in and pulls." She looked round the table at them, the mason and the book-keeper and the girl, and felt the shape of the thing stand up whole in the lamplight for the first time, and felt too, cold at the edge of it, everything she did not yet know: what such a fire would do to old sealed ware; where a sixty-year morning goes when its owner is a name on a stone; what it would be, for the one standing at the spy-hole, to be the channel it came home through. "A fire that opens its hand," she said, "and asks."

She wrote to Stonebeck the next morning, before doubt was up and dressed. The reply she sent was four lines in her own square workshop hand, and the whole middle of it was this: *I do not know if what you ask can be done. I am no longer certain it can't. Come when the roads are kind, and ask me across a teapot. I promise the tea and nothing else yet.*

She walked it down to the carrier herself, and stood a while at the foot of the lane after the cart had gone, in the spring sun, by the gate where a straight-backed old woman had climbed down stiffly from among the lime-sacks the better part of forty years ago. Then she went back up to the workshop, and took the jar down off its board, and dusted it herself, and — feeling not at all foolish, or not enough to stop — turned it to face the room.

# Chapter Four — The Careful Hand

Mercy Hale came to Wenholt in the last week of May, on the Stonebeck carrier's cart, riding — as the village would tell it for years, because the village missed nothing — in among the lime-sacks, by choice, with her boots hanging over the tailboard like a girl's.

She did not come to the gate at moonrise, the way the last stranger with a heavy asking had, standing at the last permissible edge of the rule like a man at a door he wasn't sure he was allowed to want. She came at three in the afternoon, walked once up the lane and once down it — being watched, with warm approval, from at least nine windows — and then knocked at the Copper Kettle and asked Hessa, please, what the mannerly hour was for calling on a witch, and whether one brought anything, and whether there was somewhere she might wash the lime off first. By the time she came up the lane at five, Wenholt knew everything about her that mattered and had decided the rest could wait: a schoolmistress, from Stonebeck; five-and-forty or thereabouts; a plain grey dress and a wooden box carried in both arms like a sleeping child; and manners, said Hessa, rinsing glasses with emphasis, manners like her handwriting, every letter built plumb.

Edda met her at the gate, and found herself met in turn by a steady, square-chinned woman with lime-dust in the seams of her boots still, who looked at her a long moment and then said, not at all what she had rehearsed — you could see the rehearsed thing fall away — "You'll have been the girl who poured the tea. She spoke of you, after. All the way down the valley she said nothing, and at the ferry she said: *the little one will do. She asked my name back.*"

"Come in," said Edda, when she could. "The kettle's on. It's always on; that's near enough the first rule of the place."

The box sat on the workshop bench between them through the first pot of tea, and Mercy Hale told her story to the room the way Edda had known she would from four lines of careful ink: in order, completely, without decoration, and with her hands around the warm cup the whole time, though nobody had yet taught her that rule. Her father, Davin Hale, dead at midwinter, quick and kind as deaths go — his heart, at his desk, his pen still in his hand; *he'd have chosen the desk,* she said, *though he'd have grumbled about the blot.* The lime-yard sold in the spring, there being no Hale after her minded to burn stone. And in the clearing of the house, on the top shelf of the linen press, wrapped in a good tablecloth nobody had ever been allowed to use: the box.

"I knew it at once, though I'd never seen it," she said. "That's a strange thing to say. I mean that our house had a shape, mistress, all my growing up, and the shape had a hole in it, and the box was the exact size of the hole. My grandmother was the kindest woman in Stonebeck and there was a room in her nobody ever stood in. My father was born knowing it, the way children know. He was the son of her second life, and he spent sixty years being cheerful about coming second to nobody he could name — she never once said their names, you understand. Not ever. The parish told him the water-story when he came of age, the way Stonebeck does, and he came home from hearing it and found her in the yard looking down the water, and he was a grown man before he understood that he'd been watching her check on them his whole life, the way you'd glance at a cradle." She turned the cup; caught herself; smiled slightly, as at an inherited habit discovered in the act; turned it the rest of the way. "He wasn't bitter. I want that said plainly, because I'm about to open the box and it will look like a bitter man's box and he was the least bitter man alive. He was *incomplete*, and he knew where the missing piece was kept, and he was too gentle ever to go and stand in front of your shelf and ask for what I've come to ask. He died with it unasked. So it comes to me, the way the good tablecloth does."

She opened the box.

Inside, laid straight as type: a pair of small boots, hard with sixty years, the little heels worn away at the outside edge the way they wear when a boy will not be taught which foot is which. A wooden horse the length of a hand, whittled by somebody who understood horses and made lovingly ridiculous by somebody who understood boys, one ear mended with a whipping of waxed thread. A woman's second wedding ring, thin as wire with wear. And a folded paper.

"The boots were Noll's," Mercy said. "The little one. Tam was the elder. Harl Sorrel was their father; he sang, my grandmother told the parish register once, *like a gate*, and married her anyway — she said that, they wrote it down, it's the one joke of hers Stonebeck kept. You'll notice, mistress, that I have their names. That's my father's doing. He went to the register and the flood-stone and he learned them, and once a year on the morning of the fair he'd walk me up to the stone and we'd stand there, him and me, saying nothing, two people with no memory in them at all, standing in front of three names like beggars outside a bakery." Her voice stayed level; her hands, around the cup, had gone white at the knuckle, and Edda watched her notice it and choose, deliberately, not to mind. "And the paper is hers. Annis's. It was on the top of the box, and it's addressed to nobody by name, which is to say it's addressed to me. I'll read it, if you'll let me. I've a reason for wanting it read in this room."

"Read it," said Edda.

The paper had been folded a long time; it opened along its creases like something coming awake. The hand was careful — the same hand, Edda saw, seen sixty years apart; the hand the daughter of the house had been given at a kitchen table by a man who'd been given it at another; the inheritance line drawn in ink.

"*To whichever of mine goes asking after a honey-glazed jar in Wenholt,*" Mercy read. "*Mind your manners with the witch; she is owed them. I paid for that jar with a morning, and the price was fair, and I would pay it again, and you're not to let anyone tell you I was wronged. The witch that did it wept more than I did; I had done my weeping, that was the whole trouble; a body cannot live at the bottom of a well and haul too. But hear your grandmother now on the one thing she knows that the witches maybe don't. They took my door off its hinges, as I asked, and they were right to, and it saved me. They did not burn the door. I have been to see it. It is a nice jar, and it holds my morning the way winter holds seed, which is a thing I could not feel and cannot prove and know absolutely, the way you know the sun in a cellar. A morning wants a room. Mine had none; I was the well it drowned in; that was me, and that was then. If ever there is a room for it — a table it could stand on, among people who would say the names — then what I ask of mine is this: go and ask the fire to be kind in the other direction. Not for me. I'll be past doors. For the morning. It was never mine only; I only paid for the jar.*"

The workshop was very quiet. On its board in the corner, in the lamplight, the honey-glazed jar stood facing the room, exactly as empty as it had been for sixty years, and Edda became aware that she had risen and crossed to it without any decision she could name, and that her eyes were not dry, and that this, too, Ilsabet's margin had known before her: *may some hand after me teach it better* — and here was the asking, come up the lane at last, in a careful hand, two witches late.

"Mistress Hale," she said. "Mercy. I'll tell you what I know and what I don't, and the not-knowing is the greater country, and you'll hear the whole map before either of us decides a thing. It has never been done. The fire that could do it, if any can, is a fire my teacher's own hand said would have to be kinder than hers, and building it will take this village until midsummer. And the risk is not to you, and it is not to me — those risks I'd spend and say nothing. The risk is to the jar. Old ware, sealed ware, fired once already to the bone, goes into a second fire the way an old woman goes into a second winter: most likely through, and no promising. If the jar cracks at heat, the morning doesn't come home, Mercy. It dies. Sixty years kept, and lost in a night, on my hand and yours. Your grandmother's paper gives leave to ask. It's you must decide whether asking is worth the answer *no fire can take back*." She came and sat again, and poured, because the pot was there and hands are better warm. "You needn't say it tonight."

"I can say the true thing tonight," said Mercy Hale, schoolmistress, in the tone in which sums are finally proved. "The jar has stood shut on a shelf for sixty years, safe as a debt nobody calls in. My family has stood the same way. I'm the last of us, mistress. When I die, the morning is a jar, forever, and nobody left even to come and hold it. *That's* the risk. Against that, your fire is no risk at all; it's the first door anyone's tried in sixty years." She looked over at the jar, and her chin came up, Hale and Sorrel both. "My grandmother said the little one would do. Ask your fire."

# Chapter Five — Things in the Room

The village learned it all inside two days, in the way of villages — everything, immediately, and for once hardly anything wrong, because Mercy Hale answered plain questions plainly and Wenholt, faced with sixty years, a dead schoolmistress-grandmother, and a jar most of them had never been allowed to see, rose to the occasion the way it had risen to the digging of the clay: it decided the matter was its own.

Not that there was no arguing. Wenholt would argue with the sunrise if the sunrise came late. Cobb the woodman, who carted tellings down from the hill farms with his hat in his fists and held that carrying folks' memories was graver cartage than logs, stood in the Copper Kettle and said what troubled him, which was: the dead woman had *chosen*. "A telling's private as a grave; well, and a grave's private as a telling. She asked for shut. Who are we to be prying up her floor because the times have turned sentimental?" And he was answered, to the surprise of every single person present including the answerer, by Odile Vance.

The reeve of Wenholt had buried a husband twenty-three years ago and had, this past Wintermark, after twenty-two of them, commissioned a butter dish with his whistling in it — a fact she believed to be a close secret, and which the entire parish therefore guarded like a nest. She set down her market basket, and the inn went quiet.

"The woman came back, Cobb," she said, dry as a good sherry. "Every few years, two days by lime-cart, forty years and more, to hold a jar that her own asking had made empty for her. I have been a widow long enough to tell you exactly what that is, and it is not contentment. It is a question, put to a door, on a schedule, by a woman too proud to put it to a person. And the letter in the box is the question written down, in her own hand, with her own leave on it. Prying up her floor? Man, she left the key under the mat and a note on the table. The only party that hasn't been asked its opinion yet is the fire." She retrieved the basket. "And as the fire's opinion will be had at the top of a midsummer firing in front of this entire parish, I move we all stop rehearsing our speeches and start splitting the wood. The wardens will pay for the oak. Not because it's owed," she added, at the door, in the tone of a woman closing a ledger, "but because it's *right*, and this village has recently developed a gratifying habit of knowing the difference."

So the wood was split, and the arguing turned, as Wenholt's arguing reliably did, into a rota.

Mercy Hale, meanwhile, was absorbed. It took about a week and looked, from outside, like nothing at all: a room at the Copper Kettle, paid for over Hessa's objections by a system of shifting subterfuges that both women pretended not to enjoy; then the schoolmistress found sitting on the churchyard wall of an afternoon with the Askew boys' youngest sister, sorting out her letters, because the child had been three years ashamed of writing her S's backward and Mercy Hale could no more walk past a backward S than Ansel Rooke could walk past a proud-standing hinge. By June there were four children at the wall most afternoons, and then a bench there that nobody admitted to having built, and Maud Brock was sending down batch-loaf ends "for the school," and the village had, without one word of arrangement being spoken, begun to wonder aloud in Mercy's hearing whether Stonebeck truly needed her back before autumn.

She went to the workshop most evenings. She learned the good chair, and the liturgy of the kettle, and she was there the evening the telling shelf was explained entire, all nine bowls of its arithmetic — *because you are a channel and not a cistern; because a memory kept too long in the keeper picks up the keeper's grief the way a pot picks up smoke; because the kiln keeps, it does not take* — and she wrote it down, being a schoolmistress, in the careful hand, and Edda let her, being, as she said, the first pupil in eleven years to bring her own slate.

And once, in the long blue evening of the first of June, she went down to the ferry.

Old Wim rowed her over and back for nothing, which was his custom with pilgrims, and his Griet sat in the stern in the low sun, wrapped soft, on one of her clear evenings, and named the herons on the flats — every one, same as ever, while Wim's face did its weather. Griet's memory had been leaving her the way a long tide goes out, kindly and absolutely, for years; the village knew it the way it knew the river. On the far bank Mercy sat with her a while, two schoolmistress hands around one ferrywife's, and coming back she was silent until midstream, and then said:

"The porridge bowl on her shelf. That's your work. Wim told me — mornings are her hard time, and she holds it, and there's a minute comes into her face. His lamp in the window, he calls it." She looked down the darkening water. "Mistress — I'll ask it once, and then never again, because I watched your face at the word *emptying* and I'd not use that face's opposite against you. The doors that are closing in that dear woman. Can your fire —"

"No," said Edda, and made herself say it whole, because this question deserved never to be answered small. "What shuts Griet's doors is no fire of mine or any witch's, and what it shuts, nothing opens; I'd have burned the workshop to warm one hinge of it years ago else. That's the border of the craft, Mercy, and I patrol it honestly or I'm a fairground fortune-teller with a chimney. We cannot keep Griet's rememberings in Griet." The oars breathed; the water ran; on the far thwart Old Wim rowed on, listening the way rivermen listen, with the whole back. "What we could do — all we could ever do — is what this trade has done since the first witch fired the first laugh. Keep them *near* her. In the room. Warm, of a morning, in her two hands. It isn't the door, and I'll not insult either of you by pretending. It's a lamp in the window of the house the door belonged to."

Wim brought the boat in to the landing-stage with one long practiced draw, and shipped the oars, and sat a moment.

"When my Griet is past all of it," he said, to the river generally, "and past me too — that bowl goes on, minding our snow. Fifty year, a hundred. Some other soul entirely will hold it of a cold morning and be warmed by a wedding-winter it never saw." He handed Mercy up onto the stage, courtly as a young man. "That's the trade you've come to deal with, mistress schoolteacher. It keeps things *for the room*, past all our keeping. So you go up that lane and tell the witch from me: open the Stonebeck woman's door. Some of us stand at the water every day of our lives wishing doors could be asked. Here's one can be. It'd be a sin against every shut thing in this valley to leave it standing shut out of politeness."

# Chapter Six — How a Fire Opens

The craft of it was settled — insofar as a thing never once done can be called settled — across three evenings at the workshop bench, by a council of four and a cat that adopted them for the duration: the witch, the mason, the apprentice, and the schoolmistress, who took the minutes.

The shutting, Ilsabet's ledger said, had been a starved fire: reduction, long and deep, the air choked down until the flame turned hungry and searching, the same fire that reaches into iron and pulls the breath out of it — turned, that once, on a telling, and taught to swallow. So the opening would be the fire's other temper entire. "Feed it easy and it stays shallow," Edda said, quoting herself teaching Perrin celadon, a year and a lifetime ago. "All my working life that's been the fault to fire *against* — a lazy, generous, open-breathed flame that never goes deep, never takes hold, never reaches in. Now it wants to be the whole craft. A fire fed full, drawing wide, asking nothing. A fire with its hand open. And we run it at the year's other pole — midsummer eve, the moon at the full, the shortest night there is."

"Which is the one night," Perrin observed, "there's barely dark enough to fire in. The kiln burns at night or not at all — and midsummer the dark's what, six hours? Seven? We've never once brought her to temperature in seven hours. The Wintermark load takes ten."

"The Wintermark load fills every shelf. This load is one saggar." Ansel had it half-drawn already, in raddle on a planed board: a clay box, thick-walled, river-bodied, to stand the jar in through the fire the way a keep stands a king — proof against flame-lick and ash-fall and the thousand small violences of a kiln's inside. "And she draws fast, the new barrel; you've said so yourself every firing since the proving. Seven hours is tight and possible, like everything else worth a mason's wages. What it means is nothing wasted: dry wood, small wood, a fire run clean off the top of the flame the whole night, no banking, no easing. A sprint, run gentle. Contradiction in terms; so's the whole commission."

The saggar he built over a week, out of the same clay-bend the kiln itself was born from, and he built it the way he had built the arch — as though it might be listening — and on the underside, where only the fire would ever read it, he set the yard mark, two rooks back to back, because the box rode to the fire on Rooke Brothers' account; he said none of that aloud, and everyone present heard it anyway.

The harder question was the one Edda had carried since the cottage table, and she laid it out for them plainly, because a crew that hauls in ignorance is a crew betrayed. A keeping went *teller into witch, witch through fire into clay*: the channel ran one way, and had for four generations. The opening must run the channel backward — *out* of the clay, through the fire, into the witch. That much the ledger's logic gave her. But a memory come home to the witch is not a memory come home; a witch is a channel and not a cistern, and this memory's own wellspring was forty years in the churchyard. A keeping could go home to its teller. Where does a morning go whose every rememberer is a name on a flood-stone?

"Into the room," said Mercy Hale, looking up from the minutes. "That's what the whole valley's been telling us, mistress, in one voice, since the letter was opened. My grandmother: *a morning wants a room*. Your Ilsabet: *a room to open into*. The ferryman: *it keeps things for the room*. You can't give the morning back to its people. Give it back to the ware. The jar went into the first fire a shutting. Let it come out of the second a keeping — the same jar, the same honey glaze, standing on a table at last among people who say the names. Not a strongbox any longer. A morning-jar. The door rehung on its own old frame."

Perrin was nodding before she finished, apprentice-fast; but Edda sat quite still, because she had seen the whole of it now, and the whole of it had a middle, and the middle was her.

"Then it's two fires," she said. "Hear the shape, all of you, and hear where it thins. Midsummer eve, the opening fire: the jar goes up in Ansel's box, the flame is fed wide, and at the top of the night — if the craft is true, if the door swings — sixty years of a kept morning comes out of that clay in one breath. Not thread by thread, the way tellings lift out of me all night long, a firing's slow mercy. All at once, backward, against the current the channel was cut for; and the only vessel on the premises fit to receive it is the witch. I hold it. However it comes and whatever it weighs, I hold it — days, while the kiln cools and is set fresh. Then the second fire, the keeping fire, the one this workshop knows in its bones: the morning goes out of me and home into the jar, gentled, the right way round, a keeping like any keeping since the first witch and the first laugh." She spread her hands flat on the bench, an old woman's hands, steady, clay in the knuckle-creases forty years deep. "Between the two fires you'll be sharing this village with four Stonebeck folk sixty years drowned, wearing me for a house. I've carried one grown grief before, and it read me its letters nightly. I tell you this not to be dissuaded — I'll not be — but because Ilsabet held that a crew that hauls in ignorance is a crew betrayed, and because when I am strange to you in those days, short of temper at the teapot or weeping at porridge, you'll know it for the weight and not the woman, and you'll say it back to me. Now." She rose, and took down the lamp, in the tone of a woman closing a moot. "Before any jar of mine rides any fire of Ansel's, the craft gets proven on a willing soul with her own two feet to stand on. It so happens there's a cup on that shelf has been waiting thirty years for the asking. Perrin, fetch my shawl. We're going down to the Copper Kettle after closing, and — no, not you three. This one's business is the chair's."

# Chapter Seven — The Unclaimed Cup

The Copper Kettle after closing was a different country from the Copper Kettle open: banked fire, one lamp, chairs up-ended on tables like roosting birds, and Hessa in the middle of it counting the day into a tin box. She let the witch and the apprentice in, looked from one face to the other, and said, "I'll warm the pot," which in Hessa was the same as asking what's wrong and braver.

Edda waited until the three of them were sat at the fire, and then she set on the table, wrapped in a clean cloth, the thing she had carried down the lane, and unwrapped it, and said nothing at all; because to this particular woman, about this particular pot, there had never once in thirty years been anything said, and it seemed honest to let the silence go first.

It was a wedding cup. Two-handled, wide-lipped, glazed a blue that had been fired for an April and kept its April; and it hummed. Even Perrin could feel it from his side of the table — a hope, faint and steady, thirty years past its occasion and not once given up.

Hessa looked at it for a long time. The fire ticked. When she finally spoke it was to Perrin, not Edda, in the voice she used for telling the Askew boys the truth about something.

"I was twenty-two," she said, "and his name was Col, and he was a journeyman glazier out of Harrowgate, here two summers reglazing the church and the moot-hall, and he had a way of holding a pane of glass up to the light like it was somebody's face. We were to be married in the April. I sat in that chair of hers — no; in that chair's mother; it was old Ilsabet then, and the chair before this chair — and I told the hope. Us two, and the inn we'd have someday — I had it all told, mind, down to the color of the door — and the telling went into the old witch to be fired into our cup for the wedding morning. And in the March he wrote from the coast, where he'd gone to earn the last of it, that he was sorry, and he was; he was a kind man with a short anchor-rope, and somebody nearer had hauled him in. He did well, I believe. Glazed half a cathedral, they say. And I never went back for the cup, and the old witch never once sent word about it, nor the new witch after her, and I have eaten my dinners across a yard from that shelf for thirty years, madam, knowing exactly what was on it, and if either of you ever tells this village that Hessa of the Copper Kettle is the unclaimed wedding on the witch's shelf, I will water the beer."

"The chair keeps counsel," said Edda. "Thirty years it's kept this one. I'd have kept it thirty more, Hessa, and gladly. I've come to ask something else entirely, and you're to hear the whole shape of it before your heart answers, because your heart's been answering things all its life a half-second before the question's done."

She laid it out the way she had at her own bench: the letter, the ledger's margin, the two fires of midsummer; and then the near edge of it, the part that had walked her down the lane tonight. The craft of opening had never been done. She would not run it first, untried, on a sealed jar holding four dead people's only morning; that was not courage, it was gambling with the goods of the grave. It wanted proving on a keeping — a true keeping, whose owner was alive, and willing, and standing on her own ground with her own name on the hope inside. The cup would go into a spring firing, in the mason's new-built saggar. If the craft was true, the hope would come out of the clay and home through the witch, and be told back to its own teller by the fire's own dawn — into her two hands, around something warm, the right way round at last.

"And what's left after," Hessa said, slowly, "is a cup. An ordinary cup. Empty as any cup in my racks."

"Empty as any cup in your racks. The hope doesn't die, Hessa; it comes home to the woman it belongs to, who can do with it what living women do with hopes — keep it, spend it, laugh at it, hand it on. But it'll not hum on a shelf any longer. That's the price and the point. You'd be trading a shrine for a Tuesday."

"I have had," said Hessa, "enough shrine to be going on with, thank you," and then she sat with her hands flat on the table for the length of time it takes a banked fire to shift twice, and nobody hurried her; and when she looked up her eyes were wet and her chin was square. "Thirty years I've kept an inn with a spare room in my chest, and you're offering to move the lodger down to the parlor where I can finally get a look at her. That girl with the door all picked out — I've missed her, Edda. Not him; her. Aye. Do it. Fire my cup. And on the far side of your midsummer, when the Stonebeck jar stands warm on a table at last — it stands on one of my tables first, in this inn, at a supper I will cook for that whole crew, because a morning wants a room, does it? Then it can have the warmest one in the parish. That's my fee, witch, and it's not negotiable; this is my inn."

They fired the cup ten nights later, in the ordinary June firing, a middling load of christening bowls and hill-farm tellings; and nothing about the night looked historic except two details, which between them held the whole future of the craft: a clay box on the middle shelf with two rooks on its underside, and an innkeeper sitting up past three at a stoke-bench, wrapped in a horse-blanket, watching the spy-hole like a door.

It went, Edda would write in the ledger afterward, in her square hand, for the witches after, both easier and harder than the craft had feared. Easier: the fire, fed wide and open-breathed, wanted to give; that was the night's revelation, the thing no starved-fire tradition could have guessed; generosity was not a trick you played on flame but a temper it had been waiting to be asked for, and when the cup came to the top of its heat the hope rose off it like mist off a June river, easy as breathing out. Harder: what rose came home all at once. Thirty years of a kept April, one breath, backward, against the current — and the witch at the spy-hole took it standing, and it filled her the way floodwater fills a boot, and Perrin, watching his mistress's knees with his hand already out, learned in one heartbeat more about the Sorrel commission than three evenings of council had taught him. She held. It was one hope, young and light; she held it the four hours to dawn, swaying at the stoke-bench like a woman on a deck, her own eyes full of somebody else's April; and at first light, with the kiln clammed and cooling, she sat Hessa down in the workshop's good chair, and put warm tea between her two hands, and told her back her own hope, whole — the girl, the glazier, the light through lifted glass, the inn with its door the color of new paint, every stitch of it — and the telling went home the way water goes downhill, the way a channel runs when it finally runs the way it was cut.

Hessa wept, and laughed, twice each, in the wrong order, and blew her nose on her apron.

"Well," she said at last, unsteady and entirely herself, "she was right about the door, that girl. Wrong about the man, but right about the door; I painted it that exact green four springs later and never once knew why." She looked down at the cup between her palms — just a cup now, blue, well-made, quiet; a good cup for an inn — and turned it a quarter-turn, and set it down. "It goes into the racks," she said. "Common service. Any road-worn soul that stops at my house may drink from a wedding cup and never know it. That's what I call a keeping, mistress; put that in your ledger."

# Chapter Eight — What It Costs

She slept until evening, and woke to a workshop that had been swept, stocked, and defended in her absence — Perrin had turned away two tellings at the gate with the full formula, come back before moonrise tomorrow, and had written both commissions into the day-book in a hand that was trying very hard not to be proud of itself — and to a visitor sitting patient by the cold stove with her hands folded, who did not say how do you feel, but instead, being a schoolmistress:

"I watched you at the spy-hole, mistress. When the cup gave up its April. I have seen a woman take floodwater to the knees and keep her feet, and that is what I saw at your kiln. So here is my arithmetic, and you'll not talk me past it: the cup was one hope, thirty years old, light as a girl. The jar is a whole morning, sixty years steeped, with four people in it, and the fire will hand it to you the same way — all at once, in one breath, backward. And you are the only vessel in the parish it can come home through." Mercy's voice was level; her knuckles, folded in her lap, were not. "I sat up the rest of the night doing the sum and it will not come right. I withdraw the asking, Edda. Let the jar stand shut. I've had my answer — it *can* be done, the craft is proven, the ledger is richer, and some younger witch in some kinder year can open my family's door when you're safe past being spent on it. I'll not buy a morning for the dead at the price of the living. My grandmother would box my ears."

The workshop was quiet.

"Your grandmother," Edda said at last, "walked the last half-mile of my lane on her knees in the frost, because she'd heard that was how you asked for the old mercies. I've had sixty years to think about that woman, one way and another, and I'll tell you the conclusion of it: nobody in this story gets to spare anybody, Mercy. It's not that kind of story. It's a rope, and ropes want all hands." She filled the kettle and hung it, the evening's first liturgy, and talked while it climbed. "Now hear my arithmetic, since we're showing our working. First: you'd have me leave it to a younger witch in a kinder year. There is no younger witch; there's a boy who'll be ready in ten years and a jar that has stood shut for sixty; and old ware doesn't ripen, it tires. Every winter on that shelf is a winter of frost in the glaze-crack and the seal going brittler. If it rides a fire at all, the kindest year it will ever ride one is this year. Second: the cup taught us more than you sat up counting. It taught us the fire *wants* to give — that the hard half is the catching, not the coaxing. Very well: then the craft's whole question is how the catcher stands, and standing is a thing masons know." The kettle sang; she filled the pot. "And third, Mercy Hale, and hear this one all the way to the floor, because it's the only pride I'll claim tonight: this is what a kiln-witch *is*. Not a woman who fires pots. A channel, the width of one heart, between the fire and the folk. Four generations this workshop has taken in what people could not carry alone, and carried it to the clay. Your family's morning is the single heaviest thing this parish has ever asked mine to carry, and if I let it stand on that shelf out of husbandry of myself, then I've spent my last years keeping the craft *safe*, which is the one thing Ilsabet never once asked of me. She asked me to teach it *better*. So the asking stands, if you'll stand it back up. But it wants a third pair of hands first, and he's waiting for us at the kiln, and it's a fair evening. Walk down the garden."

Ansel Rooke was at the kiln shed in the last of the light, as he was most evenings, on the older of the two stools, with his palm flat against the brick flank the way other men sit with dogs. He heard out the schoolmistress's arithmetic and the witch's, gravely, completely; and then he stood, and did a thing he had never done in front of either of them: he took the little worn oak-and-brass level out of the tool-bag that went where he went, and laid it gently along the cold iron of the stoke-mouth, dead center, under the crown.

"Last autumn," he said, "I gave this kiln the worst day of my life. Not to a pot, mistress schoolteacher — you'll have had the story from the village, they tell it kinder than I deserve — into the crown itself. My brother's death is fired into the keystone of this arch, by her hand and my asking, and every load since, it's been up there, bearing. That's what a keystone does; it's the stone that turns two falling things into one standing thing." He tapped the level once, softly, the way you'd touch a sleeping child's foot. "Now attend to what the mason knows and the ledgers don't. The cup firing — I had my hand on this flank when the hope came off, the moment your knees went, Edda; I felt the draw shift. The whole animal leaned. Toward you, into the pull, the way a wall leans into a buttress — a half-breath before your knees went, not after. The kiln moved *first*." He straightened, and in the dusk his face was the face he'd worn in the moot-hall, the craftsman looked all the way out of the shutters. "You'll not stand at that spy-hole alone at midsummer, and I don't mean me beside you, though I'll be there. I mean the animal itself is crewed. There's a mason in the crown, mistress, the best I ever knew, and he's had a year's practice at bearing what comes off this fire. When the morning comes home all at once — let it come through the arch that's already holding a grief, to the woman standing under the keystone. Two falling things," he said, and picked up his level, and it was as near to gentle as a voice made of mortar and stone was ever going to get. "One standing thing. It's a two-man lift, and we have the two. Joss and I will take the thrust. You take the morning."

The moon came up over the ridge while they stood there, one day off the full, patient as a customer; and somewhere below in the village a door opened and let out lamplight and supper-noise and shut again; and Mercy Hale looked from the witch to the mason to the great warm brick beast with its crew of the living and the dead, and set the asking back on its feet.

"Midsummer eve, then," she said. "All hands."

# Chapter Nine — The Apprentice's Bowl

There was one more telling to take before midsummer, and Edda did not take it.

This was the shape of the thing, laid out at the bench two mornings later, in the dry voice she kept for the largest gifts: the witch must go to the opening fire empty. Empty as she had never once gone to a fire in eleven years — no tellings on the shelf, no threads under the breastbone, the whole channel swept and dressed and standing open for one guest. The June commissions were fired and delivered; the day-book was shut till July; for the first time since Grams Ilsabet died, the telling shelf would stand bare on a firing night, nine bowls empty at the height of her heart. "But there's a commission in this room that can't wait for July," she said, "because its teller fires her family's jar at midsummer, and no soul should stand at that fire with her own grief still in her pockets. Mercy needs her father told, and told *before*. And I can't take it. I go in empty; that's the craft. So." She reached down the third bowl from the shelf, and the watered clay, and set them — not on her own knee — on the wedging bench, in front of the boy. "You'll take this one, Perrin Loft."

The workshop went very still, the particular stillness of a moment a boy has imagined nightly for three years arriving without appointment on a Tuesday.

"I've never —" he began, and stopped, because she'd taught him that, too: never lead with the thing you can't do. He wiped his hands on his apron, twice. "The rule says the witch carries what's told. If I take it, I carry it. Into the firing. Through the fire. Me."

"Through the second firing, aye — the keeping fire, three nights after midsummer, a gentle load, my hand on the wood and my eye on the cones the whole of it. One telling, carried five days by an apprentice with his mistress across the bench, which is precisely how Ilsabet weaned me onto the weight, and her teacher her. You've stood a year at my elbow watching tellings go in and out of me like tide; you've the sideways eye, the listening hands, the tea-knack that frightens us both; and yesterday you turned away two grieving women at the gate so kindly they left blessing you. The craft has been arriving in you for three years, boy. Today it knocks." And then, because the moment had gone as solemn as it was ever useful for a moment to go, she added: "Besides, you threw the vessel. It'd be a poor christening with another man's baby."

For that was the other half of it: the vessel for Davin Hale was Perrin's own — the first true piece of his prenticeship, thrown the week before on the big wheel with Edda's hands nowhere near it. An inkwell. Squat, wide-footed, unspillable by design — a piece for a working desk — with a brushed ash glaze that would come out of the fire the exact brown of old oak gone silver at the edges, or so the recipe promised, and a lip Ansel had pronounced *true* with the level actually out, which had done more for the boy than a knighthood.

So that evening, at the hour between the lamps and the moon, Mercy Hale sat in the good chair with her hands around the warm cup, and told her father to the apprentice witch of Wenholt.

Edda kept the stove, and watched, and did not once help, which was the hardest labor she'd done all month. She watched the boy settle the bowl in his lap wrong, and fix it himself; watched him listen the way she'd taught him, sideways, with his whole stopped body; watched the slip begin, halfway through the telling, to turn the soft iron-gall black of good ink, and the boy's face when he felt the first of it arrive under his breastbone — the fine strange ache, the swallow of too-hot tea, the guest coming into the house. And the telling itself was a schoolmistress's telling, exact and unhurried and utterly without armor: a kitchen table in the lime-yard house, a winter's worth of evenings, a big-knuckled clerk's hand closed warm over a small one, guiding the pen. *A word is a thing you can hold, Mercy. Most things go by. Ink stays. That's the whole magic there is, and it's enough.* The careful hand, given the way it had been given to him, letter by letter, like courses of brick; the smell of lime and lamp-oil; the specific creak of his chair when she got a whole line right and he leaned back to admire it as though she'd built a bridge. And at the very end, the thing Edda had been waiting to see whether the boy could catch: the teller's voice went thin on the last of it — *he checked my letters every evening of his life and I never once wrote to tell him what they were worth* — and Perrin Loft, seventeen, first telling, did not reach for comfort or for craft. He said, quietly, in the good chair's own idiom: "It's in the telling now, mistress. It'll be in the clay. You wrote it tonight."

The slip in the bowl went black as a well-kept ledger-line and shone.

Afterward, when Mercy had gone down the lane lighter — you could see it from the door, the walk of the newly two-on-the-rope — Edda banked the stove while her apprentice stood at the telling shelf, at the height of another woman's heart, looking at the one full bowl standing there among the eight empty, with his hand pressed flat below his own collarbone like a man checking a strange new pocket.

"It's heavy," he said, wondering. "It's so *little*, and it's heavy. And it's — mistress, it's *warm*. Nobody ever says the warm."

"No," Edda agreed, from the stove, not turning around, because a boy's first carrying deserves the privacy of a witch's back. "The weight you'll hear about in every ledger on that shelf. The warm you find out for yourself. That's the craft's other secret, Perrin, and the reason none of us ever leaves it: we are not pack-mules, whatever Cobb thinks. Between the telling and the fire, for a little while, we're the room the memory lives in. Keep him well, the clerk of Stonebeck. Read by his lamp while you've got it." She hung the last cup on its hook. "Now sleep. Tomorrow we sweep the kiln, the mason seats his saggar, and the village starts its wood-count for the shortest night of the year. Five days, boy. Then we go and ask the fire to be kind in the other direction."

# Chapter Ten — The Short Night

Midsummer eve in Wenholt had always been the year's loose-jointed festival, the unWintermark: no church to it, no firing, just the village on the green until all hours, proving to itself that the dark could be sat out entire with nothing worse than fiddle music. The Short Night, they called it. This year the Short Night had a kiln in it, and the green stood empty by sundown, because the whole parish was in the witch's garden, being useful or being quiet, which in Wenholt amounted to the same love.

The load had been set that afternoon with a ceremony nobody had planned and nobody broke: the saggar alone on the middle shelf, square before the spy-hole, two rooks downward to the fire; and inside it, on a bed of clean river sand, the honey-glazed jar, lid fitted, exactly as a starved flame had left it sixty years before. Ansel packed the sand himself. Mercy Hale set the jar in with her own hands, which was right, and steadied it a moment longer than setting needed, which was also right; and Edda bricked the door with Perrin at the mortar, and the sun went down on the shortest night of the year, and they lit her.

Seven hours of dark. It was a fire like no fire the workshop's ledgers held, and Edda ran it the way Ansel had named it that first evening — a sprint, run gentle. Dry oak and split apple-wood, small stuff, stick on stick on stick, the flame kept clean off the top of its appetite the whole climb, never banked, never starved, never asked to reach for anything: a fed fire, a spendthrift fire, generous as June. The new throat drew like the animal had trained for it. By the second hour the flame off the stoke-mouth stood straw-gold and easy; by the third the first cone bowed. The village kept its distance in the old way once true dark came, back to lanterns at the garden's edge; what stayed in the shed was the night's own crew: the witch at the spy-hole, empty as a swept church; the mason at the flank, palm to the brick, under the crown; the apprentice on the wood, carrying his clerk warm under his breastbone and stoking like a metronome; and in the good chair, carried down from the workshop by four Askews because the shed was where the chair was needed — Mercy Hale, wrapped against the small-hours cold, both hands around a warm cup that Hessa refilled without being asked, on the hour, from the flask, like a sacrament.

At moonhigh — the full moon riding low and golden over the ridge, midsummer's own lantern — Edda came from the spy-hole and stood before the chair.

"It's time. Say your asking, Mercy. There's no set words; there never were; the fire's no lawyer. Hands around the warm, and wish it back, and say it so the night can hear you."

And the schoolmistress of Stonebeck sat straight in the good chair, in the firelight, before the parish at the lanterns and the crew at the fire, and said, in the voice she kept for the first morning of school: "Annis. Harl. Tam. Noll." — the names first, the way you call a register — "This is Mercy, Davin's girl. We've a room ready. Come home."

Edda was back at the spy-hole before the last word settled. Afterward, no two accounts of the next minutes agreed, and all of them were true. Perrin, at the wood, said the fire changed its vowel — the same long roar, but rounder, the sound of a thing consenting. Ansel, palm to the flank, said the animal leaned, the whole barrel, a half-breath early, into the coming weight, and the keystone over his hand went — not warm; keystones are past warm in a firing — *present*, the way a man goes present at his end of a rope when the load swings. And Edda, at the spy-hole, her eye on the little box with the rooks turned down to the flame, saw the moment the door swung: no crack, no flare, nothing so cheap; only, over the saggar, a shimmer that was not heat-shimmer, and then the fine strange ache under the breastbone arriving not as a swallow of too-hot tea but as a tide, and she had time to say, clearly, to the crown of the arch, "*Now, masons*" — and the morning came home.

It came all at once, backward, sixty years deep, and it was not heavy the way stone is heavy; it was heavy the way a sleeping child is heavy, absolute and alive and given. It filled her past every mark the cup's April had left, and her knees went, and did not reach the floor: Ansel had her by one arm and Perrin by the other before the sway finished starting, the two-man lift closing exactly as built; and above the three of them the keystone bore, and the arch leaned, and the kiln — crewed, that night, by the living and the dead — took the thrust and stood, one standing thing made out of every falling thing under its roof, and Edda Marren stood with it, under the crown, in the roar of the kindest fire ever fired in Wenholt, with a whole morning in her: porridge-steam, and boys squabbling, and a man singing like a gate, and the wrong little boots, and the door, and the light through the door, and all of them going out into it happy — held, entire, at the height of her heart.

"I have them," she said. "Clam her up. We're going to be cutting it fine with the dawn."

They were. The shortest night pays out no rope; the second cone touched the shelf as the sky went grey, and they bricked every hole and mortared the animal blind against the sunrise the way sextons shut a church against weather, the fire sealed in with its work to cool at its own pace, two days at the least, and no soul on the premises would know until the unbricking whether the jar had ridden it whole.

The crack came on the second evening of the cooling, at dusk, when half the crew was at supper in the garden. One sound, from deep in the sealed dark: a single high ring, short and clean, like a small bell struck once — and then nothing, the long tick and settle of a cooling kiln, ordinary as rain. Nobody ate much after. Ansel sat the whole third night against the shed post with his level in his two hands like a psalter; and at gray dawn on the opening morning he unbricked the door himself, brick by brick, the Christmas-morning ceremony gone silent as surgery, and drew out the saggar, and lifted its cover.

The jar stood whole. Honey-glazed, sound as a bell, sixty years old and twice-fired, and — every hand that held it swore it, that morning and forever after — warm past what the cooling explained. Beside it on the clean sand, in two pieces, broken along one clean line like a thing that had finished rather than failed, lay the fitted lid.

Ansel looked at it a long moment, and then at the witch, and the June morning stood still around them.

"The door came open," he said. "And the lock came off. I'll grind the edges smooth, and that's all the mending it wants." He set the two pieces in Mercy's hands, gently, like change from a great purchase. "No morning-jar needs a lid, mistress. You can't latch a room the sun's expected in."

# Chapter Eleven — The Morning, Told

For three days between the fires, the witch of Wenholt kept house for four guests, and the village kept house for the witch.

She had warned them what the carrying would be, and was wrong, the way she was always wrong about the exact grain of a thing and right about its weight. She was not scoured; she was *crowded* — a one-hearted woman living at a table set for five. She caught herself, the first morning, salting the porridge for boys; stood in her own garden at noon with a sourceless conviction that lime wanted turning; woke the second night with a flat tune in her head and her feet already seeking the floor, and had to sit on the bed's edge naming things — my gate, my quinces, my kiln, my morning and not mine — until the guests settled. But they were kind guests. That was the thing no ledger had promised: a memory told with the wish-to-be-rid comes to the witch as a wound; this one had been asked back, wished home, called by its names off a register, and it lived in her those three days the way June light lives in a kitchen. Perrin, carrying his quiet clerk alongside her the whole way, tea at the exact minutes, said on the second evening, sideways: "The workshop's *full*, mistress. Has anyone else noticed the workshop's full?" — and everyone who came up the lane those days said some cousin of the same thing, and went away slower than they'd come.

The telling was set for the third evening, before the keeping fire that midnight, and it was held — over Edda's single mild objection, defeated four to one — at the Copper Kettle, because a fee had been named in the spring and Hessa of the Copper Kettle did not forget debts owed to *her*. The inn was shut to trade and open to the crew of the thing, which by that evening was a definition comfortably including two dozen souls: the fire-crew and the Rookes entire; Odile Vance with her butter dish, uninvited and unchallenged; Wim, over the water at dusk, with Griet on his arm, it being one of her clear evenings, and a porridge bowl in a hay-lined basket, because — he said, gruff, to the room's unasked question — *ours minds a winter morning; it can stand witness for a summer one*. And on the long table before the fire, on Hessa's third-best cloth, which was her best cloth with a story she wasn't telling: a pair of small boots, a wooden horse with a mended ear, a thin gold ring, a broken lid in two clean pieces, and the jar, open-mouthed, waiting, in the warmest room in the parish.

Edda sat in the chair by the fire — the inn's chair, but every chair she told from was the good chair now, that being how these things go — took the warm cup Perrin put into her hands, and gave the morning back to the world.

She told it entire. Not about it: *it* — the way tellings go in the one direction they had never until that summer been asked to go, out of the witch, into the room, whole. A kitchen above a lime-yard, sixty-odd years ago, on the last morning of the world that didn't know it was one, which is every morning there is. Porridge on the crane, and the steam of it on a cold window. Two boys at one bench in one shaft of early sun, at war over a wooden horse — *Tam had the age, Noll had the grip, and the horse had a career of it* — and their father at the door-post with his boots in his hand, singing. Flat. Singing flat a tune the parish had four better versions of, singing it the way a gate sings, entirely for the joy of being unfit to, while his wife told him for the ten-thousandth time that he'd curdle the milk, and he sang the reply, flatter, on purpose, and she laughed with her back to him so he'd not have the satisfaction, and he had it anyway; he always had it. And the small one's boots on the wrong feet, and Annis on her knees to change them, and the boy dancing with impatience for the fair, and her hands stopping — *this is in the telling, plain as the sun on the floor* — her hands stopping one heartbeat on the little wrong-footed boots, out of nothing, out of love with nowhere to put itself; and then the door. The door open, and the light through it, and the fair down the valley like a promise the day had every right to make; Harl first, with a boy on each side, and the horse riding in Noll's pocket ears-out; and Annis at the door-post in the warm, with the porridge-pot in her two hands, watching her whole world go down the lane happy, and calling after them the last ordinary blessing of the ordinary time — *back by dark, the three of you* — and turning back, still smiling, into the room.

That was all. That was everything. The inn heard it the way ground takes rain after a dry year. Odile Vance sat with her spine plumb and her face like a locked door behind which somebody had stopped pretending not to dance; Cobb wept frankly into his hat, converted entire; and Griet, in the warm corner with her bowl in her lap, followed the whole telling with her bright going eyes and said, into the hush after — clear as a bell across all her fogs, to the room at large — "*That's* it. That's the good of a morning. You can spend your life on the strength of one," which Wenholt wrote into itself somewhere deeper than any ledger, and which Wim did not survive dry-eyed, nor pretend to.

And Mercy Hale sat by the jar with her father's inkwell-bowl of black slip before her and her family alive in the room for one hour, sixty years late, and did the thing she had come into the world four generations to do: she listened, with her hands around something warm, and said the names back.

They fired the keeping at midnight, a small load and a gentle one, the moon one night past full: the morning out of Edda and home into the open jar, thread by thread all night, the right way round, a keeping like any keeping since the first witch and the first laugh — and beside it in the same fire, out of a boy's steady chest, a clerk into an inkwell, the first carrying of the next witch of Wenholt, delivered whole at dawn with his mistress's eye on the cones and tears nobody taxed him for on the wood. The kiln kept them both. It had never, that summer or any summer, been asked for anything else.

# Chapter Twelve — A House with a Morning in It

They opened at midmorning, unhurried, the cooling having been given its full due at last; and there was no crowd, by instinct rather than agreement — just the crew, and the June sun, and the ware coming out warm into the ordinary world: an inkwell brown as old oak gone silver at the edges, which a seventeen-year-old carried to the garden table as though carrying a bird's egg the size of his head; and a honey-glazed jar with no lid.

Mercy Hale held it a long minute, in both hands, in the sun.

Every face in the garden had seen what her face did then, but only on the far side of it: it was the exact opposite of the thing the jar had taught faces for sixty years. Attend; soften; attend deeper — and *find*. Porridge-steam on a cold window. A gate singing. The specific weight of a small boy's impatience. The warmth of a door-latch with the sun expected through it; the whole reach of an ordinary morning, present in the hands, the way kept things are — real as a heartbeat, heavier than any grief and lighter than a feather, both at once.

"It's a good jar, mistress," she said at last, unsteady, smiling, tide-wet, in a voice that was carefully, deliberately, her grandmother's register and her own forgiveness of it. "Did I pay for it?"

"Your grandmother did," said Edda. "Paid in full, sixty years since, and left the change to you. Take it home."

Home went by the long way, which was the right way. It stood first on a table at the Copper Kettle — the warmest room in the parish, a debt called in — through a supper that Hessa cooked as though feeding the field-army of some victorious and hungry queen, at which Wenholt ate, and said the names, and drank *to the morning* out of every cup in the racks, one blue two-handled cup among them doing common service exactly as commissioned. And at week's end the Rookes carted Mercy Hale and her freight to Stonebeck themselves, Ansel driving, Lena beside him with the accounts of the journey, Nell in the back with the schoolmistress and the straw-packed jar; and they went — Ansel had planned the stages a week, and told no one why, and everyone knew why — over the ford bridge, at a walk. Midstream, above the water that had taken the Sorrel men and, eleven years since, the mason who came to mend what it had made necessary, he halted the cart a moment. Nothing was said. Lena's hand came to rest on his forearm with the weight of a level laid on true stone; the water ran; the bridge bore, the way the well-built do; and the jar rode over the great flood's own crossing with a morning safe inside it, sixty years dry, going home.

It stands now — travelers on the Stonebeck road may verify it, and increasingly do — on the sill of the schoolhouse window, where the early sun gets in. The children are told what's in it, plainly, because Stonebeck has given up waiting until folk are grown; and on cold mornings the little ones are lifted up, one at a time, to put their hands around it and feel what a morning is worth. The schoolmistress keeps an inkwell on her desk that no visitor is permitted to think is only an inkwell, and writes, in a careful hand, on the first day of every school year, the same first lesson on the slate: *A word is a thing you can hold.*

In Wenholt the summer ripened and bent toward harvest, and the workshop returned to its liturgies: the queue at the gate, the kettle just short of singing, moonrise custom and the telling shelf filling and emptying like a tide with the year's laughs and lambings and last things. Ilsabet's ledger gained, in a square workshop hand, its longest entry in eleven years, ending: *The kiln keeps. It does not take. And asked kindly, in a fed fire, with a room made ready — what it keeps, it can give back. The door, not the debt. Taught to it this midsummer; may some hand after me teach it better still.* The broken lid, its edges ground smooth by a mason who wasted nothing, took up the jar's old place on the private shelf, in the hand's-width of clean board that sixty years of warning had kept bare — no longer the museum's darkest exhibit but its brightest lesson, the two pieces laid slightly open, like a door ajar.

And it was there, on a long gold evening at summer's end, that Edda found her apprentice — dusting the shelf, which was his office now, the prohibition having quietly died of obsolescence — stopped stock-still before the dish beside the lid: the old dunted dish, cooled too fast a generation ago, its fine ringing crack straight through a memory of a rowing race, so that the race ran only half its course and stopped forever mid-stroke.

He had his sideways look on it. He had, she saw with the particular joy she was careful never to show him whole, the whole craft arriving in his face at once.

"Mistress," said Perrin Loft, not turning around. "If a fire can be asked to open a door — could a fire ever be asked to finish a race?"

Down the garden the kiln stood warm at the heart-bricks in the low sun, bearing all it kept and keeping all it bore; the gate-bell hung waiting for moonrise; and up the lane, faint, came the evening sounds of a village that would be arriving all its life, with its whole year in its hands.

"Wash the dust off," Edda said. "That's a harvest story."

But this was still summer in Wenholt: the hay came in heavy, the river ran low and kind, and of an evening the ferry crossed home on the last light, where a lamp stood in a window, which was the best of it, the waiting. Mornings, the witch drank her tea from a crooked cup that smelled faintly of peas and summer, and said, to the workshop at large, "Say it back to me." And most mornings, from the wedging bench — steadier now, and no longer only elbows, with a silver-brown inkwell of his own first firing standing on the sill above his wheel — Perrin did: *The kiln keeps. It does not take.* And then, because the craft had grown that summer and the catechism with it, the new line, the one the two of them had taught the fire together, for the witches after:

*And what it keeps, asked kindly, it can give back.*
