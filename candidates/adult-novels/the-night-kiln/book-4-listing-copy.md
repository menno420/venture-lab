## Blurb (short — store/product page)

*Everyone in Wenholt knows the rule: bring Edda a memory before moonrise, and she
will fire it into clay you can hold. But the killing frost has done what sixty
winters could not — cracked the oldest thing on the witch's shelf, Grams
Ilsabet's homely, thumb-marked first pot. Whatever her dead teacher chose, at the
very start of the craft, to keep inside it will be gone by spring unless the fire
is asked to do the one thing it never has: carry a keeping out of a dying vessel
and into a new one. To save it, Edda must finally open the pot she has honored in
silence for a lifetime — and what she finds is not a locked door but an
unfinished letter, and the answer to a question her young apprentice has been
losing sleep over: what is a first pot really for? A tender, wintry cozy fantasy
about pottery, tea, found family, inheritance, and the difference between keeping
a thing and handing it on.*

## Back-cover copy (longer)

*In the deep of the year, a frost walks into the valley and puts its cold hand on
everything — and finds the one flaw in a sixty-year-old pot no one has ever dared
open.*

Edda Marren has kept the night kiln of Wenholt for a lifetime: firing her
neighbours' memories into mugs and bowls and butter crocks, so a grandmother's
laugh or a first snowfall can stay warm in the room long after the rememberer
cannot. On her private shelf, oldest of everything there, stands her late
teacher's very first pot — thumb-marked, honest, and never once heard. When the
midwinter frost cracks it, the keeping inside begins, quietly, to leak away.

Edda has six days to Wintermark and two wrong answers: honour the silence and
lose the treasure, or break a rule older than the kiln to save it. And her one
apprentice — old enough now to throw truer than she can, young enough to lie
awake certain he has no grief big enough to make him a witch — is asking the very
question the dying pot could answer, if only someone were brave enough to listen
in time.

*The Winter Wheel* is the fourth Night Kiln novella: a warm, low-stakes,
big-hearted comfort read about the craft of keeping, the mercy at the root of a
family trade, and the quiet turning of a wheel from one pair of hands to the
next. Contains grief and gentle bereavement handled with care; no violence, no
explicit content. Stands alone; sweeter still after Books 1–3.
