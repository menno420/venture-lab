## Blurb (short — store/product page)

*Everyone in Wenholt knows the rule: bring Edda a memory before moonrise, and she
will fire it into clay you can hold. But across the water, in fallen Stonebeck, the
far side had a fire of its own once — a kiln dead thirty years, its witch long gone,
and no keeper come after. When the summer water drops and the ford runs shallow, a
girl sends word: there is a pot in her family, cracked now, that her grandmother
always swore held something — and no one living has ever had the ear to hear it.
Could a fire on this side wake what a dead fire on that side kept? Can a keeping wait
longer than the witch who made it? To find out, the youngest witch of Wenholt must do
the thing no Wenholt witch has ever done: cross the water the other way, to a cold
hearth on the far bank, and carry a living fire to a dead one. A warm, low-stakes,
big-hearted cozy fantasy about pottery, tea, found family, two banks of one river,
and a light that was never as far away as it looked.*

## Back-cover copy (longer)

*The flood is long gone; the ford runs shallow over the stones; and across the water,
on a bank Wenholt has never once crossed to, a cold hearth has stood dark for thirty
years.*

Edda Marren has kept the night kiln of Wenholt for a lifetime, firing her neighbours'
memories into mugs and bowls and butter crocks so a grandmother's laugh or a child's
first steps can stay warm in the room long after the moment is gone. Now there are two
witches at the kiln — and a question carried over the summer water that neither of
them has ever been asked.

Stonebeck had a fire of its own once, and a witch to keep it, before the bridge fell
and the far side went quiet. That witch is thirty years dead, her hearth cold, her
craft gone from the far bank — but a single pot she made still stands in a fallen
house, cracked and failing, holding a true thing no one has had the ear to hear in a
lifetime. To wake it before the crack finishes it, Perrin — a witch of half a year —
must cross the water the *other* way, carry a living coal to a dead hearth, and light
a fire that everyone on the far bank had given up for gone. What he finds kept in the
old pot is not a grief but a map: the memory of a midsummer night, two young witches,
one lantern lit from two fires in the middle of a bridge that no longer stands — and a
vow that a river is never as wide as a witch is willing to cross it.

*The Summer Ember* is the sixth Night Kiln novella: a warm, low-stakes, big-hearted
comfort read about a keeping that outlasted its keeper, a friendship parted by a fallen
bridge, a whole bank of a river given its fire back, and the youngest witch learning
that the far side was never another country — only a light that needed carrying across.
Contains grief and long-past loss handled with care; no violence, no explicit content.
Stands alone; richest of all after Books 1–5.

## Series-selling note (for the product page)

Sixth in a matched cozy series (Books 1–2 ~16k, Book 3 ~23k, Books 4–6 ~15.6k
words). Read in order for the full arc — keep, give back, mend, hand on, keep the
love, wake the far fire — or start anywhere; each book is a complete standalone
firing. The frame warms across the series as the year turns Night → Morning →
Harvest → Winter → Spring → Summer, and Book 6 is the first to light a second fire
across the water.
