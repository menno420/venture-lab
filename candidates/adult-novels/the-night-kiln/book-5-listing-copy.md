## Blurb (short — store/product page)

*Everyone in Wenholt knows the rule: bring Edda a memory before moonrise, and she
will fire it into clay you can hold. On the witch's private shelf, at the far end,
stands a wedding cup that has hummed for thirty years — fired for a wedding that
never, in the end, happened, and never once claimed. When the spring flood puts the
ford in spate, a girl from across the water finally finds the courage to cross, and
asks the one thing the fire has never been asked: could you fire a wedding that
never was, so her grandmother — who "inherited the not-happening" and carried it to
her grave — can have it at last? The honest answer is no; the fire keeps only the
true, and a fired dream is a warm lie that poisons every real thing after it. But
the humming cup is not empty. It has held a true thing safe for thirty years,
waiting only for someone brave enough to cross the water and receive it — and what
Edda finds inside is not a wedding to be conjured but a love that was always real,
kept whole through a lifetime of not-happening. A tender, thawing cozy fantasy about
pottery, tea, found family, a spring flood, and the difference between the wedding
you were promised and the love that was true all along.*

## Back-cover copy (longer)

*The winter is over, the ford is under a wagon's depth of racing water — and a girl
who has stood on the far bank every spring for three years, working up the courage
to cross, finally gets into the boat.*

Edda Marren has kept the night kiln of Wenholt for a lifetime, firing her
neighbours' memories into mugs and bowls and butter crocks so a grandmother's laugh
or a child's first steps can stay warm in the room long after the moment is gone.
Now, for the first time, there are two witches at the kiln — and a caller with a
question neither of them has ever been asked.

Brida has crossed a flooded river to ask whether the fire can fire a wedding that
never happened: her grandmother's wedding, called off thirty years ago, that left
her whole family inheriting a shape where a happiness should have been. Edda cannot
do it — the fire keeps the true, and will not counterfeit a thing that never was.
But at the sound of the grandmother's name, an old cup on the witch's shelf begins
to hum louder than it has in thirty years, and Edda realises she has spent her whole
life calling a *full* thing empty.

*The Spring Cup* is the fifth Night Kiln novella: a warm, low-stakes, big-hearted
comfort read about a love that outlasted the wedding it was promised, a lost letter
kept thirty years too long, an orchard that blossoms for a man who waited his whole
life, and the crossing a grandmother could not make, completed at last by a braver
granddaughter. Contains grief and long-past sorrow handled with care; no violence,
no explicit content. Stands alone; warmer still after Books 1–4.

Fifth in a matched cozy series (Books 1–2 ~16k, Book 3 ~23k, Books 4–5 ~15.6k

Spring.
