# The Night Kiln — Series Arc

Cozy fantasy novella series. Village kiln-witch (Edda Marren, of Wenholt) who
fires spoken memories into everyday pottery. Close third on Edda, past tense,
12 chapters per book, ~16k words each (Book 3 ran longer at band). Cozy contract
throughout: no violence, low-stakes-but-true conflict, grief-work understated,
earned warmth, tea.

## The engine: one fired vessel per book

Each book turns on a single fired vessel and, through it, on one thing the fire
can be asked to do. The catechism grows a line per book, taught "for the witches
after," and the tempers close a set across the seasonal poles:

| Book | Title | Vessel | The fire is asked to… | Season | Catechism line added |
|------|-------|--------|-----------------------|--------|----------------------|
| 1 | The Night Kiln | the keystone | **keep** (a grief shared into the kiln's own crown, not erased) | autumn → Wintermark | *The kiln keeps. It does not take. Say it back.* |
| 2 | The Morning Door | Widow Sorrel's honey-glazed jar | **give back** (un-fire a shutting into a keeping) | spring → midsummer (Short Night) | *And what it keeps, asked kindly, it can give back.* |
| 3 | The Harvest Rows | the dunted race-dish | **mend** (a level fire, dark to dawn, at the equinox) | autumn (harvest moon) | *And what breaks, told together, it can mend.* |
| 4 | The Winter Wheel | Perrin's first pot (home for Grams Ilsabet's first-pot keeping) | **hand on** (flit a keeping from a dying vessel into new clay; make a witch) | deep winter → Wintermark | *And what one hand throws, another hand fills — for a first pot is not one you fill alone.* |
| 5 | The Spring Cup | the humming wedding cup (heard/given-back) + a new pair-cup for the family | **keep the love, not the loss** (hear the true love kept in a cup fired for a wedding that never happened; refuse to fire the not-happening; pair the two true halves into a new vessel) | spring (thaw, ford in spate) | *And what never happened, the fire cannot make — but a love is not the wedding it was promised, and the fire keeps the love, not the loss.* |

The four firing tempers **keep / take / give / mend** were closed across the
seasonal poles in Book 3 and are **not** reopened in Books 4–5. Book 4's firing is
the give-back temper (Book 2) run end-to-end into a keep (Book 1) — a *flitting*,
new use of existing craft, no new pole. Book 5's firing is again the *same four
tempers, still closed*: a keep (Book 1) that borrows a give-back's opening (Book 2,
to hear the true love out of the humming cup) and a carry's bringing (to fetch the
absent second half off Elias's kept letter), pairing two true halves into one new
vessel — a new *asking* of the old fire, explicitly **not** a fifth temper. The
Book-4 catechism line is a **succession/first-pot rule**; the Book-5 line is a
**truth/keeping rule** (the fire keeps only the *true*; a love is not the wedding it
was promised) — both are rules/variations, deliberately not temper lines. As Edda
puts it in Book 5: *we never learn a new fire; we only, now and then, learn a new
thing to ask the old one.*

## Hook chain (each book plants the next)

- **Book 1 → 2:** the Stonebeck letter — can a fire un-shut Widow Sorrel's jar?
- **Book 2 → 3:** the dunted race-dish — "could a fire ever be asked to finish a race?" ("a harvest story").
- **Book 3 → 4:** Grams Ilsabet's first pot — "What did Grams Ilsabet keep in her own first pot?" ("a winter story").
- **Book 4 → 5:** the unclaimed wedding cup that hums — a wedding that never happened; a granddaughter who "inherited the not-happening" and thinks about it every spring; "could a fire ever fire a thing that never was?" ("a spring story"). *(PAID in Book 5.)*
- **Book 5 → 6:** the cold far-side kiln — Stonebeck once had a fire of its own, fallen and dead these thirty years and more, and a single pot come down in Brida's family that "holds something" but has had no witch to hear it in a lifetime; "could a fire on this side ever hear what a dead fire on that side kept — can a keeping wait longer than the witch who made it?" ("a summer story"; flagged as a *young* witch's crossing — Perrin's, over the water the other way). *(Planted; unwritten.)*

## Book 4 — The Winter Wheel (spine)

Deep-midwinter succession novella that pays the Book-3 hook. A killing frost
cracks Grams Ilsabet's sixty-year-unopened first pot on the private shelf; the
keeping inside it will leak away by spring unless the fire is asked, this
Wintermark, to carry it into a new sound vessel. Reading her dead teacher's
ledgers, Edda learns the pot was never a locked door but an unfinished letter:
Ilsabet came to Wenholt at seven, an orphan out of a snowstorm, and the witch
before her (Grams Wenna) fired the child's dead mother's cradle-song into a pot
the girl threw in her lap — because *a witch's first pot is not one she fills; it
is one filled for her, so the first thing a keeper owns is proof she was kept*.
Edda gives the song back into herself (cold, gentle) and re-fires it, over the
Wintermark firing, into the first pot her apprentice Perrin throws — making him
the fifth witch of the line and giving him, as founding keeping, the craft's own
oldest mercy rather than a chosen grief. The emptied first pot is returned to the
river clay to be handed on, not kept. Fulfills the planted hook exactly, reframes
the grief-heavy series toward the founding mercy, and secures the succession.

## Book 5 — The Spring Cup (spine)

Spring-thaw novella that pays the Book-4 hook. The spring flood puts the Stonebeck
ford in spate, and Brida (21) — granddaughter of Nesta Alder, who "inherited the
not-happening" of the wedding cup that hums, and who nearly crossed once before the
frost (Book 4 close) — finally crosses on Wim's ferry to ask whether the fire can
fire a wedding that never was, so her recently-dead grandmother can have it and the
family can stop inheriting the absence. Edda's answer is a firm, gentle **no**: the
fire keeps only the *true*; a fired wedding that never happened would be a warm lie
that poisons every true thing after — the one wall the craft has never crossed. But
the cup, which Edda has dusted and dismissed as empty for thirty years, is not empty:
Perrin (now co-witch) hears its hum come up at Nesta's name. Reading Grams Ilsabet's
seventh ledger, Edda recovers the whole true story — Nesta Alder of Stonebeck and
Elias Wick, the Wenholt orchardman, were truly betrothed and truly in love; a spring
flood (the same crossing) stopped Nesta reaching the wedding, and two liars' wrong
words (each told the other had cried off) did the rest; both grieved apart their
whole lives, Nesta married kindly in Stonebeck, Elias kept his orchard alone and
died six years back. The cup holds Nesta's true love (fired whole by Ilsabet in the
betrothal, then begged by grieving Nesta to become the wedding-that-never-was —
Ilsabet refused and banked it; it has hummed unheard ever since). Ilsabet also kept,
folded thirty years in the ledger, **Elias's letter** — his true "I never believed
you cried off; the door was never shut; I'll wait my life" — meant to be carried
across the fallen water and never carried. Edda's craft-work: prove the seam small
(lesser-freight law, on a test tile carrying Tally's child's first-steps); build the
seam from the story's own substance (applewood ash off Elias's orchard, Ler ford
sand); **give back** the humming cup so Brida hears her grandmother young and happy
(the cup stills, never hums again, stays on the shelf as proof a true thing kept does
not leak); then, over a spring firing, **keep** the two true halves — Nesta's love +
Elias's carried yes — paired into a new loving-cup Brida carries home. Not the
wedding (never fired), the love. The crossing a grandmother could not make is
completed a generation late by the granddaughter. Reframes the family's inheritance
from a not-happening (a shameful jilting that never was) to a happening (a true love
thwarted by a flood). No fifth temper — same four, a new asking.

## Continuity anchors (current, through Book 5)

- Private shelf now holds: the ground-smooth jar-lid laid ajar (Book 2); the empty
  board where the race-dish stood (Book 3, gone home to the ferry as a second lamp);
  the empty board where Ilsabet's first pot stood (Book 4, returned to the river
  clay); and — at the far end where the first pot used to be — **the wedding cup,
  now stilled** (Book 5: heard/given-back, no longer hums; left on the shelf as the
  proof a kept true thing does not leak). The new pair-cup and Elias's letter went
  home over the water with Brida to Stonebeck.
- New in Book 5: a test tile carrying Tally's child's first-steps was pressed into
  the earth at the foot of the biggest pear in Elias Wick's orchard (a glad keeping
  given to the ground). The orchard (south slope, past the clay-pit) blossoms every
  spring, parish-held, half-wild.
- Kiln: rebuilt in Book 1; keystone bears Joss Rooke's death; Rooke Brothers'
  two-rook mark (borrowed by Perrin for his first pot and again for the Book-5
  pair-cup); Ansel's father's level as witness on the arch.
- Ferry: Wim & Griet; Griet living with memory loss (no cure — the porridge bowl
  and mended dish are two lamps in one window); handled gently. Griet has the one
  clear-evening beat in Books 4 & 5 (she first heard Brida; in Book 5 she blesses
  the crossing) — no living memory-loss is ever touched by fire.
- Cast: Edda Marren (witch, past 50); Perrin Loft (now 17, fifth witch, co-witch
  through Book 5); Ansel Rooke (mason, clay-pit cottage, Lena & Nell); Hessa (Copper
  Kettle); Odile Vance (reeve); Maud & Tally & Gran Brock; Wilf, Cobb, the Askews.
  New in Book 5: Brida Alder (Stonebeck, 21), her late grandmother Nesta Alder, and
  Elias Wick (the Wenholt orchardman, d. ~6 yrs before, the intended groom).
- The nine ledgers of the Wenholt witches; Perrin now writes in the ninth (and
  writes the fifth catechism line, and the Book-5 firing, in it).
