> Everyone in Wenholt knows the rule: bring Edda a memory before moonrise, and she will fire it into clay you can hold. But across the water, in fallen Stonebeck, the far side had a fire of its own once — a kiln cold now, thirty years and more, its witch long dead and no keeper come after — and a single pot, come down in Brida's family, that her grandmother always swore held something, though no one living has ever had the ear to hear it. When the summer water goes down and the ford runs shallow, a girl sends word across the river: could a fire on this side ever wake what a dead fire on that side kept? Can a keeping wait longer than the witch who made it? To answer it, the youngest witch of Wenholt must do the thing no Wenholt witch has ever done — cross the water the other way, to a cold hearth on the far bank, and carry a living fire to a dead one. It is a summer story, and it has waited a lifetime to be told.

**Content note:** Adult cozy fantasy. Contains grief and long-past loss (a bereavement, a village's decline, a friendship parted by a fallen bridge, all gently handled), and a beloved character living with memory loss — all handled with warmth. No violence, no explicit content. A comfort read for adult readers. This is the sixth Night Kiln novella; it stands alone.

# Chapter One — The Water Gone Down

The rule in Wenholt was older than anyone who kept it, and it was this: if you wanted a memory fired, you brought it to Edda Marren before moonrise, because the kiln burned at night or not at all. You came up the witch's lane with your year in your hands; you sat in the good chair by the workshop stove with your fingers around something warm, and you told the memory out loud — properly, the whole of it — and the telling went into the witch, and bided in her, and on the firing night the fire drew it out of her, through her hands on the stoke-wood, into the waiting clay, so that ever after the memory lived in a mug or a bowl or a butter crock: a thing warm past what any cooling explained, a thing that could stand in the room when the rememberer no longer could. The kiln keeps. It does not take. Say it back. Four generations of Wenholt witches had said it like a catechism, and in three short years, a hard winter, and the thaw just past the catechism had grown four lines more — *what it keeps, asked kindly, it can give back; what breaks, told together, it can mend; what one hand throws, another hand fills; and what the fire keeps, it keeps the love and not the loss* — taught to the fire and the village together, for the witches after.

There were two of them now to teach it, which was the great change and the sweetness under everything; and it was high summer, which was the second sweetness, the valley gone green and heavy and slow, the hay down in the low meadows and the bees loud in the lime, and the Ler — which in the spring had come up brown and shouldering and put the ford under a wagon's depth of racing water — gone down again to a bright shallow ribbon a child could wade, so that the gravel crossing below the ferry landing lay pale and gleaming in the sun with the water breaking over it no deeper than your knee.

"Wim says you could nearly stride it," Perrin reported, coming in from the gate with the smell of cut grass on him, taller yet than he had been at the thaw, so that Edda had entirely given up expecting him to stop. He was seventeen and a witch of Wenholt half a year and more, and he still set the milk-jug by the stove to warm and still turned it a quarter without knowing he did it. "The ford. Lowest he's seen it in years. He's rowing folk over for the market same as ever, but he says by the way it's dropping there'll be a fortnight, come the dog-days, you could cross dry-shod on the stones if you'd a mind to."

"There's always a fortnight in the dog-days you could cross dry-shod," Edda said, not looking up from the glaze she was grinding, "and there's always a village fool who tries it the one year the hills let go a summer storm upriver, and Wim fishes him out below the willows. The water's low. That's not the same as the water's gone." She tipped the muller and read the slip sideways, the way she read most things. "But it's low, right enough. It's the low-water summer that girl was waiting on, if she's the sort that keeps her word — and I think she is."

Perrin went still in the way he had, which was the whole of his half of the craft just then. "You mean Brida."

"I mean Brida," Edda said. "She said she'd send word some low-water year, and here's the lowest water in years, and I'd be a poor witch and a worse reader of people if I didn't expect it." She set the muller down. "She crossed a flood for a no, that girl, and went home with the truest yes this valley ever kept. A person who's done that once doesn't leave a thing half-asked. She'll want the rest of it. She'll want to know about the pot."

They had the far side much in mind that summer, the two of them, because of the pot; and the pot they had in mind because of the last words Brida had left with Wim on the day she crossed back home in the spring, wrapped round a cloth-covered cup with her grandmother's whole young love in it. *Clearing her gran's house*, she'd sent word, *she'd found more than the letter. Stonebeck had a kiln once. A witch of its own, or something near a witch, before either of our times. And the kiln's cold now, fallen, thirty years and more, and there's nobody left over the water who kept the craft. And there's a pot — one pot, come down in her family, that her gran always said held something, a true thing, from the old Stonebeck fire — only there's been nobody on that side to hear it in a lifetime.* And she wanted to know, someday, no hurry, when the water was low and the year was kind: whether a fire on this side could ever hear what a dead fire on that side kept. Whether a keeping could wait longer than the witch who made it.

⁂

The private shelf stood behind the glaze jars, a plain board no customer ever saw: the workshop's own small museum of the craft's mistakes and mercies. Edda's eye went to it now, as it did a dozen times a day and had all summer, the way you keep glancing at a door you're expecting someone through.

It held its griefs, all resolved: the ground-smooth lid of Widow Sorrel's honey jar, laid a little ajar; the clean bare board where the dunted race-dish had stood and stood no longer, gone home to be a lamp at the ferry; the bare board where Grams Ilsabet's homely first pot had sat all one winter and sat no longer, gone back to the river clay at Wintermark to be handed on. And at the far end, where a first pot used to be its neighbour, the small footed wedding cup, red as an iron celadon gone rich under a good reduction, with a line of apple-blossom pressed round its shoulder — the cup that had hummed thirty years for an ear and hummed no longer, because in the spring it had at last been heard. It sat there quiet and honest, the proof of a thing Edda had learned late and meant never to unlearn: that a true thing kept does not leak, however long the not-happening lasts.

"That's what she wants to know," Edda said, half to herself, standing before the stilled cup with a duster she'd forgotten she was holding. "Whether it's true the other way round, too. Ours kept thirty years and we heard it in the end, because there was a witch to hear it. But theirs — a keeping with no witch in a lifetime, and the fire that made it dead these thirty years and the house it sits in fallen." She turned the duster in her hands. "That's a colder question than the cup ever was. The cup at least had somebody dusting it. That pot's had nobody. Just a family that says it *holds something*, and no way under heaven to know what, or whether the something's lasted, or whether a fire on our side of the water could reach across and wake a thing a fire on their side laid down before I was made a witch."

"Could it?" Perrin asked, quietly, and there was something under the question that Edda heard and did not remark on, because it was too new and too tender to name yet: the sideways attention had come into his voice, the listening-across-the-water he'd first shown in the spring, when Edda had handed him, lightly, the notion that a young witch might one day cross the other way.

"I don't know," Edda said honestly. "And I'll tell you a thing about not knowing, now you're a witch and it's yours to carry too: not knowing is where the whole craft lives. Everything we're sure of we learned by being unsure of it first, out loud, in front of the fire, with somebody's whole heart depending on our getting it right. So no — I don't know if a fire this side can hear what a dead fire that side kept. But I know how you find out. You go and listen. And I've a feeling," she said, setting the duster down and looking at the boy who had grown a whole head past her in three years, "that this one's a listening you'll do on the far bank, and not me. There's water in this that I've never crossed."

She said it lightly. But it was true, and it sat in the room after she'd said it, the way a true thing does: that Edda Marren, past fifty, witch of Wenholt her whole grown life, keeper of the night kiln and four generations' worth of the parish's memory, had never once in all her days crossed the water to the other side. Nobody in Wenholt had. The far side was always a little shut off — flood or ford, bridge fallen these thirty years, a poorer, thinner, further place that Wenholt bought turnips from at the summer market and otherwise let be. The water ran between, and you stayed your own side of it, and that was the shape the world had always been.

Until, that same evening, the word came.

# Chapter Two — The Two Fires

Every witch keeps a ledger, and the ledgers keep the witches; and a thing set down by a hand long stilled has a way of waiting, patient as clay, for the year it will be wanted.

The word came the way words from the far side always came, up the lane in Wim's slow careful voice at dusk, the ferryman come himself rather than send it secondhand because he'd judged, rightly, that it wanted a face. Brida had crossed at the market that morning to leave it. She was well; the cup was set where the family set its true things, and the little ones asked after it and were told the true story now, the love and not the not-happening, exactly as Edda had bid her. And the pot — she'd sent to say the water was low, and the year felt kind, and there was a thing gone wrong with the pot that made the asking no longer a someday but a soon.

"She says it's cracked," Wim said, turning his hat in his hands by the stove. "The old pot. The one her gran kept. She was clearing the last of the house this spring after — after all of it — and she found it in the cold cupboard where it's always sat, and there's a crack come in it. A fine one, foot to shoulder. She says it wasn't there when her gran was living, or nobody marked it, and she thinks it's the hard winter did it, the frost getting into a thing that's sat cold and untended in a fallen house thirty years." He looked up. "And she remembered what you'd told her about the winter wheel — Ilsabet's first pot that cracked in the frost, and the keeping that'd have leaked away by spring if you hadn't caught it. And she's afraid. She says if her gran was right, if there's a true thing in that pot, then it's in a cracked cup now, and a cracked cup can't hold forever, and if there was ever a witch going to cross and hear it, it wants to be before the crack finishes what the frost started."

The workshop was very quiet. Perrin had gone still at the wheel. Edda sat with her hands round her own crooked cup, the one that smelled of peas and summer, and turned it a quarter, slow, and did not drink.

"A cracked vessel and a keeping in it," she said at last. "We've met that before. Frost in an old pot, a true thing that'll leak away with the season if nobody catches it." She set the cup down. "Only every other time, the pot was on my own shelf, and the fire was ten steps out the door, and the witch was already home. This one's across a water I've never crossed, in a house that's fallen, and the fire that could catch it is dead these thirty years, and the only witches left in the world to do the catching are the two of us on the wrong bank." She looked at Wim. "How long, do you think? Before the crack has it?"

"I'm no witch," Wim said. "But I've caulked a boat or two, and I know a crack that's working. Foot to shoulder's most of the way already. I'd not give it past the summer. A dry hot summer opens a crack worse than a wet one — the clay shrinks off the flaw." He turned his hat. "Weeks, maybe. Not a winter."

"Then it's not a someday," Edda said. "It's a now." And she rose, and lit the good lamp against the coming dark, and went to the shelf of ledgers — the nine of them, oldest to newest, spines gone the colour of old honey — and took down not her own eighth, nor Perrin's new-begun ninth, but the seventh, Grams Ilsabet's, the thick close-written years of the witch before her. "Because there's a thing I've been half-remembering all summer," she said, "and I'll not cross a water on a half-memory. Ilsabet knew the far side. Ilsabet fired the wedding cup for a Stonebeck girl and kept a Stonebeck man's letter thirty years. Ilsabet, of all the witches of this line, was the one who looked across the water. If there was ever a Stonebeck fire, and a Stonebeck keeper, my teacher knew of them. And I want to know what she knew before I send her apprentice's apprentice across to a cold hearth blind."

⁂

She found it far back, in the early years — the young hand, before the frost got into Ilsabet's own knuckles and slanted her letters; the years when Ilsabet herself had been the new witch, all thumbs and hope, the way Perrin was now. And there, in the summer entries of a year fifty years gone and more, was a name Edda had never heard and would not now forget.

"*Sabra,*" she read aloud, slowly, giving it its weight. "*Sabra of Stonebeck.*" She turned the leaf, and another, reading half-aloud, the workshop hushed around her, Wim stayed past his supper and Perrin come to stand at her shoulder. "There was a fire on the far side," she said. "Ilsabet says it plain. Older than ours, she thought, or near it — 'the Stonebeck fire is the elder sister and will not let me forget it,' she writes, and you can hear the two of them young and sparring in it. Sabra kept it. A witch, or as near as makes no difference — the far side called their keeper by an older word, but the craft was the same craft, keep and give back, memory into clay. And they knew each other, the two of them, when they were young. They *carried* for each other." She looked up, her eyes bright with the strangeness of it. "When the bridge still stood — the Stonebeck bridge, that went in a flood thirty years back and never was rebuilt — they'd meet on it. Middle of the water, middle of the span, each from her own bank, and they'd trade the carryings that had to go both ways, a Wenholt memory owed to a Stonebeck cousin, a Stonebeck one owed this side. Two fires, one water. Ilsabet writes it like the best thing in her year."

"Two fires," Perrin said, very low, and Edda heard the whole future in how he said it.

"Two fires," she agreed. "And then the bridge went. Read it here — 'the spring the bridge fell.' The same water that stops everything in this valley, the same crossing that drowned Nesta Alder's wedding a generation later; it took the bridge in a flood one spring, and the far side, that was always the poorer and the thinner, hadn't the hands or the stone to raise it again, and after that the two banks were two countries, and the two fires two fires with a flood between them and no safe way across but a ferry that a keeping's a chancy freight for. They saw less of each other. Then little. Then, Ilsabet says here — and the hand's older now, and heavier — 'word came over that Sabra's fire is out. She had no one after her. The last keeper of the far side, and she kept it alone to the end, and now it's cold, and there's no one on that bank to light it, and a whole side of the water is without a witch for the first time since the craft was.'" Edda stopped. The lamp ticked. "'And I could not even cross to sit with her at the end,'" she read, gentler, "'for the water was up. All our lives we were the two lamps of the one valley, and at the end I could not cross to hold her hand. — I.'"

Nobody spoke for a while. Out past the black window a nightjar churred in the summer dark. Wim had bowed his head. Perrin stood with his hand flat on the ledger-shelf, listening to something across the water that had been silent thirty years.

"So there it is," Edda said at last, closing the seventh ledger soft as you'd close a door on a sleeper. "That's what my teacher knew, and never told me, because by the time she was teaching me the far fire was already out and it grieved her past the saying. Stonebeck had a witch. Her name was Sabra. She kept a true fire her whole long life and died with no one after her and her hearth's been cold these thirty years and more — and somewhere in a fallen house on that cold bank there's one pot she made, cracked now, with a true thing in it that nobody's heard in a lifetime." She looked at Perrin. "You asked me could a fire this side hear what a dead fire that side kept. I still don't know. But I know now it's not two strangers we'd be crossing to. It's kin. The far fire was our fire's own sister. And you don't leave your sister's last keeping to leak away in the cold because there's a bit of water in the way."

# Chapter Three — A Young Witch's Crossing

There is a kind of handing-on that cannot be taught and cannot be kept, and it is this: the day the one you made goes somewhere you cannot follow, and you find your whole heart in your throat and your whole craft in his hands, and you let him go, because that is the last and hardest thing the making was for.

They sat late over it, the three of them, and then the two, when Wim had gone home to his ferry and his Griet; and the shape of the thing came clear the way the true shapes always did in that workshop, slowly, in the hands before the head.

"It has to be a crossing," Edda said. "There's no fetching it here. I thought on it — could we not have Wim bring the pot over, hear it this side, catch it in our own fire the way we caught Ilsabet's? — and I turned it every way and it won't do. For one, a cracked pot's a poor traveller; you don't ferry a failing vessel over open water and trust the crossing not to finish it. For two —" she paused, and this was the deeper part, and she felt for it "— for two, it's *theirs*. Sabra's keeping, in a Stonebeck pot, made by the far fire for the far folk. The spring taught me that much: some keepings go home, and it's the going-home that's the whole of it. Nesta's love crossed *to* Stonebeck because that's where the family was. This one's already home. It shouldn't have to leave its own bank to be heard. If a keeping can be woken, it should be woken where it belongs."

"But there's no fire there," Perrin said. "You can hear a keeping cold — you heard the cup, you didn't need a firing to hear it, only to give it a new home. But to catch it out of a cracked pot into a sound one, to flit it the way you flitted Ilsabet's — that wants a fire. And the far fire's dead."

"That's the knot," Edda agreed. "And here's how I think it unties, and you tell me where I've got it wrong, because two witches think better than one and that's the whole reason there are two of us." She leaned forward. "Hearing wants no fire, you're right — a witch makes a door of herself and asks the true thing to say itself back, and that we can do cold, on any bank, the moment we're stood before the pot. So first you cross, and you hear it, and we learn at last what Sabra kept and whether it's lasted. But the catching, the flitting into sound clay before the crack has it — that wants a true fire, level and honest, and there's none on that side." She spread her hands. "So we make one. We wake theirs. The Stonebeck kiln's fallen but a fallen kiln's not a gone one — I rebuilt this very kiln from a cracked ruin the first winter you knew me, Perrin, and a summer's a kinder season to build a fire in than that winter was. We cross, we hear the pot, and then we do the thing that's never been done in this valley: we light a cold far fire from a living near one. We carry our own fire across the water and wake Sabra's hearth, and in a woken far fire we catch her keeping into new clay, on her own bank, where it belongs."

Perrin was quiet a long moment, working it in his mind the way he worked clay, turning it to find the true. "Carry the fire across," he said slowly. "Not the pot to the fire. The fire to the pot."

"The fire to the pot," Edda said. "It's the same craft we've always had, understand — there's no new fire in it, no fifth thing the kiln can do that it couldn't yesterday. It's a hearing, which we do; and a flit, which we've done; and a kiln lit from a coal, which is the oldest thing there is, older than the catechism, older than keeping — every fire in this valley was lit from another fire once, back and back, and no fire ever kindled itself out of nothing. We only, now and then, learn a new thing to ask the old craft. This is a new asking. Same fire. New water."

"And it's mine," Perrin said. Not a question. He had understood the whole of it, and understood the part Edda hadn't said, and said it for her so she wouldn't have to. "The crossing. You said in the spring — a young witch's crossing, over the water the other way. Not an old one's. You meant it."

"I meant it," Edda said, and found her voice was not quite level, and let it not be, because you don't lie to a witch with your voice while you're telling him a true thing with your words. "I'll cross with you — I'll not miss the one chance in my life to set foot on the far bank, and there's a grave over there I owe a visit to a generation late, my teacher's own dear sister I never knew she had. But the craft's yours, this one. The hearing, the throwing, the lighting, the catching — all of it your hands, and me stood back keeping you the way the village keeps us. Because that's the shape of it now, and I'll not pretend otherwise to spare my own heart: there are waters I've crossed for the last time, and this is the first of the ones that are yours and not mine, and a witch who can't hand on the far crossings has made a poor job of the near ones." She reached across and, for a moment, put her old clay-roughened hand over his young one on the bench. "You were made out of the oldest mercy this craft has, that Wintermark. Go and carry it across the water to a bank that's had no mercy in thirty years. That's a fair errand for a witch of half a year. Go and light Sabra's fire."

⁂

There was the getting-ready, which took two days, because you do not cross to wake a dead fire on a whim. Perrin threw a new pot — the vessel of record, the sound clay the old cracked one's keeping would go home into — throwing it slow the way a first pot of a firing is thrown, round-shouldered and deep and two-handled, though he did not yet know the whole of what it would hold. He wrapped it in fleece for the crossing. Edda packed the things a witch carries to build a fire: not much, for the far side would have its own clay and its own stone and its own cold ash, and a seam should be built from the story's own substance and not lugged over water in a sack. What they carried, in the end, that mattered, was small enough to sit in a covered iron pot no bigger than a loaf: a live coal off the Wenholt kiln, banked in ash, breathing slow — a Wenholt ember, to light a Stonebeck hearth. Perrin carried it himself, in the crook of his arm, all the way down the lane, the way you carry a sleeping thing you must not wake and must not let die.

They went by the ferry, though the ford ran shallow enough to wade, because it was Wim's boat that had carried every crossing in this tale and it seemed right to make this one by it too; and at the ferry-house window the two lamps stood — the porridge bowl and the mended dish, two lamps in one window, minding the mornings for a woman who mostly could not — and Griet was in the doorway, on one of her clear evenings, and knew them, which she did not always do.

"You're crossing," she said, bright and present for that little while, looking at the covered pot in the boy's arms with a knowing that came from somewhere deeper than the day's weather. "The both of you. And you've got a fire in that pot, I can smell it, warm through the iron." She smiled her wandering smile that was, just then, entirely her own. "There was two fires once, over this water. My mother told me. Two lamps, she said, one each bank, when she was a girl. And then there was one." She looked at Perrin, and her clear eyes were very kind. "You take that fire over careful, love. It's a long time cold on the far side. It'll be that glad to see you." And she turned back to her two lamps in the window, gently, the tide going out kind, and said, drifting, to no one, "Two lamps. There ought always to have been two lamps."

# Chapter Four — The Far Bank

To cross a water you have looked at your whole life and never crossed is to learn that the far bank was never as far as the looking made it — that the distance was mostly in the not-going, and the going undoes it in the space of one slow boat.

Wim rowed them over on a bright, breathless summer morning, the Ler run down to a wide easy shallow, the willows standing dry-footed now where in the spring they'd stood knee-deep in brown flood. Edda sat in the stern with her hands folded and her heart, she found, going faster than a still summer crossing had any right to make it, because a whole life's held distance was closing under the boat with every pull of the oars. Perrin sat amidships with the covered fire-pot in his lap and the fleece-wrapped new cup at his feet, and watched the far bank come up the way he watched a firing, sideways and whole. And the far bank came up.

It was a poorer bank. There was no unkindness in the seeing of it, only truth: Stonebeck was thinner than Wenholt, further up the cold valley, less of everything — fewer roofs, and some of those with the rafters showing; a landing gone to soft rot at the edges; the stumps of the old bridge standing out of the shallow water on both sides, two rows of black teeth with thirty years of the river gone between them and nothing joining them across. Above the landing the village straggled up a stony slope, and near the top of it, a little apart, stood a house that Edda knew for what it was before anyone told her, because a witch's house has a look, even fallen: low, and long, and turned a little away from its neighbours, with a squat round shape beside it under a lean of collapsed thatch that could only be a kiln — a kiln fallen in on itself, its arch sprung, its mouth choked with three decades of blown leaves and nettle, cold as a stone in a stream.

Brida was on the landing, and she was not alone. She'd the look of someone who'd been up since dark and hadn't been able to sit still, and when the boat touched she came forward and gave Edda both her hands before a word was said, and Edda saw the girl was changed from the spring — steadier, sadder in a way that had healed rather than festered, a young woman now where a shattered girl had crossed the flood. Behind her stood a knot of the far-side folk: an old, old man leaning on two sticks with a face like a walnut and eyes still sharp under the wreck of his brows; a tired kind-faced woman that Brida named as her mother, Cass; a scatter of cousins; and, half-behind her mother's skirt, a small girl of six or seven with a solemn stare, whom nobody introduced and everybody let be.

"You came," Brida said, and her voice went, and she laughed at herself for it, wet-eyed. "I don't know why it takes me so, every time, the crossings. You came the other way. Nobody comes the other way. Nobody's come from Wenholt to Stonebeck in my whole life, nor my mother's."

"Nobody had a reason worth the water," Edda said gently, keeping the girl's hands. "Now somebody has. This is Perrin — you'll remember him. He's the witch that's come to do the asking; I've only come to watch and to be an old woman who's crossed a water at last that she should have crossed thirty years ago. He's the one you'll want. He's got a fire in that pot he's holding like a christening."

The old man with the two sticks came forward then, slow, and peered at the covered iron pot in Perrin's arms, and then up into Perrin's young face, a long time, until it grew nearly uncomfortable, and then he made a sound that might have been a laugh and might have been something older.

"A fire," the old man said. His voice was a rusty gate. "You've brought a fire over the water. In an iron pot. Well." He worked his mouth. "I'm Tam. Old Tam, they call me now, though I was a boy at that hearth when it still burned bright, and a grown man when it went cold, so you can reckon my years off that if you've the arithmetic. I knew her. Sabra. I was a boy at her knee for the catechism, the far-side words of it, before the fire went out and there was no more catechism to learn on this bank." He looked at the fallen kiln up the slope, and his walnut face worked. "Thirty years I've watched that hearth stand cold and thought the craft was dead in the world on this side of the water for good and always. And here's a boy come over in Wim's boat with a live coal in a loaf-pot." He reached out one shaking hand and laid it, very light, on the warm iron of the fire-pot, and left it there a moment. "You'd best come up," he said, gruff, "and see the pot my Sabra made, that's been waiting in the cold for somebody exactly this daft to cross the water and hear it. Mind the nettles by the door. Nobody's minded them in thirty years."

# Chapter Five — The Cold Cupboard

A keeping does not know how long it has waited. That is the mercy and the terror of it both: that the true thing laid down in clay lies as fresh at the end of a lifetime as at the end of an hour, patient past all patience, needing only an ear — so that the whole of the waiting is borne by the living, and none of it by the kept.

Sabra's house was cold with the particular cold of a place the sun has given up on, a cold that had nothing to do with the summer morning blazing outside. They went in past the choked hearth and the fallen kiln, into a low room where the light came grey and sideways through a shutter Cass unlatched, and there was the smell of long-cold ash and old stone and a house that had grieved a long time; and along one wall stood a cupboard, plain and heavy, that Brida went to and opened with the care of someone opening a thing she was afraid of.

"Gran kept it here," she said. "Always here. She'd take it out sometimes, when she thought no one saw, and hold it, and put it back. I never knew why. She'd say it held something, and go quiet, and that was all. After she died and I found it — that's when I saw the crack." She lifted it out with both hands and set it on the scrubbed table in the grey light, and stood back.

It was a good pot, and had been a fine one. Not a cup like the wedding cup — a pot, a proper standing pot, the size of a big loaf, round and deep and footed, with a lid; the sort of thing you'd keep a keeping in that was meant to stand a long time and be lifted seldom. Its glaze had gone the deep breathing brown of an old wood-ash over iron, and round its shoulder, pressed in the wet clay by a maker's thumbnail a lifetime ago, ran a line of little marks — and Edda, bending to it, felt the years close, because it was the same running line of marks, near enough, that Ilsabet had pressed round the wedding cup: not blossom, on this one, but a line all the same, the far fire's own version of the near fire's hand, two sisters' work, a river apart. And down the pot's side, from foot to shoulder, ran the crack: a fine dark line, working, most of the way already, exactly as Wim had said.

Perrin set his own new pot down beside it, and his covered fire, and stood before the old pot a long time, and did not touch it. Edda watched him, and said nothing, and let him. This was his. She had crossed a water to watch it and she would watch it and keep her hands behind her back and her old craft to herself.

"Is it — " Brida started, and stopped, and Perrin lifted a hand, gently, not unkind, only the way a witch quiets a room, and she quieted.

He tipped his head. The sideways-listening way. And he listened to the cold pot in the cold room on the cold far bank the way Edda had taught him without ever being able to say how she taught it — properly, the whole of it, with the wrong sense on purpose, the way you'd listen for a sound you weren't sure was there. The house was very still. Old Tam had lowered himself onto a stool by the dead hearth and sat with both hands on his sticks and his eyes shut. Cass held the small solemn girl against her. Outside a lark went up.

And Perrin's face changed. Edda saw it change — saw the thing come into it that she had not felt herself since she was young and the world was still mostly ahead of her: the astonishment of a witch who has asked a question with his whole self and been *answered*, the door made and something come through it. His breath caught. His eyes filled without his seeming to know it.

"It's there," he said, hushed, wondering, not turning from the pot. "It's — Mistress. It's *there*. Thirty years, no witch, cold as a stone, and it's — it's as full as the day it was fired. It didn't leak. It didn't fade. It waited." He put out one hand at last and did not quite touch the pot, holding his palm a hair above the cold glaze the way you'd hold your hand near a warmth. "You were right. You said a true thing kept doesn't leak however long the not-happening lasts, and I believed you the way you believe a thing you've been told, and now I —" his voice went "— now I've heard it. Thirty years old and more, and no witch in all that time, and it's *whole*." He looked round at last, at Brida, at the old man, at his own witch stood back with her hands behind her. "There's a true thing in this pot," he said, "and it's been waiting a lifetime, and it's still every bit itself, and it wants to be heard. Sabra kept something in here worth the keeping. And the crack —" he looked down at the working dark line, and his young face set into something Edda had not seen in it before, something that had grown up all at once in the space of a hearing "— the crack's real, and it's soon, and we haven't got forever. But we've got now. We came on the right tide. It's whole, and it's caught before it went, and now we've only to hear the whole of it and give it a sound home before that crack finishes." He let out a shaking breath and, absurdly, laughed, the pure helpless laugh of relief and wonder. "It waited for us. Thirty years and more. It waited."

# Chapter Six — What Sabra Kept

There is a question every witch asks the first time a keeping comes through the door whole from the far side of a life, and it is not *what is it* but *how did it dare* — how did a thing so fragile as a felt hour last so long, uncracked at the heart, in a cracking pot, in a fallen house, alone. And the answer, when you have it, is always the same, and it is the whole faith of the craft: because it was true, and the true does not tire.

They did not rush it. You never rush a hearing; and this one, thirty years banked and thirty witchless, wanted its own time to come whole. Perrin sat before the pot through the long grey morning, and the far-side folk came and went soft-footed, and Old Tam stayed on his stool by the dead hearth, and it was Old Tam, in the end, who told them the shape of it from the outside while Perrin gathered the whole of it from within — the two tellings meeting in the middle, the way the two fires used to meet on the bridge.

"She'd no one after her," Old Tam said, his rusty voice going gentle for it. "That was the grief of Sabra's last years, and she bore it plain, the way she bore everything. The far side was dying round her — the bridge gone, the young ones crossing away to Wenholt or downriver for the work, fewer roofs every winter, and no child born on this bank with the ear, not one, in all the years she watched for one. She used to say a witch can't make her own successor, she can only wait for the fire to send one, and the fire never sent." He worked his mouth. "So she knew. She knew, at the end, that when she went the far fire went with her, and there'd be no keeper on this bank, maybe ever again. A whole side of the water going dark. And there was a thing she couldn't bear to have go dark with it — one thing above all the rest she'd kept over a lifetime's keeping — and so she did the last firing anyone did in that hearth up there. Her own last firing. And she put it in that pot, and she gave it to the Alders — to your gran, girl, to Nesta, who was kind to her, and used to bring the old witch her supper and sit with her, the only one on the bank who'd sit with a lonely old woman for the company of it — and she said: *keep this. A witch will come for it. Not soon. Maybe not in your life. But the fire always sends one in the end, and one day, some low-water year, a witch will cross the water and ask what's in this pot, and you or yours must be here to give it them. Keep it till the fire sends someone. The keeping will wait. Mind you do.*" The old man's eyes were wet, and he did not wipe them. "And Nesta minded. Thirty years, near enough, that pot's been minded — Nesta the rest of her days, and this child after her. *It holds something.* She never knew what. She kept it anyway. Because a lonely old woman she loved told her a witch would come, and she'd not be the one to fail the coming, however long it took." He looked at Perrin. "And here you are. The fire sent, at the last. Thirty years late and dead on time."

Perrin had gone very white and very still, listening to the two tellings at once — Tam's from the outside, and the thing itself coming whole from within — and now he began, softly, haltingly, the way a witch speaks a keeping he is still receiving, to say what Sabra had kept.

"It's — " he said, and stopped, and started again. "It's a night. On the bridge. When she was young." He listened. "The two fires. Sabra's and — and yours, Mistress. Not yours. The witch before yours. Before the one before you."

"Ilsabet," Edda said, very quietly, and her own heart had begun to go. "My teacher. Grams Ilsabet. Sabra's own age, near enough. The two of them young witches together, one each bank."

"Ilsabet," Perrin breathed, and the name went into the hearing and came back changed, and Edda understood that the boy was hearing her own teacher — young, before the frost got into her hands, before she was Grams anybody, a girl with a fire and a friend across the water. "It's a midsummer night," Perrin said, his eyes shut now, his voice gone soft and sure the way it went when the keeping had him whole. "The longest night — no, the shortest. Midsummer. The water low, like now. And the two of them met on the bridge — the old bridge, when it stood — in the middle, over the middle of the water, each come out from her own bank in the dark with a light. And they'd each brought — " he smiled, without opening his eyes, the keeping's own gladness moving through him " — they'd each brought a coal. From her own fire. A live coal in an iron pot, each of them, carried out to the middle of the bridge. And they lit one lantern between them. One lantern, from both fires, in the middle of the water, at midsummer, when they were young. And they set it on the parapet and they sat either side of it with their feet hanging over the water and they —" his voice broke, and he laughed through it, the way you do at a joy too big for the face "— they *swore* it. They swore the two banks were one craft split by a river and not two crafts at all. They swore that as long as either fire lived, neither bank was without a witch, because a fire can always be carried across to light its sister. One flame, Sabra said — she said it to Ilsabet and Ilsabet said it back — one flame in two hearths, and a river's no wider than a witch is willing to cross it. And they burned the one lantern down to nothing on the bridge and went home each to her own fire before dawn, and Sabra kept that night her whole life, and fired it last of all, into this pot, because —" Perrin opened his eyes, and they were streaming, and he did not care "— because she wanted it to be here. On the cold bank. Waiting. So that whenever the witch came that the fire finally sent, the first thing they'd hear, crossing over, would be *this*: that the far fire was never a stranger's fire. That it was sister to the near one. That it was always meant to be lit again, one flame from the other, across the water. She didn't keep a grief. She kept the *how*. She kept the instructions. She kept, her whole life, the one night that told whoever came exactly what to do — carry a coal across and light the cold hearth from the warm one, because that's what the two fires always were, and always meant, and one flood was never going to be the end of."

The cold room was silent but for the lark outside and an old man weeping without shame on a stool by a dead hearth. Edda stood with her hands behind her back and her whole face wet and made no move to wipe it, because some griefs and some gladnesses you owe the courtesy of letting them fall. Her teacher. Young, on a bridge, with a friend she'd loved across the water and never been able to cross to at the end. A lifetime the two lamps of one valley. And here, in a cracked pot in a cold cupboard, the proof of it, kept against every likelihood by a lonely old woman and a girl who brought her supper, waiting all this while to tell the next witch: *you were always meant to come. The fire was always meant to be relit. Carry it across.*

"She knew," Edda said, when she could. "Sabra knew, fifty years ago, sitting on that bridge, exactly what you'd have to do today. She didn't keep a memory to be wept over. She kept a *map*." She looked at the covered iron pot Perrin had carried across the water, the Wenholt ember breathing slow inside it, and then at the choked cold hearth where the far fire had died, and something in her — the last of the held distance, the last of the thirty years of *the far side is another country* — went out of her like a breath. "You carried a coal across the water in an iron pot, Perrin, not knowing why past that we needed a fire. And it turns out that's the whole of the keeping. That's the exact thing the two of them did fifty years back on the bridge. You've done the keeping without knowing it was a keeping." She let out a shaking laugh. "Well. Then let's do it knowing. Let's light Sabra's fire the way Sabra's own memory says it's to be lit. Sister to sister. One flame across the water. It's a summer for it, and the water's low, and the ember's still warm." She turned to Old Tam. "How much of that kiln is standing, under the nettle and the fallen thatch? Because I rebuilt one just like it from a worse ruin one hard winter, and this boy watched me do it, and it's high summer, and we've a fire to wake."

# Chapter Seven — The Cold Hearth

Every fire in the world was lit from another fire once. There is no first flame that any hand ever struck from nothing; back and back it goes, hearth from hearth, coal from coal, to a warmth older than memory — so that to light a cold fire from a living one is not a new thing under the sun but the oldest thing, the very oldest, the thing the whole craft of keeping is only a late and tender branch of.

They cleared the kiln that afternoon, all of them, the far side and the near — Perrin and Old Tam directing, for a kiln is a particular beast and you don't clear one wrong, but the hands were everyone's. They pulled the fallen thatch off it and carried away thirty years of blown leaf and nettle-root and the little bones of birds that had died in the cold mouth of it. And under the ruin the kiln stood better than its look had promised, the way Edda had hoped: the far-side masons had built for the long haul, same as the near, and the round bulk of it was sound to two-thirds its height, only the arch sprung and fallen at the crown and the door-throat tumbled. A summer's work. Not a winter's.

"Ansel would weep to see it and then mend it in a fortnight," Edda said, running her hand over the cold brick, reading it the way Ansel Rooke had taught her to read the near kiln's bones. "It's the same trouble ours had — the frost got into the crown mortar over years and sprung the arch, and once the arch goes the weather's in and it's downhill. But the heart of it's honest. We reset the arch, we throat the door, we let it dry a week in this good sun, and she'll take a fire." She looked at Perrin. "But that's your call now, and your hands. I'll fetch and carry and tell you what I know. You're the witch that's lighting this one."

So Perrin lit the cold hearth of Stonebeck, and it took him the better part of a week, and Edda would remember it her whole life after as the week she watched the boy she'd made become, past any more doubting, a witch entire.

He reset the arch. Old Tam knew where the far-side clay-pit was, up behind the village, and the clay came out of it the same red river-clay as Wenholt's, only thinner, hungrier, wanting more temper — the poorer bank's poorer clay, but clay, honest and biddable once you knew its wants. They dug it and they tempered it and they set the sprung arch true again, brick by brick, a Wenholt witch's hands and a far-side old man's memory and a girl's whole willing heart, working in the long gold summer evenings while the swifts screamed round the eaves and the village that had watched the hearth stand cold for thirty years came, one by one, to watch it stand ready to burn again. And when the arch was set they let it dry, because you cannot rush green mortar into a fire any more than you can rush a keeping, and the good hot summer sun did the drying that the winter had once made Edda fight for, so that this time the season was a friend and not a foe.

And Perrin, in the drying days, worked out the seam. Edda watched him do it and said nothing, because he did it right, out of his own head, the way she'd have done it and never told him to.

"It wants to be theirs," he said, to Brida, to the room, thinking aloud the way Edda thought aloud, in his hands before his head. "The seam. Not ours. A keeping wants a home that already knows it. Sabra's keeping goes into a new pot, and the new pot's body wants to be — Stonebeck. All of it. The far side's own." He counted it on his fingers, the way you'd tell a recipe. "The clay's the far pit's, that Sabra dug from her whole life. The temper —" he looked at the old cracked pot, sitting still in its grey light with the keeping safe in it yet, waiting "— when the keeping's out of it and it's an empty pot at last, we grind the old pot's own body, shard and all, into the new one's slip. So Sabra's own last-firing clay carries her keeping on. The pot she made becomes part of the pot that keeps it. And the ash —" he turned to the cold hearth, to the deep bed of thirty-years-cold ash they'd raked out of it and set by, not thrown away, because Edda had stopped him, quiet, when he'd gone to throw it: *never throw a dead fire's ash till you know the story*, she'd said, and been right "— the ash is Sabra's own fire's ash. A lifetime of her firing, gone cold in that hearth. We wash it and sweeten it and grind it into the glaze, so the new pot's very colour comes partly out of the dead fire that made the keeping. Her fire, woken into the glaze of the pot that carries her memory of fire." He stopped, a little amazed at his own reckoning. "The far clay, the old pot's body, the dead fire's ash. And the water — " he smiled " — the Ler. The crossing itself. A pinch of the ford where the two fires used to meet before the bridge went. So the whole of it's in the cup before ever the keeping goes in: her clay, her pot, her fire, and the water that was never as wide as everyone let it be."

"That's the seam," Edda said, and had to look away out the door at the swifts for a moment. "That's exactly the seam. I couldn't have built it better and I'll not pretend I taught you the whole of it, because I didn't. The last of it you found yourself." She cleared her throat. "There's a line wants saying, when you've worked a thing out that true. Do you feel it? The catechism's grown a line every book of the craft since you came to me. There's one in this. Do you have it?"

Perrin was quiet, listening for it the way he'd listened for the keeping, and when it came he said it low, half to the cold hearth, half to the old cracked pot: "You light a cold fire from a warm one." He turned it. "A keeping waits longer than the witch." He turned it again, and it came whole, the way the true lines came to him whole. "And what one fire keeps, another can wake — for a keeping waits longer than the witch who made it, and no fire is so long cold it cannot be lit again from a living one across the water."

Edda stood in the doorway of a dead witch's house on the far bank of a water she'd crossed for the first time at past fifty, and heard her craft grow its sixth line in the mouth of the boy she'd made, and thought her heart would not hold it.

"Write it in the ninth ledger tonight," she said, when she trusted her voice, "before you sleep, while it's warm. That's a witch's own line. That's the sixth. And it's yours, and it's true, and it's the one the whole far side of this water has been waiting thirty years to have somebody say out loud." She looked at the cold hearth, near dry now, near ready. "Now. Let's wake her fire."

# Chapter Eight — One Flame Across the Water

There is an hour in the lighting of a long-cold hearth that is like nothing else the craft knows: the hour you kneel at a dead mouth with a living coal in your hand, and you are not making a fire — you are giving one back, handing a warmth across from a hearth that never went out to one that has been thirty years dark, and if you have the wit to feel it you feel the whole line of every fire that was ever carried from another, back and back to the first warmth in the world.

They woke Sabra's fire at dusk, because the far side kept the old hours too, and a fire is best lit into the dark that shows it. The whole of Stonebeck came — it was not a large village, and every soul of it that could walk came up the stony slope in the summer dusk to the witch's house that had stood cold their whole lives, drawn by the thing that had gone round the bank all week like warmth through iron: that a witch had crossed the water the other way, and was going to light the dead fire. They came the way Wenholt came to a firing that mattered — not to watch the fire, which was the witch's, but to keep the witch, which was theirs, though most of them had never had a witch to keep in their lives and were learning it new, and shy about it, and did it anyway. Old Tam sat where he could see. Cass came with the small solemn girl. And across the water — Edda saw it and said nothing, only touched Perrin's sleeve and turned him to look — on the Wenholt bank, tiny in the dusk, a scatter of lights had gathered at the ferry landing: Wenholt come down to the water to watch the far bank, for once, and Wim's boat drawn up, and two lamps burning steady in the ferry-house window, and someone — Griet, it could only be Griet — stood on the very edge of the landing with a lantern held up high, so that the near bank should show a light to the far one on the night the far fire came back.

"Two banks watching one fire," Edda said, low. "The way it was, once. Go on. Your hands."

Perrin knelt at the cold mouth of Sabra's kiln. He had laid the fire that afternoon the way you lay a first fire in a hearth you mean to keep, dry tinder and kindling and the good split wood above, everything ready and only the warmth wanting. And he took the covered iron pot he had carried across the water — the Wenholt ember, banked in ash, breathing slow, a live coal off his own fire that had crossed the Ler in the crook of his arm — and he uncovered it, and the coal was there, red and patient, a small living warmth that had made the whole journey the two young witches had made on the bridge fifty years before, sister to sister, one flame carried to another across the water.

He did not say anything grand. Edda loved him for that. He only knelt at the dead hearth of Stonebeck with the living coal of Wenholt cupped in his two hands, and he tipped it gentle into the waiting tinder, and he bent his young face down and breathed on it, soft, the oldest thing there is, the breath that wakes a coal — and the tinder caught, and the kindling caught, and the split wood caught, and the cold hearth of the far side, dead thirty years and more, its witch a lifetime gone, filled all at once with a living light.

The whole of Stonebeck made a sound. It was not a cheer, quite. It was the sound a people make who did not know, until the light came up, how dark it had been — a long low breath let out together, and under it, here and there, the sound of the old ones weeping, the ones like Tam who remembered when the hearth had last burned and had never thought to see it burn again. The firelight went up the stony slope and out over the fallen bridge-stumps and across the low summer water, and on the far bank a tiny answering light lifted and swung, Griet's lantern, Wenholt hailing the woken fire; and Perrin knelt at the mouth of it with the firelight on his wet face and fed it, gently, up, the way you feed a thing back to life that you mean to keep alive now, for good, for a long while yet.

"One flame," Old Tam said, from his stool, in his rusty gate of a voice, to nobody, to everybody, to a woman thirty years dead. "One flame, Sabra. He carried it across. Same as you always said. The fire sent one, and he carried it across, and he lit you again." And the old man bowed his walnut head over his two sticks and let the firelight dry his face, and the far fire of Stonebeck burned up into the summer dark, alive.

# Chapter Nine — Proving It Small

The kindest law the craft has is the one that says a witch never practises on another soul's whole heart: you prove a firing small before you trust it large, and you carry the risk yourself, on a plain tile, with a little true thing that will take no harm from the trying — so that if a thing goes wrong it goes wrong on your own tile and not on the one keeping in the world that cannot be replaced.

The woken fire wanted teaching before it could be trusted, and Perrin spent two days at it, and Edda, watching, thought she had never seen him so wholly a witch as in the patience of those days. For a hearth thirty years cold does not come back willing all at once, any more than a spring kiln that has drunk the winter's wet. There was water in the old brick, and years of it — damp got deep into thirty winters' worth of cold stone — and if you drove the fire hard into that the trapped wet would flash to steam with nowhere to go and spall the brick and crack the arch they'd only just reset. So Perrin water-smoked it, exactly as Edda had water-smoked the spring kiln in the flood-year, small slow fires held two days in the mouth of it, coaxing the long cold damp out of the far-side brick a degree at a time.

"You can't drive the fire in till you've let the water out," he told the small solemn girl, who had attached herself to the proceedings and stood at a careful distance with her thumb near her mouth, watching everything. "Rush it and she cracks. Same on any bank." He fed the little fire. "It's the most patience the whole business asks — sitting a low fire two days doing next to nothing, letting the old wet leave slow, so the true fire, when it comes, comes to a hearth that can bear it." He glanced at Edda, and there was a grin in it, the boy still there under the witch. "There's a sermon in it, my mistress says. I mostly just feed the fire."

And when the hearth was dry and warm through and breathing right, he proved the seam small, because the law was the law and a kind one. He threw a plain test tile of the new tempered clay — the far pit's clay, tempered as it wanted — and he asked the far side for a small true thing to trial it with, a glad one, whole and fresh and unharmable; and it was Cass who gave it, shyly, the memory of the small solemn girl — whose name, it came out, was Wynn — taking to the water for the first time that very summer down at the low ford, wading out to her knees in the bright shallows over the stones where the bridge used to stand and shrieking with the cold and the joy of it and refusing, roundly, to come out. A first crossing, of a sort; a far-side child's first brave wade into the very water that had cut the two banks apart. Perrin took it into himself, the bright cold shriek of it, and fired it into the little tile in the newly-woken far fire — a small keeping, caught and held on the far bank in the far fire for the first time in thirty years — and it came out of the cooling next dawn true and whole and glad, the child's first wade bright and unbowed in the clay.

He turned it in the morning light a long moment, the first thing the woken fire had kept, and then he gave it — not to himself, not to the shelf — to Wynn, who took it in both small hands and looked at it with the astonished dignity of the very young, and then looked up at the fire, and then, Edda saw, tipped her head, just slightly, the wrong way, the listening way, at the small warm tile that held her own morning; and looked at the fire again; and said, to no one, in the plain flat voice of a child stating a fact, "It's making the humming."

Nobody remarked on it. Edda caught Perrin's eye, and Perrin caught hers, and neither of them said a word, because you do not name that thing out loud when it shows in a child, you only mark it and let it be and wait to see if the fire means it. But Edda's heart turned over, quietly, in the summer morning, at a far-side child of seven tipping her head the wrong way at a warm tile and hearing, in a fire lit only two days, the hum that only a witch's ear can hear.

"Sound," Perrin said, of the tile, aloud, for the room. "The seam holds. The far fire keeps as true as the near — truer, maybe, it's that glad to be burning. Now we can trust it with the pot." And Brida, who had not slept for the two days' proving, let out a breath she seemed to have been holding since the spring.

# Chapter Ten — The Fire Keeps

There comes an hour in every true firing when the witch stops feeding the kiln and starts feeding the keeping — when the fire is right and the clay is ready and the thing held in the witch begins, through the hands on the wood, to go into the ware; and it is the hour a witch is for, and no one has ever found the words for it, and every witch dies still trying. That it should come, that hour, in a hearth thirty years cold, on the far bank, in a fire lit from a coal carried over the water — Edda had not known, until she stood and watched it come, that she had waited her whole life to see it and never known to hope for it.

They fired on the third night, when the woken hearth stood dry and true and the new pot leather-hard and marked — for Perrin had pressed into its foot the two-rook mark of the Rooke brothers, the mark on his own first pot, his witch's sign; and then, after a moment's thought, round its shoulder, over the pressed line that echoed Sabra's own, he had added with the point of a trimming tool a second small mark that was neither Wenholt's nor his: a tiny lantern. "For the bridge," he said, when Brida asked. "For the one they lit between them. So this pot knows the other one, across the years, the way the cups did. Lantern to lantern."

But first, before the firing, there was the hearing given whole — the give-back, because the true thing in the old cracked pot had to be heard and held before it could be caught anew; you cannot pour from a cup you have not first lifted. And so, in the firelight of the woken hearth, with the far side gathered and the near side's lights watching across the water, Perrin took up the old pot — Sabra's own last-firing pot, cracked foot to shoulder, that had held its keeping whole through thirty witchless years — and he warmed it in his two hands the way you warm a thing you mean to ask a great favour of, and he opened it, the way a give-back begins, making of himself a door and asking, gently, of the true thing kept: *say it back. So it can be held.* And the pot, thirty years full and thirty years silent, gave it back.

It came into the firelit dark like a warmth, and it was the midsummer night on the bridge, whole and thirty-years-fresh and fifty-years-young: the two of them, Sabra and Ilsabet, girls with fires, meeting in the middle of the water in the dark, each with a coal carried out in an iron pot; the one lantern lit between them from both flames; the two young witches sitting either side of it with their feet hung over the low summer water and the whole warm night around them, swearing the two banks one craft, one flame in two hearths, a river no wider than a witch is willing to cross it — and Edda, standing back with her hands behind her, heard her own dead teacher young and laughing on a bridge that fell before Edda was grown, heard Ilsabet's whole young heart, the friend across the water she'd loved all her life and never been able to cross to at the end; and Old Tam heard his Sabra young and glad; and Brida heard the old lonely woman her grandmother had sat with, before the loneliness, when she was a girl with a fire and a friend and the whole two-lamp valley ahead of her; and the far side heard, all of it together, the thing their bank had not known in living memory — that it had been loved, and equal, and never truly alone; that there had been two fires here once, sisters, and the far one every bit the elder, and the water between them a thing to be crossed and not a wall.

"That's what she kept," Perrin said, low, holding it whole, his face wet in the firelight. "Not the grief of the fire going out. The gladness of it being lit. She kept the beginning, not the end. She kept the night the two fires were one, so that whoever came would know the ending was never meant to be an ending — only a long cold waiting for the flame to come back across the water." He looked at the woken hearth, roaring true and level now, ready. "And here it's come. Let's give her keeping a home that'll hold it another lifetime."

Then he fired it. When the fire stood right — the far hearth breathing that deep even gold a witch feels in the chest before the eyes read it, the ash of Sabra's own dead fire melting in the glaze and running its faint warm veils down the new pot's shoulder over a boy's pressed lantern, so that the vessel took its colour partly from the living fire and partly from the dead one woken into it — Perrin set his hands to the stoke-wood and gave the keeping into the new cup: the whole of the midsummer night, the two coals, the one lantern, the vow over the water, caught out of the cracking old pot into the sound new one before the flaw could have it, held level and whole and true, the far side's own keeping home at last in the far side's own new clay, from the far side's own re-woken fire.

He felt the moment it took — every witch does, the small settling, like a key turning true in a lock — and it settled easy, the way a thing settles that was always meant to be where you're putting it; and he took his hands off the wood and stood in the fierce warm dark of a hearth that had been thirty years cold, and said, low, the words he'd written three nights back in the ninth ledger and would carry now his whole life, the sixth line of the catechism, spoken first on the far bank because it was the far bank that had earned it:

"*And what one fire keeps, another can wake — for a keeping waits longer than the witch who made it, and no fire is so long cold it cannot be lit again from a living one across the water.*"

"Say it back," Edda said, because that was the rule, older than either of them and older than the catechism and older than the fire, near enough.

And he said it back, and she said it with him the second time, the two witches of Wenholt at the true hour of a firing on the far bank of a water they'd both crossed at last; and around them the far side kept them, and across the water the near side's lights burned steady, and up the slope in a fallen house a cold cupboard stood empty of the pot that had waited in it a lifetime, because the waiting was over, and the keeping was home.

# Chapter Eleven — Two Lamps and a Third

The best of a firing is the morning after, the slow cool, the ware come out still warm; and the best of that best is when the thing you hoped for is truer in the hand than it was in the hope. But there is a better thing yet, rarer than any firing, that comes to a witch maybe once in a long keeping: the morning you understand that the thing you crossed the water to save has saved something you did not know was drowning.

They clammed up the far kiln at dawn, luting the door with wet clay for the slow cool, and let it come down unhurried through the day, and opened it at the cool of the next afternoon; and there in the middle where the fire ran truest stood the new pot, round-shouldered and two-handled and deep, its glaze come out the warm breathing brown of an old wood-ash over iron — Sabra's own ash — run down its shoulder in faint gold veils over the pressed lantern, and beside the lantern the line that echoed the old pot's line, so that anyone setting the two pots side by side, the cracked old one and the sound new, would say at once, *a pair; those two were always a pair*, the way the two cups had been a pair across the water, the way the two fires had been.

Perrin took it out still warm and held it a moment and then, because it was not his and had never been, put it into Brida's two hands.

"Lift it and hold it," he said. "The whole of it. It's Sabra — young, on the bridge, glad, before any of the cold. And it's your gran, who sat with her when she was old and lonely and kept the pot thirty years because she was asked. And it's the two fires, that were always one flame. It's the far side's, and it stays the far side's — I'll not carry it home across the water; it's home already, it was always home, it only wanted its fire lit and a witch to catch it before the crack." He closed her hands round the warm pot. "Set it where your family sets its true things, beside your gran's cup, the one that came the other way in the spring. Two pots now, one from each crossing. And when the little ones ask — and they will, they always ask after the warm ones — you tell them the true story. Not the far side dying. The far side lit. You tell them there were two fires here once, and there are two again, because a witch crossed the water the other way and carried a coal."

Brida held the warm pot and could not speak for a while, and then said the thing that undid them, which was small, the way the undoing things are: "There'll be a fire in that hearth now. Up the slope. Where there's been nothing but cold my whole life. I can look up the hill and there'll be a light." She looked up at the witch's house, where the woken hearth still breathed its warmth into the summer evening. "I never knew you could — that a thing could be *un*-ended. I thought ended was ended. Gran ended. The far side ended. And you crossed the water and un-ended a fire." She laughed, wet. "That's a greater thing than the cup. The cup gave me back a love. This gave a whole bank back its fire."

⁂

Edda did a small thing then that was not in any plan, the way she had knelt in Elias Wick's orchard in the spring and pressed a glad tile into the gladdest ground in the parish. She had brought, folded in her apron, the seventh ledger — Ilsabet's — carried across the water though she'd told herself it was only for reference; and now she knew why she'd truly brought it. She went up the slope to the little burying-ground behind the far-side church, Old Tam leaning on her arm to show her which stone, and there among the far graves she found it, worn but legible, older than the fallen bridge: *Sabra of Stonebeck, keeper of the fire, who kept it alone to the last.* And Edda knelt, at past fifty, at the grave of a woman she'd never known, her own teacher's own dear sister across the water, and she opened the seventh ledger to the young summer page where Ilsabet had written of the bridge, and she read it aloud, quiet, to the stone — Ilsabet's own young hand, the two lamps of one valley — so that the two friends' words met at last over the far grave the way they used to meet on the bridge, a generation late and not one word too late.

"She could not cross to you at the end," Edda told the stone, when she'd read it through. "The water was up. It grieved her past the saying, and she carried the grief the rest of her life, and I never knew she had it. But her apprentice's apprentice crossed, in the end. And your keeping told him how. And your fire's alight." She laid her old hand flat on the warm summer stone. "Two lamps, Sabra. Same as you swore. Two lamps in the valley again. You were right and the flood was wrong, and it only took a lifetime to prove it, and a lifetime's nothing to a keeping. Rest easy on your bank. There's a witch on it again, near enough — and if the fire's kind, there'll be one born to it before long." And she thought, kneeling, of a small solemn girl tipping her head the wrong way at a warm tile, and said nothing of it to the stone, because you don't promise a fire's business; but she thought it, and it warmed her more than the sun did.

# Chapter Twelve — The Crossing Home

Everyone in Wenholt knows the rule, and the newest witch knows the newest line of it; but the oldest thing the craft knows, older than the kiln and the catechism both, is that no fire lights itself, and every warmth in the world was carried from some other warmth by some hand willing to cross the cold between — so that the whole of the craft, in the end, is only the carrying of a light to a place that has gone dark, and the trusting that the dark was never the end of it.

They stayed three days more on the far bank, because you do not light a fire that's been thirty years cold and then row away and leave it to gutter. Perrin taught Old Tam's grandson and two of the steadier cousins the tending of it — not the craft, which cannot be taught to those the fire hasn't sent, but the plain keeping of a hearth, the drying and the feeding and the banking, so that the woken fire would not go cold again the moment the Wenholt witches crossed home. "It wants keeping warm now," he told them, "even between firings. A hearth once lit shouldn't be let go cold — bank it of a night, wake it of a morning, the way you'd keep any fire in a house you meant to live in. There'll be firings when I cross — I'll come, low-water years, and do the catching a witch has to do. But the fire itself is yours to keep alive between times. Don't let it out. Whatever else. Don't let it out again."

And on the fourth morning they crossed home, on a bright still summer day, the Ler run down to its lowest, so that Wim's oars near dragged the gravel; and the whole of Stonebeck came down to the landing to see them over, which no living soul on that bank had ever done for anyone crossing to Wenholt, because no one from Wenholt had ever crossed to be seen off. Old Tam stood on his two sticks with the firelight of three days' warmth still in his face. Cass held Wynn. And Brida stood at the very edge with the new pot held safe against her — she'd carry it up the slope after, to set beside the cup, two pots from two crossings — and she could not find the words, and gave up on them, which was right.

"You crossed a flood for a no, in the spring," Edda told her, "and went home with a yes. And now you've had a witch cross to you, and a fire lit that your whole bank thought was dead forever. That's a fair year's work for a girl of two-and-twenty. Mind the fire. Mind that child." She did not say more of the child, and Brida, who was quick, only looked at her, and understood there was a thing being not-said, and nodded, and would watch, and wait, the way the well-taught wait. "Go on. We'll be back low-water years. And you'll cross to us other years — the water's a ferry, not a wall, and never was. Two banks. Two fires. The way it was meant."

⁂

Wim rowed them home over the quiet low water, and on the Wenholt bank the near side had gathered — word having gone over three days of a fire lit on the far bank — and they came up the landing with the whole of the near valley there to meet them, and it was, without anyone deciding it, a small glad homecoming in the summer noon. Maud Brock had brought bread and argued about paying. Ansel came up from the clay-pit with Lena and the girls, and heard about the reset arch, and wanted every brick of it, and Edda promised him the whole account over a firing. Gran Brock, eighty-eight and bright as a robin, took Edda's hand in her two old ones and said, "You crossed the water. In your life. I never thought to see it," and Edda said, "Nor I, and it turns out the far bank's just a bank, with folk on it, same as ours, and I'm ashamed it took me fifty years and a dead witch to learn it."

And at the ferry-house, in the window, the two lamps stood — the porridge bowl and the mended dish — and Griet was in the doorway, on one of her clear evenings, and she looked across the water to where, up the far slope, a new light showed now against the dusk, a hearth-glow where thirty years there'd been only dark; and she smiled her whole own smile.

"There's two lamps over the water now," she said, bright and present and, for that little while, entirely herself. "Look. There's a light on the far hill. There's been no light on the far hill in all my life." She looked at Perrin, and her eyes were very kind. "You lit it. You took the fire over in your loaf-pot, careful as a christening, and you lit it. My mother said there ought always to have been two lamps." She turned back to her window, gently, the tide going out kind. "Now there's two again. One each bank. That's the right number. That was always the right number." And she settled to mind her two lamps, content, while across the water a third lamp burned that she had counted rightly and would not, tomorrow, remember counting.

⁂

That evening, the workshop quiet and the village gone home, Perrin came in from banking the near kiln with something turning in his face that Edda was learning, still, to read — the knowing that comes first, before the understanding limps after.

"The far fire's alight," he said, sitting. "And it wants a keeper of its own. I've been thinking it the whole way home. I can cross low-water years and do the catching. But a fire wants a witch on its own bank — a hearth's not truly kept by a witch who lives across a flood-prone water and comes when the ford's kind. Sabra kept it her whole life because she was *there*, every day, banking it, waking it, living beside it. It needs that again. It needs its own." He looked at Edda. "And there's — you saw. The child. Wynn. She tipped her head at the tile. She heard the humming. I didn't say, and you didn't say, and I know why neither of us said. But I heard her say it, Mistress. *It's making the humming.* A far-side child, in a fire two days lit, hearing the hum." He turned his own new-marked cup a quarter, the way he'd caught the habit off Edda. "Could the far side have its own witch again? A born one, this time, sent by its own woken fire? Could a fire, once relit, raise a keeper of its own on its own bank — the way Sabra always waited for and the fire never sent, because maybe a cold hearth can't send one, and a warm one can?"

Edda was quiet a while. She looked at the private shelf, at the stilled red cup at the far end where a first pot used to be, and she turned her own crooked cup a quarter, slow, the one that smelled of peas and summer, and let it turn all the way round.

"I don't know," she said. "And there's that word again, that the whole craft lives in. I don't know if a woken fire can send a witch where a dying one couldn't. I don't know if the child's ear is the fire's doing or only a child's tipped head that means nothing. But I know a keeper takes years in the making — you were three years in the making before the fire ever spoke, and it wanted watching, and waiting, and a whole village's patience, and mine." She shook her head slowly, but it was the shake that means *I don't yet see how* and not the shake that means *no*. "If the fire's sending her, it'll not be this year nor next, and it'll want a witch to watch for it, and to teach the far-side words of the catechism to a far-side child the way Sabra taught them to Tam sixty years back. And that's a crossing too, and a long one, of a different sort. But we'll cross low-water years, you and I, and you'll mind that child the way I minded you, and if the fire means her, we'll know, in time — and then the far side will have its own again, and there'll be two witches for the two fires, sisters across the water the way there were when Sabra and Ilsabet were young." She looked at him, and something in her look handed him a thing, lightly, the way she'd handed him the small pot at Wintermark and the crossing in the spring. "That'd be your keeping to make, I think. A far-side witch. Not mine. I'll not live to see her come to the chair, most like — but you might, and you'd be the Ilsabet to her Sabra, the near fire that watched over the far one till it stood on its own. That's a long carry. But you're young enough for it. That's what young witches are *for*."

Perrin said nothing, but his eyes had the sideways attention in them, already listening, across the water, to a fire two days lit and a child of seven who'd tipped her head the wrong way.

"But that's a longer story than a summer," Edda said, "and this one's told, and there's a firing to be paid for and bread owed to Maud we'll never once be square on, and my old bones have crossed a water for the first and maybe the last time and want their chair. Wash the dust off. Bank the stove. And say it back to me."

And Perrin Loft, witch of Wenholt, who had crossed the water the other way and carried a fire to a cold hearth and woken what a dead fire kept a lifetime, said it back — the whole of it, word for word, for the witches after, on both banks now:

*The kiln keeps. It does not take. Say it back.*

*And what it keeps, asked kindly, it can give back.*

*And what breaks, told together, it can mend.*

*And what one hand throws, another hand fills — for a first pot is not one you fill alone.*

*And what never happened, the fire cannot make — but a love is not the wedding it was promised, and the fire keeps the love, not the loss.*

*And what one fire keeps, another can wake — for a keeping waits longer than the witch who made it, and no fire is so long cold it cannot be lit again from a living one across the water.*

And across the water, up the far slope, in a hearth that had been thirty years cold, a fire burned that a boy had lit from a coal carried over in an iron pot; and beside it, on a shelf that had held nothing in a lifetime, stood a warm new pot with a lantern pressed on its shoulder, keeping a midsummer night when two young witches swore a river was no wider than the willingness to cross it; and in a low far house a small solemn girl slept, who had tipped her head the wrong way at a warm tile and heard a hum that only a witch can hear; and up the near lane the lamp stood in the workshop window, which was the best of it, the light kept burning against the coming of whoever might need it; and the kiln kept what it was given, and did not take, and would go on keeping, now, with two fires and two banks and two witches and maybe, in a low-water year to come, a third — for a good long while yet.

THE END

## About the series

*The Summer Ember* is the sixth Night Kiln novella. The five-book arc that opened with one old woman afraid she was the last of her line closed in *The Spring Cup* with two lamps lit at the Wenholt kiln; *The Summer Ember* opens the ground beyond it, carrying the craft — for the first time — across the water the other way, to a fallen far bank that had a fire of its own once and lost it. Book by book, the fire of Wenholt is asked to do a new thing with an old craft — to *keep*, to *give back*, to *mend*, to *hand on*, to keep *the love, not the loss*, and now to *wake* a cold fire from a living one across the water.

By the end of this book the valley has two fires and two lamps again, one on each bank, as it had a lifetime ago — and on the far side a child has tipped her head the wrong way at a warm tile and heard a hum that only a witch can hear. Whether the woken far fire raises a keeper of its own is a longer story than a summer, lightly left open at the close.

If *The Summer Ember* is the first Wenholt book you have read, you can begin the arc from the beginning with **Book One, *The Night Kiln***, and watch the craft, and its catechism, grow line by line to the sixth.
