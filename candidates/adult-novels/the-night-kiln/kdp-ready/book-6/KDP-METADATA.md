## Description (KDP book description — plain text, paste as-is)

The flood is long gone; the ford runs shallow over the stones; and across the water, on a bank Wenholt has never once crossed to, a cold hearth has stood dark for thirty years.

Edda Marren has kept the night kiln of Wenholt for a lifetime, firing her neighbours' memories into mugs and bowls and butter crocks so a grandmother's laugh or a child's first steps can stay warm in the room long after the moment is gone. Now there are two witches at the kiln — and a question carried over the summer water that neither of them has ever been asked.

Stonebeck had a fire of its own once, and a witch to keep it, before the bridge fell and the far side went quiet. That witch is thirty years dead, her hearth cold, her craft gone from the far bank — but a single pot she made still stands in a fallen house, cracked and failing, holding a true thing no one has had the ear to hear in a lifetime. To wake it before the crack finishes it, Perrin — a witch of half a year — must cross the water the other way, carry a living coal to a dead hearth, and light a fire that everyone on the far bank had given up for gone. What he finds kept in the old pot is not a grief but a map: the memory of a midsummer night, two young witches, one lantern lit from two fires in the middle of a bridge that no longer stands — and a vow that a river is never as wide as a witch is willing to cross it.

The Summer Ember is the sixth Night Kiln novella: a warm, low-stakes, big-hearted comfort read about a keeping that outlasted its keeper, a friendship parted by a fallen bridge, a whole bank of a river given its fire back, and the youngest witch learning that the far side was never another country — only a light that needed carrying across. Contains grief and long-past loss handled with care; no violence, no explicit content. Stands alone; richest of all after Books 1–5, and the first Night Kiln novella to light a second fire across the water.
