> Everyone in Wenholt knows the rule: bring Edda a memory before moonrise, and she will fire it into clay you can hold. But on the witch's private shelf, at the far end where a first pot used to sit, stands a small wedding cup that hums — fired thirty years ago and more for a wedding that never, in the end, happened, and never once claimed. When the spring flood puts the ford in spate, a girl from across the water finally finds the courage to cross, and asks the one thing the fire has never been asked: could a fire ever fire a thing that never happened at all — a wedding that never was? What Edda finds, when at last she listens to the humming cup, is not a wedding to be conjured but a love that was always true, kept safe through thirty years of not-happening, and waiting only for someone brave enough to cross the water and receive it. It is a spring story, and it is time it was told.

**Content note:** Adult cozy fantasy. Contains grief and long-past sorrow (a broken engagement, a recent bereavement, both gently handled), and a beloved character living with memory loss — all handled with warmth. No violence, no explicit content. A comfort read for adult readers. This is the fifth Night Kiln novella; it stands alone.

# Chapter One — The Water Coming Up

The rule in Wenholt was older than anyone who kept it, and it was this: if you wanted a memory fired, you brought it to Edda Marren before moonrise, because the kiln burned at night or not at all. You came up the witch's lane with your year in your hands; you sat in the good chair by the workshop stove with your fingers around something warm, and you told the memory out loud — properly, the whole of it — and the telling went into the witch, and bided in her, and on the firing night the fire drew it out of her, through her hands on the stoke-wood, into the waiting clay, so that ever after the memory lived in a mug or a bowl or a butter crock: a thing warm past what any cooling explained, a thing that could stand in the room when the rememberer no longer could. The kiln keeps. It does not take. Say it back. Four generations of Wenholt witches had said it like a catechism, and in three short years and the hard winter just gone the catechism had grown three lines more — *what it keeps, asked kindly, it can give back; what breaks, told together, it can mend; and what one hand throws, another hand fills* — taught to the fire and the village together, for the witches after.

And there were two of them now, which was the great change, and the sweetness under everything that spring. Edda still woke each morning slightly surprised by it, the way you are surprised by a step that is no longer where the stairs used to end. The winter had made her a second witch out of the boy she had spent three years making a potter, and the plain arithmetic that had frightened her all through the frost — one old woman, one kiln, and no one after — had thawed along with the river, so that now when she stood at the wedging bench in the grey spring light there was another bench filled, another pair of hands folding clay over on itself until it showed smooth as cheese, another quiet in the room that was the particular quiet of two people who did not need to talk.

"The ford's under," Perrin reported, coming in from the gate with the wet spring cold rolling off his coat, taller yet than he had been at Wintermark, so that Edda had given up expecting him to stop. He was seventeen and a witch of Wenholt these three months, and he still set the milk-jug by the stove to warm and still turned it a quarter without knowing he did it. "Wim's ferrying anyone who can't wait. Says it's the highest he's seen the Ler run since the year the Stonebeck bridge went. Brown as a barn all the way to the willows."

"It always comes up like this when the hills let go at once," Edda said, not looking up from her clay, which had gone loose and generous under the spring, as biddable now as it had been sullen in the frost. "A hard winter and a quick thaw. The water has to go somewhere, and it chooses the low road, same as everything does." She set the mass down and cut it with the wire and looked at the smooth cut face, reading it the way she read most things, sideways. "There'll be no telling carted from the far side till the ford falls. Anybody Stonebeck-way with a memory to fire will hold it a week rather than trust it to Wim's boat in this."

"There's a deal being held, then," Perrin said. "The whole far side's shut off but the ferry."

"The whole far side is always a little shut off," Edda said, "flood or no. That's the water for you."

The spring had come the way it did after a killing winter: not gently, but all at once and half in tears. For three weeks the frost had held the valley like a fist, and then in a single soft grey night the fist had opened, and the snow on the high ground had begun, everywhere at once, to weep. The lanes ran. The wells came up sweet and brimming. The Ler, which all winter had lain roofed bank to bank with ice that Wim crossed on foot with a pole and a lantern, had woken with a groan one dawn and shrugged its whole white roof downstream in a single grinding afternoon, and then risen, and risen, brown and shouldering, until the ford below the ferry landing — the flat gravel crossing where in a dry summer a child could wade — had gone under a wagon's depth of racing water and stayed under. The willows stood knee-deep in it. The world smelled of turned earth and cold water and, faintly, under everything, of the first green thing pushing up somewhere out of sight, which is a smell with no name and every hope in it.

It was the kind of spring, Edda thought, wiping down the bench, that made the old remember their springs. Water coming up over a familiar crossing had a way of bringing things up with it.

They had one telling that morning, carted no distance at all, which was Tally Brock come up from the bakehouse with flour still on her forearms and a thing she wanted kept before the year took it from her. She sat in the good chair with her hands round the cup Perrin gave her and told, shy at first and then not shy, the memory of her own small daughter's first steps, taken three days back across the bakehouse floor from the flour-bin to Tally's knees — four of them, then a sit-down, then the roar of pure outrage that the walking had stopped — and how the whole warm room of the bakehouse had gone still to watch and then broken into cheering, and the child, startled into stillness by the noise, had looked round at the lot of them with the astonished dignity of the very young, as if to say, *yes, quite, and about time you noticed.* Edda took it into herself, the small bright wobble of it, the outrage and the dignity, and it settled under her breastbone warm as a swallow of good tea; and she carried it to the telling shelf and set it there, and the slip in the little bowl she'd wait to glaze had gone, already, the downy new-leaf green of the first thing pushing up.

"There," she said, "that'll fire the next night we fire, and it'll be a cup she can drink out of at her own wedding, if she likes, and be told she named the whole bakehouse a fool for not clapping sooner." And Tally laughed, and went back to her bread, and it was, Edda thought, exactly the right telling to have carried on the morning of a rising flood: a first walking, a first crossing of a small warm floor, a child going bravely from one safe thing toward another with nothing between but a stretch of open ground and the whole watching room holding its breath for her.

She did not think, that morning, of the cup at the end of the private shelf. But it hummed, as it had hummed for thirty years and more, so low and so constant that no one in the workshop heard it any longer, the way you stop hearing a mill-race you have lived beside your whole life — until the day it stops, or the day a stranger, standing in your doorway, tips her head and says: *what is that sound?*

The private shelf stood behind the glaze jars, a plain board no customer ever saw: the workshop's own small museum of the craft's mistakes and mercies. It held less than it once had, and Edda had come to count its emptying the way you count grey hairs, with a rue that was mostly gratitude. There was the ground-smooth lid of Widow Sorrel's honey jar, laid a little ajar beside the hand's-width of board it had haunted for seventy years — the door left open, the whole tale of the emptying-that-was-given-back written in two pieces of clay for anyone who could read them. There was the clean bare board where the dunted race-dish had stood twelve years and stood no longer, gone home to a window at the ferry to be a lamp beside a lamp. There was the newer bare place, and Edda's eye still went to it and still ached kindly at it, where all winter Grams Ilsabet's homely thumb-marked first pot had sat, and sat no longer, gone back to the river clay at Wintermark to be handed on the way a first pot should be.

And at the far end, alone now where a first pot used to be its neighbour, stood the wedding cup that hummed.

It was a pretty thing, and had been a fine one — better made than most of the shelf's exhibits, because it had not been made as a lesson but as a gift. A footed cup, two-handled, the size to be lifted by two hands or shared between two, with a glaze the warm deep red-brown of an iron celadon gone rich under a good reduction, and around its shoulder, pressed in the wet clay by a thumbnail before the firing, a plain running line of little marks that Edda had decided long ago were meant to be either wheat or a river, and had never been able to settle which. It had been fired, Edda knew, before she was properly a witch — thirty years ago and more, in the last of Grams Ilsabet's own long keeping of the kiln, when Edda was twenty and all thumbs and knew the glazes but not yet the fire. All she carried of it now was the fact of the thing: a wedding cup, and the wedding not to be, and it would keep on the shelf until it was wanted. The not-to-be of it had seemed to her then a small grown-up sorrow of no great weight, the sort that happened to other people in other years; and she had gone on to the next pot, as the young do, and the cup had kept, and hummed, and thirty years had gone over it, and she had never once, in all that time, been able to bring herself to break it or to ask it whose hope it held.

She dusted it that morning, as she dusted it every morning, and set it back, and did not listen to it, and went to put the kettle on.

# Chapter Two — The Girl Who Crossed

There is a kind of courage that takes a season to gather and is spent in a single crossing, and it does not look, from the outside, like courage at all. It looks like a girl standing very still at a garden gate.

She came at the wrong end of the afternoon, when the light was going and the good chair should by rights have been empty, and she came up the lane on foot and wet to the knee, and she stopped at the gate and did not ring the bell. Perrin saw her first, from the shed, and came and told Edda in the low voice he used for the ones who needed handling gently.

"There's a girl at the gate," he said. "Been there a while. Stonebeck, by her boots. She's not rung. She keeps putting her hand to the bell and taking it back."

Edda knew, before she looked, and was surprised to find she knew. She had been half-expecting this caller since Wintermark — since the longest morning of the year, when Perrin had walked up from the ferry in the dark with a thing Griet had told him folded in his chest, and told it to Edda whole at the workshop window: a girl who had come to the ferry weeks before the frost and asked did the witch still keep a cup that hummed, a wedding cup from a wedding that never was, and had gone pale when Griet said *cross and ask her yourself*, and gone back the way she'd come. Edda had said, that Wintermark morning, *she'll cross, that girl, one brave spring; they always cross in the end.* And here was the spring, and here, standing at the gate with her hand on the bell she could not ring, was the crossing.

"Put the kettle on," Edda said, "and open the gate before she talks herself back down the lane."

Edda went out herself, in the end, because a girl who cannot ring a bell wants a face and not a summons; and she opened the gate and did not say *what do you want* or *are you coming in*, but only, "You'll be wet through. There's a stove and a chair and a kettle already thinking about it, and you can tell me your business or say nothing at all and just get warm, whichever suits. Both are allowed here." And the girl, who had plainly braced herself against every hard or grand or witchy thing a witch might say, and had no brace ready for kindness, came apart a little at that, the way people do; and came in.

She was slight and dark and wind-scoured, with a good plain face and the reddened eyes of someone who had been doing her weeping privately for months, and she sat on the very edge of the good chair as if she had not earned the whole of it, and she held the tea Perrin put in her hands and turned the cup a quarter without drinking, and looked about the warm workshop — the ranked glaze jars, the wheel, the telling shelf at heart-height with its two spring bowls, the stove, the two witches, one old and one absurdly young — with the wonder of someone who has carried a picture of a place in her head for three years and is finding the real one both smaller and far kinder than the picture.

Her name was Brida, and she was one-and-twenty, and she had crossed on Wim's ferry that afternoon in the brown roaring flood with her heart in her mouth the whole way and her hands white on the gunwale, which Edda learned not because the girl said so but because she said, at last, "I thought the water would have me. I nearly turned back at the landing. I've nearly turned back — oh, a hundred times. Every spring for three years I've stood on the Stonebeck bank and looked at the water and thought, this year I'll cross, and every year the water's been too high or I've been too — " she stopped, and turned the cup a quarter, and Edda saw her notice she'd done it, and blush, as if she'd been caught at something. "My gran did that," she said. "Turned her cup. I've caught myself doing it since she died."

"When did you lose her?" Edda asked.

"Wintermonth. The hard frost. She was eighty-one and she went in her sleep and it was a good going, everyone says, and it was, only —" Brida looked at her tea. "Only I've been clearing her house since. You know how it is. All the small things. And it's the small things that undo you, isn't it, not the big. The big you brace for. It's finding the darning still in the basket."

"It is exactly the small things," Edda said, "and I've never known why we brace for the wrong end of it. Go on. You crossed the water. That's a great deal for a small thing. What is it you came to ask?"

And Brida, one-and-twenty, sat in the good chair in a witch's workshop in a strange village across a flooded river, and gathered the last of the courage she had been three years gathering, and asked it.

"I came to ask," she said, "whether you can fire a wedding that never happened."

The workshop was very quiet. Out at the gate the wet lane held the last of the light. On the far bench Perrin had gone still in the way he had learned to go still, which was the whole of his half of the craft just then — to be present and to be silent and to let a thing be said all the way to the end.

"My gran was to be married," Brida said, "here, in Wenholt, thirty years ago and more, before I was thought of, before my mother was thought of. To a Wenholt man. And it — didn't happen. The wedding. It never happened, and she went home across the water and married my grandfather instead, who was a good man and good to her, and had my mother, and had me, and I loved her, and she never once in her life spoke of it — the other. The one that didn't happen. Not once. But we knew. The whole family knew, the way you know a thing that's never said. There was a — a not-happening, in her. In all of us. A shape where a thing should have been. She thought about it every spring, I know she did, I'd catch her at the window looking at the water when the ford came up. And she died in the winter with it never said and never mended and never — " Brida's voice went, and she took a breath, and steadied it, and finished. "And I found, clearing her house, that there'd been a cup. A wedding cup. Fired, they said, and never claimed, and it — my great-aunt told me, before she went too — she said it hummed. That the witch of Wenholt kept a cup that hummed, from a wedding that never was, and that it was my gran's, in a way, though she never had it. And I thought — " the girl looked up, and there it was, the whole of the three years' crossing in her face " — I thought, if you can fire a memory, could you fire that? The wedding? The one she never had? So she could have it. So we could stop — inheriting the not-happening. Could a fire ever do that? Fire a thing that never happened at all?"

And Edda, who had been asked in her life to keep a grief and to give one back and to mend a broken thing and to hand a whole craft on, and who had thought, at past fifty, that she had been asked most of what a fire could be asked, sat with her hands around her own crooked cup, and did not answer straight away, because the honest answer was *no*, and she could hear, now, for the first time in thirty years, the cup at the end of the shelf, humming.

# Chapter Three — What the Fire Will Not Do

The first thing a witch owes anyone is the truth, and the second is not to give it so fast it lands like a stone. Edda took her time.

"Drink your tea," she said, "it's gone past helping you but it'll give your hands something honest to do. And let me tell you what I can and can't, because you've crossed a flooded river for the asking and you're owed it plain." She set down her own cup. "The fire keeps what's true. That's the whole of it, under all the rest. A telling goes into me and it has to be a true one — not true like a court oath, but true like a thing that was really felt, really lived, really *is*. When your gran's neighbour tells me her husband's laugh, the laugh happened; I'm keeping a real thing, only making it a thing you can hold. I've kept griefs and given them back and mended what broke. But I've never once, in all my years, been able to fire a thing that wasn't. And I'll tell you why, because it matters and because you'll feel the difference when you hear it: a fire that would keep a thing that never happened isn't keeping. It's making up. It's a lie you could hold in your hand, warm, and take down of an evening and believe, and there is nothing — " she said it gently, but she said it " — there is nothing crueller you could do to a grieving family than give them a lovely warm lie to hold. Your gran's wedding didn't happen. I can't make it have happened. If I fired you a wedding that never was, I'd be firing you a dream, and a dream fired into clay is the one thing this kiln has never done and never should, because it would feel, ever after, exactly like the truth, and it wouldn't be, and one day you'd know, and it would break your family worse than the not-happening ever has."

"How do you know," Brida said, not defiant, only wanting it understood, "that it'd be a lie? If you fired it so real it felt true — how's that different from the true things? You never saw her husband's laugh either, the neighbour's. You take folk's word. Why's her word for the wedding she wanted any less?"

It was, Edda thought, a better question than most grown people managed, and she gave it the honest answer. "Because her word for the wedding isn't a memory. It's a wish. And I can tell them apart the way you can tell a stone from an egg in the dark, by the weight of them in the hand. When your neighbour tells me the laugh, there's a thing behind the words — a real morning, a real sound, a happening the words are only pointing at, and it's the happening I keep, not the words. When a body tells me a thing they wanted and never had, the words point at nothing; there's no happening behind them, only the wanting, and the wanting's real but the thing wanted isn't, and if I fire the words anyway I make a pointing-finger aimed at empty air and dress it up warm so it looks like it points at something. That's the lie. Not that the feeling's false — the feeling's the truest thing there is — but that I'd have made a keeping of an event that never was, and a keeping says, every time you lift it, *this happened.* I'll not fire a *this happened* onto a thing that didn't. That's the one wall this craft has never crossed, and the day it crosses it, it stops being a keeping-craft and starts being a lying one, and I'd sooner let the kiln fall cold."

Brida had gone pale, and Edda watched the three years' hope go down in her like a lamp turned low, and hated the necessity of it, and held to it, because the girl was owed the truth first and the mercy after and never the other way round.

"So that's the no," Edda said. "That's the thing the fire will not do. I'm sorry. You crossed a river for a no, and I'd have spared you it if I could."

"Then there's nothing," Brida said, low. "It stays. The not-happening. It just — stays."

And that was when Perrin, from the far bench, spoke — quietly, and out of turn, and exactly rightly, because he was a witch now and a witch's first duty is to hear, and he had been hearing something the whole while that Edda, too close to her own no, had not yet let herself turn and hear.

"Mistress," Perrin said. "The cup's humming."

"It's always hummed," Edda said.

"It's humming *louder*," Perrin said, and stood, and went to the private shelf, and stood before it with his head tipped in the sideways-listening way that no one had ever taught him, that had made Edda know, three years back, that he was a witch before he knew it himself. "Since she started talking. It's been getting louder since she came in. Come and hear."

Edda went and stood beside him. And she listened to the cup the way she had not let herself listen in thirty years — properly, the whole of it, with the wrong sense on purpose — and she heard, under the low steady hum she had stopped hearing before this girl was born, that Perrin was right: the note had come up, the way the river had come up, brown and shouldering and impossible to ignore, and it was not the hum of an empty thing. It was, she understood, standing there with a stranger's grief at her back and her dead teacher's cup warm under her hand, the hum of a *full* thing. A kept thing. A thing with something true in it that had been waiting, humming, thirty years, for an ear.

"Brida," Edda said slowly, not turning round. "This cup. You said it was your gran's, in a way, though she never had it. Tell me her name. Your gran's. The girl she was, thirty years ago, before she was anyone's gran."

"Nesta," said Brida. "Nesta Alder, she was, before she married. Of Stonebeck."

And the cup, at the name, hummed like a struck bell going on and on, and Edda took her hand off it as if it had warmed past bearing, and stood a moment with her heart going, because a cup that had hummed low and empty for thirty years does not answer to a name unless there was, all along, a name in it to answer.

"It's not empty," Edda said. "I've been calling it an empty thing all my life and it isn't. There's something in it. There's been something in it the whole time." She turned to the girl. "I can't fire you a wedding that never was. That much is still true and always will be. But I begin to think there's a true thing in this cup that nobody living has ever heard — not you, not your gran, not me — and I begin to think it's been waiting a very long time to be let out. And that's a different question altogether." She looked at the humming cup, red and warm and thirty years patient. "That's a question I'll cross a river to answer. But not tonight, and not blind. Perrin — bank the stove and light the good lamp. I've ledgers to read."

# Chapter Four — The Year of the Cup

Every witch keeps a ledger, and the ledgers keep the witches; and the nine of them stood on their own shelf in Wenholt, oldest to newest, the spines gone the colour of old honey, so that to take down a year was to take down a hand long stilled and let it write to you again.

The ninth ledger was Perrin's, three months begun. The eighth had been Edda's these many years. But it was the seventh she wanted — Grams Ilsabet's, thick and close-written, the years of Ilsabet's own long keeping before Edda came to the chair — and it was the year thirty-odd back that she hunted, turning the stiff pages under the good lamp while the spring rain came on soft outside and Perrin sat across the bench with the humming cup between his two hands, listening to it the way you'd hold a bird, and Brida sat in the good chair not drinking her third tea, because Edda had said *stay the night, the ford's under and you'll not cross back in the dark, and this concerns you, and you should hear it as it comes.*

She found it by the drawing first. Ilsabet had a way of sketching a commissioned piece in the margin beside its telling — a quick sure line, a potter drawing a pot — and there, thirty-two years back by the ledger's own reckoning, was the cup: two-handled, footed, the running line round the shoulder, unmistakable. And beside it, in Ilsabet's close upright hand, the telling.

"'A wedding cup,'" Edda read aloud, slowly, giving each word its weight, "'for Nesta Alder of Stonebeck and Elias Wick of this parish, to be wed at Wenholt come the apple-blossom. A loving-cup, two hands or two mouths, as they asked it. They came together to give the telling and it was the sweetest hour's work of my year. I have fired a deal of grief into this kiln and it is a strange gladness to fire, for once, a plain gladness — two young people who mean it, telling me their wanting of each other so that they might drink it at the wedding and keep it after, the way the old couples used to and the young ones have mostly stopped. He talks too much and she laughs at him and he does not mind being laughed at, which is the whole of a good marriage told in a sentence. The telling took strong. It is a full cup. I have rarely fired a fuller. It will keep them their whole lives if they let it.'"

Edda stopped. The workshop was very still. Brida had come out of the good chair without anyone seeing her move, and stood at Edda's shoulder, and was reading the dead woman's hand with her own hand pressed to her mouth.

"That's her," Brida whispered. "That's my gran. *She laughs at him and he does not mind being laughed at.* That's her whole life. She did that to my grandfather forty years. I never knew it started — I never knew there was a first one she — " She could not finish.

And Edda, reading it, was twenty again for a moment — twenty and all thumbs in this same shed on a night thirty-two years gone, watching Grams Ilsabet draw a red loving-cup from a cooling kiln and set it by, and telling the apprentice, in the sparing way she told a girl a thing not yet hers to carry, only that it was a wedding cup and the wedding was not to be and it would keep on the shelf till it was wanted. She had not asked more. You did not ask Ilsabet more; and besides, at twenty, another woman's called-off wedding was a small grey grown-up weather that happened elsewhere, and the apprentice had gone on to the next pot with the whole of the true thing standing unregarded at her elbow, and had walked past it ten thousand mornings since with a duster. *Thirty years I've dusted it,* she thought, *and never once heard it, because I decided at twenty it was empty and never troubled to ask again.* There was a lesson in that for a witch, and it was not a comfortable one, and she filed it to be uncomfortable about later.

"Elias Wick," Edda said, half to herself, and something turned over in her memory, slow and cold and then warm. "Elias Wick. I knew an Elias Wick. Old Elias, the orchardman, up past the clay-pit — kept the pear trees and the little apple orchard on the south slope, gave the children fruit at picking, kept himself to himself, never married, never said why, died — six years back? Seven. Ansel dug his grave. A kind quiet old man that everyone knew and nobody knew, and every spring his orchard came up in blossom like a bride whether he tended it or no." She looked at the cup, at the running line she had never been able to read. "Wheat or a river, I always said. It's neither. It's apple-blossom. He asked her for apple-blossom round the cup, because they were to wed at the blossoming." She sat back. "Elias Wick. I have walked past that man's orchard a thousand times. I have eaten his pears. And I never once knew he was the other half of the cup on my own shelf."

"But they didn't marry," Brida said, and her voice had the old ache in it, the family ache, the shape where a thing should have been. "It's a full cup, she says — *the sweetest hour's work of my year* — and then they didn't marry. What happened? Why didn't they —"

"That's the next page," Edda said, "and I'm afraid of it, and I'll read it anyway." And she turned the leaf.

# Chapter Five — The Flood and the Letters

There is a grief that is nobody's fault, and it is the hardest kind to lay down, because there is no one to forgive and so it never ends. Edda had put down griefs with a name on them all her life. This one, she came to understand that rainy night, had only ever had a river's name on it.

The next page was written later — the ink a shade different, the hand a shade heavier — and it was headed, in Ilsabet's careful way, with a date: *the week after the blossom that was to be the wedding.*

"'The wedding did not happen,'" Edda read, "'and I set it down here though it grieves me to, because a witch should keep the true thing even when the true thing is a sorrow, and this is the truest and the sorriest I have kept in a long year.

"'It was the water. The spring ran high that year, higher than this — the Stonebeck bridge went in the night and the ford stood a wagon deep for nine days, and the blossom came and went on the trees while the two banks might as well have been two countries. Nesta was to cross for the wedding and could not cross. That is the whole of the true thing, and if the world were kind it would be the end of it: they would have waited for the water to fall and wed a fortnight late and laughed about it for fifty years. But the world put a second thing on top of the first, and the second thing was a wrong word, and between the water and the wrong word they were undone.

"'Someone — I will not write who, for they meant no harm and are shamed enough, and the harm is done and naming it mends nothing — someone carried word across to Stonebeck, in the flood days, that Elias had been seen and had cried off; and someone carried word back to Elias that Nesta would not come, that her people had thought better of a Wenholt match; and neither word was true, and each was believed, because a person waiting nine days on the wrong side of a flood for someone who does not come will believe the cruelest thing sooner than the kind one, for the cruel thing at least explains the silence. By the time the ford fell, Nesta had gone home to Stonebeck with her heart shut, and Elias had shut his after her, and each was too proud and too hurt and too young to cross the fallen water and ask the other to their face: did you? Did you cry off? Because to cross and ask is to risk the answer yes, and they had each already had the answer yes, secondhand, from a liar, and could not bear to have it again from the mouth they loved.'"

Edda stopped, and had to. Brida was crying, quietly, the clean quiet crying of someone finally given the true shape of an old ache; and Perrin sat with the humming cup in his hands and his young face gone grave and old; and the rain came on soft against the black window.

"There's more," Edda said gently. "There's a last of it. Do you want it tonight, child, or —"

"Tonight," Brida said. "All of it. She waited thirty years. I can wait to the bottom of a page."

So Edda read the last of it.

"'Nesta came to me before she crossed home. She was not weeping. She was past it — that flat far place past it, that is worse to see. She asked me to break the cup. I would not. Then she asked me — and I set this down because it is the strangest asking of my life and I have thought on it since and not got to the bottom of it — she asked me whether I could fire the wedding into the cup. The wedding that was not to be. She said: you fire what's real; make it real; fire me the wedding I'm not to have, so I can have it somewhere, so it happened somewhere, so I'm not a woman a whole village watched not get married. And I told her no. I told her the fire keeps the true and will not make the false, and that a wedding fired that never was would be a lie she'd hold warm all her life and it would poison every true thing after it. She said then I was cruel, and she was right that it felt cruel, and she crossed home, and I have not seen her these many years, though I hear she married well over the water and is happy, and I am glad of it, and it does not touch this.

"'I could not break the cup, for it is full of a true thing — their love, which was real, whatever the water did to it. And I could not give it to either of them, for neither would have it. So I have banked it on the shelf. It holds a true love and no wedding, which is a thing the fire was never meant to hold, a full cup with nowhere to be poured, and it hums. It has hummed since the night the wedding was to be. I do not think it will stop until the thing in it is heard by someone who can bear to hear it, and I do not know who that will be, or when, and I will likely not live to see it. So I set it down here for the witch after me, and the witch after her: there is a true thing kept in the humming cup at the end of the shelf, and it is a love and not a wedding, and one day someone will come who is owed it. Give it them gently. — I.'"

The workshop was silent but for the rain and the cup, which hummed on, full and patient, in Perrin's careful hands.

"There's one thing more," Edda said, very quietly, "and I think I know now what it is, and where." She rose, and took the lamp, and went to the seventh ledger's back board, where a witch tucks the loose things — the pressed leaf, the paid bill, the letter meant to be carried and never carried — and she drew out, from where it had lain thirty years flat against the cover, a folded paper gone soft as cloth at the creases, and on the outside of it, in a different hand, a man's plain hand: *For Nesta Alder, Stonebeck. To be carried when the water falls.*

"Ilsabet kept it," Edda said. "Elias's letter. He wrote it after — you can feel it, it's the paper of a man who's had a week to think — and he gave it to the witch to carry across when the ford fell, because he'd no other way to reach her, and Ilsabet took it, and meant to carry it, and —" She turned it in the lamplight. "And the water fell, and by then Nesta was married-word gone home, and to carry it then would have been to break a marriage or break a heart, and Ilsabet — I think Ilsabet couldn't decide, and a thing you can't decide you set down to decide tomorrow, and tomorrow is thirty years, and then you die." She looked at Brida. "It's your gran's. It always was. It's thirty years late and it's yours to open, or yours to leave folded. I'll not read a word of it without you say."

# Chapter Six — What a Love Is

There is a question every apprentice asks and every witch keeps answering their whole life, which is: what is the fire *for*? And every book of the answer is the same book, and every year it says something new.

Brida read the letter alone, in the good chair, with the lamp pulled close, while Edda and Perrin took themselves to the shed on the excuse of the clay and stood in the doorway watching the rain and giving the girl the room. When she was done she came out with her face washed clean by it, the way a face is after the right weeping, and she did not tell them what was in it — that was hers, and Nesta's, and Elias's, and rightly no one's else — but she told them the shape of it, which was all they needed.

"He never believed it," she said. "He wrote it to tell her he never for one hour believed she'd cried off — that he knew the wrong word for a lie the moment he heard it and knew it in his bones was a lie, only by then she was gone, and he waited three days at the fallen ford for her to come back and ask him, and she didn't, and he understood she'd had the same lie and believed it, and he wrote — he wrote that he'd wait. That he'd not go looking, because to go looking after a thing like that is to make a woman choose you against her own shut heart, and he'd not do that to her; but that he'd be at Wenholt, at the orchard, and the door was not shut, whatever the water had made her think, and if ever any spring she wanted to cross and ask him to his face, the answer was the answer it had always been, and would be till he died." Brida folded the letter, small, and held it in both hands like a warm thing. "And she never came. And he waited. And he kept the orchard, and gave the children pears, and every spring it blossomed like a bride, and he waited his whole life, and she never knew the door wasn't shut. She died thinking he'd cried off. He died knowing she hadn't and never able to tell her. And the cup —" She looked at the private shelf, at the red cup humming. "The cup knew the whole time. It's had the true thing in it the whole time. The love. It was never a lie in there. The lie was outside, in the village, in the wrong word — and inside the cup it was always true."

"That's it," Edda said softly. "That's the whole of it. That's what I couldn't do and what I can." She sat down slowly on the wedging stool. "I can't fire you the wedding, Brida. That never happened, and the fire won't make it, and it shouldn't. Your gran was right to ask and Ilsabet was right to refuse — a fired wedding would have been a lie, and it would have poisoned every true spring after. But listen to me now, because this is the thing I crossed thirty years of not-listening to hear." She leaned forward. "A love is not the wedding it was promised. They're two different things, and we muddle them because they mostly come together, but they're not the same, and the fire has always known the difference even when we don't. The wedding didn't happen. But the love happened. The love was as real as the laugh in your neighbour's cup, as real as a first snowfall, as real as anything I've ever kept — realer, for it lasted two whole lives, one on each side of a flood, and never once turned false. That's not a not-happening. That's one of the truest happenings this valley ever grew, and nobody ever got to hold it, because everybody was so busy grieving the wedding that never came that not one soul thought to keep the love that did."

Brida was very still. "You mean —"

"I mean the fire can't give you the wedding," Edda said, "but the fire can give you the love. It's had it thirty years, waiting. I can open that cup and let the true thing in it be heard — your gran's love, the full cup of it, the sweetest hour's work of Ilsabet's year — and I can take Elias's true word, his door-not-shut, out of that letter and off your telling of it, and hold it, and keep it; and I can fire them, the two true halves, into a cup you can carry home. Not a wedding that never was. A love that always was, told whole at last, both sides of the water in one vessel." She looked at the humming cup, and then at the girl. "Your family didn't inherit a not-happening, child. They inherited a happening that nobody had the heart to hand them. Thirty years everyone kept the empty place where the wedding should have been, and nobody kept the full place where the love was, and so all you had was the shape of the missing thing. Let me hand you the thing itself. Then you can stop inheriting the not-happening. You'll inherit the love instead. It was always the better inheritance. It was just never given."

"But how," said Perrin, who thought in clay the way other people thought in words, "do we hold two things at once and keep them whole? A give-back's one thing lifted. A carry's one thing brought. This is two — her love out of the old cup, his word off the letter — and they've to go into one vessel without one speaking over the other. That's near the mend, isn't it. Two tellings, level, neither drowning the other."

"It's near the mend and it isn't the mend," Edda said, pleased, because a question like that was the sound of a witch thinking. "The mend was two tellings of one broken thing — the dish and the race, the old telling in the ware and the fresh in the carrier, and we held the fire dead level so neither spoke over the other. This is different. This isn't two tellings of one thing quarrelling to be heard. It's two halves of one thing that have never once met — his side and hers, thirty years apart, never in a room together, not at the wedding that didn't happen nor any day since. They don't need levelling apart. They need bringing together. It's not a mend and it's not a give-back and it's not a flit and it's not a keep, not quite any of them alone — it's a keep that takes a give-back's opening and a carry's bringing and does with them a thing the four of them were always able to do and were never yet asked. There's no new fire in it, mind. Same four tempers, closed as they've always been. Only a new asking of them. That's the whole history of this craft, Perrin, if you want it in a sentence: we never learn a new fire. We only, now and then, learn a new thing to ask the old one."

She stood, and went to the shed door, and looked out at the wet spring dark, thinking it through the way she thought through every firing that mattered, in her hands before her head.

"And we prove it small first," she said, "the way we always do. The lesser-freight law: you never trust a new asking on the true piece cold. We'll throw a plain test tile, and put a small true thing in it — I'll ask Tally for the leave to use her little one's first-steps, it's fresh and it's whole and it's glad and it'll not be harmed by the trying — and we'll fire it the new way, a keeping brought and a keeping given-back set together in the one small clay, and if it comes out true and whole and neither half drowned, then the asking's sound and we trust it with the cup. If it comes out muddled, we've learned it on a test tile and not on Nesta Alder's whole heart, and we think again." She came back in. "That's the law, and it's a kind one. Nobody's grief is ever the thing we practise on."

Then there was the seam, which is the body a keeping is carried in, and Edda thought on that too, and knew it before she'd finished thinking it, the way you know the right word. "And the clay and the glaze want to be theirs," she said. "Not ours. A seam should belong to the telling. For the mend we ground the dish's own batch and burned the oar Wim caught the race on. For this —" She looked at Brida. "Applewood. Ash of applewood, off the orchard, off Elias's own trees — a branch the winter's brought down, there's always one, there's no need to cut a living limb. His trees, in the glaze. And the clay's the river's, same pit as every pot in this valley, but we'll temper the slip with a little of the ford sand — the crossing itself, the very gravel your gran couldn't cross and you did. His blossom, her crossing, the river between them, all three in the body of the cup before ever a keeping goes in. So the vessel's true before the fire's even lit. That matters more than folk think. A keeping carried in a stranger's clay never sits as easy as one carried home in its own."

The rain had stopped. Somewhere out in the wet dark a first frog had started up, the very first of the spring, absurd and hopeful. Perrin, at the shelf, said in his quiet way the thing that turned out to be the line — the way he'd said the humming, the way a witch says a true thing before he knows he's said it — "The fire keeps the love. Not the loss."

Edda looked at him a long moment. "Say that again."

"The fire keeps the love, not the loss," Perrin said, and heard himself, and coloured.

"Write it in the ninth ledger tonight," Edda said, "before you sleep, while it's warm. That's a witch's own line, that is. That's the fifth. *And what never happened, the fire cannot make — but a love is not the wedding it was promised, and the fire keeps the love, not the loss.*" She turned to Brida. "But it's a firing, and firings have their laws, and the first is we prove the seam small before we trust it large. Stay the week. The ford'll not fall before it and neither should we. There's a cup to throw and a fire to tune to the spring, and a village that's going to want to know why the witch is asking after old Elias Wick's orchard. Cross-back can wait for the water and the work both."

# Chapter Seven — The Throwing

A pot begins as a lump that could be anything, and a witch's whole art is knowing which nothing to leave out; and a first pot of a new firing is thrown slow, because the hands are asking a question and the clay is the only one who can answer.

They threw the cup on the third morning, when the sky had cleared to the high washed blue of a spring that has cried itself out and means to be cheerful now. Perrin threw it, at Edda's word — "you're a witch and it wants a witch's hands, and mine threw the last four; the craft's yours now too, and this one's better yours" — and Edda sat by and watched him ask the clay the question, and the clay, being spring clay and generous, answered kindly.

"Two hands or two mouths," Edda said, reading him Ilsabet's own line off the ledger open on the bench. "A loving-cup, footed, two-handled, the way she made the first. Not a copy — you can't copy a full cup and you shouldn't try; the first one's hers, Ilsabet's and Nesta's and Elias's, and it stays on the shelf and it stays theirs. This one's the family's. The love handed on, not the love moved. So: her shape, honoured, but your hand in it. Round enough for two to hold. Deep enough to keep a whole love and not rattle. And you'll leave your mark on the foot, where the fire finds it, because a witch signs the pot she throws for another; it's owed."

Perrin threw it round-shouldered and deep and true, two handles pulled and set so a person on each side could lift it together, and when it stood leather-hard on the board he took the two-rook mark that the Rooke brothers had lent his craft — Ansel's people's mark, the mark on his own first pot — and pressed it clean into the foot, and then, after a moment's thought, with the point of a trimming tool he added, round the shoulder where Ilsabet had pressed her line thirty years back, a running line of small marks of his own.

"Apple-blossom," he said, when Edda raised an eyebrow. "You said hers was blossom. It seemed —" He shrugged, colouring, a boy again for a second under the witch he'd become. "It seemed like the two cups should know each other. Across the thirty years. Blossom to blossom."

"It seemed exactly right," Edda said, and did not tell him how her throat had closed, because a swelled prentice is no use to anyone, and a swelled young witch even less.

They went up to the orchard the next morning for the seam, Edda and Brida and Perrin together, before the blossom was full out, when the trees stood in tight pink bud like a held breath. The orchard was quiet and half-wild, the grass long, the old trees leaning companionably the way old trees do when no one prunes them straight, and it was, Edda thought, a good place — a place a solitary man had made and tended forty years and left the better for his tending, whether he'd meant it as a monument or only as somewhere to put a grief that had to go somewhere. There was a branch down under the biggest pear, a good one, winter-broken and clean, no need to lay a knife to any living wood; and Perrin cut it into billets while Brida gathered the smaller fallen sticks, and they carried the applewood down the slope with the first bees blundering round them and the whole hill smelling of sap and cold grass and the coming white.

Back at the shed they burned it slow in the little glaze-kiln to a soft pale ash, and Edda washed the ash through water three times the way you do — the lye of it drawn off, the ash sweetened, so it would melt kind in the fire and not bite the glaze — and she ground it fine on the slab with a muller, and blended it into the base glaze, the iron celadon that would come up red-brown under reduction, so that the cup's colour, when it came, would be partly the colour of Elias Wick's own trees turned to fire. And the slip she tempered, as she'd said she would, with a pinch of the ford sand Perrin fetched up in a jar from the gleaming shallow crossing now the flood had begun to fall — fine river gravel, the very ground a bride couldn't cross thirty years back — worked into the clay body so the seam carried, in its own plain substance, his blossom and her crossing and the river that had stood between. Brida watched the whole of it, the burning and the washing and the grinding and the blending, and said, once, quietly, "You make the pot out of the story before you even put the story in it," and Edda said, "That's the half of it nobody sees, and it's the truer half. Anyone can fire a telling into any old clay. It'll keep. But a keeping wants a home that already knows it, the way a child sleeps sounder in a bed that smells of the house. We're building the cup a home before we ask the love to live in it."

The village got wind of it, the way the village got wind of everything, through the bakehouse. Edda had walked up to the clay-pit to ask Ansel who held Elias Wick's old orchard now, and Ansel — who had dug the man's grave and remembered him kind — had said the orchard was nobody's and everybody's, gone half wild, the parish let it stand for the blossom and the children's scrumping, and why did the witch want to know; and by the time Edda was home Maud Brock had it that the witch was asking after old Elias, and by evening the whole of Wenholt had turned the thing over, and the old ones had begun, one by one, to remember.

It was Gran Brock who remembered most. She came up the lane on Maud's arm, eighty-eight and bright as a robin, and sat in the good chair Brida gave up for her, and looked at the Stonebeck girl a long time, and said, "You've the Alder look. About the eyes. There was an Alder girl once, over the water, that Elias Wick was to have. Nobody speaks of it. I was a young wife. I remember the blossom that year, and no wedding under it, and Elias going quiet, quiet all his life after, and none of us ever knowing the right of it, only that the water'd been up." She reached out and took Brida's hand in her two old ones. "He gave my children pears every autumn of their lives. He gave your gran nothing, in the end, that anyone could see. And here you are, come across the water with the right of it thirty years on. Well." She patted the hand. "Well. That's a thing worth a flood."

# Chapter Eight — The Giving-Back

To give a thing back you must first be brave enough to hear it, and the hearing is the harder half; for a keeping given back is not a page read aloud but a life let into the room, and the room is never the same after.

The giving-back was set for the fourth night, before the firing, because the true thing in the humming cup had to be heard and held before it could be fired anew — you cannot pour from a cup you have not first lifted. Edda had done a give-back only once before that mattered, the honey jar, one midsummer Short Night; but this was spring, and the thing to be given back was not a shutting to be opened but a fullness to be, at last, received, and Edda found as she made ready that she was steadying her own hands.

"You don't have to be in the room," she told Brida. "It's your gran's whole heart, girl, the private of it, said to a witch thirty years before you were born. Some things a family's owed and some a family should let lie."

"She died with it never said," Brida said. "I'll not let it lie a second time. I'm the one that crossed the water. I'll hear it."

So they sat, the three of them, in the lamp-lit workshop with the good stove ticking, and Edda took the humming cup down off the shelf, and warmed it in her two hands the way you warm a thing you mean to ask a great favour of, and she opened it — not with fire, that night, but the way a give-back begins, with the witch making of herself a door and asking, gently, of the true thing kept: *say it back. To me. So it can be held.* And the cup, thirty years full, thirty years humming for an ear, gave it back.

It came into the room like weather. Edda had known it would be love and had not known it would be *this* love — the whole full cup of it, Nesta Alder at nineteen telling a witch her wanting of a man who talked too much: the way he'd waited for her at the Stonebeck market three weeks running pretending to want turnips he plainly had no use for, a Wenholt orchardman buying Stonebeck turnips by the armful just to stand at her mother's stall a quarter-hour and be laughed at; the way he'd walked her home the long way and named her every tree they passed and been wrong about half of them on purpose to hear her put him right; the day at the blossoming he'd asked her, not grand, not on one knee, but leaning on a gate with an apple-bough in his hand, whether she'd think about crossing the water for good and being a Wenholt wife, and how she'd said *I'll think about it* and thought about it for the length of one held breath and then said *I've thought,* and the whole orchard, she'd told Ilsabet, had seemed to lean in with them; the way he laughed when she laughed at him and never once, not one time in a year of courting, been small or sour about being the one laughed at, which she had known even at nineteen was the whole of a good man told in a single habit; the particular certainty, rare in the young and rarer kept, that this was the one, this exact talkative wrong-about-trees pear-growing man, and no other, world without end. It came in whole and true and thirty years fresh, because a thing the fire keeps does not age, and Edda held it, and let it fill the room, and across the stove Brida sat with the tears running plain and unwiped down her face and her mouth open on a soundless thing, because she was hearing, for the first and only time, her grandmother happy — young, and unshut, and wholly in love, before the flood, before the wrong word, before the long good married life that had a shut place in it none of them had ever been able to name.

"That's her," Brida said, when she could. "That's — I never met her. The girl. She was always my gran, she was always the window and the shut place. I never once met the girl who —" She pressed her hand to her mouth. "She was so *happy*. She was so happy and I never knew, I thought — we all thought the not-happening was the whole of it, the shame, the empty place, and all this time the cup had *this* in it. This. She was so happy."

"She was," Edda said, and her own voice was not steady. "That's what nobody kept. That's what everyone let leak away into the not-happening for thirty years, because the missing wedding was easier to feel than the present love. Hold it, child. That's your gran. That's the true one. That's the inheritance."

And the cup, its full true thing lifted at last and held and heard, went quiet in Edda's hands — not empty, for the love was still in it, and would stay, that being where it was fired; but quiet, the thirty-year hum gone down at last to a whole and restful silence, the silence of a thing that has finally been received. It did not hum any more. It never hummed again. It sat on the shelf ever after a plain red cup, and the young witches who came after would be told: that one used to hum, before it was heard.

# Chapter Nine — Water in the Fire

A spring firing is the hardest firing of the year, because the kiln has drunk the whole wet winter and must be taught, slowly and with great patience, to let the water go before it can be trusted with the fire; and there is no hurrying it, and the trying to is how you crack a kiln.

They proved the seam first, because the law is the law and a kind one. On the fifth afternoon Edda threw a plain test tile of the same tempered clay and, with Tally's glad leave, fired into it the little one's first-steps the new way — Tally's telling of it carried and held, set together in the one small clay with a scrap of a give-back Edda drew from Widow Sorrel's old jar-lid, the two brought and paired the way the cup's two halves would be. It was a small honest trial with nothing precious risked, and it came out of the little glaze-kiln the next dawn true and whole: the first-steps bright and unbowed, the given-back scrap beside it clear, neither drowning the other, two true things sitting easy in one tile. Edda turned it in the morning light a long moment and then set it down and said, "Sound. The asking holds. Now we can trust it with the cup," and Brida, who had not slept for the waiting, let out a breath she seemed to have been holding since the ford.

They fired on the sixth night. Edda had spent two days besides on the drying alone, small slow fires in the mouth of the kiln from dawn, coaxing the winter's damp out of the brick a degree at a time — because a kiln fired hard while it still held water will spall and crack, the trapped wet flashing to steam with nowhere to go, and the whole arch put at risk. "Water-smoking," she named it for Brida, who had never seen a firing and asked to understand all of it. "You can't drive the fire in till you've let the water out. Rush it and she cracks. It's the most patience the year asks of us, this — sitting a low fire two whole days doing next to nothing, letting the old wet leave slow, so the true fire, when it comes, comes to a kiln that can bear it." She'd smiled, feeding the small fire. "There's a whole sermon in it, if you're the sort that wants one. I mostly just feed the fire and let the sermon preach itself."

The village came, the way the village came to a firing that mattered — not to watch the fire, which is the witch's, but to keep the witch, which is theirs. Maud Brock brought bread, warm still and wrapped in a cloth, and argued about paying, and was never once square; and when Edda said, as she always said, that a firing's bread was owed the other way, Maud said, as she always said, that she'd take her owing out in being allowed to grumble, which was the truest coin the two of them had traded forty years. Hessa brought a crock of something hot from the Copper Kettle and set it on the shed sill and stayed to see the lighting, wiping her hands on her apron in the way that was Hessa's whole manner of caring. Odile Vance the reeve came up quiet after her rounds and stood at the back, off duty, a neighbour and not an office. Ansel came up from the clay-pit with Lena and the girls, Nell riding his shoulders half-asleep, and stood the way the village stood, quiet and near, so that the witch at the fire was never alone with it. Wilf and Cobb stood by the wood-stack passing the barrow between them without being asked. And they had, that spring night, a thing they had not had at a firing before: a reason to speak of Elias Wick, and they spoke of him, low, around the warm dark, the way you speak of the good dead — the pears he'd press on the children by the coatful every autumn; the way he'd mend a gate for you and not stay to be thanked; the quiet of him; the orchard that blossomed like a bride whether he tended it or no. And it came out, in the speaking, the way things come out around a fire, that half of them had loved the old man in the small daily way you love a fixture of a place, without ever once wondering what a man so gentle and so alone might be carrying alone; and the not-having-wondered shamed them a little, kindly, the way a village is now and then rightly shamed by its own dead; and they were glad, this spring, to know at last, and to be, tonight, keeping the witch who was keeping his love for him thirty years too late and not one hour too late for it to matter.

Brida, at Edda's word, walked down to the ferry at dusk, before the lighting, to ask Wim whether the ford would fall by week's end so she could cross back with the cup; and it was there, at the ferry-house window where the two lamps stood — the porridge bowl and the mended dish, two lamps in one window, minding their mornings for a woman who mostly could not — that she met Griet, on one of her clear evenings.

Griet knew her at once, which she did not always do with anyone. "You're the girl that asked," she said, bright and wandering and, for that little while, entirely herself. "Weeks and weeks back. Before the frost. You asked did the witch keep a cup that hummed, and you went pale, and you didn't cross, and off you went. I said cross and ask her yourself." She looked at Brida with her clear present eyes. "And you crossed, this time. In the flood, no less. Wim says. Well done, girl. It's the crossing that's the whole of it. Everything else waits, if you'll only cross the water." She turned back to her two lamps, gently, the tide going out kind. "There was a wedding once that a flood stopped. Long back. Somebody didn't cross." A pause, drifting. "You crossed, though. You tell your witch. Somebody finally crossed."

# Chapter Ten — The Fire Keeps the Love

There comes an hour in every true firing when the witch stops feeding the kiln and starts feeding the keeping — when the fire is right and the clay is ready and the thing held in the witch begins, through her hands on the wood, to go into the ware; and it is the hour a witch is for, and no one has ever found the words for it, and every witch dies still trying.

The kiln took the fire kindly, the water two days gone out of her, the arch sound. Edda brought it up through the spring dark with Perrin reading the cone-packs beside her — the two of them at the fire now, which was still so new that Edda kept feeling the wonder of it under the work, *there are two of us, I am not the last* — feeding the firebox in the long steady rhythm that is more like breathing than like work, watching the ware go from dull to red to the orange that lies to you to the clean straw-gold that does not; and the applewood in the glaze began, somewhere past the red, to do its slow work, the ash melting and running its faint warm veils down the shoulder of the cup where a boy had pressed a line of blossom, so that the vessel took its colour, as Edda had promised, partly from the fire and partly from Elias Wick's own trees. When the fire stood right — the cones bowing true, the reduction pulled just so, the box breathing that deep even gold that a witch feels in her chest before she reads it with her eyes — Edda set her hands to the stoke-wood and began to give the keeping into the cup.

She did not fire the wedding. She had told Brida she would not and she did not, and it was the not-doing that made the firing true. There was no wedding to fire; the wedding had never happened; and a witch who fires what never happened fires a lie, and Edda Marren had kept a true kiln fifty years and would keep it true to the last. What she fired was the love — the two true halves of it, held in her whole. Out of the given-back cup, Nesta's full young wanting, the sweetest hour's work of a dead witch's year. And out of Elias's letter, carried on Brida's true telling of it and Edda's own holding, the man's whole steady answer: *I never believed it; the door was never shut; I'll wait my life.* Two true things, one on each side of a flood, thirty years apart and never once made false — and Edda fed them into the fire together, and the fire, which keeps the true, took them, and kept them, and made of the new red cup a thing that held not a wedding that never was but a love that always had been, told whole at last, both banks of the water in a single vessel a family could lift with two hands.

She felt the moment it took — every witch does, the small settling, like a key turning true in a lock, the two halves coming home to one another in the clay with an ease that told her, surer than any cone, that the asking had been a right one: that these two true things had never wanted to be apart, had only been kept apart, and given the one warm vessel and the one true fire had come together the way water finds its level, without her having to force a hair of it. That was how you knew a firing was true and not forced — that at the last it did more of itself than you did, and all your craft turned out to have been only the making of a place clean enough and warm enough and honest enough for a thing to happen that had always been trying to. She took her hands off the wood and stood in the fierce warm dark and found her face wet and did not mind. Perrin, beside her, was reading the fire still, holding it steady on its long slow soak so the glaze would mature and the keeping set deep and not shallow, but his eyes had gone bright too, and after a while he said, low, the words he'd written three nights back in the ninth ledger and would carry now his whole life:

"*And what never happened, the fire cannot make — but a love is not the wedding it was promised, and the fire keeps the love, not the loss.*"

"Say it back," Edda said, because that was the rule, older than either of them.

And he said it back, and she said it with him the second time, the two witches of Wenholt at the true hour of a spring firing, and outside the shed the village kept them, and down at the ferry two lamps stood in a window, and across the water a girl's grandmother, thirty years dead to the whole truth of her own best year, had it kept for her at last in clay, by a fire, on the far side of the flood she never crossed.

# Chapter Eleven — The Cup Carried Home

The best of a firing is the morning after, the slow cool, the clamming-up unclosed at last and the ware come out still warm; and the best of that best is when the thing you hoped for is truer in the hand than it was in the hope.

They clammed up the kiln at dawn, luting the spy-holes and the door with wet clay so the cooling would come slow — because a keeping cooled too fast can craze and a crazed keeping is a keeping half-heard ever after — and then there was the waiting, which is the last patience a firing asks and in some ways the hardest, a whole day of a shut warm kiln and nothing to do but trust it. Brida spent it walking the lanes of a village that was, she found, no longer quite strange to her; Perrin spent it in the ninth ledger, writing up the firing in the plain close hand he was still growing into; and Edda spent it as she spent every such day, doing small unnecessary tasks near the kiln, the way you sit near a sleeping child you have no need to watch.

They opened it at the cool of the next afternoon, when it had come down slow and safe the way Edda made it come down, unhurried, the heat let go as patiently as the water had been let go. She broke the clammed door away herself and reached in, and there in the middle where the fire ran truest stood the new cup, round-shouldered and two-handled, its glaze come out the deep warm red of an iron celadon under a good reduction, the applewood ash run down its shoulder in faint gold veils over the pressed line of blossom — the very red, Brida saw at once, the very red of the old cup on the shelf, blossom-line to blossom-line, the two of them knowing each other across the thirty years exactly as Perrin had meant, so that anyone setting them side by side would say at once, *a pair; those two were always a pair.*

Edda took it out still warm and held it a moment and then, because it was not hers and had never been, put it into Brida's two hands.

"Lift it and hold it," she said. "Properly. The whole of it. It's your gran, and the man your gran loved, and the love that was true on both banks the whole time and nobody kept — until now. It's not the wedding. I'll say that till you're sick of it, because it matters: it is not the wedding, that never happened and never will and shouldn't. It's the love. And a love is the better keeping anyway, for a wedding's a day and a love's a life, or in this one two lives, one each side of a flood." She closed the girl's hands round the warm cup. "Take it home over the water. Set it where your family sets its true things. And when the little ones ask — and they will, children always ask after the warm cup — you tell them the true story. Not the not-happening. Not the shame and the shut place and the empty chair. The love. You tell them your gran was so happy once, the spring the blossom came, that a witch fired it whole thirty years on so they could hold it. That's the inheritance now. You crossed the water and changed what your family inherits. There's not a queen in any story did more."

Brida held the cup, and could not speak for a while, and then said the thing that undid them all, which was small, the way the undoing things always are: "She'll be at the window still, in my head. My gran. I can't unmake that. But now when I see her at the window looking at the water — I'll know she wasn't only grieving. She was remembering being happy. That's — I can live beside that. I couldn't live beside the other. I can live my whole life beside that."

They did not return the old cup to the clay. Edda had thought, in Book-Four habit, that perhaps she should — hand it on, the way she'd handed on Ilsabet's first pot; but Brida, wise now the way the well-grieved are wise, said no, leave it, and said why, and was right. "It was safe here the whole time," she said, standing before the private shelf where the red cup sat quiet at last among the shelf's other resolved and honest griefs. "Thirty years my family thought there was nothing but a not-happening, and all that while the true thing was here, kept safe, humming away for an ear. That's the mercy, isn't it. Not that it's given back. That it was never lost. Leave it here. Let it be the proof, for whoever comes after, that a true thing kept doesn't leak, however long the not-happening lasts. I've the love to carry home. You keep the cup that kept it. That's — that's the right shape."

"That's the right shape," Edda agreed, and it was, and the stilled red cup stayed on the shelf, one more of the workshop's small resolved mercies: the lid laid ajar, the bare board where a dish went home, the bare board where a first pot went back to the clay, and now the cup that used to hum, and hummed no more, because it had at last been heard.

Elias's letter, thirty years late, Brida carried too — folded soft, to be read once at the orchard before she crossed, and then to go home over the water with the cup, so that both true halves went where the family was, and Wenholt kept only what Wenholt had always kept: the love, safe on the shelf, and the memory of a quiet man who gave the children pears.

# Chapter Twelve — The Crossing Back

Everyone in Wenholt knows the rule, and the newest witch knows the newest line of it; but the oldest thing the craft knows, older than the kiln and the catechism both, is that the water always falls in the end, and the brave always cross, and the fire's whole work is only to keep warm what the crossing was for.

The ford fell by week's end, as Wim had said it would, the brown flood shouldering itself away downstream and the gravel crossing coming up gleaming and shallow, so that Brida could have waded it; but she went by the ferry all the same, on a bright blossom-blue morning, because it was Wim's boat she'd come over in with her heart in her mouth, and it seemed right to go home the same way, changed.

She went first up the south slope to Elias Wick's orchard, and Edda and Perrin walked her there, and half the village drifted up after in the way a village does, so that it became, without anyone deciding it, a small glad procession through a spring morning. The orchard had come into its blossom — every tree of it, apple and pear, gone white and pink and humming with the first bees, blossoming like a bride whether the man who planted it tended it or no, exactly as it had every spring of thirty years and would every spring after. Brida stood under it with her grandmother's love warm in the new cup in her two hands, and read her grandfather-that-never-was his own letter, quietly, once, aloud, under the blossom he'd asked a witch to press round a cup so a wedding could be married under it. Nobody spoke. Gran Brock had a hand on Maud's arm and her old face turned up into the white; Ansel had lifted Nell down and stood holding her; Hessa had her apron twisted in her hands. It was not a sad thing, quite, though there was sorrow in it — it was more the shape a village makes when it is being, for a little while, better than its ordinary self, gathered under a dead man's trees to hear read aloud the love he never got to give and never once took back. And if a quiet man six years in the churchyard can be said to hear a thing at last, then Elias Wick heard it, that spring morning, under his own white trees, thirty years late and not one word too late for the only thing that had ever mattered, which was that she'd known, in the end. She'd known the door was never shut. Somebody had finally crossed the water and told him she'd know.

Then Edda did a small thing that was not in any plan. She had brought the test tile in her apron pocket — the plain one with Tally's first-steps in it, that had proved the seam sound — and she knelt at the foot of the biggest pear and pressed it a little way into the soft spring earth among the roots, face up to the blossom, and left it there. "For the orchard," she said, when Brida looked a question at her. "A first walking, at the foot of the trees a man waited his whole life under. It's a glad thing, and it wants a glad place, and this is the gladdest ground in the parish whether the man who made it ever knew it or no. Let the orchard have a keeping of its own. It's owed one, I think, more than most ground is." And they left it there, a small warm true thing in the roots of the blossoming trees, and went down to the water.

Then Brida crossed back. She stood at the landing a moment first, the new cup wrapped in a cloth in the crook of her arm, and looked at Edda, and could not find whatever the words were meant to be, and gave up on them, which was right, because there weren't any. "You crossed a flood for a no," Edda told her, "and you're going home over quiet water with the truest yes this valley ever kept. That's a fair week's work for a girl of one-and-twenty. Go on. Your people are on the bank." And Brida laughed, wet-eyed, and got into the boat.

Wim rowed her, unhurried, the way Wim did everything, and the whole small procession stood on the Wenholt bank and saw her over; and on the Stonebeck side other figures had gathered, family, the far-side kin come down to the landing to meet the girl who'd crossed the flood and come home with the thing three years and thirty had been waiting for. Edda watched the little boat go over the fallen quiet water, one figure in it and one at the oars and a cloth-wrapped cup held careful against a girl's heart, and thought that it was the crossing a grandmother could not make thirty years gone, made whole at last by a granddaughter on a fair blossom morning — the wedding-crossing that the flood had stopped, completed a generation late, not for a wedding now, for there was no wedding and never would be, but for the truer thing that had been under the wedding all along and had outlasted it by two whole lives: carried home, at last, in a cup, to the side of the water it had always belonged on.

⁂

That evening, the workshop quiet and the village gone home and the two lamps lit down at the ferry, Perrin came in from banking the kiln with something turning in his face that Edda was learning, still, to read — the knowing that comes first, before the understanding limps after.

"Brida left word," he said. "With Wim, to bring up. About the Stonebeck side." He sat. "She says — clearing her gran's house, she found more than the letter. She found that Stonebeck had a kiln once. Long back. A witch of its own, or something near a witch, before either of our times — and the kiln's cold now, fallen, thirty years and more, nobody left over the water who kept the craft. And there's a pot, she says. One pot, come down in her family, that her gran always said held something — a keeping, a true one, from the old Stonebeck fire — only there's been nobody on that side to hear it in a lifetime, and it's sat in a cupboard all these years, a full thing with no ear, the way our cup sat, only longer, and colder, and with the fire that made it dead these thirty years." He looked at Edda. "She wants to know — someday, she says, no hurry, when the water's low and the year's kind — whether a fire on this side could ever hear what a dead fire on that side kept. Whether a keeping can wait longer than the witch who made it. Whether we could cross the water the other way, for once, and wake a thing that's had no witch to hear it in a lifetime."

Edda was quiet a while. She looked at the private shelf, at the red cup sitting stilled and honest at the end where a first pot used to be, and she turned her own crooked cup a quarter, slow, the one that smelled of peas and summer, and this time she let it turn all the way round.

"Well," she said. "There's a thing the fire's never been asked, and it's a fair one. We've kept, and given back, and mended, and handed on, and now kept a love the loss near buried — but a keeping in a cold house on the far side, made by a fire long dead, with no witch in a lifetime to hear it?" She shook her head slowly, but it was the shake that means *I don't yet see how* and not the shake that means *no*. "I don't know if a fire this side can hear what a dead fire that side kept. I don't know that a keeping keeps that long, alone, unheard, with the maker's fire gone cold. But she'll ask again, that girl, some low-water year. They always cross in the end, the brave ones — she's proved that once already. And when she does, there'll be two of us, now, and one of us young enough to be the one to cross the *other* way, over the water, and go and listen at a cold far kiln." She looked at Perrin, and something in her look handed him a thing, lightly, the way she'd handed him the small pot at Wintermark. "That'd be a young witch's crossing, I think. Not an old one's."

Perrin said nothing, but his eyes had the sideways attention in them, already listening, across the water, to a cold kiln thirty years silent.

"But that's a summer story," Edda said, "and this is still the tender green of the spring, and there's a firing to be paid for and bread owed to Maud that we'll never once be square on. Wash the dust off. Bank the stove. And say it back to me."

And Perrin Loft, witch of Wenholt, second of the two lamps now lit in the valley, said it back — the whole of it, word for word, for the witches after:

*The kiln keeps. It does not take. Say it back.*

*And what it keeps, asked kindly, it can give back.*

*And what breaks, told together, it can mend.*

*And what one hand throws, another hand fills — for a first pot is not one you fill alone.*

*And what never happened, the fire cannot make — but a love is not the wedding it was promised, and the fire keeps the love, not the loss.*

And across the water, in a cold cupboard in a fallen house in Stonebeck, a single pot sat full and unheard as it had sat a lifetime, waiting the way kept things wait, patient past the death of the fire that made it, for somebody brave enough to cross the water the other way and ask; and up the lane the lamp stood in the workshop window, which was the best of it, the waiting; and on the shelf a red cup sat quiet that had hummed thirty years and hummed no more; and the kiln kept what it was given, and did not take, and would go on keeping, now, with two to keep it, for a good long while yet.

THE END

## About the series

*The Spring Cup* is the fifth and final Night Kiln novella, and it completes the five-book arc begun in *The Night Kiln*. Book by book, the fire of Wenholt is asked to do a new thing with an old craft — to *keep*, to *give back*, to *mend*, to *hand on*, and at last to keep *the love, not the loss*. With Perrin Loft made a witch and two lamps now lit at the kiln, the arc that opened with one old woman afraid she was the last of her line closes here, in the spring, with the crossing a grandmother could not make completed at last by a braver granddaughter.

There is no sixth book waiting at the end of this one: the five-book cycle is complete. If you have just closed the whole series — thank you for keeping the witch company from Night to Spring.

And if *The Spring Cup* is the first Wenholt book you have read, you can begin the arc from the beginning with **Book One, *The Night Kiln***, and watch the craft, and its catechism, grow line by line to the fifth.
