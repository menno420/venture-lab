## Description (KDP book description — plain text, paste as-is)

The winter is over, the ford is under a wagon's depth of racing water — and a girl who has stood on the far bank every spring for three years, working up the courage to cross, finally gets into the boat.

Edda Marren has kept the night kiln of Wenholt for a lifetime, firing her neighbours' memories into mugs and bowls and butter crocks so a grandmother's laugh or a child's first steps can stay warm in the room long after the moment is gone. Now, for the first time, there are two witches at the kiln — and a caller with a question neither of them has ever been asked.

Brida has crossed a flooded river to ask whether the fire can fire a wedding that never happened: her grandmother's wedding, called off thirty years ago, that left her whole family inheriting a shape where a happiness should have been. Edda cannot do it — the fire keeps the true, and will not counterfeit a thing that never was. But at the sound of the grandmother's name, an old cup on the witch's shelf begins to hum louder than it has in thirty years, and Edda realises she has spent her whole life calling a full thing empty.

The Spring Cup is the fifth Night Kiln novella: a warm, low-stakes, big-hearted comfort read about a love that outlasted the wedding it was promised, a lost letter kept thirty years too long, an orchard that blossoms for a man who waited his whole life, and the crossing a grandmother could not make, completed at last by a braver granddaughter. Contains grief and long-past sorrow handled with care; no violence, no explicit content. Stands alone; warmer still after Books 1–4 — and the close of the five-book arc for readers who have come the whole way from Night to Spring.
