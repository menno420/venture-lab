## Description

(Plain text for the KDP description field — no markdown headers. KDP allows basic
formatting; italics/bold may be applied in the KDP editor if desired. ~250 words;
trim the closing series paragraph first if a shorter description is wanted.)

In the deep of the year, a frost walks into the valley and puts its cold hand on everything — and finds the one flaw in a sixty-year-old pot no one has ever dared open.

Edda Marren has kept the night kiln of Wenholt for a lifetime: firing her neighbours' memories into mugs and bowls and butter crocks, so a grandmother's laugh or a first snowfall can stay warm in the room long after the rememberer cannot. On her private shelf, oldest of everything there, stands her late teacher's very first pot — thumb-marked, honest, and never once heard. When the midwinter frost cracks it, the keeping inside begins, quietly, to leak away.

Edda has six days to Wintermark and two wrong answers: honour the silence and lose the treasure, or break a rule older than the kiln to save it. And her one apprentice — old enough now to throw truer than she can, young enough to lie awake certain he has no grief big enough to make him a witch — is asking the very question the dying pot could answer, if only someone were brave enough to listen in time.

The Winter Wheel is the fourth Night Kiln novella: a warm, low-stakes, big-hearted comfort read about the craft of keeping, the mercy at the root of a family trade, and the quiet turning of a wheel from one pair of hands to the next. Contains grief and gentle bereavement handled with care; no violence, no explicit content. Stands alone; sweeter still after Books 1–3.
