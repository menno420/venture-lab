> Everyone in Wenholt knows the rule: bring Edda a memory before moonrise, and she will fire it into clay you can hold. But on the witch's private shelf, oldest of everything there, stands Grams Ilsabet's first pot — thumb-marked and honest, and never once opened — and the killing frost has just put a whisker of a crack in it. Whatever Edda's dead teacher kept in the first pot she ever made will be gone by spring unless the fire is asked, this deep midwinter, to do the last thing it has never done: carry a keeping from a dying vessel into a new one thrown by a hand that isn't yet a witch. What did Grams Ilsabet keep in her own first pot? It is a winter story, and it is time it was told.

# Chapter One — The Deep of the Year

The rule in Wenholt was older than anyone who kept it, and it was this: if you wanted a memory fired, you brought it to Edda Marren before moonrise, because the kiln burned at night or not at all. You came up the witch's lane with your year in your hands; you sat in the good chair by the workshop stove with your fingers around something warm, and you told the memory out loud — properly, the whole of it — and the telling went into the witch, and bided in her, and on the firing night the fire drew it out of her, through her hands on the stoke-wood, into the waiting clay, so that ever after the memory lived in a mug or a bowl or a butter crock: a thing warm past what any cooling explained, a thing that could stand in the room when the rememberer no longer could. The kiln keeps. It does not take. Say it back. Four generations of Wenholt witches had said it like a catechism, and in three short years the catechism had grown two lines more — *what it keeps, asked kindly, it can give back; what breaks, told together, it can mend* — taught to the fire and the village together, for the witches after.

And there would need to be witches after. That was the thing sitting under Edda's breastbone all that deep winter, heavier than any telling, because it was not a telling at all; it was the plain arithmetic of a woman past fifty in a trade with one apprentice, in the coldest turn of the year, with the frost coming down like a landlord.

It came down that first week of Wintermonth as it had not come in twenty years. Not the soft feathering frost of book-keepers and poets — the other kind, the iron kind, the frost that walked into a valley at dusk and put its hand flat on everything and pressed. The Ler skinned over in the slack water by the ferry landing and then, over three still nights, roofed itself bank to bank so that Old Wim tied his boat up and took to the ice with a pole and a lantern, ferrying by foot, which he claimed to remember his grandfather doing and to have been waiting his whole life to do himself. The wells rang short. The Copper Kettle's pump froze at the neck and Hessa thawed it every dawn with a kettle of its own boiled water, muttering, which was Hessa's way of praying. In the workshop the water in the slop-bucket wore a lid of ice by morning that Perrin broke with the heel of his hand and never mentioned, because he had learned that year that not-mentioning was half the craft.

Edda felt it in the clay first, as she felt most things first. The pit-clay behind the woodshed, weathered a full year and usually so kindly by midwinter, had gone sullen and short under her palms at the wedging bench, its water half-locked, fighting the spiral the way it had not fought her since the wet spring she buried her teacher. She leaned into it anyway, heel of one hand rolling the mass, folding the cut face over on itself until it showed smooth as cheese; and she thought, not for the first time and not idly, that clay in a hard frost was a fair picture of a village in one — everything in it drawn tight and slow and holding, and the whole craft, in weather like this, a matter of patience and a warm room and not asking more of a cold body than it could give.

"Two before moonrise," Perrin reported, coming in from the gate with the cold rolling off his coat and a jug of Maud Brock's milk gone half to slush in his hand. He set it by the stove to remember what it was. He was seventeen that winter, and had stopped being made of elbows somewhere in the summer of the ferryman's dish, so that the workshop was still getting used to the new long reach of him, the way you get used to a doorway that has grown a lintel. "The Askews' little one's minder, for the naming-bowl's mate — she wants his first word in a cup, before he learns a second and she can't hear the first any more. And Cobb, with a carrying down from the Stonebeck road."

"In the frost? Cobb came over the hill in this?"

"Says the memory wouldn't cart in spring and he'd not trust it to thaw." Perrin turned the milk-jug a quarter, to bring its cold side to the heat, and did not notice he had done the winding gesture, which Edda noticed and kept. "He's got his hat in his fists already and he's still in the lane."

"Then put the kettle on and let him in," Edda said, "before the good chair freezes to the notion that nobody's coming."

So the evening went the way the good evenings went, which was slowly. The Askews' minder told a first word — *duck*, roared with total conviction at a hen, in the manner of the very young, who name the world boldly and are usually about right — and Edda took it into herself, the small bright absurdity of it settling under her breastbone like a swallow of tea a shade too hot, and the slip in the bowl went a warm downy yellow. Then Cobb, who never told his own and priced other folk's memories as the gravest cartage in the parish, sat with his eyes shut and his hat crushed to a wad and gave, word for word off a Stonebeck doorstep, an old man's memory of his wife threading a needle by firelight, the particular tilt of a head that had done a thing forty thousand times and would not do it many more; and that one went in cool and steady and deep, and the slip turned the grey of ash with a thread of something warmer under it, and Edda sat a moment after with her hand flat on the bowl, because some tellings you thanked before you shelved them.

She carried them both to the telling shelf — the old warped board of black oak set, not by accident, at exactly the height of her heart, because it had been set at the height of Grams Ilsabet's heart and the two women had been of a size. Two bowls stood on it now where nine could stand. Grams Ilsabet had held that nine tellings was the most any witch should carry into a single night, and had made Edda say the reasons back like catechism: *because you are a channel and not a cistern; because the kiln keeps, Edda, it does not take.* Two bowls, on the eve of the year's great firing, was thin. It should have been a shelf near full by now, Wintermark six days off. But nobody carted tellings up an iced lane if they could hold them a week more in their own warm chests, and the whole village was hunched into the frost waiting for it to break, and Edda let it wait, and banked the stove, and did not yet let herself think the thought that was standing in the cold corner of the workshop, patient as a customer, where the private shelf was.

She thought it that night anyway, the way you always did, lying awake listening to the frost tick in the beams like a slow clock: *six days to the biggest firing of the year, and a boy who can throw truer than I could at his age and has never once fired a keeping of his own.* And under that, colder: *and I am the only witch in Wenholt, and I am one hard winter from being an old one.*

The moon came up over the ridge, small and white and very far, the way it did in a killing frost, like a lantern somebody had carried into the next parish. Edda watched it a while from the workshop window, and then went to bed, because there was no telling to carry and no fire that night, and a witch with nothing to hold should sleep, which is a rule she had taught others and mostly failed to keep herself.

# Chapter Two — What a Witch Is Made Of

There is a moment in every apprenticeship the master dreads, which is the moment the apprentice becomes able and starts wondering, out loud, whether they are enough.

It came for Perrin over the wedging bench, two mornings into the frost, in the flat cold light that makes people honest before noon.

"Wilf Brock asked me at the smithy," he said, working his own clay, which no longer chased around the bench like a cat after a beetle but sat down and did as it was told, "when I'm to be made. A witch, I mean. He asked it kind. His gran's got the mug, and Tally's got her cup, and he said — he said the whole village reckons on the kiln lasting, and it can't last on you alone, mistress, no offence, and I said none taken and stood there like a post." He turned the clay, folded it. "And then I couldn't sleep for it. Because I don't know how it's done. Being made. I've thrown pots since I was fifteen and fired an inkwell that's mine, and I've taken a telling and carried it, that summer of the jar, and I know the glazes and the cones and I can nearly read the fire now without the cones at all. But there's a thing I've never done and nobody's ever told me the shape of, and it's the one that matters, isn't it. I've never fired a keeping. Been the channel myself, at the fire, drawn a thing out of me into clay and made it stay. That's the pot that makes a witch. My first pot. And I don't —" the clay stopped moving "— I don't know what I'm supposed to put in it."

Edda kept wedging, because a thing said hard is easier heard by a bent back than a watching face. "Go on. You've thought about it or you'd not have lost sleep. What's your trouble with it?"

"That it has to be enough." It came out of him in a rush, then, the whole tangle. "Every firing that ever mattered in this workshop was a grief. The keystone was a man's dead brother. The jar was a drowned family. The dish was fifty years and a woman who's going where Griet's going. The great ones are all the heavy ones, mistress, I've watched you carry every one and it's near broke you twice, and a first pot's meant to be the truest firing a witch ever does, the one that proves them — so it has to be a grief, doesn't it, a real one, deep enough to prove I can bear it. And I've looked. That's the shameful part. I've gone round my own life these two nights like a man going round a barn looking for something big enough to lift, some grief worthy of the pot, and I haven't got one. My gran's alive. My mother's alive and vexing. Nobody's drowned. I've had a good, plain, lucky life, and I've been going round it half-ashamed there's nothing in it heavy enough to make me a witch, and that's — that's a terrible thing to catch yourself wanting, mistress. To go wishing for a sorrow so you'll have something worthy to fire."

The workshop was very quiet. Out at the gate the frost held the lane like a held breath.

Edda set down her clay. She had known this was coming — had known it, if she was honest, since the summer the boy first went sideways-quiet at the private shelf and named a laugh from before his birth — but she had not known it would come out so whole, or catch so exactly on the one nail she had no clean answer for, because it was a nail she had left in the wall herself, years back, and walked past ten thousand times.

"Wash your hands and come sit by the stove," she said. "That's not a wedging-bench question."

He came. She put tea in his hands because they were troubled and empty, and she sat in her own chair with the crooked cup that smelled of peas and summer, and she took a while about it, because he deserved the true answer and she was some way from sure she had it.

"You've caught the craft doing a dishonest thing," she said at last, "and the dishonest thing is mine, not yours, and it's older than you. Listen. You're right that the firings you've watched were the heavy ones. That's because the heavy ones are the ones people can't carry alone, so they bring them to me, and the years you've been here have run heavy — the mason, the jar, the ferry. But you've been in this workshop through five hundred firings you've forgotten, boy, because they were light. A first word roared at a hen. A grandmother's laugh. The smell of the first pippins. A man's flat singing on a Sunday. You carried Maud's butter crock to the table yourself and thought nothing of it. The kiln does not keep only grief. It keeps whatever's worth having in the room later, and most of what's worth having in the room later, Perrin, is not grief. It's Tuesday. It's a good ordinary morning you'd give your teeth to stand in one more hour."

"Then why does the first pot have to —"

"It doesn't. That's the dishonest thing. Somewhere I let you believe — the whole village half-believes it, the way villages love a thing to be solemn — that a witch is proved by the weight she can lift, that the first pot's a test of strength, the heaviest thing you can carry and not drop. And I've never once corrected it, because I didn't have the correcting of it. My own first pot —" she stopped, and turned the crooked cup a quarter, and caught herself doing the winding gesture, and set it down flat, which is a thing she did perhaps twice a year "— my own first pot I chose myself, and it was a grief, because I was made a witch by my teacher dying, and there was nothing light anywhere near me the year I came into it. So I've taught you the shape of my own making without ever meaning to, and my making was a hard road, and I'm sorry for it. There should be another shape. I think there was, once, and I think I've forgotten it, or was never told." She looked into the crooked cup as if the answer might be at the bottom of it, in the pea-leaves. "There's a thing I've never let myself wonder about, boy, and you've just walked me straight up to it."

Perrin, who was going to be a witch someday whatever he currently believed, and who had the terrible directness that was half the reason she'd kept him, said: "The first pot. On the shelf. Grams Ilsabet's."

"Grams Ilsabet's," Edda agreed.

"You've never told me what's in it. Everything else on that shelf I know now — the lid, the cup that hums, where the dish stood. But not that. You always say it's a winter story." He was very still, and his eyes had the sideways attention in them, though the pot was in the next dark corner and he was not looking at it. "It's winter, mistress."

"It is," said Edda, "the deep of it." And she did not say the rest, which was that in fifty years of the trade she had never once taken that pot down and listened to it, because a telling is private as a grave, and a teacher's most private of all, and there are doors a good apprentice does not open on her master even after the master is thirty years in the ground. She had dusted it the way you tend a grave. She had never asked it a thing. And now a boy who could throw truer than she ever had was asking her what the first pot a witch ever made was for, and she found she did not know — did not know what her own teacher had kept, or why, or what it might have taught them both if either of them had ever had the nerve to hear it.

"Finish your tea," she said. "We've the Askews' cup to glaze and Cobb's needle-memory to bed down before the light goes. The winter story will keep. It's kept sixty years; it'll keep till the frost decides what it's doing."

The frost decided that night. It decided against her.

# Chapter Three — The Whisker

She found it the next morning, dusting, because dusting the private shelf had become Perrin's office and the boy was out splitting kindling, and Edda had drifted to the dark corner with a cloth the way you drift to a window when you are not ready to answer a letter.

The private shelf stood behind the glaze jars, a plain board that no customer ever saw: the workshop's own museum of the craft's mistakes and mercies. It held less than it once had, and Edda counted its emptying now the way you count grey hairs, with a rue that was mostly gratitude. There was the ground-smooth lid of the honey jar, laid slightly open beside the hand's-width of board it had haunted for seventy years — no longer the darkest exhibit but the brightest, the door left ajar, the whole tale of the emptying-that-was-given-back written in two pieces of clay for anyone who could read them. There was the clean bare board a little along where the dunted race-dish had stood twelve years and stood no longer, gone home to a window at the ferry to be a lamp beside a lamp. There was the wedding cup that hummed — fired for a wedding that had never, in the end, happened, unclaimed these thirty years and more, still faintly warm with a hope that had outlived its occasion, which was why Edda had never been able to bring herself to break it or to ask it whose hope it was.

And at the end, alone, oldest of everything there, with a little space around it as though even the other pots gave it room: Grams Ilsabet's first pot.

It was a homely thing, and that was the whole charm of it. A small round-shouldered pot, no bigger than two fists, thrown by someone who could not yet throw — you could see the girl in it, the walls uneven, one side pulled thin where a young thumb had leaned too hard and left its print baked in for sixty years, the foot trimmed crooked, the whole thing off its own centre by a hair so that it sat on the board with the faint list of a boat at a mooring. It had a plain wood-ash glaze gone the colour of weak honey, streaked where the ash had run, and it had no lid and had never had one, and it was, by any measure a potter would use, a bad pot. Ilsabet had kept it at the shelf's end all her life and never said why, and Edda had kept it there after her, and dusted it, and never asked.

She was dusting it now, the cloth going round the round shoulder, when she felt the cold come off it.

Not the ordinary cold of a thing in a cold room. A different cold, a wrong cold, a draught where no draught should be — the particular breath of an emptiness that has started to leak. Edda's hand went still. Then she turned the pot to the window light, slow, the way you turn an ailing thing, and she found it: across the thin-pulled side, straight through the girl's own thumbprint, fine as a cat's whisker at its ends and hair-wide at its centre, a crack. Old clay, thin-walled, sixty years in a dry corner, and then a frost that walked into the valley and put its flat hand on everything and pressed. Somewhere in the killing nights the water the old glaze had never quite given up, the ghost of moisture that lives in the oldest fired clay, had found the flaw where a young thumb once leaned, and frozen, and swelled, and opened its eye.

And out of the eye, when she brought it close, came that wrong cold, and under the wrong cold — very faint, going, the way a fire is going when you come down in the morning and find one coal — the sense of something kept, and starting not to be.

Edda sat down on the stool by the glaze jars with the pot in both hands and her heart going hard, because she had seen this once before, from the other side, and she knew exactly what it was and exactly how long it took.

A keeping lives in fired clay the way water lives in a sound jug: held by the whole vessel, every part of it leaning on every other part, the fire's own bond around it like the arch's own lock. Crack the vessel and you have not spilled the keeping all at once — clay is slower and kinder than that — but you have opened the bond, and what a sound pot holds forever, a cracked pot begins, quietly, to let go. She had watched it happen to a dunted naming-bowl, years back, before she'd learned the mending: a hairline in the cooling, and over one winter the child's whole naming-day thinning out of it, week by week, until by spring the bowl held nothing but the shape of having held something, the way a room holds after the people have gone. Slow. Sure. And there was no mending a keeping back into the same clay once the clay itself was cracked, because the mending needed a sound body to bind to, and this body would never be sound again; the frost had seen to that.

Six days to Wintermark. And in her two hands, thumb-marked and honest and sixty years unopened, her teacher's first pot, beginning to forget whatever her teacher had chosen, at the start of everything, to keep.

"Perrin," she called, and her voice did a thing that brought the boy in from the woodpile at a run with the axe still in his hand, because he had never in three years heard his mistress's voice do that. "Put the axe down. Wash your hands, and be quiet, and come here. There's a thing I have to decide, and I find I can't decide it alone, which my teacher would tell you is the whole of what she spent thirty years trying to teach me and mostly failed."

He came. He looked at the pot in her hands, and at the whisker of a crack running through the print of a girl's thumb, and being who he was, he understood the size of it before she said a word.

"The winter story," he said quietly. "It's dying."

"It's dying," said Edda. "And I have never once heard it. And now I have six days to decide whether I ever will."

# Chapter Four — The Two Wrong Answers

The thing about a hard choice, Grams Ilsabet used to say — usually while shelling peas, for maximum unanswerableness — is that the world rarely offers you a right answer and a wrong one; it offers you two wrong ones and asks which you can live beside.

Edda laid them out that afternoon at the Copper Kettle, because a decision that shouldn't be made by one tired woman in a cold workshop was exactly the kind Hessa's fire was for, and because Odile Vance happened to be at the inn going over the wardens' fuel accounts, and Edda had learned long ago that the reeve's dry, exact mind was worth three of anyone's for cutting a tangle into its actual pieces.

"There are two things I can do," Edda said, when the tea was poured and the door shut against the cold, "and I think both of them are wrong, and I'd be glad of company in choosing which."

"My favourite kind of problem," said Odile, laying down her pen. "Drainage is full of them. Go on."

"The first is to do nothing. Let the pot be. A telling is private as a grave — you both know the rule, it's older than the kiln — and a teacher's telling is the most private of all. Ilsabet kept that pot sixty years and never told me what was in it, which was her right, and never fired it into anything a soul could read, which was her choice, and if I take it down now and ask it, I'm opening a door the woman deliberately left shut, thirty years after she's past objecting. She could have told me. She told me everything else — the glazes, the cones, the reasons behind the reasons, the whole trade. She left that one pot silent on purpose, and 'do nothing' means I honour that, and let the frost have it, and never know. And it means the first keeping the Wenholt witches ever made goes out of the world this spring, and I stand and watch it go, out of respect. That's the first wrong answer."

Hessa, who cooked like a genius and read a room like a surgeon, said: "And it'd eat you the rest of your life, wondering."

"It would. But that's my grief to carry and not a reason. A witch who opens graves to ease her own wondering is spending the craft's honesty to buy her own comfort — Ilsabet's words, and true. So. The second thing." Edda turned her cup, and let it turn, and did not stop it. "I can save it. The frost has cracked the pot, but not spilled it, not yet; there's a keeping in there still, going but not gone. And we've learned, these three years, how to ask the fire to give back what it holds — the jar taught us that, the summer of the morning door. So I could ask the fire to give this one back too: draw the keeping out of the dying pot, into me, the way any telling comes into me — and then fire it fresh into a new pot, a sound one, and keep it safe another sixty years. Move it. Re-home it, from a body that's failing into one that'll hold. We've never done that — given-back and kept-again into new clay, one firing into the next — but there's no new magic in it, only the two we have, run end to end. It can be done. I'm near sure it can be done."

"But," said Odile, who could hear a *but* under a floorboard.

"But to move it I have to receive it. All of it. The whole of whatever my teacher kept, into me, the way I received the mason's brother and the ferryman's race — the full thing, more than words, the smell and the weight and the truth of it, whatever it is. I can't move a keeping I hold at arm's length. To save the pot I have to open the grave after all — not to ease my wondering, but to save the thing; and the saving and the opening are the same act, there's no doing one without the other. So the second wrong answer is: I break the rule to keep the treasure. I hear the private thing, because it's the only way to stop it being lost. And I'll have done to my teacher exactly what she trusted me for sixty years not to do." She spread her hands flat on the table, the old gesture. "Honour the silence and lose the keeping. Or save the keeping and break the silence. Those are the two, and they're both wrong, and Wintermark's in six days, and I can't hold it much past that whatever I choose, because the crack won't wait for spring."

The inn's fire ticked. Somewhere in the back a pot of Hessa's soup made its small domestic thunder.

Odile Vance was quiet a long time, which from Odile was a form of respect. Then she said, in the voice she used for the one true thing at the bottom of a moot: "You keep saying she left it silent on purpose. What if she didn't."

Edda looked up.

"You've told me a hundred times your teacher told you everything," Odile went on, dry and level and gentle, all at once. "The reasons behind the reasons. The whole trade. That doesn't sound like a woman who was keeping a secret from her apprentice, Edda. That sounds like a woman who meant to tell you a thing and ran out of days. You said yourself she made you a witch by dying — I'd wager she died the way most of us will, with a list of things still to say and no warning she'd reached the end of the saying. A silence you're sure was chosen is one thing. A silence that was only ever *not yet* is another. You're about to honour a locked door. Are you certain it was locked? Or was it just a door she always meant to walk you through, some winter, when you were old enough — and the winter came, and she didn't?"

And Hessa, refilling the pot, said the thing that decided it, in the flat kind voice she kept for the truest register: "There's a boy up your lane, love, asking what a first pot's for. And the only person who ever knew is a pot on your shelf that's dying because nobody asked it in time. Maybe the rule isn't 'don't open the grave.' Maybe the rule is 'don't let the last one who knows go out of the world with nobody holding the rope.'"

Edda sat with that a while. Outside, the frost held the whole valley in its flat cold hand, and the light was already going, mid-afternoon, the way it did in the deep of the year.

"Two on the rope," she said, at last, mostly to herself. "That's what I told the mason. It's heavy of a morning and heavier at owl-light, and no load's meant to be a one-man lift." She stood, and gathered her shawl, and there was something settled in her face that had not been there when she came in. "I'll not do it alone, and I'll not do it for my own wondering, and I'll not do it without asking her leave the only way that's left to me, which is to read what she wrote before I hear what she kept. She kept ledgers, my teacher. Nine of them, and I've the lot. If there's a right to any of this, it'll be in her own hand, and I've never once looked, because I never had a reason a witch could stand behind. Now I've got one."

"And if the ledger says she meant it silent?" Odile asked.

"Then I close the book, and I let the frost have it, and I carry the wondering to my own grave, and I teach the boy that some doors stay shut," said Edda. "But I'll have asked. Thank you, both. That's what the tea's for."

# Chapter Five — The Nine Ledgers

The ledgers of the Wenholt witches lived in a chest of river oak under the telling shelf, and Edda had read in the newest of them all her working life and never once opened the oldest, the way you live in a house for years and never go up to the attic where the people before you left their lives in boxes.

There were nine. The ninth was hers and Ilsabet's, the fat current one, its later pages in Edda's own square hand and its last entries, this past year, in a younger hand trying hard to be worth the paper. The eighth and seventh were Ilsabet's, cover to cover, that upright teacher's script Edda would know among a thousand — the same hand that had painted the names on the glaze jars, *wood ash, ochre, ball clay, feldspar*, forty years ago and still legible on the wall. And before those, going back and smaller and browner, the books of the witches before: names Edda knew only as names, taught to her as a child recites the kings, each one a woman who had stood where she stood and fed the fire and let go.

She and Perrin brought the chest to the stove and opened it that evening, with the frost ticking in the beams and the two commissioned tellings safe on the shelf and no firing to tend, and they read backward through the craft. It took the whole night, and neither of them minded, because it was the best kind of night: the kind you don't know you'll remember all your life while you're having it.

They read Ilsabet's hand grow younger and surer in reverse — the confident middle-years entries, terse and dry and sometimes funny, *fired the smith's grief for his mare, took reduction shallow, he cried and denied it, gave him the good chair twice*; and back and back to her first years in the ninth-generation book's earliest leaves, a rounder hand, less sure, a young witch finding her feet.

And in the reading of it, something happened that Edda had not looked for and would not have known to want: she met her teacher young. There were entries in that rounder hand she had to stop over and breathe — a girl-witch's terror the first time she carried two tellings at once and lay awake sure she'd muddle them; a proud shaky note the day she first took a reduction alone and *the celadon came blue, it came BLUE, I did not spoil it*; a line, dated a spring Edda could not place, that only said *asked Wenna why the kiln burns at night and she said "so you'll sleep after," which is not the whole reason and I mean to have the whole reason before I die*, and then, three pages and some years on, in the same hand grown steadier: *have the whole reason now. Will tell my own prentice sooner than she told me, if I am spared to have one.* Edda read that one twice and did not read it aloud, because it was hers, was the plain truth of it — *if I am spared to have one* — and the prentice Ilsabet had hoped for and mostly not spoken of was sitting by this stove sixty years later reading the hope in the dark. The whole ninth ledger, read backward, was a woman's life told in fires: the griefs she'd carried for the village and the small dry jokes she'd allowed herself and the reasons she'd hunted down one at a time and meant, always meant, to hand on. It was the least secretive book Edda had ever held. It only wanted reading.

And there, at the very start of Ilsabet's own entries, was something Edda had never known and stopped breathing to read: not Ilsabet's writing at all, for the first two pages, but the hand of the witch before her — the eighth witch, whose books these had been first — writing about the girl.

Her name had been Wenna. Grams Wenna, they'd have called her, the way they called Ilsabet Grams and would someday, Edda supposed with a strange lurch, call Edda herself. And in a spidery, careful, old-woman's hand, on a page dated in a winter sixty-odd years gone, Grams Wenna of Wenholt had written this, which Edda read aloud to a boy leaning close in the lamplight because some things must be said into the air to be believed:

*The child came up the lane out of the snow on the eve of Wintermark, and I have taken her in. She is seven or near it, and she came from over the Stonebeck hills alone, which no seven-year-old should live to do, walking three days in the worst of the frost after the fever took her mother on the road, the two of them bound here to the fair and only the one of them arriving. She would not come to the stove at first. She stood at my gate in the dark with her mother's shawl about her and would not say a word, until I saw she was not silent from cold or fright but from carrying — she was holding something with her whole small body the way you hold water in cupped hands, terrified to move for spilling it, and it was her mother's voice she was holding, the cradle-song her mother sang her, the last of the woman she had left, and she had walked three days over the hills saying it to herself without stopping for fear that if she stopped she would lose it, and it was going, the way a thing held too long and too hard in one small keeper will. I know that look. I have worn it. So I did not offer her the stove. I offered her the fire. I told her what I was, and what the kiln does, and I asked would she like her mother's song kept in a pot so she need never hold it alone again, and could set it down and sleep. And she said yes, and stopped shaking, and came in.*

Perrin had gone very still. Edda read on, and her voice was not quite steady, and she let it not be.

*She is too small to throw, but I would not fire her mother's song into a pot she had no hand in, for it is to be hers past my dying. So I sat her at the wheel in my lap, my hands over her hands, and we threw it together, and it is a bad little pot and she is prouder of it than I have been of anything I ever made alone, and she has left the print of her own thumb in the thin of it, which I have told her to leave and not smooth, for a first pot should be honest and hers is. Tomorrow, Wintermark eve, I will fire her mother's song into it — not a keeping she made, for she has made nothing yet, but a keeping I make for her, the first she will own, filled by my hand because her own hands are too new and too full. This is how it was done for me, and how it should be done and mostly is not, in these hurried years: a witch's first pot is not one she fills. It is one filled for her, by the one who takes her in, so that the first thing a keeper ever owns is proof that she was kept. I will teach her the trade in time. But tonight I only want the child to sleep, and to wake owning her mother's voice on a shelf where the frost can't have it, and to know from her first hour in the craft that the fire is a mercy before it is a skill. Her name is Ilsabet. She is asleep by the stove as I write. The song is safe in her, one more night, and tomorrow it will be safe for good.*

The workshop was silent but for the frost and the stove. Edda sat with the ledger open on her knees and the lamplight on sixty-year-old ink, and for a long moment she could not have said a word if the fire had asked her to.

"She never told me," she said finally. "In sixty years. Not that she came from over the hills, not the fever, not the walking — not any of it. I thought she was Wenholt-born. I thought she'd always been here, the way you think the hills were always there." She touched the page, lightly, the way you tend a grave. "And it's not a secret she was keeping from me, Perrin. Look at it. It's the opposite. It's the whole answer to your question, and she wrote it — no, her own teacher wrote it — right here in the front of the book she left me, where she knew I'd read it one day. A witch's first pot is not one she fills. It's one filled for her. She didn't keep a secret. She left me a letter, and I was sixty years too proud of the rule to open it."

"So it's decided," Perrin said, low. "You save it."

"It's decided." Edda closed the ledger, gently, keeping the place with her finger, and looked down the dark workshop toward the corner where a homely thumb-marked pot sat leaking a dead woman's mother's song into a frost that didn't want it and would take it anyway. "Not to ease my wondering. To keep a promise a witch made a frightened child sixty years ago — *so she need never hold it alone again* — and to keep the pot from breaking that promise now, at the end, when the child who owned it can't hold it any more and neither can she." She stood. "The frost breaks in four days, the smell of the air says so, in time for Wintermark. We've a great deal to make ready, and one pot to throw that matters more than any I've thrown in my life, and I'll not be the one throwing it."

"Mistress?"

"A witch's first pot is one filled for her," Edda said, "by the one who takes her in. You've a first pot owing, Perrin Loft, and I've the filling of it owing to you, and I've only just this hour learned the shape of the debt. We'll come to that. But first — first, the new home. The song has to go somewhere sound, and it has to be thrown by a witch's hands, and there's only one pair of witch's hands in this parish under fifty. So." She almost smiled, for the first time that hard week. "Go to bed. Tomorrow you throw the pot that saves my teacher's mother's voice. Wash your hands well. You'll want to be steady."

# Chapter Six — The New Home

He threw it the next morning, in the cold clear light, and Edda did not touch the wheel.

That was the whole of the ceremony, though neither of them named it a ceremony: the master stood back, and the apprentice sat to the wheel alone, and the thing they had circled for a year came quietly to pass in a frost-bright workshop with the stove ticking and no witnesses but the ware on the shelves. Edda mixed slip at the bench and watched him sideways, the way you watch a telling, and thought that the boy who could not centre two pounds of clay at fifteen without chasing it round the bench now sat down to the wheel the way Ansel Rooke sat down to a stone — with a stillness first, a settling, hands finding the clay's own centre before the wheel had made three turns.

"What am I throwing?" he'd asked, and she'd told him the truth, which was that she wasn't certain, and that he'd have to feel his way to it, because the pot had to fit the keeping and the keeping was a cradle-song sixty years old that neither of them had heard yet.

"Small," she'd said. "It's a child's song, and a small thing held too big rattles. Round-shouldered, to hold the sound in — a song wants curving in over it, not a straight wall it'll bounce off. And a lid, this time. Grams Wenna gave it no lid, and I think she was right for a frightened child who'd want to see her mother's voice on the shelf and know it was there — but this one's to keep sixty years more, and a lidded pot holds a keeping the way a closed hand holds warmth. Throw the lid to the pot, not the pot to a lid; they're to be one animal." She'd paused. "And Perrin. It's river clay, same pit, same as its dying grandmother came from and the kiln came from and every pot in this village. But you're to leave your mark in it. Not a thumbprint by accident like a scared child. On purpose, where the fire will find it. A witch signs the first pot she throws for another. It's owed."

So he threw it, and it was good — not showy, not a potter proving himself, but a plain sound generous small-shouldered pot with a fitted lid that sat down onto it with the soft click of a thing made for its place; and when it was leather-hard the next day he took the smallest trimming-tool and cut, into the foot where it would look down into the fire forever, not a flourish but the plainest mark there is: a single line, and beside it a second, close, leaning — two strokes, back to back, near enough touching. Edda looked at it and did not have to ask.

"The mason's mark," she said. "Two rooks. You've borrowed Ansel's yard-mark."

"He said I might." Perrin coloured a little but held his ground, the way he'd learned to. "I asked him, this morning, at the pit. I told him what it was for — not the whole of it, the private part's yours, but that it was to save the first keeping the craft ever made, and needed a mark, and I'd none of my own yet. And he stood there a while and then he said — " the boy's voice went careful, holding the words like ware " — he said a first mark's a serious thing and a man shouldn't take it light, and then he said the two rooks weren't a yard-mark any more, they were a Wenholt mark now, in the crown of the kiln and on his hearth and it was time they meant more than one trade. He said: two rooks back to back is two who lean on each other and stand. He said that's a witch and her prentice as much as it's two brothers. He said I could carry it till I'd a firing worth my own, and then he clapped me that hard I near dropped the pot." Perrin rubbed his shoulder, and grinned, and looked about twelve. "Uncle Anvil claps like a falling wall."

Edda laughed — the real one, which she did not spend often — and something in the hard week eased. It was proper, was the truth of it. It was more than proper. The mason had built the kiln that would fire this; his brother's death lay in the crown that would bear the heat; and now his mark, that had begun as a grief between two stubborn men and become a keystone, would go down into the fire on the pot that carried the craft's own founding mercy. *Two who lean on each other and stand.* Two falling things into one standing thing. The whole workshop was made of it, if you knew how to look.

They readied the rest over the next two days, and the readying was its own quiet joy, the way readying always was. The frost broke on the third morning exactly as the air had promised — a soft grey warmth rolling up the valley off the sea, the ice on the Ler groaning and shifting and going by noon to black water and floating plates, Old Wim back in his boat and loudly disappointed to be there. The wells ran again. The village unhunched. And up the witch's lane, once the lane was walkable, the queue began at last: Wenholt bringing its held-back year all at once now that the frost had let go, so that in three days the telling shelf went from two bowls to eight, the whole parish's Wintermark keepings settling in under Edda's breastbone — the naming-cup and the needle-memory joined by a courtship and a christening and a last good harvest and Gran Brock's year and a dog that had died old and beloved and would be missed into a feeding-bowl so the empty corner by the hearth might have a warmth in it. Eight bowls of slip, gold and grey and green and one the particular blue-black of a good old dog, ranged along the black-oak shelf at the height of Edda's heart.

And in the ninth place, the place kept for the one that mattered most, went no bowl of slip at all — because its slip was not yet made, and could not be, until Edda took down a dying pot and asked it, for the first and last time, to tell her what it held.

"Nine," Perrin counted, that evening, looking at the shelf. "Eight bowls and one empty place. You always said nine's the most a witch should carry."

"The most a witch should carry alone," Edda said. "And I'll not be carrying the ninth alone. Nor firing it alone. Nor —" she looked at him, and there was something in her face he would spend years learning to read and never quite finish "— nor deciding alone what becomes of it, in the end. But that's the last chapter, and we're not there. Tomorrow's Wintermark eve. Tomorrow, before we load, I take the first pot down and give it back the only way it can be given. Sleep, if you can. I shan't, but you're young, and one of us should come to the fire rested."

# Chapter Seven — The Giving-Back

She took it down on Wintermark eve, in the last of the afternoon light, with the kiln candling warm at the bottom of the garden and the village not yet arrived and the workshop for one hour entirely still.

She had thought hard about how to do it, because it had never been done — not this, not a keeping drawn from a dying pot into the witch and held, to be re-fired whole into a new one. The jar, the summer of the morning door, had been given back into the hands of the living woman it belonged to; the dish had been mended, old half and new half joined in the ware. But this was neither. This was a flitting — a keeping carried from a failing house to a sound one, through the witch, over one dark night, the way you carry a coal from a dying hearth to a new-laid fire cupped in your two hands and praying at every step.

The craft of it she reasoned out from what she knew. To give back, the jar had taught them, you asked the fire — but she could not put a cracked pot in a firing to draw it, for the heat would finish the crack and spill the lot. No: this giving-back had to be done cold, and slow, and by the witch alone, hand to clay, the way the very oldest and gentlest work was done, before ever there was a kiln to help. She would sit with the pot as she sat with a teller, and she would open herself to it as she opened to a telling, and instead of pouring in she would draw out — coax the going keeping across into herself the last of the way it was already, sadly, going, so that what the frost was stealing thread by thread she took instead, whole, into safe keeping under her own breastbone. And then it would live in her, as any telling lived in her between the chair and the fire, until the night drew it out and up and into the new small round-shouldered pot with the two rooks on its foot. A giving-back into the witch, and then a keeping into the clay. The two tempers they had, run end to end, over one Wintermark night.

She set Grams Ilsabet's first pot on the telling shelf, in the ninth place, at the height of her heart. She sat close, on the stool, with an empty bowl of slip in her lap and her hands cupped around the cold, cracked, homely little thing, and she did what she had never in fifty years done: she opened the grave. Gently. Asking leave, though the only one who could give it was ink in a book. *You left me the letter,* she said, not aloud. *I'm only slow at reading. I'll keep her promise for you. She'll not hold it alone.*

And the song came across.

It came the way the ferryman's snow had come, cool and slow, but smaller, and older, and stranger for being second-hand — for it was not Ilsabet's memory that came into Edda but the memory Ilsabet had owned, her mother's voice, kept sixty years in clay, worn soft as a step at a doorway by all the times a girl and then a woman had taken it down and held it and put it back. A cradle-song. A river-song, the kind mothers on the Ler had sung time out of mind — Edda knew the shape of it before she had the words, the way you know a hymn from the first note in a strange church — a slow small tune about the boat that carries you and the star that steers it and the shore that keeps your bed warm, *sleep now, the water knows the way, sleep now, the lamp is in the window.* A woman's voice, young, tired, sweet, singing over the rattle of a cart on a hill road three days from anywhere, singing it and singing it because as long as she was singing the child was warm and the road was bearable; and then — because a keeping gives the whole of it, more than the keeper ever reached herself — under the song, the thing the frightened seven-year-old had held without knowing she held it, the thing Grams Wenna had caught in one look at a gate in the dark: not the fever, not the fear, not the three days' walking. The warmth. The specific warmth of being sung to. A small body against a larger one, the cart-jolt, the cold beyond the shawl and the heat within it, the total animal safety of a child who has not yet learned that the voice can stop. That was what the pot had kept. Not the loss. Grams Wenna had been too good a witch and too kind a woman to fire a dead mother's loss into a living child. She had fired the *before* — the last warmth, the song itself, the being-kept — and left the loss outside the pot where it belonged, to be lived through and grown around and, in the end, laid down. *Take the warmth,* the pot said, in Wenna's long-dead judgment. *Leave her the grief but keep her the warmth. It's the warmth a child can't walk three days holding. The grief will keep itself.*

Edda sat a long time in the empty workshop with a sixty-year-dead woman's cradle-song safe under her breastbone and tears on her face she did not sort or name, because for once they wanted no sorting; they were the plain overflow of a thing too full to hold level, and she let them run and did not stand up naming her quinces. The slip in the bowl had gone, while she wasn't watching, a colour she had no name for — pale, and warm, and faintly luminous, the colour of a lamp seen from far off across dark water, the colour of a window you are walking toward through the cold.

The little pot in her hands was empty now. Truly empty — not the wrong-cold leaking emptiness of that morning a week ago, but the clean quiet emptiness of a jug poured out and set down, its work done. She turned it in the last light. The whisker of a crack ran still through the print of a girl's thumb, but it held no cold now, because there was nothing left in it to leak. It was only a bad little pot again, thumb-marked and honest, sixty years old, thrown by a child in an old woman's lap on the worst night of the child's life and the making, though the child never knew it, of them both.

"There," Edda said aloud, to the empty pot and the empty workshop and the ninth generation of a craft, and to a teacher thirty years in the ground who had left her a letter and trusted her to be slow. "There. I've got her. She's safe. Go and rest."

She set the little pot down, not on the private shelf, but on the wide sill of the workshop window that looked down the garden at the kiln — the window where the lamp stood on firing nights — and she left it there in the last grey light, facing the fire it would not go into, keeping it company one final Wintermark, while up the lane the first lanterns of the arriving village came bobbing through the dusk with the whole year in their hands.

# Chapter Eight — Wintermark Eve

Wintermark, for Wenholt, was the pole the year turned on, and the Firing was its heart, and the whole parish came to the lighting now — that had been the new custom since the year the kiln was rebuilt, born the way all Wenholt's customs were born, out of one year's necessity and the village's incurable habit of keeping anything that worked.

They came up the lane in the early dark with lanterns and chestnuts and the Wintermark loaf, and they packed the garden and the open-sided shed the way they packed the church, and Ansel Rooke stood at the stoke-mouth with the taper as he had every Wintermark since the first, and the two rooks on the keystone bore over all their heads whatever it was they had come to give the fire. It should have been, Edda thought, standing in her own workshop doorway with eight bowls of slip behind her and a ninth thing carried safe within her and a boy beside her white with what was coming, an ordinary great night. It was not ordinary. She could feel it not being ordinary the way you feel weather coming, in the joints.

They loaded through the early evening, the whole familiar liturgy of it: the naming-cup and the needle-memory and the courtship and the christening and the harvest and the old dog and Gran Brock's year, each bowl of slip brushed into its waiting vessel, each vessel glazed — ash for the laughs, celadon for the cold clear things, the iron that would go winter-glass under reduction if she starved the fire of air at the right hour, all of it stacked into the kiln on its shelves with the cones wedded upright where she could watch them through the spy-hole.

It was a loading like every Wintermark loading and like none of them, because Edda kept catching herself doing the small rites of it — the wadding of a footring so a running ash glaze wouldn't weld the cup to its shelf, the last cool tongue-of-the-wrist test of a glaze's thickness, the setting of the cone-pack square to the spy-hole so the bowing would read true — and thinking, each time, *this is a thing I have not written down, and there is only one other pair of hands in Wenholt that knows it, and they are loading the far shelf humming*. She had spent the hard frost-week frightened of exactly that arithmetic. Tonight it steadied her instead. Every wadded footring was a thing already handed on, whether she'd meant to hand it or not; the boy had learned the craft the way clay learns the pit, by being weathered in it, and it was in him now past any losing. She set Gran Brock's mug beside its sister on the second shelf — three years the old woman's laugh had come home from this kiln, and would keep coming, she thought, long after the witch who first fired it — and found the thought no longer had a cold edge on it. Perrin worked beside her, steady-handed and silent, and only once, loading, did his hand shake, and it was when Edda gave him, to set on the very middle shelf where the fire ran truest and evenest, the small round-shouldered lidded pot with the two rooks on its foot.

"That's the middle shelf," he said. "That's the best place in the kiln. The steadiest fire. You keep that for —"

"For the keeping that most needs the steadiest fire," Edda said. "Set it home, Perrin. Your pot, your hands. And then come and I'll tell you the last of it, before we light, because you're to be in this to the elbows and you'll do it better knowing why."

She took him to the window, where the empty thumb-marked pot sat facing the garden, and she told him — the whole of it now, the private part too, because he was to help fire it and a telling given ignorant was against every rule of the chair, but more than that because he had asked, a week ago over the wedging bench, what a first pot was for, and here at last was the entire answer, and he had earned every word.

"I have it," she said. "Grams Ilsabet's mother's cradle-song, the whole warmth of it, safe under here since this afternoon. Tonight the fire draws it out of me, thread by thread, all night, the way it draws every telling — and it settles into the new pot you threw, on the middle shelf, and it keeps. That's the flitting done: out of the dying house, through me, into the sound one. The song lives sixty more years, and more, in clay a witch made. That's the first thing tonight does, and it's the thing I set out to do, and it's enough on its own to make it a great night."

"But there's a second thing," Perrin said. He had learned to hear the shape of what she wasn't saying.

"There's a second thing." Edda turned the empty little pot on the sill, and set it down, and faced him. "The pot you threw is a new home for an old song. But it's something else as well, and I didn't fully see it until I read the ledger, and now I can't unsee it, and I think it's the truest thing I'll ever have the doing of. *A witch's first pot is not one she fills. It is one filled for her, by the one who takes her in, so that the first thing a keeper ever owns is proof that she was kept.* Grams Wenna filled Ilsabet's for her. Nobody filled mine — I came into the craft by a death and I filled my own with grief, and I never knew there was another way, and I've half-broken you these three years teaching you my hard road as if it were the only road. It isn't." Her voice was not quite level and she let it not be. "The pot you threw tonight is your first pot, Perrin. And I'm going to fill it for you, the way it's meant to be done and mostly isn't in these hurried years. The song goes in — but the song goes into *your* pot, thrown by your hands, marked with your mark, set by you in the best place in the fire. So that the first keeping you ever own is the oldest mercy this craft ever made: the proof, sixty years running and handed down witch to witch to witch, that this trade began with a frightened child being taken in out of the cold and given back the one voice she couldn't bear to lose. That's what a first pot's for. Not a grief you're strong enough to bear. A mercy you were lucky enough to be given. You were asking for a sorrow worthy of the pot. I'm giving you the opposite, because it's what I have to give and what I wish someone had given me: you don't earn your way into this craft by proving you can carry weight. You're carried into it. That's the whole of it. That's the first line and the last."

Perrin Loft, seventeen, no longer made of elbows, stood in the workshop where he had been made and looked at a small pot he had thrown with his own hands, on the best shelf of a fire his master's mason had built, about to receive a dead woman's mother's voice as his founding keeping and his making, and he did the only sensible thing, which was to sit down rather suddenly in the good chair, and put his face in his hands the way the mason once had, and let the workshop give him the minute he was owed.

"Does the village get told?" he asked, when he surfaced, and it was the right question, which was how Edda knew he was ready.

"A telling's private as a grave," Edda said, "and always has been. What Wenholt will be told is what's true and sufficient: that the apprentice fired his first pot tonight, and was made, and that the keeping in it is the craft's own — the oldest keeping the Wenholt witches hold, brought home to a new vessel because the old one was failing. They'll like you the better for it, and they'll be right to. Though I'll say this." She almost smiled. "You could do worse, someday, when you're old and there's a prentice of your own losing sleep over a first pot, than tell them the true whole of it. It's a good story. It deserves telling right, in the end. But that's your winter, not mine, and a long way off. Tonight —" she held out her hand, and pulled him up out of the chair by it "— tonight we light the fire. Wash your hands, Perrin Loft. You're about to become a witch."

# Chapter Nine — The Lighting

Ansel Rooke lit the kiln at full dark, as he had every year, but this year Edda stopped him with a hand on his arm before he touched the taper to the kindling, and turned to the packed and steaming garden, and did a thing that had not been done in Wenholt in living memory, because there had not been the occasion.

"Before we light," she said, and the village quieted the way a village quiets for its witch. "You all know the shape of tonight — the year's keepings, the great firing, the ware out warm at dawn into the longest morning. But there's a thing more tonight, and it's fitting you know it, because you're the ones the craft is for. Tonight my apprentice is made. Perrin Loft fires his first pot in this firing, and comes up out of it a witch of Wenholt, the fifth of the line, so that this village will have its kiln kept after I'm past keeping it." She had to stop a moment, over the noise the crowd made, which was a good noise, Maud Brock's voice loud in it. "And the keeping in his first pot is not a private grief but the craft's own oldest mercy — the first keeping the Wenholt witches ever made, brought this night into new clay because the old pot that held it these sixty years has cracked in the frost, and would lose it by spring. So tonight the fire does two things it's never done together: it makes a witch, and it carries a keeping home from a dying vessel to a sound one. Both by the same fire. Both in the same night. It seems to me," she said, "that's a thing worth lighting a lamp for. Ansel."

And Ansel Rooke, mason, of Rooke Brothers as was and Wenholt as is, who had come to this workshop years ago a shuttered house of a man asking to be rid of a weight and had stayed to build the very kiln he now stood at, put the taper into the kindling and said, in the voice that only shook once, "The old builders gave a wall a coin or a shoe. This crown had of me a telling, and bears it still. And tonight it fires a boy into a witch, which I reckon is the best load it's carried yet." And the new kiln took its first Wintermark breath, and the flame ran orange up the stoke-mouth, and the whole village made the sound a village makes, and somewhere at the back Old Wim's voice said, low, like a mooring-rope taking strain, "Aye. That's proper," and the hush agreed.

They fired all night, the way the custom was.

The village stayed till midnight with chestnuts and lanterns, in and out of the shed, and then drifted home by that old good instinct that a witch fires best with few watchers, and the small-hours crew was the crew the night always chose for itself: Perrin on the wood at first and then, tonight, not on the wood; Hessa's supper basket and Hessa herself for the early hours; Ansel with his palm flat on the flank of his own animal, feeling it draw; and the witch, carrying the year and the song both, standing in the fire's long company while it unpacked her.

It drew them out of her the way it always did, thread by thread, hour by hour, through her hands on the stoke-wood and her eye at the spy-hole: the naming-cup's first word, the needle by firelight, the courtship, the christening, the old dog, Gran Brock's year, each lifting away with the small pang of a hair pulled loose, each going home into its waiting clay. A laugh left you like a hair. And the song —

The song did not leave like a hair. Edda had known it wouldn't; she had carried it only an afternoon, but it had rooted deep in that afternoon, the way the oldest and gentlest things root fastest, and when the fire began to draw it — around the third hour, as the first cone bowed its head and the flame off the stoke-mouth burned clean straw-gold — she felt it come up not like a hair and not like the tree of a man's death that the keystone firing had cost her, but like a lullaby leaving a house: slow, and complete, and taking the warmth of the room with it as it went, so that she stood at the spy-hole with tears running unregarded down her face for the second time that day and stoked, and stoked, and let the mother's voice go out of her and up through the new kiln's clean strong throat and down into a small round-shouldered pot on the middle shelf, where a boy's two-rook mark looked up into the fire and the heat set the song into the clay layer upon layer, the boat and the star and the lamp in the window, *sleep now, the water knows the way*, safe for sixty more years and more, in a home a witch had thrown.

⁂

# Chapter Ten — The Making

At the fourth hour, with the first cone down and the second beginning to bow, Edda called Perrin from the wood-stack to the stoke-bench, and gave him the last of what a master has to give, which is the fire itself.

"Come. It's yours to bring home now. I've carried it up; you take it the rest of the way." She moved over on the bench and put the stoke-iron into his hands, and it was the first time in three years she had let him lead a firing at the true heart of it, the hour that makes or spoils, and they both knew what it was. "You know this fire better than I do already, if you'd credit yourself, which you won't, so I'll tell you: read the second cone, not the clock. When it touches its tip to the shelf, you call the soak, and you hold it, and then you take the reduction — starve her, close the dampers to the hair I taught you, and let the iron forget it was ever rust — and you bring your own pot home under your own hand in your own reduction. I'll be here. I'll not touch a damper. Two on the rope, and the near end's yours tonight."

So the boy fired. And Edda sat beside him on the stoke-bench in the roar and the straw-gold light and watched Perrin Loft become a witch, which is not a thing that happens in a flash of anything, but slowly, over an hour, the way all true things happen: the settling first, hands and breath finding the fire's centre the way they'd found the clay's; then the long attention, the whole stopped body listening the way Cobb listened to a telling and Ansel to a stone; the small right movements, a damper eased, a stick fed low and to the back where the fire ran hungry, the reduction taken not by the clock but by the colour and the smell and the particular sulky green the flame goes when the iron begins to give up its air. He read the second cone down himself, and called the soak in a voice that only cracked once, and held it, and took his reduction clean and deep and unhurried, and never once looked to her to check — which was the whole of it, in the end; the moment an apprentice stops checking your face is the moment they've become the thing they were checking for.

And the small pot on the middle shelf, in the steadiest truest heart of the fire, took its keeping and set it and kept it.

At the top of the reduction, in the deep of the night, with the whole kiln roaring soft and even and his own pot bearing its song in the middle of it, Perrin did a thing Edda would keep the rest of her life without ever telling him she'd seen it, the way you keep the best things: he sang. Under his breath, barely, over the roar, so quiet she felt it more than heard it — the river-song, the boat and the star and the lamp in the window, because he had heard it once, that evening at the window when she told him the whole of it, and it had gone into him the way it went into everyone, and now he stood in the fire that was keeping it forever and sang it back to the pot he'd thrown, low, to help it home. *Sleep now. The lamp is in the window.* A boy singing a dead woman's cradle-song into a fire on the longest night, to keep a promise made to a frightened child sixty years before he was born. Edda sat very still and let him not know she'd heard, and thought that in fifty years of the trade she had not seen the craft do a truer thing, nor a warmer, and that whatever she'd been afraid of that hard cold week — the arithmetic of one old witch and one winter — was answered now, sitting beside her on the stoke-bench, reading a fire better than she could.

The second cone touched its tip to the shelf near dawn. They held the last soak, and let the reduction come back gentle to a clean finish, and clammed her up as the grey came into the east — every hole bricked and mortared blind, the fire shut in with its work to cool as slow as it had risen, because ware chilled too fast rings cracked and dunted and spoiled, and even endings, especially endings, had to be done at the fire's own pace.

Then Edda took the empty thumb-marked pot down off the workshop sill, where it had kept the fire company all night, and brought it out to the shed in the dawn cold, and put it into Perrin's hands.

"There's a last thing owing," she said, "and it's yours to do, being the witch of it now. This pot's done its work. Sixty years it held a mother's voice for a girl who couldn't hold it alone, and it held it faithful right to the crack, and there's no keeping it now — you can't fire a keeping back into cracked clay, and it's empty besides, its song safe in your own on the middle shelf. So the question the craft leaves us is what becomes of the vessel when the keeping's flitted. And I've thought about it, and I think there's only one right answer, and I think you'll see it."

Perrin turned the little pot in his hands, the thumbprint, the whisker of a crack, the crooked homely honest whole of it. He was quiet a while, being a witch now, and thinking like one.

"It came from the river," he said. "Same pit as everything. Same as mine." He looked up. "Give it back to the clay. Not break it — that's spite, and it did nothing but its work. Take it down to the bend below the ferry and let it go back into the pit it came out of, so it's there in the ground with all the rest of the clay, and someday — years on, when we dig — some of it comes up again in a new pot, and gets thrown, and fired, and holds something new. It doesn't get kept. It gets handed on."

"It gets handed on," Edda agreed, and her voice did the thing again and she let it. "That's the answer. That's exactly the answer. And it's the answer to more than the pot, boy, but you've the rest of your life to work that out. Come on. The village will be here for the opening at full light, and there's the small matter of you being made in front of the lot of them, and I want tea in me before I have to be dignified at Maud Brock."

# Chapter Eleven — The Longest Morning

They opened at dawn on Wintermark morning, into the packed and steaming garden of the whole parish, and the ware came out singing.

It was a kind firing — better than kind; the new kiln had drawn cleaner and truer under Perrin's reduction than Edda had ever coaxed from it, and she said so, out loud, to the boy and to the mason both, because credit ungiven is a debt and she paid her debts. The celadon had gone a blue there was no name for outside a first snowfall. Gran Brock's year came out with the laugh streaked gold down one side. The old dog's feeding-bowl was so warm to the hand that its owner, a hard man not given to display, had to go and stand behind the woodshed a while, and came back and thanked the witch and thanked, unexpectedly and to everyone's lasting delight, the apprentice.

And on the middle shelf, warm past all explaining, sat a small round-shouldered lidded pot with two rooks on its foot, and Perrin Loft lifted it out into the longest morning with both hands, the way you carry a bird's egg the size of your heart, and held it, and his face did the thing every face did — attend, soften, attend deeper — and found. The boat. The star. The lamp in the window. The whole animal warmth of being sung to, safe in the hands, real as a heartbeat, sixty years dry and going nowhere now, ever again. The first keeping he owned. The proof that he was kept.

Then Edda Marren stood up before the parish, and made her apprentice a witch, in the plainest words there are, because the plain words are the ones that hold.

"Wenholt," she said. "You brought this village its year, and the fire kept it, as it always will. Here's the fire's other work tonight." She took the small pot from Perrin's hands and held it up, warm, in the cold bright morning, and then she gave it back to him, which was the whole ceremony. "This is Perrin Loft's first pot, thrown by his own hands, fired by his own reduction, marked with his own mark. It holds the oldest keeping the Wenholt witches have — brought home last night from a vessel that was failing into this one that will not fail. He carried a fire through its true hour without once looking to me, and he'll keep this kiln after me, and he does it not because he's strong enough to bear a grief but because this craft took him in when he was a boy made mostly of thumbs, and he never once let go the rope. That's a witch. There's your fifth of the line. Be as good to him as you've been to me, which is to say bring him bread on firing nights and argue about paying and never once be square." And Maud Brock, right on the beat, called "We have never once been square, Edda Marren," and the whole frosted morning of Wenholt laughed, the real laugh, the bakehouse bell of it catching down the lane and into the square the way it always had, a village laughing for gladness on the longest morning of the year, with a new witch standing in the middle of it holding a mother's cradle-song in his two hands.

Later, when the gate had shut at last past noon and the crew of the night sat around the workshop stove in the wrecked and holy tiredness of the morning after — too spent for anything but tea, which was the point of tea — Edda took down the ninth ledger and pushed it across the bench to Perrin, ink and all.

"Your firing. Your hand. And your line to add, for the witches after — the fourth one, that this night taught the fire and the village both. You'll know the shape of it."

And Perrin Loft, witch of Wenholt, the fifth of the line, took up the pen and wrote beneath his mistress's hand, slow, and true, the record of a flitting and a making on one Wintermark night; and then, at the front of the ledger, under the three lines that were already there, he wrote the fourth, while his mistress dictated nothing at all and had some trouble with the kettle:

*The kiln keeps. It does not take. Say it back. And what it keeps, asked kindly, it can give back. And what breaks, told together, it can mend. And what one hand throws, another hand fills — for a first pot is not one you fill alone.*

"There," said Edda, reading it twice, and squaring the book. "That's a witch's own line, that is. First of yours. There'll be others." She looked at him a while, over the crooked cup that smelled of peas and summer. "There's one thing more I owe you, and it's not for the ledger, it's just for you, and then I'll leave you be. You asked me once what to put in a first pot, and you were ashamed you'd no grief big enough. I never told you the memory I'd have put in it, if I'd known then what I know now, and if the song hadn't had first claim. So I'll tell it you plain, unpotted, the old way, mouth to ear." She set down the cup. "The day you named the laugh. Your first autumn here, fifteen and all thumbs, couldn't centre two pounds of clay — and you walked past the telling shelf and went sideways-quiet the way nobody ever taught you, and named Gran Brock's laugh off a bowl of slip, from before you were born. And I stood at the bench behind you and knew, right then, the whole of it — that you were a witch and would be a great one and that I'd not die the last of the line after all. Absurdly proud, I was. I've been absurdly proud of you every day since and let you see about a tenth of it, on principle, because a swelled prentice is no use to anyone. But there it is. That's the keeping I'd have chosen for you, if the song hadn't needed the pot. The day I knew. You can put it in a pot yourself, someday, if you like. Or you can just have it, the way you have most of the best things — warm, and unfired, and yours."

Perrin did not say anything, which was the right thing to not say. He turned his tea a quarter, winding, and did not notice he'd done it, and Edda noticed, and kept it.

⁂

# Chapter Twelve — Say It Back

That evening, Wintermark itself, the true longest night, Perrin walked down to the bend below the ferry landing with the empty thumb-marked pot in his coat, and gave it back to the clay.

He did it at dusk, alone, which was right; some things a witch does with witnesses and some he does with only the river. He waded a little into the cold slack water at the pit's edge and set the small crooked pot down into the soft grey clay where the diggers went every autumn, and pressed it under with his palm, gently, the way you'd tuck a child, until the pit took it and the water closed grey and quiet over the place, and Grams Ilsabet's first pot went home into the ground it came from, its work done, its song safe up the lane in a new home, to lie in the clay with all the rest until some year some hand dug it up again and it came round once more into a pot and held something new. Handed on. Not kept — handed on. There was a difference, and he'd spent the day learning it, and would spend years learning it deeper, which is what the difference is for.

He was drying his hands by the ferry-house fire after, because Old Wim had seen him from the window and would no more let a lad stand wet in the frost than fly, when the thing happened that starts the next story, the way the next story always starts in Wenholt: quietly, at the edge of an ending, with somebody's grief in somebody's hands.

Griet was by the fire in the best chair, wrapped soft, on one of her clear evenings — the porridge bowl at her elbow and the mended dish beside it, two lamps in one window, minding their mornings for a woman who mostly could not. She watched Perrin dry his hands, and knew him, which she did not always, and said, in the bright wandering way she had now: "You're the witch's boy. The one that's a witch now, Wim says. There'll be two of you, then. Good. It wants two." And then, drifting, looking into the fire: "There was a girl asked after the humming cup. Weeks back. Before the frost. Came to the ferry to cross and asked did the witch still keep a cup that hummed, a wedding cup, from a wedding that never was. I said cross and ask her yourself, and she went pale and didn't cross, and went back the way she'd come." Griet's clear eyes came up, and for a moment they were entirely present, entirely her own. "It was her grandmother's, she said. The wedding that never happened. And she said — I remember it because it was a sad pretty thing to say — she said she'd inherited the not-happening. The whole family had. And she didn't know if a fire could ever fire a wedding that never was, but she thought about it every spring, and one spring she'd be brave enough to cross the water and ask." The eyes drifted again, gently, the tide going out kind this time. "You tell your witch. A cup that hums. A wedding that never was. Some spring, a brave girl. You tell her."

Perrin walked back up the iced lane in the dark with the longest night whole around him and the whole of it turning in his chest — the pit, the pot, the ledger, the making, and now this, a hummed cup and a wedding that never was and a girl who inherited the not-happening and thought about it every spring. He found Edda at the workshop window with the lamp lit, looking down the garden at the kiln, warm at the heart-bricks even cooling, bearing all it kept and keeping all it bore; and he told her, the whole of it, the way she'd taught him — properly, out loud, leaving nothing out.

Edda listened the way she listened to everything, sideways, with the wrong sense on purpose. And when he'd done she looked a long time at the private shelf, at the wedding cup that hummed, alone now at the end where a first pot used to sit — thirty years and more of a hope that had outlived its occasion, still faintly warm, waiting the way kept things wait, patient as a customer, for somebody brave enough to cross the water and ask.

"A wedding that never was," she said. "And a granddaughter who inherited the not-happening." She turned her crooked cup a quarter, and this time let it turn, all the way round. "Well. There's a thing the fire's never been asked. Give back, mend, flit a keeping home — but fire a thing that never happened at all, fire a hope into the shape of the wedding it never got to be? I don't know that it can be done. I don't know that it should." She was quiet a moment, and then something came into her face that Perrin was only beginning to be able to read, and would spend his own long apprenticeship learning: the knowing that comes first, before the understanding limps after. "But she'll cross, that girl, one brave spring. They always cross in the end. And when she does, there'll be two of us to meet her at the gate, and a chair, and tea. Which is the point; the pots are how we pay for it."

She looked at him, her apprentice, her witch, the fifth of the line, and the workshop was warm and full of the ware of the year and the frost ticked in the beams like a slow clock and down the lane a village slept off its longest night with its whole kept year in its cupboards.

"But that's a spring story," Edda said, "and this is still the deep of winter. Wash the dust off. Bank the stove. And say it back to me."

And Perrin Loft, witch of Wenholt, standing where he had stood at fifteen made of thumbs and standing there now made of something better, said it back — the whole of it, word for word, for the witches after:

*The kiln keeps. It does not take. Say it back.*

*And what it keeps, asked kindly, it can give back.*

*And what breaks, told together, it can mend.*

*And what one hand throws, another hand fills — for a first pot is not one you fill alone.*

And down at the bend below the ferry, under grey water and the longest night, a small thumb-marked pot lay in the cold clay among all the years of clay, its work done and its song handed on, waiting to come round again; and up the lane the lamp stood in the workshop window, which was the best of it, the waiting; and the kiln kept what it was given, and did not take, and would go on keeping, now, after Edda, for a good long while yet.

THE END

## About the series

*The Night Kiln* is a series of standalone cozy fantasy novellas set in the village of Wenholt, where the kiln-witch Edda Marren fires her neighbours' spoken memories into everyday pottery — so a grandmother's laugh or a child's first word can stay warm in the room long after the moment is gone. Each book turns on a single fired vessel and one new thing the fire can be asked to do, and each adds a line to the witches' catechism, taught "for the witches after." Low-stakes, big-hearted, and warm: pottery, tea, found family, and grief handled with care.
