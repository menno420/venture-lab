# THE SEED CATALOGUE COURTSHIP

*A Novel in Letters*

> Prowse & Daughters, Seedsmen since 1931, is closing. Edith is sixty-one, the last daughter, and almost relieved — until order No. 4,117 arrives from a man rebuilding a walled garden that has been derelict since the war, with a question inked neatly in the margin: *"What would you plant, if it were yours?"* Told in order forms, packet instructions, and letters across one growing year — a love story for anyone who has ever written more in the margin than on the line.

---

**Content note:** Adult epistolary romance, clean & wholesome. Contains remembered bereavement (a sister, a spouse), the winding-up of a family firm, and one badly kept promise. No explicit content, no swearing, nobody harmed but feelings — and those are mended. Closed-door throughout.

---

*The documents in this book — order forms, compliments slips, packet instructions, catalogue pages, one auctioneer's account, and letters — are presented in the order in which they were kept, which is not always the order in which they were sent. Marginalia are given where they survive. They mostly survive.*

---

# Part One — Catalogue Weather

*[Printed notice, inside front cover of the Prowse & Daughters seed catalogue, one hundred and somethingth edition, January.]*

**TO OUR CUSTOMERS**

Prowse & Daughters, Seedsmen since 1931, will cease trading at the end of the present year.

The firm was founded by Noah Prowse with one field, one bicycle, and a conviction that the vegetables of this county deserved better ancestors. It passed to his son Ezra, who in 1968 renamed it for his daughters, to the lasting distress of the trade. It has been carried since by the younger of those daughters, who now finds that a firm, like a crop, has its season.

Orders will be filled while stocks last. The trial grounds at Dyke Field will be sown once more, out of respect rather than economics. Customers wishing to secure old favourites are advised not to wait: when the seed store is empty it will not fill again.

The firm thanks three generations of gardeners for their custom, their patience in poor springs, and their letters, which were read more carefully than was ever admitted.

Catalogues are provided with margins. That is not an accident.

*In the margin:*

The parsnips are in. Your catalogue says of 'Moonglow': *patient, unglamorous, and worth the wait — like most things in the Fens.* I have read a great many technical documents in my life, and exactly one of them made me laugh and then order the component described. Whoever writes your entries has been wasting themselves on parsnips.

*[Letter, on the firm's letterhead, dated 6 February. A round, disciplined hand that loosens by the third paragraph, like handwriting remembering it is also a person.]*

Dear Mr Tull,

Your order No. 4,122 goes to the post with this letter. I have substituted fresh stock of 'Marian's June' for the packets on the shelf, the shelf packets being last season's; the difference would have been three plants in a hundred, but the firm has never seen the sense of selling a customer three disappointments it could prevent.

Now, your margins. You are sowing, if your two orders are read together, as though Marchfield were Norfolk. It is not. You are in the west, on rising ground, inside a brick enclosure that will hold the sun like a grudge. Your wall will give you a fortnight the rest of the parish does not get; your ground, if the Court's gardeners knew their business before the war, is the best-fed soil in the county, and eighty years of neglect will not have unfed it. Sow your 'Norfolk Morning' a month earlier than the packet says and be ready to defend the flowers from frost with something undignified. Gardening is mostly the willingness to look ridiculous in April.

You ask what I would plant. The firm repeats that it does not advise on hypotheticals; the firm, however, is two people and a cat, and one of the people answers letters after six o'clock. Since you persist: sweet peas. Item one, sweet peas. The firm has never yet sold to a garden that needed fewer of them than its owner believed. I decline to continue the list at this time. A barometer that yielded to every tap would be no instrument at all.

Yours faithfully,
E. M. Prowse (Miss)
*for* Prowse & Daughters

*[Letter, received 18 February. Written on graph paper, the lines conscripted.]*

Dear Miss Prowse,

Item one, sweet peas. Noted, minuted, and acted upon: see enclosed order No. 4,126, which is all sweet peas and no apology.

You will want to know what your seed is getting into. A survey, then, of the Walled Garden, Marchfield, conducted by its owner in a hard frost with a borrowed theodolite:

1. The wall is brick, English bond, twelve feet at the north side, and 214 years old. It encloses a shade under four acres. It is the only structure I have ever owned that is older than the profession that taught me to doubt it.
2. The house it served — Marchfield Court — was requisitioned in 1940, burned in 1944 by an unattended stove and, it is said, a corporal's cat, and pulled down in 1951. The garden has been derelict since the war: not abandoned, which is sudden, but derelict, which is slow. There is a difference, and it took me a year of clearing brambles to learn to respect it.
3. The north wall is hollow. I put a lamp to a vent and found flues — the wall was heated, Miss Prowse. Fires were kept burning inside the brickwork so that fruit could be grown against it in a county that had no business growing it. I retired from bridges and appear to have purchased a chimney 300 feet long, lying on its side.
4. Frost-shattered coping to 40 yards of the east wall. Repairable. Everything is repairable; the question — always the only question — is what a thing can be asked to bear while you repair it.
5. Under the bramble at the south-east corner: a range of glasshouse footings, a rusted boiler, and a zinc plant label, which I enclose. It reads, in stamped letters: P & D No. 31. Your pea, unless I mistake, 'Norfolk Morning'.

Which raises a question I would rather ask than tap. Does the firm's archive go back far enough to say whether Prowse & Daughters supplied Marchfield Court? The label says yes. I find I would very much like the particulars. A man who buys a ruin buys its debts, and I am beginning to suspect this ruin owes your firm a great deal more than 40p.

Yours faithfully,
H. Tull

*PS — The question stands, at barometric intervals. Item two may be submitted at your convenience.*

*[Letter, on the firm's letterhead, 3 March.]*

Dear Mr Tull,

Your zinc label sat on the order desk for two days before I could bring myself to open the books, which I mention not to be dramatic but because the books in question weigh nine pounds apiece and the shelf is high.

Yes. Marchfield Court, per the order ledgers, was a customer of this firm from March 1933 to April 1940. Head gardener a Mr E. Speed, who wrote a spiked, impatient hand and ordered like a man feeding a regiment, which, latterly, I suppose he was. Fourteen orders. Peas, always 'Norfolk Morning', always double quantity; onions; a standing spring order of sweet peas for the house — cut flowers for the hall table, I imagine, in the years when the hall had a table.

The last entry is the one you will want, so I copy it exactly. *Order No. 812, 9 April 1940. Marchfield Court, per Mr Speed. Sweet pea 'Saltmarsh Pink', 6 pkt; pea 'Norfolk Morning', 4 pkt; sundries as list. TO BE COLLECTED —* and there the entry stops. The dash is my grandfather's. The order was made up — there is a tick against the making-up — and it was never collected, and nobody crossed it out. Ledger-keeping was my grandfather's one sentimentality: he would strike through a cancelled order with a ruled line and initial it. An order never collected he left open. There are four such open orders in ninety years of books. Yours — I notice I have begun calling it yours — is the oldest.

'Saltmarsh Pink', since you will ask: my grandmother's sweet pea, raised here, named for the village, salt-pink at the wing and pale at the standard, scent like the week before a holiday. We have never dropped it from the catalogue. Some varieties earn their keep in the ground; that one earns it in the list.

The archive, I should say, is my sister's doing as much as anyone's — Marian kept it, and the trial grounds, and the breeding work, while I kept the books and the words. She died seven years ago, and I have kept everything since, at first because grief is tidy-minded and afterwards because it was March, or June, or sowing time, or harvest. A firm is a calendar that will not stop to be mourned in. I found I did not altogether mind.

Yours faithfully,
E. M. Prowse

*[Letter, on the firm's letterhead, 2 April. Enclosed with the redeemed order: the original invoice, made out in 1940 in a copperplate hand, across which has been written, in fresh ink:* Settled in full, with thanks, P & D. *]*

Dear Mr Tull,

Your postal order is returned herewith, all but the 1940 sum itself, which the firm accepts at the 1940 price. The interest calculation was inspected, admired, and declined. We are seedsmen: we charge for the packet, never for the years. If time improved our stock we should be millionaires, and if it were billable nobody could afford March.

The order is despatched. You should know what you now hold. Seed is not a relic; it is a promise with a deadline, and every variety in that parcel has been kept alive since 1940 by somebody's hands — my grandfather's, my father's, Marian's, mine — growing it out, year upon year, so the promise never lapsed. When 'Saltmarsh Pink' flowers on your hot wall this summer it will not be history. It will be the same undertaking, kept continuously, arriving at last at the address on the label. You wrote once that this ruin owes my firm a great deal. I find it is the other way about. A firm that is closing wants very badly to be told its ledgers still bind somebody, somewhere, to a future. You have contrived to place the one order we could not have filled for anyone we liked less.

You asked in January — barometrically — whether I mind the closing. Nobody else has asked; the village assumes, the solicitors schedule, and the trade papers got the founding date wrong by a decade. Here is the truth as I find it at six o'clock: I am sixty-one, I have kept these books since I was twenty, and what I feel most mornings is not grief. It is something adjacent to relief, arriving early, like the post. I expected to be ashamed of that. I am unsettled to report that I am not — or was not, until a man in Herefordshire began redeeming my grandfather's dashes, since when the relief has developed a complication I have not yet had time to classify.

Item two, since the barometer will only be tapped otherwise: a hot wall, apparently. You might see whether yours still draws.

Yours sincerely,
E. M. Prowse

*[Letter, received 30 April. Graph paper. A pressed sweet-pea leaf — first true leaves — folded in.]*

Dear Miss Prowse,

Germination report, the Walled Garden, Marchfield, as at the 28th:

1. 'Saltmarsh Pink' (the 1940 order, living refill): 53 of 60 seeds up. Chipped, not soaked. The news was told; the seed took it well.
2. 'Norfolk Morning': through the ground in 11 days, a fortnight earlier than the packet promised, exactly as a certain letter promised. I looked ridiculous in April — horticultural fleece, clothes pegs, one disgraceful umbrella — and the flowers are unmarked. You were right about the wall's fortnight. You are right at a range of 200 miles, which in my former profession would have got you a consultancy.
3. 'Moonglow', 'Dyke Field Scarlet', the leek, the kale: all serving. No failures to report among your stock. The failures on site are all my own work.
4. The hot wall drew. I lit a trial fire in the western furnace on the 19th — the flues roared like a congregation, thirty starlings left in protest, and for one afternoon a brick wall in Herefordshire was warm on a cold day for the first time in eighty-five years. I put my palm flat against it and I will not attempt the engineering justification for how long I kept it there.
5. The swifts came back on the 26th. I record this under germination for want of a better ledger. Some things come up out of the air instead of the ground; a good garden apparently orders both.

The 1940 invoice, marked settled, has been framed and hung in the bothy, which is what I have instead of a hall table.

Yours sincerely,
H. Tull

*PS — You have now supplied items one and two. The question of 4,117 remains on file, and I am keeping the file where I can see it.*

# Part Two — The Growing Season

*[Letter, on the firm's letterhead, 14 May. Enclosed, per a note on the reverse: one galley slip, which will matter.]*

Dear Mr Tull,

Dyke Field was sown for the last time this morning. You will forgive a report in kind; you have set the precedent, and precedents, as my solicitor keeps telling me, are binding.

The trial ground is eleven acres outside the village, rented from the church since 1934 at a rate the diocese has been embarrassed by since 1957. Every variety on our list is grown out there in rotation — that is what a seedsman sells, Mr Tull, not packets: continuity, proved annually, in public. This morning it was 'Granary Longpod' at the north end, drilled by a contractor who took off his cap at the headland, which nearly finished me; then the roots; then the flower rows along the dyke, where my grandmother's sweet peas have gone in every May since before the war, and went in again today, closing notice or no closing notice. The lease ends at Michaelmas. I have grown that field in my mind's eye since I was six. I find I cannot yet make the mind's eye read the notice.

Marian's bench is at the dyke end, where the breeding rows were. I should tell you about the breeding rows, because their contents will outlive the lease and are presently my favourite problem. My sister left, among much else, seven numbered crosses of sweet pea — 41 to 47 — unfinished, unnamed, seven little envelopes in the seed store in her shorthand, which I alone now read. Cross 44 was the one she spoke of the way other women speak of daughters who might do anything. They have sat seven years because a breeder's line is not stock, it is an argument half-made, and I could not bear either to finish it badly or to let the argument die in a drawer. A problem, as I say. Firms in dissolution collect them.

The catalogue for next year will not exist, which leaves me with the writing hours empty. I mention this administratively.

Yours sincerely,
E. M. Prowse

*[Enclosed in error, folded inside the above: a printer's galley slip for catalogue entry No. 44, parsnip 'Moonglow', the proof corrected in ink — a comma inserted, one word struck out, the correction initialled E.M.P. The corrections are in the same hand as the letter.]*

*[Letter, received 26 May. The galley slip returned, flattened, between two sheets of card.]*

Dear Miss Prowse,

I return an enclosure which I believe strayed into your letter of the 14th. I have taken a card backing to it because paper that has been read as often as I read your letters develops creases, and this one seemed to deserve better. I make no further comment upon it. An engineer knows when he is standing on someone else's drawing.

Wall report, since precedents bind us both:

1. The east coping is rebuilt, 40 yards, lime mortar, no cement — the wall has flexed for two centuries and I will not be the man who teaches it rigidity in its old age. Everything I know about repair I learned on bridges and it amounts to this: respect what the structure has been carrying, and how long, before you add so much as a coat of paint.
2. The western furnace now heats 90 feet of wall to blood temperature on one barrow of wood. Whoever built these flues wasted nothing and signed nowhere, and I have stopped work more than once simply to stand in his chimney and take off my hat.
3. Against the hot wall I have planted the wrong things enthusiastically, per your April instruction, and 'Saltmarsh Pink' is three feet up its strings and climbing like rumour.
4. You asked no questions in your last letter, which I notice you do when a letter has cost you something. So I will supply both halves. You are wondering whether to send the seven envelopes to a man who has been gardening for eighteen months. The answer is that a breeder's argument needs ground, patience, record-keeping, and somebody too stubborn to let it die politely — and that I have four acres, no employer, a theodolite, and nothing left in the world I am obliged to finish except what I choose. Ruth — my wife — died three years ago this July. She would have looked at this garden once and started giving orders; I bought it, I think, because it was the one place I could still hear what her orders would have been. That is as much as I can write on that subject, and more than I have managed to say aloud in three years.
5. The file marked 4,117 remains open. Items one and two are growing against my wall. I am a patient man, Miss Prowse. Ask the parsnips.

Yours sincerely,
H. Tull

*[Order form No. 4,141, received 15 June. The last order form H. Tull would ever submit to the firm, though nobody knew it then. Successional sowings: lettuce, kale for winter, green manures. In the margin:]*

The firm's produce is cropping at Marchfield. The first peas were eaten on 11 June standing up, from the pod, in a manner Mr Speed would have sacked me for. It has occurred to me — the thought arrives daily at six o'clock, when a letter would — that the supplier of every growing thing inside this wall has never seen the garden. If the winding-up permits an inspection tour, the gardener would meet any train, in any month, at any notice. The spare room in the bothy has a bolt on the inside, a view of the hot wall, and no draughts; I re-hung the door myself and I certify the workmanship. The firm could inspect its continuity. The undersigned could stop describing swifts and simply point.

*[Letter, on the firm's letterhead, 24 June.]*

Dear Mr Tull,

No — and I must do you the courtesy of a real answer, because the invitation was a real invitation, bolt, workmanship and all.

Part of it is the winding-up, which is genuine: two people and a cat cannot dissolve ninety years between them and take excursions. Part of it is older. I have not slept away from Saltmarsh St Peter in eleven years, Mr Tull. The firm has been my geography as well as my calendar — I know the world three counties deep in customers' addresses and no deeper. That is not a complaint. It is a measurement, and you are a man who respects a measurement honestly taken.

But the truest part is this, and you may as well have it plainly. Your garden and I are correspondents. I know it by post: the wall by its coping, the swifts by their date, the peas by a pressed leaf. If I come and stand in it, I become a visitor, and visitors see gardens as they are — bindweed, botched corners, the disgraceful umbrella. Correspondents see gardens as they are becoming. I have reached the age where I know which view I trust my heart to. Let me keep the becoming a little longer. When the firm is wound up — when I am no longer anybody's supplier — ask me again, if the file is still open.

You write more in the margin than on the line, Mr Tull. I have begun reading your orders margin-first. I should be obliged if you would not tell anyone at this address.

Item three, since you have earned it and the barometer is looking smug: a bench at the dyke end of something, facing west. Every garden worth the name needs somewhere to sit and refuse to work.

Yours sincerely,
E. M. Prowse

*[Letter, on the firm's letterhead, 17 July.]*

Dear Mr Tull,

The plan is pinned above the order desk, where the year planner used to hang. Dilys — the firm's other person — looked at it for some time on Tuesday and said, "He draws a tidy line for a man who blathers in margins," which I pass on because praise from Dilys should never be wasted, there being so little of it and such long winters between.

I have spent more evenings than I will admit standing in your garden. I can do it from here: in at the door in the south wall — you drew its hinges, you absurd man — left along the hot wall with my hand flat on the warm brick, down the central path with the peas going over and the kale coming on, to a bench at a dyke that is not a dyke, facing west, at the hour when the wall gives back its heat and the swifts go up like sparks. I sit there most evenings between the last post and supper. The commute is nothing and the company is very quiet. You have built the one garden in England I can reach.

And so — the blank bed. I have decided, and I had decided, I think, before your drawing finished telling me the truth about what I was looking at. The seven envelopes come to you with this letter, registered post, insured for a sum that made the counter clerk in Fakenham ask if it was jewellery. Crosses 41 to 47, my sister's unfinished argument, the whole of it: her shorthand keys I have copied out fair, her selection notes, all seven years' silence. A breeder's line needs ground and a stubborn man, and the firm can no longer supply the former and never could supply the latter. Grow them out. Rogue without pity — M.'s phrase; you will find it in the notes, underlined twice. Finish the argument, and if 44 is what she believed, the middle bed will know what it was for.

I am trusting you with the only thing in the seed store that cannot be reordered. I find the sensation surprisingly like standing on a wall while somebody else certifies it.

Yours,
E. M. Prowse

M. crossed 'Saltmarsh Pink' onto her dark seedling line six times and kept nothing. The seventh — this — she kept everything. Her note reads, decoded: *"Pale standard, salt at the wing, scent doubled. If it holds true it is the one. *[Letter, received 29 July.]*

Dear Miss Prowse,

The envelopes are here. I signed for them and then sat down on item three for a while.

Undertakings of the Walled Garden, Marchfield, given this 29th day of July, witnessed by a wall:

1. The seven lines will be grown out in the middle bed, full sun, best soil, beginning with an autumn sowing under glass — the future argument has been won early; a secondhand greenhouse is being re-glazed as I write, which is to say the argument cost me £180 and two barked knuckles.
2. Records will be kept to a standard the firm may audit without notice. Rogue without pity is entered at the head of the book, underlined twice, and will be obeyed, though I have met your sister only through a shorthand key and already know she was the sort of engineer I was always glad to be checked by.
3. Nothing will be named, sold, shown, or so much as bragged about without the authority of the firm — meaning you, Miss Prowse, whatever the solicitors say the firm is or isn't by then. A line is not finished until its breeder's executor says so. I looked up the law of it. Then I decided the law had nothing to do with it.
4. They will be grown as if she were watching. This is not a figure of speech. It is a working method, and I have used it for three years in another connection, and I can certify that it holds load.

You signed your last letter differently. I am an engineer, Miss Prowse: I notice when a structure sheds a word it has carried for six months, and I know what it means about the loading underneath. I have made no remark. I am making no remark now, at some length.

Yours,
H. Tull

# Part Three — Lifting Season

*[Letter, on the firm's letterhead, 19 August.]*

Dear Mr Tull,

You will have gathered from the gap in my letters that the winding-up has entered the stage where it is conducted in schedules. The auction is fixed: the 14th of October, here, on the premises, Whitlow & Deane presiding. Everything goes. The seed-cleaning machinery my father mortgaged the house for in 1962; the counter I did my school work under; the seed store itself — some nineteen hundred accessions, which the auctioneers propose to offer as a single lot, there being, as young Mr Deane explained to me kindly, "no trade in it, but you sometimes get an eccentric." May we all be so lucky.

The order books, Marian's notebooks, and the herbarium were scheduled for disposal — of no commercial value, the phrase is — unless lotted. I have lotted them. I sat one whole evening with the alternative, Mr Tull, and I find I would rather they were sold to a stranger who wants them a little than skipped by a schedule that wants them not at all. Better sold than skipped. It is not a maxim I ever expected to live by. The winding-up is an education in the maxims one can live by.

I have one thing to ask of you, and I ask it plainly because our correspondence has earned plain asking. Do not come to the sale. I know what your kindness would want to do with the day — I have had a spring and summer of your kindness and can plot its curve. But I could not stand in that yard, watching the counter go for less than a decent spade will cost by then, and also be watched by you. You are the correspondent of the best of this firm — the catalogue, the continuity, the becoming. Let the trestle tables happen to strangers. Remember us as the list.

The swifts will have left you by now. They go all at once, I am told, on a morning you can never predict and always recognise.

Yours,
E. M. Prowse

*[Letter, received 26 August.]*

Dear Miss Prowse,

You have my word. I will not set foot in Saltmarsh St Peter on the 14th of October. You may have the word in writing, witnessed by the wall, and you may plot its curve: my word, once given, does not deflect.

Since I am forbidden the day itself, accept instead a short course in decommissioning, from the only branch of it I know:

1. When a bridge is taken out of service, the engineers do not pretend it never stood. They record it. The drawings are archived, the loads it carried are entered as historical fact, and the men who maintained it are, if there is any decency in the district, stood a dinner.
2. Nothing sold on the 14th unhappens. Every packet that firm ever posted is out in the world, still germinating in gardens that have forgotten where the habit of 'Norfolk Morning' came from. Lots 41 to 43 — the seed store, the books, Marian — went together, and went strangely. *[Letter, dated 1 November. The engineer's italic, pressed harder than usual. Docketed, in the recipient's hand: "Rec'd 3 Nov. Unanswered."]*

Dear Miss Prowse,

You are owed a full account, and here it is, with nothing load-bearing left out.

When your August letter told me the books and notebooks had been scheduled for disposal — no commercial value — I was frightened. That is the accurate word; an engineer is trained to report the accurate word. Lot 41 was nineteen hundred living accessions with a germination deadline; Lots 42 and 43 were your firm's mind and your sister's argument; and the room, as you yourself wrote, wanted the brass and not the paper. I saw one chance in three of a dealer, one in three of a pulper, one in three of an eccentric. I decided to be the eccentric. I instructed Coker because a known bidder drives prices against himself, and — write it plain, Tull — because you had asked me not to come, and an agent let me keep the letter of your asking while I broke what your asking was made of. I kept my word by its weakest member and let the rest of the structure fail. I have inspected enough failures to know that is how the worst ones always go: not the bolt, but the assumption behind the bolt.

What I ought to have done was write to you in September and ask leave. I did not, and I will tell you the true reason, which does me no credit: I was afraid you would say no — that pride would rather see the mind of your firm skipped than rescued conspicuous­ly — and I decided my judgement of what you could bear was sounder than your right to decide it. That is the whole error, Miss Prowse. It has a long engineering pedigree and it has drowned better men than me.

What I want you to know is narrow, and it is this: I did not buy your firm. I am not a collector, and this was never acquisition. Every accession in Lot 41 is racked in the bothy at the humidity your own storage notes specify — I found the notes in Lot 42, which is the only use I have made of Lot 42. Nothing will be sold, shown, lent, or bragged of. Nothing is mine except the caretaking. It was bought so that it could not be bought, and it waits — all of it — on the only authority the garden recognises.

I ask nothing, which is as well, since I appear to have taken the asking privileges out with the foundations. The file marked 4,117 is not closed. But it is yours, and so is the closing of it.

H. Tull
