# Nederlandse editie (NL) — NOTES

## What this is

A complete literary Dutch (Netherlands register) translation of *The
Sweetwater Sea*. Source text: `../../en/the-sweetwater-sea.md` at its
merged state on `main` @ `0375099b918481412611d33e1157ee1fb1679918`
(verbatim `wc -w` **15,243** words, including title, front matter, and
chapter headings; re-measured identical this session). All 12 chapters
translated in full — every line finished prose, no summary placeholders.
The EN manuscript stays the source of truth for the story; fixes
propagate EN → NL, never the reverse.

This title is the inverse translation case of the catalog's cozy-fantasy
NL editions: the EN text describes *Dutch* realities to English readers
(Wieringen 1927–1932, the Afsluitdijk, the death of the Zuiderzee
fishery), so the NL edition is not a transplant but a **repatriation** —
the book returns to the language of its own setting, and the EN's
explanatory glosses of Dutch terms must be reversed, not translated (see
the Gloss reversions section, the first nonempty one in the catalog).

Provenance: run under **ORDER 011 item 2 remainder** (owner night
directive 2026-07-14: NL editions of the catalog's EN-only titles —
this title and Night Kiln Book 3 were the two named remainders after
the 11-of-13 NL line). The NL title was **pre-named in
`../../DECISIONS.md`** ("Natural Dutch-market title: *De zoete zee*
(subtitle *Een novelle van de Zuiderzee*)") and echoed by the EN
packet §3; this slice carries it out.

## Title decision

**Chosen: *De zoete zee*** (subtitle ***Een novelle van de Zuiderzee***
— **mandatory**, carried from the EN packet §2 where the subtitle is the
standing mitigation, and re-argued for the NL market below). **The title
was pre-named, not re-decided here:** `../../DECISIONS.md` records it
with its reasoning ("the pun in 'zoet' (sweet/fresh water) restores what
the English title paraphrases"), and the EN packet
(`docs/publishing/vetting/the-sweetwater-sea.md` §3) echoes the flag.
In Dutch the pun is native and exact: *zoet water* IS the ordinary term
for fresh water, so the title says in two words what the book does in
twelve chapters — the sea turning sweet is the sea dying and the season
continuing.

Collision scan, dated **2026-07-14** (`date -u`: Tue Jul 14 03:22 UTC
2026; WebSearch, three queries — run despite the pre-naming, and it
found real neighbors; recorded honestly):

- **`"De zoete zee" boek roman titel`** — the phrase is a **live Dutch
  literary commons**, not an empty coinage: *De sprong der paarden en de
  zoete zee* (Harry Mulisch, 1955 novella,
  [nl.wikipedia](https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_sprong_der_paarden_en_de_zoete_zee))
  carries it inside a longer title; **an exact-title nonfiction
  collision exists** — *De zoete zee* (Cherry Duyns, photo book on the
  IJsselmeer and surroundings, photographs 1945–2000,
  [bol.com](https://www.bol.com/nl/nl/p/zoete-zee/666862342/),
  [boekwinkeltjes](https://www.boekwinkeltjes.nl/b/199431040/De-zoete-zee/));
  and the Zuiderzee-memory site
  [100jaarzuiderzeewet.com/zoete-zee](https://www.100jaarzuiderzeewet.com/zoete-zee)
  uses the phrase for the IJsselmeer itself.
- **`"zoete zee" bol.com boek`** — **the material finding: *Zoete zee*
  (Arie Kok, novel,
  [bol.com](https://www.bol.com/nl/nl/f/zoete-zee/9200000085931828/)) is
  a near-exact-title NOVEL in the SAME register** — per the storefront
  description, a fishing community struggling against the water, the
  plans of officials and engineers, and a fading belief in providence.
  Same water, same era-shelf, one article short of the same title. Also:
  *Vissen op een zoete zee* (Gmelich Meijling-Van Hemert, nonfiction on
  IJsselmeer professional fishery,
  [bol.com](https://www.bol.com/nl/p/vissen-op-een-zoete-zee/1001004005652928/)).
- **`"zoete zee" Nederlands`** — non-book noise confirming the phrase is
  a living IJsselmeer epithet: a Medemblik beach pavilion named De Zoete
  Zee ([dezoetezee.nl](https://www.dezoetezee.nl/)) on the IJsselmeer
  shore, plus Rijkswaterstaat zoet-zout material.

**Verdict: Moderate — and honestly higher than any prior NL scan in the
catalog.** The exact-title collision (Duyns) is out-of-genre
(photography); the in-genre collision (Kok) is out-of-title by one word
but on the same shelf. This is the Dutch mirror of the EN packet's
finding ("Sweetwater Sea" = the Great Lakes' *La Mer Douce* epithet
commons): in both languages the title names a shared poetic commons
rather than an unclaimed coinage, and in both the subtitle does the
disambiguation work — which is why *Een novelle van de Zuiderzee* is
**mandatory on cover and listing**, and why NL title ratification is
queued as a genuine owner decision **with the Kok and Duyns findings in
view**, not a rubber stamp. No alternative was scanned: the pick is
pre-named and the pun is the book's own closing logic; if the owner
declines it, renaming is a new decision slice. Standing caveat carried
from all prior NL packets: Dutch self-pub titles are unevenly
web-indexed; final KDP/bol.com recheck at upload is the owner's §7 step.

## Gloss reversions

**The catalog's first nonempty reversion list.** The EN text glosses
real Dutch terms for English readers; a Dutch reader needs no gloss for
Dutch words, so those glosses are REVERSED (dropped, or reworked so the
Dutch does the work natively). Every reversion, logged:

1. Ch 1 — "ansjovis, the anchovy, a green-backed sliver of a sea fish"
   → the EN translation-pair collapses to the one native word: "de
   ansjovis, een groenruggig splintertje zeevis".
2. Ch 1 — "a Wieringer aak … the local workboat of these shallows": the
   explanatory clause dropped (the clog simile kept — it is imagery,
   not gloss).
3. Ch 1 — "Zwart's kuiperij — the cooperage —" → "de kuiperij van
   Zwart" (the dash-gloss dropped).
4. Ch 1 — "the size of a stuiver, a five-cent piece" → "ter grootte
   van een stuiver" (the coin gloss dropped).
5. Ch 2 — "boulder clay brought up dripping…, keileem, the old
   blue-grey clay the ice ages had left" → the leading EN tag "boulder
   clay" dropped; "keileem" leads and the ice-age description stays as
   prose (it is Dirk's wonder, not a dictionary entry).
6. Ch 3 — "RIJKSDIENST — the state service for the support law, the
   Zuiderzeesteunwet, the act of 'twenty-five…" → the office gloss
   dropped; the dog-name joke about "de steunwet" survives natively.
7. Ch 3 — "the law grants a tegemoetkoming, a coming-toward; the word
   is exact" → REWORKED, not just cut: the clerk's etymology point is
   made directly in Dutch morphology — "het woord is nauwkeurig …
   tegemoetkomen is geen aankomen."
8. Ch 5 — "men were making zinkstukken — sinking-mattresses, brushwood
   mattresses, osier willow…" → both EN translation glosses dropped;
   the craft description (teenwilg, wiepen, gesjord) kept as what it
   is, awe.
9. Ch 6 — the ledger heading "NA. Afterward." → "NA." (the EN gloss
   "Afterward." dropped — NA is already the Dutch word; "het vaatje
   van naderhand" keeps the echo).
10. Ch 11 — "the one word *bruiloft*. Wedding." → "het ene woord
    *bruiloft*." (the EN gloss-sentence dropped).
11. Ch 11 — "designated the IJsselmeer — the lake of the IJssel —" →
    "aangeduid als het IJsselmeer" (the etymology gloss dropped; no
    Dutch reader parses their own lake's name).
12. Ch 11/12 — "*Visserij, voortgezet.* Fishery, continued." → the
    Dutch line stands alone; the EN translation dropped (twice-held
    line, the book's account-settling entry).

Kept deliberately (not reversions): the EN's *in-scene* teaching
appositions that are speech or lived description rather than translator
gloss — the fyke description in Ch 7 ("gehoepelde netvallen zo lang als
een man") stays as imagery inside Klaas's gear-inventory; the anchovy
cure exposition of Ch 1 is the trade's own catechism and translates as
prose; "dertien uur twee" keeps its in-text expansion because the EN's
"thirteen hours two, the engineers logged it" is narration, not gloss.

## Market note (honest)

  and the drowned Zuiderzee fishery are core Dutch cultural memory

  collision scan surfaced a same-register Dutch novel (*Zoete zee*, Arie
  Kok) and a same-title photo book (Duyns). That cuts both ways and is
  recorded both ways: the shelf demonstrably exists; the title lands on
  it beside neighbors.
