# THE SWEETWATER SEA

*A novella of the Zuiderzee*

> Wieringen, 1927. Jaap Rotgans has salted anchovy for forty years on a sea the whole country has decided to close. His only son has taken work on the dam that will do it. Five years remain — of tides, of runs, of a marriage and a trade — before the last gap shuts and the salt goes out of the water for good.

---

**Content note:** period hardship and the end of a fishing trade; a quiet book — no violence.

# Chapter One — The Salting Shed

The anchovy was a fish you waited for, and Jaap Rotgans had built a life on the waiting.

It came into the Zuiderzee on the warmth, in the middle of May, in the years it chose to come at all: ansjovis, the anchovy, a green-backed sliver of a sea fish that wintered out beyond the islands in water no Wieringer had ever seen, and arrived wanting the one thing the shallow sea had to sell, which was warmth over sand to spawn on. Some springs it came in such numbers that the nets lifted silver out of the dark like coin out of a purse, and a man paid off his boat in six weeks. Some springs it did not come at all, and you ate the winter twice. Nobody could say beforehand which sort of spring had been sent; his father had fished it, and his father before that, and none of the three of them had ever claimed to know the fish's mind, which Jaap considered the beginning of honesty in the trade.

This spring it had come.

They went out from Den Oever in the dark, he and Dirk, the boat sliding down the harbor on the last of the ebb — a Wieringer aak, broad and flat-bottomed as a clog, the local workboat of these shallows, leeboards up along her sides like folded wings, WR 43 painted on the bow above a name, *Twee Gebroeders*, that his father had chosen for reasons that had not outlived him. The nets stood where they had set them the evening before, a long fleet of fine-meshed wall strung on staves at the edge of the tide-flats, and in the first grey light the corks were stuttering, which is a thing a man will get out of bed at two in the morning his whole life to see. They hauled by hand, the two of them, the net coming in over the gunwale with a sound like rain going backwards, and the fish hung in the meshes by their gills in the hundreds, then in the thousands, each one the length of a finger and the width of a knife blade, green over the back and silver under, and the silver not one silver but many, the way the sea itself is never one grey.

"Good wall," said Dirk, meaning that stretch of net, and it was all either of them said for an hour, there being nothing else that needed establishing.

By seven they were back inside the harbor with the hold shining. By half past, the shed had them.

The salting shed stood behind the house with its doors to the north, because salt likes cool and a curing fish likes it better, and it had been his grandfather's before it was his, and smelled of the whole of both intervening lives: brine and tar, oak and net-bark, fish oil gone old in the grain of the benches, and under everything the dry grey presence of the salt itself, which was not a smell at all, Trijn said, but the place where a smell would have been. Trijn had the tubs standing ready. Four women from the harbor houses came in at eight with their own knives and sat to the fish as they had sat to it every good May since girlhood: a twist of thumb against blade and the head came away with the gut following it out in one soft string, the little body dropped in the rinsing tub, the heads going by the thousand into the offal barrow, and the count kept aloud in the old way, so that a woman's wage and a fish's fate were the same arithmetic in the same air.

Jaap did the salting himself. That was not pride, or not only pride; it was that salting was judgment, and judgment was the one part of a trade that could not be hired. Coarse grey salt, a fistful thrown ringing across the keg bottom. Then the fish laid in courses the way a bricklayer lays headers, bellies up, tails to the stave, each course crossing the one beneath it so the keg was bonded like a wall. Then salt, then fish, then salt, to the crown. The kegs themselves came from Zwart's kuiperij — the cooperage — two streets in from the harbor, oak ankertjes hooped tight enough to hold weather in, and Simon Zwart still burned his mark into every head as though anyone on Wieringen would mistake his work for another man's. On the crowned top course went the round loose lid, and on the lid a beach stone worn to the shape of exactly this duty by forty years of performing it, and by evening the brine had risen up over the wood of its own accord, drawn out of the fish by the salt, which always looked, however often a man had seen it, like a small act of weather happening indoors.

He proved his pickle with a hen's egg. Float it high and the brine would burn the fish to leather; float it low and the keg would soften and sweeten and be lost entire; between the two there was a right height, a cap of shell standing above the water the size of a stuiver, a five-cent piece, and his thumb knew it a breath before his eye did. Then the kegs went to the dark side of the shed, ranked on rails off the floor, and began the one part of the work no man could hurry, which was becoming themselves. Anchovy is not herring. Herring you can eat green out of the barrel. Anchovy goes into the salt a stiff bright silver thing and lies in the dark a year at the least — two was better — and comes out the far side of that year transformed: the flesh gone the deep red-brown of old brick, the bone gone to nothing, the taste gone from fish to something spread thin on rich men's bread in Amsterdam and Paris and called by other languages. The red was the cure and the cure was the price. Last May's kegs stood curing behind this May's, and the May before stood behind those, ready money in oak, so that the shed held three years at any hour of the day, and a man who walked its aisle walked in his own past and future both, ranked and stenciled and quietly turning red in the dark.

Trijn kept the ledger. That was the shape of the marriage, or the part of the shape that showed: he judged the fish and she judged everything else, the salt bills and the keg bills and the women's tally, the buyer's opening offer and the distance it would travel before she let it rest. She had been keeping it thirty years, in a hand as upright as the day the schoolmaster had approved it, and what she wanted from the world was not what Jaap wanted, which was for it to hold still; what Trijn wanted was to see it coming. Certainty first, she said. Pride is for afterwards, when it is paid for.

The buyer came up from Enkhuizen in the second week of June, as the buyers of that firm had come for longer than anyone living, and stood in the shed aisle in his town shoes while Jaap drew the bung of a keg of the 'twenty-five cure and lifted two fish out on the blade of a knife. The buyer ate one there, standing, head back, the way a man drinks. He looked at the roof beams for a moment.

"Roof-tile red," he said. "You could price this one in French."

"You could," said Trijn, from the doorway, "and then we would both know what it is worth while you offered me less. Come inside where the coffee is."

The dance took an hour, as it always took an hour, and ended where both parties had known by the second cup it would end, which was, Jaap understood, the whole use of the hour: not to move the price but to prove the walls of it still stood. He carried the samples down to the buyer's motor launch afterwards, and it was there, on the harbor, in the flat gold of six o'clock, that the buyer paused with one shoe on his gunwale and nodded out and to the north, past the harbor light, at the two black shapes standing off the shore where no shapes had stood at Whitsun.

"You've seen your visitors, I suppose," the buyer said.

Jaap had seen them. The whole of Den Oever had seen them; men had walked up on the sea-dike after church on Sunday and stood in a line looking at them and come down again saying very little. Dredgers. Iron ones, sea-going, each the size of a church laid on its side, lying to moorings off the harbor with barges rafted alongside and the spoil coming up out of the sea bottom bucket after bucket after bucket, a sound you could hear when the wind lay right, a slow chained clanking like an anchor being weighed that was never done weighing. The law had been passed in 'eighteen — everyone knew that, the way everyone knew the date of a distant relative's marriage — and for nine years the law had stayed paper, a thing in The Hague, argued about in the papers by engineers and ministers, while the sea went on being the sea. Now the paper had put on iron and come to anchor within sight of his nets.

"They're starting the big one, then," the buyer said, in the voice of a man who has read about it. "Here to Friesland in one line, they say. Shut the whole box." He got down into his launch and looked up, and something in him — Jaap gave him this much, then and later — had the grace to be embarrassed. "Well. There's years in it yet, Rotgans. Nothing closes tomorrow."

"No," Jaap said. "Nothing closes tomorrow."

He walked back up the harbor in the long light. On the flats to the north the tide had turned and was coming in over the sand the way it had come in over that sand ten thousand times for every year of his age, and the dredgers rode on it, working; they did not keep tides. He and Dirk rowed out at dusk and set the walls of net in the old order, stave by stave, and the last thing Jaap saw over his shoulder as they pulled for home was the riding-light of the nearer dredger burning on the water like the lamp of a ship at anchor — a ship at anchor, he thought, with no intention, now or ever, of getting under way.

# Chapter Two — Dry Wages

In July, with the anchovy done and the kegs lidded and the nets stripped off their staves for barking, Dirk bicycled to Den Oever village in his church trousers and was gone the length of a tide, and came back and stood in the doorway of the shed with his cap in both hands, which was how Jaap knew before a word was said. Nobody holds his cap in both hands to bring good news to a shed.

"They're taking men on at the works," Dirk said. "Grondwerk first, then on the stone if you show yourself worth it. I put my name in." He turned the cap a quarter-turn, as though setting it by compass. "They took it."

Jaap went on doing what he was doing, which was paying tarred twine off a spool for the net-mending, because a man's hands in those first moments are best given something they already know. The quarrel that followed was conducted the way the Rotgans family had always conducted its weather, in single sentences set well apart, in a shed, quietly, with both parties working.

"Whose gang?"

"Van der Oord's. Wieringers mostly. Two Urkers." A pause of some minutes, net-needle going. "It's the company has the contract — the MUZ, they call it, the big combine, all the dredging firms in the country under one hat. They're paying ground-workers more than I clear in a middling May."

"In a good May," his father said, "you clear more than any of them."

"And in a bad May," Dirk said, not loudly, "I mend nets and eat your winter." He said it with the tarred twine in his own hands, working, matching his father knot for knot, which took some of the sting out and left all of the truth in. He was twenty-four that summer, made like his mother's brothers, big through the shoulders, easy on the water, better with the wall-nets than his father would ever say aloud; and he had been to school two years longer than Jaap had, and read the paper down to the shipping columns, and knew to the guilder what the works were paying, and to the year — this was the part Jaap could not forgive the paper for printing — what the engineers said would happen to the salt when the box was shut.

"The dike gets built whether a Rotgans carries stone for it or not," Dirk said, at the end, when the net between them was whole again. "That's not me talking for the dike. That's arithmetic. It gets built. The only question left standing is whether the wages of it come into this house or go past it."

"And the fish?"

"I'll fish the Mays with you. Every May, as long as there's a May." Dirk stood up and put his cap on, and being his father's son he could not leave without one plain nail driven in to hold the rest. "You've said it yourself, every spring I can remember: nobody can say beforehand what sort of year is coming. Well," he said, "now somebody can. That's all that's changed. Somebody can."

He went in to his supper. Jaap stayed in the shed until the light went, not working, sitting on the salting bench with his hands loose between his knees, in the smell of brine and bark and three ranked years of cure, and found that what was in him was not anger, or was anger only the way the skin of a thing is the thing. Under it sat something older and worse, which he would not name that evening and did not have to: that the boy had done his sums, and the sums were right, and being right they would now begin, guilder by guilder and season by season, to come true.

⁂

Trijn said nothing at all.

That was the loudest part of the month. She had opinions the way the sea had water, and a known custom of issuing them, and on this she said nothing: not at the table when Dirk told her, not in the kitchen after, not in the dark where thirty years of the day's residue had always been settled between them before sleep. She heard the boy out with her hands still on the bread knife, and looked at him a long moment, and said, "Wash before you come to my table from that clay," which was consent, or at least was not war, and then she opened the ledger.

Jaap watched her do it from the doorway. She sat under the lamp with the book open to the current page, and she took the steel pen, and she ruled, with two clean strokes against the edge of the almanac, a new column down the right-hand side of the household's life. She did not blot it. She sat looking at the ruled emptiness of it for a while, the way you look at a field in March.

Dirk started on the Monday. He went off before light with his bread in a tin and his boots over his shoulder, on the bicycle, eastward along the sea-dike where the works had their yards, and came back the first Saturday grey to the eyebrows with the ground of the sea itself — for that was what they were shifting, he said, eating like a stove, the sea's own floor, boulder clay brought up dripping from under the sand, keileem, the old blue-grey clay the ice ages had left, that gripped like a fist when it dried; the engineers had found the sea was lying on the one material in the world you could build its coffin from — and he laid his first week's board money on the table by his mother's place, and went to bed at eight like a child.

Trijn entered it. Jaap, passing behind her chair with his pipe, looked once — he had promised himself he would not, and looked once — at the head of the new column, at what she had chosen, in that upright schoolroom hand, to call the money the dike paid into a fishing house.

She had written *D.*

Only that. Not the boy's name, not the work's. One letter, serviceably ambiguous, standing at the head of the column like a man at a door with his cap in both hands; and Jaap put his pipe in his mouth and went out into the yard, because he could read his wife's book the way she could read his brine, and he knew a kept option when he saw one. *D* for Dirk, if the sea won. *D* for dijk, if it didn't. She was not going to spend the difference before it was paid out. Certainty first; pride is for afterwards, when it is paid for.

Out on the dark flats to the north, small and steady, the dredger's riding-light. He smoked the pipe out facing it, not from defiance, which he would have been ashamed of in a man of his age, but because a fisherman looks at the weather that is coming, that being the whole of what looking is for; and this, he had begun that summer to understand, was the weather that was coming: a light on the water that did not rise or fall with the tide, and his son asleep in the house behind him with the sea's floor under his fingernails, and in the ledger, drying, a single initial that his wife — who loved them both, the man and the boy, the salt and the wage, with the same undivided practical ferocity — had left herself room to resolve in either direction.

# Chapter Three — The Counting

The clerk came out to Wieringen in the last week of February, 1928, when the boats were up and the sea had nothing for anyone, which showed, Trijn said afterwards, that somebody in that office knew fishermen: you count a man in the season when counting is all he has.

He came on the bus that ran the new dike-road from the mainland — for Wieringen was an island only in its bones now; the short dam at the Amsteldiep had tied it to North Holland four years since, the first knot in the whole long lashing, and the island had grumbled and then bought bicycles — and he walked down the harbor at Den Oever asking for the Rotgans house by name, with a portfolio under his arm and his trouser-ends bicycle-clipped though he had no bicycle, a precaution, Jaap said later, which described the entire man. He was perhaps forty. He had a printed card: RIJKSDIENST — the state service for the support law, the Zuiderzeesteunwet, the act of 'twenty-five that everyone on the island called simply the steunwet, the way you call a dog by the half of its name it answers to.

"It is an assessment, not a levy," he said, standing in the kitchen with his hat already off. "Nothing is taken. Nothing, I am bound to say plainly, is promised either — the law grants a tegemoetkoming, a coming-toward; the word is exact, mevrouw, and I am required by honesty to point out that a coming-toward is not an arrival. What the law requires today is only that the fishery be written down as it stands: vessel, gear, premises, and the average yield of recent years. So that when the water changes, what was lost is a matter of record and not of memory."

"And a record kept by whom?" Jaap asked, from beside the stove.

"By me," the clerk said, without irony, and set his portfolio on the table like a man setting down something heavier than it looked. "I have written down four harbors since Michaelmas. I will write down every boat on this sea before I am finished, or the man who replaces me will."

Jaap looked at the forms — the state's grey columns, boat and gear and shed and man, all of it to be reduced to figures and the figures filed in The Hague against the day the water went — and what rose in him was not reasonable and he knew it was not reasonable and it rose anyway, the way the tide comes up through the floor of a boat with a started plank. They wished to measure him for the coffin while he stood in his boots. Write down the aak so the aak could be grieved bureaucratically. He took his cap off the hook.

"You'll excuse me," he said. "I have work in the shed," which was true in the sense that a shed always has work in it, and he went out and split kindling that did not need splitting and racked kegs that were racked, and through the north wall, for above an hour, he could hear it going forward without him: the clerk's low voice putting its patient questions, and Trijn's voice answering from the doorway between kitchen and shed, where she had planted herself with her arms folded — inside her own house, on the sill of her own trade, positioned, he understood even in his anger, exactly where a woman stands who intends to cooperate with the state and guard her husband's back in the same afternoon.

She answered everything. She answered it from the ledger, without once opening the ledger. The aak, built at the Lastdrager yard, rebuilt forward of the mast in 'nineteen, and worth — she named a figure that was correct to within five guilders, and Jaap, splitting kindling, heard the clerk's pen hesitate as though the exactness itself required a different ink. The wall-nets, by fleets and by age. The shed, the tubs, the stones, the standing stock of empty kegs from the kuiperij. The women hired by the May, and their tally-wages. The yield: and here she gave him the last five years one by one, the fat 'twenty-four, the lean 'twenty-six, in kegs and in guilders, and then, unasked, the ten years before that, "because five years of this fishery is a sample of nothing, meneer; the anchovy does not keep your accounting periods."

"That," said the clerk, writing, "is the most useful sentence anyone has said to me since Michaelmas."

At the end there were the personal columns. Age of the fisherman: fifty-eight. Other trades practiced: none. Sons in the fishery: and there the house went quiet enough that Jaap's axe, out in the shed, went quiet with it.

"One son," Trijn said. "Twenty-four. You may leave his line empty."

"He fishes with his father, mevrouw. The May run, I have it from the harbormaster."

"He fishes the Mays," Trijn said, "and the rest of the year he is on the works at a wage. He is not a claim on your law, meneer. He is already on your side of the book."

The pen wrote it. What the pen made of it the pen did not say.

The clerk left on the evening bus. At the door, hat on, portfolio buckled, he paused in the blue dusk and looked down the harbor at the boats standing black on their winter legs, and he said the only thing he said all day that was not on a form. "My father fished from Vollenhove," he said. "Over on the Overijssel shore. I grew up to the smell of what your shed smells of. I am aware, mevrouw — the office in The Hague is aware, whatever the island thinks of us — that we are counting something that cannot be paid for. The law does not pretend the sea can be kept open. The law only means that when it closes, no man's loss shall be lost. It is little. I would rather carry little than nothing."

When he was well gone, Jaap came in from the shed and stood by the table where the forms had lain. Trijn was setting out the supper. He watched her back for a while.

"You gave them everything," he said. "To the guilder."

"I gave them numbers." She set down the pan and turned round to him, and her face had in it the thing that was always in it, which he had married her for and been governed by for thirty-one years, the refusal to let a feeling stand where a fact would bear more weight. "The numbers were never the part worth keeping, Jaap. If the water stays, the paper rots in a drawer in The Hague and no harm done. If the water goes" — she said it flatly, the first time either of them had said it indoors — "then the only Rotgans fishery left standing anywhere on this earth will be the one I put into that man's portfolio this afternoon, written down true. Refuse the count and you refuse the record. I won't have you kept out of the book, not by them and not by your own stiff neck. Now sit. It's eel."

He sat. Later, when she was asleep, he took the lamp to the dresser and opened the ledger, not for the figures but because a man checks his moorings; and he found, folded small into the back board where she kept the papers that were owed and owing — the buyer's standing contract, the kuiperij receipts, his own father's discharge from the naval militia in 'seventy-one — the carbon flimsy of the clerk's completed form, every column in that upright hand, the whole salt kingdom of his life set down in grey duplicate. Across the top margin, where the state had printed FISHERY, ZUIDER ZEE, CATEGORY A, she had written nothing at all; and it was a measure of the winter, and of the year, and of the light on the water to the north of the flats, that Jaap Rotgans stood in his stocking feet reading his wife's silence in the margin of a government form and found it, of all the documents in the house, the one he believed.

# Chapter Four — Roof-Tile Red

The 'twenty-eight anchovy came in early and fat, on a warm south wind at the middle of May, and ran for five weeks, and was the best season Jaap Rotgans had taken off that ground in eleven years; and the whole of it, from the first stuttering cork to the last crowned keg, landed on the island like good news read out at a sickbed.

That was the thing no one had warned him of, and it would have been beyond warning: that a good year could be made unbearable by standing where it stood. The women sat to the fish and the count went aloud and the money came into the houses, and everyone worked with a kind of doubled attention, doing the thing and watching themselves do it, the way you eat at a funeral. Klaas Lont, hauling beside him at dawn on the shared ground east of the harbor, put it the way Klaas put things. "Fine wall this morning," he said, swinging a full stave aboard. "They can carve that on us. *Fine wall that morning.*"

In June the buyer from Enkhuizen ate his sample fish and looked at the roof beams and said "roof-tile red," as he had said it every June that anyone kept track of; and then, over the second cup at Trijn's table, he did the new thing. He took this year's kegs at last year's price and a shade over. He praised the cure. And when Trijn slid the standing contract across the cloth for its yearly renewal — the firm's paper, three seasons at fixed terms, signed in that kitchen every June since before the war — he did not take out his pen. He turned his cup a half-turn on its saucer. The firm was consolidating its forward positions, he said. The word had come down from the partners; it was general, it was no reflection on the house of Rotgans, whose cure the firm would take gladly year by year at the year's price, for as long — and here the man had at least the decency to look at the window and not at either of them — for as long as the fishery stood.

"Year by year," Trijn said.

"Year by year, mevrouw."

"A trade that must lie two years in the keg," she said, "bought one year at a time." She left it there, it not being her custom to make a guest eat his own arithmetic; but after the launch had gone she stood a long while at the shed door, looking down the aisle at the ranked cure — the 'twenty-eight salting that would not be money until 'thirty, the 'twenty-seven behind it — at three years of the family's labor lying in oak in the dark, ripening on a promise that the world at the far end of the ripening would still be there to want it. That was what the man had come to say, in the town manner, with compliments: that Enkhuizen had done its sums on the closing. Everyone was doing sums now. The state on its forms, the boy in his wage-book, the firm in its forward positions. The only party to the matter that had not yet put in figures was the sea.

⁂

The young men went that summer the way sand goes off a bar, grain by grain and then all at once. It was not only Dirk; Dirk had merely been early. By August, of the six young men who had crewed regularly out of the harbor within Jaap's sight, four were on the works — two at the great locks building at Den Oever, one on the dredgers, one gone across to the far side entirely, to the Frisian shore, to the second work-front driving west to meet the first; and where the young men's wages went, other things followed. Aal Snoek's daughter, who had headed fish at the Rotgans bench three Mays running, married a crane-greaser in September and moved to the new houses by the works, and her mother sat to the fish the next May with one eye forever on the door, as though the trade itself might get up and follow the girl out of it.

You could not even hold it against them, and Jaap, who tried, said so to Klaas on the water. The works paid Saturday money every Saturday. The sea paid what it paid when it paid. A man of fifty-eight could set those two truths side by side and choose the sea, because his choosing was forty years done and the sea had, on the whole and averaged out, kept its bargain with him. A man of twenty-two, with a girl, in 1928, standing between a wage he could name and a water the engineers had already named — there was no argument to have with him. There was only the watching him go.

The dike, that second summer, came up over the horizon and stayed.

It had been shapes before: dredgers, spoil-barges, a smudge of activity off Den Oever that a man could sail away from. Now, from the fishing grounds north and east of the island, there was a line. It lay on the water to the northward like a low bank of weather that never lifted and never came on, dark, level, longer every month, with the cranes standing over its working ends like herons over a ditch; and on clear evenings the sun went down behind the western reach of it and threw its shadow up the tide, and the fishermen worked their nets inside that shadow and said nothing about it, because what was there to say that the line did not say better.

It was in October, running home before a freshening northeaster with the season's last shrimp in the hold, that Jaap caught himself. The light was going, the island low and grey ahead, and he stood at the tiller and took his bearing as he had taken it ten thousand times — church tower over harbor light — and found that his eye, before the tower, before the light, had gone first and of itself to the dike: had picked up the long dark certainty of it lying across the north and steadied the whole grey world against it, and come home along it as along a handrail.

He did not tell anyone that. But he stood differently at the tiller the rest of the way in, like a man who has heard, in his own chest, the first tick of a change of wind; for a thing you take your bearings from is no longer the weather, it is the coast; and he had felt it happen, between one glance and the next, thirty months before the engineers would close their gap: the sea he worked had begun, quietly, in the eye of its own fishermen, to have a far shore.

# Chapter Five — The Willow Island

In the first week of November, 1929, with the boats not yet up and a spell of still grey weather lying on the sea like a held breath, Jaap loaded the aak with his son's winter oilskins, two sacks of his wife's potatoes, and a nine-year-old keg of anchovy with the Zwart mark on the head, and sailed out alone to Breezanddijk, the island the works had made in the middle of the sea, to look at the thing that was eating his water. The oilskins were the reason he gave. The keg was for the cook of Dirk's barracks, because a man does not arrive anywhere with one hand as long as the other. The look was the truth of it, and everyone concerned, including Trijn, who had packed the potatoes, knew the truth of it, and nobody said it aloud, the family gift.

Three hours out, the sound reached him before the shape did — a low working rumble across the still water, punctual iron, tipping-wagons and crane-chain and somewhere under it the deep breathing of a pump that never stopped — and then the shape itself stood up out of the sea, and Jaap, who had promised himself on the way out that he would be just to it, found that justice was not going to be the difficulty. The difficulty was going to be size.

Where there had been water — where he had fished, where his father had fished, four fathoms of good anchovy ground with a clean sand bottom, a place with no name because the sea does not need names inside itself — there was a harbor. Not a landing, not a stage: a harbor, moles and quays and shear-legs, and behind it an island of made ground with a town on it: barracks in ranks, a canteen, a shop, a water tower, lines of rail everywhere with wagons rolling, a church-shed with a bell, electric light strung pole to pole and burning yellow in the grey afternoon, the whole of it standing where the chart in Jaap's head said *four fathoms, sand*; and out from the island, west toward Friesland and east toward home, went the dike itself, the double line of it, cranes along the crown, men on it small and many as mussels on a stage, and the sea on the north side lying already a shade different from the sea on the south, like the two halves of a thing being told apart.

Dirk met him at the mole, cap back, easy among it all, and took the keg under one arm and his father's mooring line in the other hand, and if there was triumph anywhere in the boy it had the grace to stay below. He walked his father through the works in the last of the light, and Jaap looked at everything and priced it in the coin he had, which was craft.

Some of it he had no coin for. The keileem he knew by now from Dirk's Saturdays — the boulder clay, the stiff blue-grey stuff of the sea's own floor, that the ice had left and the grabs brought up dripping in bites the size of a keg apiece; he took a cold handful of it off a wagon at Dirk's word and worked it in his fist, and it took the print of his fingers and held it, greasy and dense and unforgiving, and he thought: mortar. The sea had been lying all its life on the makings of its own wall. That was a hard thing to think about a sea, and he put the clay down and wiped his hand on his trousers with a thoroughness the clay did not require.

But at the mattress ground, down by the south mole where the low sheds ran, he stopped pricing and stood.

They were weaving. That was the whole of it, and it was the last thing on that iron island he had thought to find: long low staging floors by the water, and on them, in rafts bigger than the church floor at Hippolytushoef, men were making zinkstukken — sinking-mattresses, brushwood mattresses, osier willow out of the river country woven and bound into fascines thick as a man's thigh, and the fascines laid and lashed crosswise, layer on layer, into one sprung pliant enormous mat that a hundred men walked on as they built it. Basketry. He watched an old foreman go down a seam tightening lashings with a hook, wrist and elbow, the exact economy of a man mending a fyke, and Jaap stood in the cold with his hands driven in his pockets and understood the thing at last from underneath, the way you understand a boat. The sea worked at the foot of whatever you set against it; every fisherman's son knew that; it would gnaw a bare dike's base out from under its crown in a winter. So they were flooring the sea. Mat by mat, towed out on the tide and sunk with stone, they were laying a woven road across the bottom for the dike to walk on, keileem above, willow below, and the fish swimming through the dark over a made floor; and it was handwork, thumb-and-eye work, work his grandfather would have crossed the island to watch; and it was going to hold.

"Well," said Dirk beside him, quiet. It was not a question, and got no answer, and did not need one; on that ground, that afternoon, between the clay and the willow, was as near as the two of them ever came to settling it, and neither ever said less about it or meant more.

In the canteen, over coffee the color of harbor water, Dirk turned his mug around twice and mentioned, in the voice he had used at nine years old for confessing a torn net, that on Sundays the supply launch ran east to the far work-island, Kornwerderzand, on the Frisian side; and that the foreman of the stone-tippers there, a Frieslander called Hoekstra, had a daughter, called Froukje; and that he, Dirk, had been across eleven Sundays now, not counting one that was weather.

"Eleven," Jaap said, "not counting one," and drank his coffee. A man learns from his wife how to leave a thing standing without leaning on it. But rowing back out to the aak in the dark he found the fact had settled into him warm side down: the boy counted his Sundays. Rotgans men counted what mattered to them; it was in that family the whole of poetry.

He came out of the made harbor on the young flood and it was black night with the sky broken clear, and behind him the island burned — poles of yellow electric down every lane, the canteen windows, the crane-lights swinging — a lit town riding the middle of the sea where the chart in his head still said four fathoms, sand; and all the way home, with the tide under him and his own harbor light in his eye at last, small and old and oil-fed on its stone head, he sailed with that other light on his shoulder like a thing at his back in a room, and would not turn again to look at it, and knew it was there.

# Chapter Six — Fat Water

The strangeness began in the spring of 'thirty, and the first mark of it was that the shrimp came wrong — early, thick, and crowded up along grounds where a man had never needed to look for them. Then the May came, and the anchovy with it, and the fishermen of Wieringen went out onto a sea that had begun, that year, in its last narrowing, to concentrate.

The engineers could have told them; had, in fact, in the papers Dirk left folded by the lamp: the dike stood in the sea now for the better part of its length, two work-fronts closing on the middle gaps like hands being brought slowly together, and what water still went in and out of the whole vast basin went through those last openings, harder every month. And the fish that had come in through them to spawn — the fish that had come in because ten thousand springs had taught them warm water lay this side — were in a room with the doors going shut, milling, bunched by tide and channel; and the fishing, in consequence, was such as no man living had ever seen or would see again, and every net on the island was wet, and the money came in over the gunwales, and it was the worst spring of Jaap Rotgans's life.

He fished it. That must be set down first: he fished it hard, dawn and dusk, every stave and every wall, and when the aak came in loaded past the marks he went out again on the next tide. Dirk took his May weeks from the works as promised and stood across the net from his father, and on the second Friday they hauled a wall so hung with fish that the two of them together could not lift it, and had to bring it in over the roller a fathom at a time, resting like old men between, the meshes coming up solid silver out of the grey water; and Dirk laughed, twenty-six years old with the sea running off his forearms, laughed at the plain animal too-muchness of it, and Jaap heard the laugh and could not join it and could not blame it, and turned his face to the work.

Because it was theft. He had gone round and round it on the night tides and settled on the word deliberately, as a man settles on a mooring: not spoils, not providence — theft. Not from the state; the state had bought its fish with its steunwet and would call the account square. From the sea itself. The plenty in his nets was not the sea's bounty, it was the sea's substance; it was the difference between interest and capital. Forty years he had lived on what the water could spare, taken the year's increase and left the stock, and the stock had been there every spring of his life, swimming in at the deeps by the islands, and now the deeps were a gap with a date on it and the stock was in the room with him and the doors were closing, and he was carrying it out by the kegful while it circled. Every fine haul had the feel of lifting furniture from a house where a man lay dying, with the man's own son on the other end of the sideboard.

Trijn salted until the shed was full, hired a fifth woman, bought kegs from Zwart's kuiperij until Simon Zwart ran short of seasoned staves and his hoops came in bright from the mainland; and she opened, that summer, on a fresh page of the ledger, a heading the household had never carried before. NA. Afterward. Not a word more; a column, like the other column, ruled clean and waiting; and into it, week upon week, she moved the overplus of the fattest year the family had ever taken — not spent, not promised, banked in the Rijkspostspaarbank at Hippolytushoef in the forenoons, in her church coat — building, guilder by guilder out of the sea's last capital, the one keg no salt was needed for: the afterward keg, the keg of days, provision for a season she did not name and did not need to, whose weather she had seen coming for three years off her own doorstep.

⁂

And the catches ran strange, all that year, over and above their fatness — out of season, out of place, out of the sea's own grammar. Herring came up in the fine anchovy walls where herring had no business; flatfish crowded up the wrong channels; garfish showed a month early, and old men stood over the tubs on the harbor picking through silver and naming what they saw in the voices of men reading a letter with bad news folded somewhere in it still to come. On a dead-calm evening in July, hauling the last wall with Klaas Lont's botter lying a stave-length off, Jaap brought in, rolled and thrashing in three fathom of net, a sturgeon — a fish of the great rivers, armored, ancient, longer than the tallest Snoek girl, a thing he had seen twice in his life and his father perhaps five times — driven up from the deeps at the Vlie side, where the dredgers were, into water no sturgeon wanted. Klaas came alongside; the two of them looked down at it working its slow bony shoulders in the meshes, worth a week of anchovy at the Alkmaar market, and neither said what both were thinking, which was that the sea had been turned over like a drawer, and everything in it was out of its place, and the things from the very bottom were coming up into the light because the bottom was no longer theirs.

"Well," said Klaas at last. "There's your dike money, Rotgans."

Jaap knelt on the wet boards and worked the meshes off the great fish backwards, spine-plate by spine-plate, taking his time, taking a cut across the palm and not hurrying for that either; and he put it over the side head-first the way you launch a boat, and stood holding his cut hand and watching the swirl of its going, and made no answer at all. It was one fish given back out of a sea he was stripping by the kegful, and it changed no ledger and saved no fishery, and he knew all that better than any man alive. He had wanted, for one evening of that fat and dreadful year, to stand on the other side of the account; and Klaas, who had earned the right, hailed his own boat off and left him standing there, and never once, in all the years that were left to the two of them, told the story to anyone.

The buyer from Enkhuizen had taken everything back in June, paid in full, and not stayed for the second cup. There had been anchovy on every quay of the closing sea that month; his firm was buying the funeral baked meats of the whole Zuiderzee at once. He had stood on the harbor by his launch, hat in hand a moment.

"I first came to this shed with my father," he said. "You had the price of us that year, mevrouw, over the matter of the short kegs. I have never enjoyed an afternoon of business more." He looked north, where the line lay on the horizon with its cranes. "There will be sea fish in that water two years yet, they tell me. Even three. The salt goes out of it slowly."

"So does it out of a keg," said Trijn, "if you stand it in the rain. It goes out."

That October, when the boats came up and the walls were stripped, Jaap Rotgans stood in his own shed in the smell of forty years and did the year's last sums in his head, not Trijn's kind, his own kind: so many kegs ranked and curing, more than any year of his life or his father's; and all of it, every fish of it, taken out of a sea that could no longer replace one keel of it, from stock that would never spawn its way in again past the closing doors; and he took down the lantern and turned it out, and said to the dark of the shed, because it was true and no one but the cure was listening: "Fat water. God forgive the lot of us. We are eating the seed corn, and it is the best year we ever had."

# Chapter Seven — Eel Money

Klaas Lont turned to the eel in the spring of 'thirty-one, early, publicly, and without one word of apology, which was the only way Klaas Lont had ever done anything.

Jaap came round the harbor head on a March morning and found him at it: the Lont botter hauled out, and Klaas with his sleeves rolled, cutting into her afterhold to build a live-well, while a mainland carpenter shook his head over the drawings and did as he was told. On the quay beside them stood the new gear in bundles — fykes, hooped net-traps long as a man, soft-meshed, dark-tanned, a hundred of them — eel gear, inshore gear, patience gear, as different from the bright walls of the anchovy fleet as a snare is from a scythe.

"Say it or don't," Klaas said, not looking up.

"I've nothing to say."

"You have a sermon in you the size of a keg." Klaas drove his chisel, freed a plank, straightened. He was Jaap's age within a year; they had learned the grounds together under Jaap's father, and buried, between them, two fathers, a brother, and forty years of weather; he had earned the right to be listened to standing in his shavings. "Then I'll give you mine. They shut the box next year. The salt goes out of it — two years, three, the engineers say, and on this the engineers have been right every time we called them wrong. The anchovy dies with the salt. The herring dies with the salt. And the eel" — he pointed his chisel at the harbor water, at the whole grey sweetening sea beyond it — "the eel gets the best of it. Fresh water, warm shallows, the biggest eel-pond in Europe, and every fool on this island staring at it with his cap in his hands singing psalms for the anchovy. I fish water, Jaap. Not a flavor of it. My father fished before the dike, I'll fish after it, and the man who calls that treason can row out and argue with the dike, which answers about as well as you do." He bent back to the hold. "There's grief and there's freight, Rotgans. A boat carries one or the other. Mine's spoken for."

⁂

The polder meeting was held at Hippolytushoef in April, in the hall behind the church, and half the island's boats were represented in it, in church coats, on borrowed chairs. The Wieringermeer lay dry now — the first of the great polders, pumped out the summer before last, twenty thousand hectares of new-made land steaming just south across the old channel where Jaap had shrimped as a boy; and the gentlemen at the front table had come about the future. There were to be courses, the first gentleman said: field drainage, dairy work, machine work, paid instruction for fishermen and fishermen's sons, the state was not blind to what the works had asked of the fishery. There was to be land, said the second gentleman, more carefully — leaseholds on the new polder in due course, applications weighed, and men of the region, men of proven industry, need not think themselves at the back of any queue. Sign here, said the table, in effect, and the queue proved the argument: names Jaap had known his whole life went up to that table and wrote themselves down for the land — sons of anchovy men, two anchovy men entire, Aal Snoek's widower brother — each signature a small dry sound like a plank starting.

Jaap sat through the whole of it with his cap on his knee and signed nothing, and walked the sea-dike home in the blue evening with the new polder on his left hand lying flat and vast and lampless, smelling of turned bottom-clay, of drowned shell rotting sweet in open air — the smell, he thought, of the sea with its skin off — and the live sea on his right hand saying at the stones what it had always said; and he made the whole four miles between them like a man walking a plank set between two boats, and came in at his own door with his mind as set as it had been at the church-hall door, and one thing changed that he did not yet know about, which was that he had run out of ways to call the set of his mind a plan.

Trijn was at the ledger. She heard him out — the courses, the leaseholds, the queue, the names, Klaas and his fykes — and she let him get all the way to the end of the sermon Klaas had never got, the salt, the trade, the cure, the forty years, the shed his grandfather built; and then she put down her pen and looked at him with the lamp between them and named it.

"Jaap. If a course of the anchovy died out there tomorrow, of weather, of God's plain will, no dike in it anywhere — you would grieve it one winter and be fitting fykes by March, and you know it, because I have watched you turn with the sea's turns for thirty-four years. It is not the fishing you cannot leave." Her voice did not rise; the pen lay where she had set it, squared to the page. "It is the grave. They have told you the day your sea dies, and you have decided to sit up with it — you will fish at the funeral and starve at the wake, and call it faith with the trade. It is not faith, Jaap. It is mourning, and you have signed this house on for it, and I would only ask" — and here she took the pen up again, because certainty came before pride, even now, even in this — "that a man who will not choose the land, and will not choose the eel, and cannot keep the salt, tell his wife which column he is to be entered under. I have kept every figure of this family true since 'ninety-seven. You are the first one I cannot get to stand still."

He stood a long time by the stove. The clock went. Out on the flats, faint when the wind leaned, the works.

"Enter me under the salt," he said at last, "one more year. There is one salting left in that water, and it is mine, and I will take it and cure it right if it is the last judgment I am ever let make. After the closing —" and there he stopped, not because the sentence failed him but because he heard, saying it, that it was the first time in four years the words *after the closing* had come out of him pointed forward; and Trijn heard it too, and bent her head, and ruled nothing, and wrote nothing, and let the entry stand open, which was, in the practice of that house, how mercy was extended.

# Chapter Eight — Two Futures at One Table

Froukje Hoekstra came to the house on a Saturday in October, 1931, on the back of Dirk's bicycle from the bus, in a good grey coat and a red scarf tied under her chin, and she came up the path a half-step ahead of Dirk, which Trijn observed from the window and priced instantly and correctly, the way she priced everything.

She was twenty-three, a Frieslander from the Makkum shore, brown-eyed, broad-cheekboned, with a handshake like her father's trade; and her Dutch came out dressed in Frisian, the g's gone soft and the vowels rounded seaward, so that the first thing she brought into the kitchen was the sound of the opposite coast — the far shore of the whole undertaking, the other end of the dike, the place the stone had been closing toward for four years. Her father tipped stone at Kornwerderzand, foreman of a tipping-gang; her mother was six years dead; and she herself — she said it plainly, hanging her own coat, a girl squaring her cargo — kept the count for the canteen stores on the work-island, and had for three years: eleven hundred men's bread and coffee and tobacco through her book by the week.

"Eleven hundred," said Trijn, and put the coffee down, and looked at her — one keeper of a ledger looking at another across two generations and a sea — and from that moment, as Jaap told Klaas Lont afterward, the outcome of the visit was published, and the men might as well have gone out and left them the house.

At table the girl talked, because Dirk was beyond talking, and the talk was the dike from inside — not the engineers' dike of the newspapers, not the fishermen's dike of the harbor head, but a third dike Jaap had not heard before, a dike made of gangs and shifts and standing jokes and men's names: which crane-driver could set a five-ton skip down on a laid handkerchief; how her father's gang and the gang of a man called Bootsma had tipped stone in competition for a fortnight, a barge against a barge, until the engineer put a stop to it for the sake of the survey; what the barracks sang; what the barracks ate — and how, when a southwest gale had stood the sea up against the unfinished work two winters back and taken a bite of it in the night, the whole island had turned out in the dark without being ordered, her father among them, and the cook, and the pay-clerk in his Sunday boots, because — and she said this with her whole hand flat on the table, a foreman's daughter stating the trade's one article of faith — "the sea does not knock off shift, so the work may not either. Ninety men in the water to their belts at four in the morning, and not one of them there for the wage. You cannot pay a man to do that. He does it because the work is his."

"The work is his," Jaap said, and it did not come out as anything: not a challenge, not an agreement; a man turning a fish over in his hand.

She looked at him then, straight on, the red scarf loose on her shoulders, and showed him she had known all along whose table she was eating at. "My grandmother's people were sea-folk, meneer Rotgans. Makkum, and Workum before that. In 'sixteen, the year of the flood, the water came into my mother's kitchen a foot deep on Sint Anthonis's day, and my grandmother sat up on the table with the psalm-book and would not leave the house. I was eight; I remember her carried out the door with the book still in her hand. So I'll not sit at your table and tell you the dike costs nothing — I keep books, I know to the stuiver what things cost. I'll say only what my father says: that it was the sea set the price, and the dike only pays it. And that I am sorry, truly, that so much of the paying falls on houses like this one; and that I'll drink your coffee gladly and eat your anchovy with more respect than the gentlefolk who buy it, because in our barracks, meneer, your fish is what the men ask for on pay-Saturdays, and I have entered it in my book three years running, and it is the best thing that comes onto that island."

There did not seem to be anything wrong with any of that, and Jaap, looking for it, took his time.

They walked her to the evening bus, all four, and Dirk and the girl went ahead down the harbor road with the light failing, their two heads bent together and turning each to each, his low Wieringer growl, her seaward vowels, two coasts conversing; and it was there, watching his son's back going away up the road toward the works, toward Friesland, toward the far shore of everything — going easily, going gladly, half a step behind his girl — that Jaap saw the plain masterful sum of it at last, the entry that had been accruing under his roof for four years while he watched the water: that the boy was not going to the other side someday. The boy was across. Had been across for years — wintered there, sang there, drew his bread through Froukje's book there — and what was left on this side of the gap, in the house behind Jaap's back, was not Dirk; it was Dirk's Mays, three weeks of silver a year, lent home across a closing sea by a young man courteous with love; and the closing, when it came, would take even those, and be called by everyone, correctly, a wedding.

Trijn put her arm through her husband's on the walk back, which was not her public custom.

"Well," she said. "That's the boy settled, then."

"That's the boy settled," Jaap said, and they went home along the dark harbor together, two people with one column fewer to carry open, and did not need the lamp lit to know their own door.

# Chapter Nine — The Last Run

The anchovy came into the Zuiderzee for the last time in the middle of May, 1932, through a gap in the world eight hundred meters wide.

The Vlieter, the engineers called that final opening — the deep tidal channel off the Frisian middle-ground where the two ends of the five-year work now stood facing each other across a strait of running water, cranes on both headlands, the whole sea's breathing forced through the one throat. The tide went through it like a mill-race, in and out, six hours and six hours, harder every week as the tipped stone narrowed it; and on that tide, on the warmth, obedient to ten thousand springs, the fish came in. What must it have been in the dark under the barges, Jaap wondered once and then did not let himself wonder again — the shoals feeling their way up the funnel of the only door, over sunk willow and new clay, between the piers of their own tomb — and spreading out inside, southward over the warm sands, silver into the closing room; and behind them the buckets swung and the stone went down, and everyone on both sides of the water, fish-men and dike-men alike, knew the date now, because the date was in the papers: the closing was set for the end of the month.

Every boat that could swim went out. That was the last May, and no one who fished it ever needed to describe it to anyone else who had, and none of them ever managed to describe it to anyone who hadn't. The anchovy fleets of the whole doomed sea worked side by side in water that had never been shared: Wieringer aken and Volendammer botters, Urkers, Harderwijkers, boats from dead-quiet harbors that had not fitted out whole fleets in twenty years, all of them crowded up along the closing grounds among the works themselves, setting walls of net under the loom of the spoil-barges, hauling silver within a cable of the cranes; and the works went on above them, and the fishing went on below, the two trades interleaved at last in one strip of grey water, each at its own harvest. On the crown of the dike the ground-workers would straighten from the stone when a heavy wall came dripping up a mast-height away, and stand, and watch it in over the roller — men of Wieringen and Urk themselves, half of them, watching their fathers' trade fill its hold under their boots — and nobody called anything across the water, in either direction, all that month. It was not a silence with any anger left in it. It was the silence of the two gangs at a lowering, when the ropes run and everyone has hold of one.

Dirk took his May weeks, the last of them, and stood across the net from his father, twenty-eight now, banns as good as called, and hauled like a man paying off a debt in the only coin the creditor would honor. They fished the tides round. Trijn hired six women and salted to the roof-beams; Zwart the cooper, seventy-one years old, worked Sundays, and was seen to do it by the whole street, and was not spoken to about it by anyone, the island having agreed without a meeting that this year the sixth day's rule was the sea's to keep or waive. The buyer came early, wired his firm, and stayed a week in the room over the harbor inn, buying the funeral of the Zuiderzee anchovy fishery keg by keg as it came off the benches with the brine not yet risen.

On the last evening of the run — the fish thinning, the month dying, the works' lights burning now on both horns of the Vlieter so that the one door of the sea stood lit like a town gate — Jaap and Dirk hauled the final wall together in a flat calm at dusk. It came up moderate: a working catch, no marvel, the sea declining, with its last strength, to be theatrical. They picked it, and cleared it, and then Jaap did the thing he had been forty years not doing, which was to break the order of work. The order said: strip the stave-lines, coil, stow, home. Instead he stood in the stern with the last net over his arm, wet to the shoulder, and looked at the water a long moment — north, where the lit gap was, and south, where the warm dark spawning sands lay, and down, last, at the grey tide alongside, at the sea itself, close to, the way you look at a face —

and set the wall again.

"One more tide," he said. "Not for the fish."

Dirk, his hand already on the halyard, stopped, and looked at his father's back, and asked nothing; and they left the net standing in the dark sea overnight, sentinel, pointless, correct, the way the family had always left a light in the window over a coffined man; and rowed home unspeaking on the young flood, the last of its kind that would ever carry them, with the smell of the salt going off the water into the May night all around them like something being said slowly, once, and not again.

# Chapter Ten — Thirteen Two

The twenty-eighth of May, 1932, came in bright and moderate, wind at northwest, a working day's weather; and the sea, which had never once in the memory of man been told anything, had an appointment.

Jaap took the aak out alone. He had thought about that through most of a short night — whether to go at all, whether to take Trijn, who had settled it by taking the bus instead, with half the village, to watch from the dike-road at Den Oever like a Christian; whether it was a thing a man ought to see or a thing a man ought to be working through the hour of, the way you kept a child busy while the doctor was upstairs. In the end he went because of the tide. There would be an afternoon ebb that day, the papers and the moon agreed on it, due to begin running out through the Vlieter a little after noon; and it would be the last one. The last ebb of the Zuiderzee. A man who had lived sixty-two years on that tide's punctuality owed it, he found he had decided, what you owed any faithful servant discharged through no fault of its own, which was to see it off the premises yourself, standing, with your cap in your hand.

He was not alone out there. From a mile south of the works the water that day looked like a regatta with no course: botters and aken and skiffs strung loosely across the whole southern approach, sails slack, engines off, riding; men who had fished against each other's fathers lying gunwale to gunwale, passing tobacco. Beyond them the dike stood complete except for itself — the two great arms run out from either world, Holland and Friesland, five years of clay and willow and stone, and between the arms the last of the sea's one door: the Vlieter gap, a few hundred meters now and going, with the fleets of the closing crowded on it. Barges of keileem lay ranked to both sides like shot beside a gun. The cranes stood over the gap from both headlands. There were flags up on the finished crown — he could see the color of them even at the distance, orange and the tricolor, snapping — and grandstand planking, and the small dark general blur of the important; a loudspeaker's voice came across the water in pieces on the wind, like a man heard preaching through a wall, the words gone, the certainty arriving.

Dirk was in it somewhere. Not watching — in it; his gang had been moved up to the Vlieter a month since, and somewhere on one of those barges or one of those headlands the second Rotgans stood at the sea's deathbed among the deathbed's builders, in his work boots, on shift. Jaap had found, examining this, that it sat right with him, or as right as anything sat that day. If it must be done — and it must; he had got that far in five years, the length of a whole grief — then let it be done by men who knew what water was, and let one of them be the boy, whose hands he had trained himself, on nets, in the same water, to the same first rule of both trades: that the sea does not forgive slack work.

At noon the tide turned, offshore, under all the riding boats, the way it had turned since before there were Wieringers: without ceremony. The ebb gathered and set north and began to run out through the gap, and Jaap, lying to a light anchor with his hand over the side, wrist-deep, felt it go past his fingers — the whole vast basin easing itself seaward through the one throat, this water around his hand due at the gap within the hour, this exact water among the last of all water that would ever leave the Zuiderzee as the sea's own act — and on the headlands the loudspeaker stopped, and a horn went, and the cranes and the barges moved.

They worked it like the trade it was. That was what an anchovy-salter saw, at anchor, a mile off, that the newspapers' men on the grandstand did not: that the closing of the Zuiderzee was done at slack-water speed and rhythm, clay-bite by clay-bite, the grabs swinging and dipping and swinging, barges warped up in their order and emptied and gone, the gap's brown race narrowing between spoil-heaps that stood up out of the sea now like the backs of cattle fording; no haste anywhere, and no pause anywhere, the fatal patient competence of men doing a thing they have done ten thousand times, one more time, onto the neck of the last running water in the world. He watched it the whole hour and could not hate one motion of it. That had been taken from him too, over the five years, motion by motion, Saturday by Saturday, and a girl's flat hand on his own table: the right to hate it. All that was left standing in him by that last hour was what stands in a man at any master-work, which is recognition; and under the recognition, going out with the ebb through his trailing fingers, everything else.

At two minutes past one — thirteen hours two, the engineers logged it, and the whole country would know the figure by supper — the grabs put the last of the boulder clay into the last of the gap, and the Vlieter ceased.

He did not hear it; there was nothing to hear; that was the immense, the unrepeatable thing about it. The horns began — every craft at the works, every tug and dredger and barge, and then the church bells carried thin from two coasts at once, Holland and Friesland ringing to each other over a dead strait for the first time in the history of either — and under all that noise, beneath his hand, around his anchor-warp, the water went still. The ebb, half-run, leaned on the shut door, and stopped. It did not fail like a tide easing to slack, with its next turn already in it. It stopped like a clock stops: mid-stroke, finally, with the rest of its motion still inside it and nowhere ever to go; and the boats of the dead fleet rode on over their anchors all across the southern water, silent under the horns, each man alone at his own gunwale, feeling with the flats of his hands what could not be seen and would never be undone: the sea, at one afternoon o'clock, in fair weather, at the height of its strength, becoming a lake.

Around three, the flags still snapping on the crown and the grandstand emptying into motorcars, Jaap Rotgans stood up in the aak, and put his cap back on, and did what every other boat in that scattered fleet did within the same half hour, there being nothing else whatever to be done, and no tide now to wait for, ever again: he made sail for home across the flat obedient water, on the wind alone.

# Chapter Eleven — The Kept Keg

The sea did not die of the closing. That was the thing no one had quite told him, and it was worse. It began, instead, to forget.

Through that first summer the water south of the dike lay to Jaap's eye exactly as it had always lain — grey, working, wind-ruled, gulls over it — and was, under the surface, unbecoming itself by the week. The tides were gone from the first afternoon: the flats at Den Oever stood permanently half-drowned at the new fixed level, the harbor never emptied and never brimmed, and the whole grammar of a Wieringer day — early on the ebb, home on the flood, the moon consulted like a foreman — went out of use in a month, leaving the older men reaching for it the way the tongue reaches for a drawn tooth. The salt went more slowly. The IJssel and the rains poured their fresh water into the shut basin day upon day, and the salt, with no door, could only thin: the engineers gave it two years, three in the deeps, and the fish confirmed the engineers. The anchovy that had come in through the Vlieter in May spawned in the warm south sands and its fry swam in fresh-tasting water no anchovy had use for; the shrimp thinned; the herring men of Volendam and Urk hauled walls that came up strange — sea fish logy and wandering, and among them, more every month, the sweetwater kinds moving out from the river mouths into their inheritance, bream and pike-perch and, above all, in every fyke and every trap, sliding into the vacated sea like an heir into a front room, the eel.

Klaas Lont landed eel all summer and said nothing to anyone, which, from Klaas, was a mercy performed daily.

The salting sheds of Wieringen fell idle one by one, not on any single day, but the way lamps go out along a street. There was nothing for them: the 'thirty-two salting stood curing in the dark and needed only time; there would be no 'thirty-three salting; and a trade that had turned on the year's wheel since before anyone's grandfather now had in front of it only aftermath and inventory. Some men sold their gear north, over the dike-road by lorry, to the Wadden harbors where the real sea still ran tides. Some sold their boats. The Rotgans aak stayed on her mooring, hauled out in October, tarred as she had been tarred every October since the yard built her; Jaap did it himself, both coats, and if it was the tarring of a boat with no trade under her, it was done to the standard of one that had, because the boat was not to blame, and because — Trijn's phrase, delivered at the stove, unanswerable — you do not stop washing a man's shirts while he is looking for work.

⁂

The shed was settled in October, and it was settled the way everything in that house was settled, which was out loud once and then forever. A salter came down from Harlingen — a Wadden man, from the outer coast, where the real sea still ran its tides against the far side of the new dike and would run them till Judgment — having heard, in the way of the trades, that the Rotgans benches were the best on the dead side of the water; and he went down the aisle of the shed pricing with his eyes, and made a fair offer, being that kind of man, for the lot: tubs, benches, rails, the salt-bin, the good north light of the whole standing business. Jaap sold him the tubs and the spare tubs, the heading benches, four dozen empty kegs, and the salt remaining in the bin, and helped him load the lorry himself, and shook his hand on the road; and the man, no fool, looked at what had visibly not been brought out of the shed, and looked at Jaap, and said only that if the stone and the rest of it ever wanted a working home, Harlingen knew the address of Den Oever. The proving stone stayed on its shelf. So did one bench, the short one, his grandfather's, and the brine-thief hanging over it, and a single ranked rail's worth of room in the cool north end — because the eel would want tarred fykes stored dry and mended in the wet months, and a shed with its trade cut out of it is a barn, but a shed with the next trade already hanging in it is a shed still; and when the lorry had gone north over the dike-road with the tubs of five Rotgans generations roped under canvas, Jaap swept the empty half of the floor himself, to the boards, to the corners, the way you sweep a church and not the way you sweep a barn, and hung the fykes where the walls of net had hung, and stood the barrow in the tub-corner, and told nobody what the sweeping had cost him, and was not asked, the family gift being by then in its third generation and good, like the cure, for years in the dark.

The clerk came back in November. The same clerk — greyer, heavier about the portfolio, for the Rijksdienst that would administer the steunwet's promises had been stood up that year and he was of it now, with a rubber stamp of his own — and he sat at the same table and took the family through the claim: the vessel written down in 'twenty-eight, the gear, the shed, the yields; the tegemoetkoming assessed and entered; the first order of payment to come quarterly, a sum that would keep a modest roof dry and was not pretended by anyone at the table to be a fishery. He was thorough and he was decent and at the end, buckling the portfolio, he looked down the aisle of the shed at the ranked kegs of the last cure reddening in the dark, and asked, entirely off the forms, in his Vollenhove voice, what would become of them.

"They'll be sold in the spring," Trijn said, "when they're ready. Enkhuizen has bespoken the lot."

"All the lot?"

"All," said Jaap, from the bench, "but one."

It stood already apart, that keg, at the cool north end of the lowest rail, turned once a fortnight like the others but chalked on the head where the others were stenciled: one anker of the final salting of the Zuiderzee anchovy, headed by the Snoek women, coopered by Simon Zwart in his last working May, salted by Jaap Rotgans out of the last May run of a five-hundred-year fishery, and not for sale; and every soul on the island who heard of it — for Zwart talked — understood the chalk mark without explanation, the mark being the two letters of Dirk's initials and the one word *bruiloft*. Wedding.

The papers, that same autumn, carried a small notice out of The Hague, four lines under the shipping news, which Dirk read aloud at the table on a Saturday because somebody in the house was going to have to: that by decision of the ministry, the twentieth of September, the waters enclosed by the Afsluitdijk would henceforth in all official usage be designated the IJsselmeer — the lake of the IJssel — and the name Zuiderzee retired from the charts, kept only where it named the works themselves and the law and the support of the fishermen. A sea unmade by act of parliament and renamed by circular. Trijn looked, when the reading was done, not at her husband but at her book; and in her book, in due course, in the upright hand, at the head of the column where forty years of anchovy money had gone and the eel money of the years to come would go — for Jaap had signed the permit application in January, on his own two feet, at his own kitchen table, Klaas Lont's thumbed instruction-list for the fykes lying beside the ink — she wrote the heading the whole book had been steering toward since the evening she ruled the column called *D.*, the entry that settled the house's accounts with the century:

*Visserij, voortgezet.* Fishery, continued.

# Chapter Twelve — Sweet Water

Dirk Rotgans married Froukje Hoekstra at Hippolytushoef in the first week of May, 1933, in the season that had been anchovy season when the world was differently arranged; and the two coasts of the drowned strait came to the wedding and were, by the end of it, one family in the manner of such things: her father the stone-foreman and Klaas Lont discovered a shared contempt for a contractor of their common acquaintance and were thereafter inseparable; the Frisian cousins sang; the Wieringer cousins, after sufficient advocaat, also sang, and the two singings met somewhere over the middle of the room like weather systems, to the general advantage of neither language and the great advantage of the evening.

The young couple would live at Den Oever. That was the turn no one had foreseen five years back and everyone claimed afterward to have foreseen: the works were ending, the dike stood finished from shore to shore with a motor-road building along its crown, and the state's next great labor — the new polders, the whole promised land-making that the closing had been for — wanted exactly what Dirk had spent six years becoming, a ground-worker who understood water. He had his place already on the drainage gangs of the Wieringermeer, ten minutes' bicycle from his mother's table; and Froukje had the books of the polder co-operative store at the new village rising out of the old sea bottom, thirteen hundred farmhands' bread and coffee through her ledger by the week, which she declared, deadpan, in her seaward vowels, to be a demotion in salt but a rise in tonnage. The far shore, in the end, had folded itself home. That was the dike's last unadvertised work, Jaap thought, watching them dance: it had carried his son out across the water year by year, and then it had lain down and become the road that brought him back.

The keg was presented at the giving of gifts, wheeled in on Zwart's own barrow by Zwart's own grandson, and the hall, which knew what it was, went quiet around it in a way that no other object in that room of clocks and linen could have commanded. Jaap had thought out what to say and said it standing, in four sentences, which was for him an oration. "This is the last of the old sea. Salted the last May, in the old shed, by the old judgment. It is a year in the wood and it wants another; anchovy will not be hurried, and nor will anything else worth the wait — your mother waited three years for me to speak, and has had value of the patience ever since." (Laughter, and Trijn's face doing the thing it did instead of laughing.) "So it goes to your cellar sealed, and you will broach it next May, on your first anniversary, when it is ready and you are; and if there is any left the May after, you are eating it too fast."

Froukje kissed his cheek. Dirk shook his father's hand and held it a moment past the shake, neither man being equipped to do more in a hall with the lamps lit; and the keg went to the cellar in Den Oever, chalk mark outward, to wait its year among the seed potatoes, the last hundredweight of the Zuiderzee lying in the dark under the feet of the marriage it had been kept for, becoming itself.

⁂

On a still evening a fortnight after the wedding, alone, between supper and dark, Jaap Rotgans rowed out from Den Oever onto the lake.

He had eel gear down off the eastern grounds and the pretext of looking to it, but he went past the last of his floats and kept rowing, out onto open water, to where the land sat low all around and the dike lay across the north with the sunset running along it like a lit fuse that had decided, after all, to burn slowly and harm no one. Out on the crown of it, small with distance, stood the beginnings of the monument they were raising at the closing place — a tower over the spot where the Vlieter had run, so that motorists on the new road might stop and climb it and look at what had been done; there were to be words set on it, Dirk said, cast in bronze, about a living people building at its future, and Jaap had nodded at that, because it was true, and had thought privately that the sea had been given no line on the plaque, and that this was also, in its way, exact: the sea had built at nobody's future. The sea had merely kept every bargain it ever made, to the hour, to the last afternoon ebb, and been closed for it, and he would not stand on a tower to read about it. He would do this instead.

He shipped the oars and let the boat ride. No tide took her; nothing took her; she lay where he left her on water flat as a held breath, in a silence that still, after a year, had a piece missing from it — the piece that had been the sea working, the piled small forever noise of moving water that a coast-born man does not hear until it is subtracted. The evening smelled of warm shallows and green weed and faintly, up from the south, of hay: the smell of the new country. Bream rose ringing the stillness where anchovy had run in their millions. Somewhere east his fykes hung in the dark doing their patient work, and the eel money in Trijn's book stood already, she had shown him, at a figure his father would not have believed for June.

Jaap put his hand over the side, wrist-deep, where the last ebb had gone through his fingers, and cupped up a palmful of the lake, and drank it.

Sweet. Not river-sweet yet, not rain-sweet; a ghost of the old bite lingered at the back of it, the way a keg once brined stays a brined keg though you stow flour in it for years. But sweet: water for cattle, water for polders, water a man could live beside a whole life now and never once taste the thing it had been. He held the last of it on his tongue the way the buyer used to hold the cure, head back, judging, giving it its hour; and there it was at the finish, right at the floor of the taste and going, faint as faith — salt, the old signature, the sea's hand on its own receipt: not a flavor anymore but the place where a flavor had been.

He sat with it a while, in the dark coming down, in the boat his father had named for reasons no one living knew. Then Jaap Rotgans wiped his hand on his knee and took up the oars, and turned her, and rowed in across the still sweet water toward the lit windows of the house, where the lamp was on over the ledger and the columns all stood open; and the sound of the oars went out over the lake in rings, and was, for a wonder, the loudest thing in that whole made country — pull, and feather, and pull — an anchovy-salter rowing home late, on fresh water, through the first of the season he had once been promised nobody could name beforehand: and there it was after all, mind you, arrived without asking anyone. A May with no run in it. A calm like brine settling. The first spring of the lake.
