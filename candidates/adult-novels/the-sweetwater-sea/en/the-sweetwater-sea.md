# THE SWEETWATER SEA

*A novella of the Zuiderzee*

> Wieringen, 1927. Jaap Rotgans has salted anchovy for forty years on a sea the whole country has decided to close. His only son has taken work on the dam that will do it. Five years remain — of tides, of runs, of a marriage and a trade — before the last gap shuts and the salt goes out of the water for good.

---

**Content note:** period hardship and the end of a fishing trade; a quiet book — no violence.

# Chapter One — The Salting Shed

The anchovy was a fish you waited for, and Jaap Rotgans had built a life on the waiting.

It came into the Zuiderzee on the warmth, in the middle of May, in the years it chose to come at all: ansjovis, the anchovy, a green-backed sliver of a sea fish that wintered out beyond the islands in water no Wieringer had ever seen, and arrived wanting the one thing the shallow sea had to sell, which was warmth over sand to spawn on. Some springs it came in such numbers that the nets lifted silver out of the dark like coin out of a purse, and a man paid off his boat in six weeks. Some springs it did not come at all, and you ate the winter twice. Nobody could say beforehand which sort of spring had been sent; his father had fished it, and his father before that, and none of the three of them had ever claimed to know the fish's mind, which Jaap considered the beginning of honesty in the trade.

This spring it had come.

They went out from Den Oever in the dark, he and Dirk, the boat sliding down the harbor on the last of the ebb — a Wieringer aak, broad and flat-bottomed as a clog, the local workboat of these shallows, leeboards up along her sides like folded wings, WR 43 painted on the bow above a name, *Twee Gebroeders*, that his father had chosen for reasons that had not outlived him. The nets stood where they had set them the evening before, a long fleet of fine-meshed wall strung on staves at the edge of the tide-flats, and in the first grey light the corks were stuttering, which is a thing a man will get out of bed at two in the morning his whole life to see. They hauled by hand, the two of them, the net coming in over the gunwale with a sound like rain going backwards, and the fish hung in the meshes by their gills in the hundreds, then in the thousands, each one the length of a finger and the width of a knife blade, green over the back and silver under, and the silver not one silver but many, the way the sea itself is never one grey.

"Good wall," said Dirk, meaning that stretch of net, and it was all either of them said for an hour, there being nothing else that needed establishing.

By seven they were back inside the harbor with the hold shining. By half past, the shed had them.

The salting shed stood behind the house with its doors to the north, because salt likes cool and a curing fish likes it better, and it had been his grandfather's before it was his, and smelled of the whole of both intervening lives: brine and tar, oak and net-bark, fish oil gone old in the grain of the benches, and under everything the dry grey presence of the salt itself, which was not a smell at all, Trijn said, but the place where a smell would have been. Trijn had the tubs standing ready. Four women from the harbor houses came in at eight with their own knives and sat to the fish as they had sat to it every good May since girlhood: a twist of thumb against blade and the head came away with the gut following it out in one soft string, the little body dropped in the rinsing tub, the heads going by the thousand into the offal barrow, and the count kept aloud in the old way, so that a woman's wage and a fish's fate were the same arithmetic in the same air.

Jaap did the salting himself. That was not pride, or not only pride; it was that salting was judgment, and judgment was the one part of a trade that could not be hired. Coarse grey salt, a fistful thrown ringing across the keg bottom. Then the fish laid in courses the way a bricklayer lays headers, bellies up, tails to the stave, each course crossing the one beneath it so the keg was bonded like a wall. Then salt, then fish, then salt, to the crown. The kegs themselves came from Zwart's kuiperij — the cooperage — two streets in from the harbor, oak ankertjes hooped tight enough to hold weather in, and Simon Zwart still burned his mark into every head as though anyone on Wieringen would mistake his work for another man's. On the crowned top course went the round loose lid, and on the lid a beach stone worn to the shape of exactly this duty by forty years of performing it, and by evening the brine had risen up over the wood of its own accord, drawn out of the fish by the salt, which always looked, however often a man had seen it, like a small act of weather happening indoors.

He proved his pickle with a hen's egg. Float it high and the brine would burn the fish to leather; float it low and the keg would soften and sweeten and be lost entire; between the two there was a right height, a cap of shell standing above the water the size of a stuiver, and his thumb knew it a breath before his eye did. Then the kegs went to the dark side of the shed, ranked on rails off the floor, and began the one part of the work no man could hurry, which was becoming themselves. Anchovy is not herring. Herring you can eat green out of the barrel. Anchovy goes into the salt a stiff bright silver thing and lies in the dark a year at the least — two was better — and comes out the far side of that year transformed: the flesh gone the deep red-brown of old brick, the bone gone to nothing, the taste gone from fish to something spread thin on rich men's bread in Amsterdam and Paris and called by other languages. The red was the cure and the cure was the price. Last May's kegs stood curing behind this May's, and the May before stood behind those, ready money in oak, so that the shed held three years at any hour of the day, and a man who walked its aisle walked in his own past and future both, ranked and stenciled and quietly turning red in the dark.

Trijn kept the ledger. That was the shape of the marriage, or the part of the shape that showed: he judged the fish and she judged everything else, the salt bills and the keg bills and the women's tally, the buyer's opening offer and the distance it would travel before she let it rest. She had been keeping it thirty-one years, in a hand as upright as the day the schoolmaster had approved it, and what she wanted from the world was not what Jaap wanted, which was for it to hold still; what Trijn wanted was to see it coming. Certainty first, she said. Pride is for afterwards, when it is paid for.

The buyer came up from Enkhuizen in the second week of June, as the buyers of that firm had come for longer than anyone living, and stood in the shed aisle in his town shoes while Jaap drew the bung of a keg of the 'twenty-five cure and lifted two fish out on the blade of a knife. The buyer ate one there, standing, head back, the way a man drinks. He looked at the roof beams for a moment.

"Roof-tile red," he said. "You could price this one in French."

"You could," said Trijn, from the doorway, "and then we would both know what it is worth while you offered me less. Come inside where the coffee is."

The dance took an hour, as it always took an hour, and ended where both parties had known by the second cup it would end, which was, Jaap understood, the whole use of the hour: not to move the price but to prove the walls of it still stood. He carried the samples down to the buyer's motor launch afterwards, and it was there, on the harbor, in the flat gold of six o'clock, that the buyer paused with one shoe on his gunwale and nodded out and to the north, past the harbor light, at the two black shapes standing off the shore where no shapes had stood at Whitsun.

"You've seen your visitors, I suppose," the buyer said.

Jaap had seen them. The whole of Den Oever had seen them; men had walked up on the sea-dike after church on Sunday and stood in a line looking at them and come down again saying very little. Dredgers. Iron ones, sea-going, each the size of a church laid on its side, lying to moorings off the harbor with barges rafted alongside and the spoil coming up out of the sea bottom bucket after bucket after bucket, a sound you could hear when the wind lay right, a slow chained clanking like an anchor being weighed that was never done weighing. The law had been passed in 'eighteen — everyone knew that, the way everyone knew the date of a distant relative's marriage — and for nine years the law had stayed paper, a thing in The Hague, argued about in the papers by engineers and ministers, while the sea went on being the sea. Now the paper had put on iron and come to anchor within sight of his nets.

"They're starting the big one, then," the buyer said, in the voice of a man who has read about it. "Here to Friesland in one line, they say. Shut the whole box." He got down into his launch and looked up, and something in him — Jaap gave him this much, then and later — had the grace to be embarrassed. "Well. There's years in it yet, Rotgans. Nothing closes tomorrow."

"No," Jaap said. "Nothing closes tomorrow."

He walked back up the harbor in the long light. On the flats to the north the tide had turned and was coming in over the sand the way it had come in over that sand ten thousand times for every year of his age, and the dredgers rode on it, working; they did not keep tides. He and Dirk rowed out at dusk and set the walls of net in the old order, stave by stave, and the last thing Jaap saw over his shoulder as they pulled for home was the riding-light of the nearer dredger burning on the water like the lamp of a ship at anchor — a ship at anchor, he thought, with no intention, now or ever, of getting under way.

# Chapter Two — Dry Wages

In July, with the anchovy done and the kegs lidded and the nets stripped off their staves for barking, Dirk bicycled to Den Oever village in his church trousers and was gone the length of a tide, and came back and stood in the doorway of the shed with his cap in both hands, which was how Jaap knew before a word was said. Nobody holds his cap in both hands to bring good news to a shed.

"They're taking men on at the works," Dirk said. "Grondwerk first, then on the stone if you show yourself worth it. I put my name in." He turned the cap a quarter-turn, as though setting it by compass. "They took it."

Jaap went on doing what he was doing, which was paying tarred twine off a spool for the net-mending, because a man's hands in those first moments are best given something they already know. The quarrel that followed was conducted the way the Rotgans family had always conducted its weather, in single sentences set well apart, in a shed, quietly, with both parties working.

"Whose gang?"

"Van der Oord's. Wieringers mostly. Two Urkers." A pause of some minutes, net-needle going. "It's the company has the contract — the MUZ, they call it, the big combine, all the dredging firms in the country under one hat. They're paying ground-workers more than I clear in a middling May."

"In a good May," his father said, "you clear more than any of them."

"And in a bad May," Dirk said, not loudly, "I mend nets and eat your winter." He said it with the tarred twine in his own hands, working, matching his father knot for knot, which took some of the sting out and left all of the truth in. He was twenty-four that summer, made like his mother's brothers, big through the shoulders, easy on the water, better with the wall-nets than his father would ever say aloud; and he had been to school two years longer than Jaap had, and read the paper down to the shipping columns, and knew to the guilder what the works were paying, and to the year — this was the part Jaap could not forgive the paper for printing — what the engineers said would happen to the salt when the box was shut.

"The dike gets built whether a Rotgans carries stone for it or not," Dirk said, at the end, when the net between them was whole again. "That's not me talking for the dike. That's arithmetic. It gets built. The only question left standing is whether the wages of it come into this house or go past it."

"And the fish?"

"I'll fish the Mays with you. Every May, as long as there's a May." Dirk stood up and put his cap on, and being his father's son he could not leave without one plain nail driven in to hold the rest. "You've said it yourself, every spring I can remember: nobody can say beforehand what sort of year is coming. Well," he said, "now somebody can. That's all that's changed. Somebody can."

He went in to his supper. Jaap stayed in the shed until the light went, not working, sitting on the salting bench with his hands loose between his knees, in the smell of brine and bark and three ranked years of cure, and found that what was in him was not anger, or was anger only the way the skin of a thing is the thing. Under it sat something older and worse, which he would not name that evening and did not have to: that the boy had done his sums, and the sums were right, and being right they would now begin, guilder by guilder and season by season, to come true.

Trijn said nothing at all.

That was the loudest part of the month. She had opinions the way the sea had water, and a known custom of issuing them, and on this she said nothing: not at the table when Dirk told her, not in the kitchen after, not in the dark where thirty-one years of the day's residue had always been settled between them before sleep. She heard the boy out with her hands still on the bread knife, and looked at him a long moment, and said, "Wash before you come to my table from that clay," which was consent, or at least was not war, and then she opened the ledger.

Jaap watched her do it from the doorway. She sat under the lamp with the book open to the current page, and she took the steel pen, and she ruled, with two clean strokes against the edge of the almanac, a new column down the right-hand side of the household's life. She did not blot it. She sat looking at the ruled emptiness of it for a while, the way you look at a field in March.

Dirk started on the Monday. He went off before light with his bread in a tin and his boots over his shoulder, on the bicycle, eastward along the sea-dike where the works had their yards, and came back the first Saturday grey to the eyebrows with the ground of the sea itself — for that was what they were shifting, he said, eating like a stove, the sea's own floor, boulder clay brought up dripping from under the sand, keileem, the old blue-grey clay the ice ages had left, that gripped like a fist when it dried; the engineers had found the sea was lying on the one material in the world you could build its coffin from — and he laid his first week's board money on the table by his mother's place, and went to bed at eight like a child.

Trijn entered it. Jaap, passing behind her chair with his pipe, looked once — he had promised himself he would not, and looked once — at the head of the new column, at what she had chosen, in that upright schoolroom hand, to call the money the dike paid into a fishing house.

She had written *D.*

Only that. Not the boy's name, not the work's. One letter, serviceably ambiguous, standing at the head of the column like a man at a door with his cap in both hands; and Jaap put his pipe in his mouth and went out into the yard, because he could read his wife's book the way she could read his brine, and he knew a kept option when he saw one. *D* for Dirk, if the sea won. *D* for dijk, if it didn't. She was not going to spend the difference before it was paid out. Certainty first; pride is for afterwards, when it is paid for.

Out on the dark flats to the north, small and steady, the dredger's riding-light. He smoked the pipe out facing it, not from defiance, which he would have been ashamed of in a man of his age, but because a fisherman looks at the weather that is coming, that being the whole of what looking is for; and this, he had begun that summer to understand, was the weather that was coming: a light on the water that did not rise or fall with the tide, and his son asleep in the house behind him with the sea's floor under his fingernails, and in the ledger, drying, a single initial that his wife — who loved them both, the man and the boy, the salt and the wage, with the same undivided practical ferocity — had left herself room to resolve in either direction.

# Chapter Three — The Counting

The clerk came out to Wieringen in the last week of February, 1928, when the boats were up and the sea had nothing for anyone, which showed, Trijn said afterwards, that somebody in that office knew fishermen: you count a man in the season when counting is all he has.

He came on the bus that ran the new dike-road from the mainland — for Wieringen was an island only in its bones now; the short dam at the Amsteldiep had tied it to North Holland four years since, the first knot in the whole long lashing, and the island had grumbled and then bought bicycles — and he walked down the harbor at Den Oever asking for the Rotgans house by name, with a portfolio under his arm and his trouser-ends bicycle-clipped though he had no bicycle, a precaution, Jaap said later, which described the entire man. He was perhaps forty. He had a printed card: RIJKSDIENST — the state service for the support law, the Zuiderzeesteunwet, the act of 'twenty-five that everyone on the island called simply the steunwet, the way you call a dog by the half of its name it answers to.

"It is an assessment, not a levy," he said, standing in the kitchen with his hat already off. "Nothing is taken. Nothing, I am bound to say plainly, is promised either — the law grants a tegemoetkoming, a coming-toward; the word is exact, mevrouw, and I am required by honesty to point out that a coming-toward is not an arrival. What the law requires today is only that the fishery be written down as it stands: vessel, gear, premises, and the average yield of recent years. So that when the water changes, what was lost is a matter of record and not of memory."

"And a record kept by whom?" Jaap asked, from beside the stove.

"By me," the clerk said, without irony, and set his portfolio on the table like a man setting down something heavier than it looked. "I have written down four harbors since Michaelmas. I will write down every boat on this sea before I am finished, or the man who replaces me will."

Jaap looked at the forms — the state's grey columns, boat and gear and shed and man, all of it to be reduced to figures and the figures filed in The Hague against the day the water went — and what rose in him was not reasonable and he knew it was not reasonable and it rose anyway, the way the tide comes up through the floor of a boat with a started plank. They wished to measure him for the coffin while he stood in his boots. Write down the aak so the aak could be grieved bureaucratically. He took his cap off the hook.

"You'll excuse me," he said. "I have work in the shed," which was true in the sense that a shed always has work in it, and he went out and split kindling that did not need splitting and racked kegs that were racked, and through the north wall, for above an hour, he could hear it going forward without him: the clerk's low voice putting its patient questions, and Trijn's voice answering from the doorway between kitchen and shed, where she had planted herself with her arms folded — inside her own house, on the sill of her own trade, positioned, he understood even in his anger, exactly where a woman stands who intends to cooperate with the state and guard her husband's back in the same afternoon.

She answered everything. She answered it from the ledger, without once opening the ledger. The aak, built at the Lastdrager yard, rebuilt forward of the mast in 'nineteen, and worth — she named a figure that was correct to within five guilders, and Jaap, splitting kindling, heard the clerk's pen hesitate as though the exactness itself required a different ink. The wall-nets, by fleets and by age. The shed, the tubs, the stones, the standing stock of empty kegs from the kuiperij. The women hired by the May, and their tally-wages. The yield: and here she gave him the last five years one by one, the fat 'twenty-four, the lean 'twenty-six, in kegs and in guilders, and then, unasked, the ten years before that, "because five years of this fishery is a sample of nothing, meneer; the anchovy does not keep your accounting periods."

"That," said the clerk, writing, "is the most useful sentence anyone has said to me since Michaelmas."

At the end there were the personal columns. Age of the fisherman: fifty-eight. Other trades practiced: none. Sons in the fishery: and there the house went quiet enough that Jaap's axe, out in the shed, went quiet with it.

"One son," Trijn said. "Twenty-five. You may leave his line empty."

"He fishes with his father, mevrouw. The May run, I have it from the harbormaster."

"He fishes the Mays," Trijn said, "and the rest of the year he is on the works at a wage. He is not a claim on your law, meneer. He is already on your side of the book."

The pen wrote it. What the pen made of it the pen did not say.

The clerk left on the evening bus. At the door, hat on, portfolio buckled, he paused in the blue dusk and looked down the harbor at the boats standing black on their winter legs, and he said the only thing he said all day that was not on a form. "My father fished from Vollenhove," he said. "Over on the Overijssel shore. I grew up to the smell of what your shed smells of. I am aware, mevrouw — the office in The Hague is aware, whatever the island thinks of us — that we are counting something that cannot be paid for. The law does not pretend the sea can be kept open. The law only means that when it closes, no man's loss shall be lost. It is little. I would rather carry little than nothing."

When he was well gone, Jaap came in from the shed and stood by the table where the forms had lain. Trijn was setting out the supper. He watched her back for a while.

"You gave them everything," he said. "To the guilder."

"I gave them numbers." She set down the pan and turned round to him, and her face had in it the thing that was always in it, which he had married her for and been governed by for thirty-one years, the refusal to let a feeling stand where a fact would bear more weight. "The numbers were never the part worth keeping, Jaap. If the water stays, the paper rots in a drawer in The Hague and no harm done. If the water goes" — she said it flatly, the first time either of them had said it indoors — "then the only Rotgans fishery left standing anywhere on this earth will be the one I put into that man's portfolio this afternoon, written down true. Refuse the count and you refuse the record. I won't have you kept out of the book, not by them and not by your own stiff neck. Now sit. It's eel."

He sat. Later, when she was asleep, he took the lamp to the dresser and opened the ledger, not for the figures but because a man checks his moorings; and he found, folded small into the back board where she kept the papers that were owed and owing — the buyer's standing contract, the kuiperij receipts, his own father's discharge from the naval militia in 'seventy-one — the carbon flimsy of the clerk's completed form, every column in that upright hand, the whole salt kingdom of his life set down in grey duplicate. Across the top margin, where the state had printed FISHERY, ZUIDER ZEE, CATEGORY A, she had written nothing at all; and it was a measure of the winter, and of the year, and of the light on the water to the north of the flats, that Jaap Rotgans stood in his stocking feet reading his wife's silence in the margin of a government form and found it, of all the documents in the house, the one he believed.
