- **Premise fidelity** — Delivers the coordinator brief exactly: Wieringen, North Holland, 1927–1932; Jaap Rotgans, anchovy-salter and fisherman in his late fifties with his own Wieringer aak, watches the Afsluitdijk built across the mouth of his sea while his only son Dirk takes work on the dike; five years from the first dredgers off Den Oever to the closing of De Vlieter, then the freshening. No villain — the antagonist is a public work of genuine national good, and the book's tragedy is held to the brief's point: the dike was right. What he keeps (the boat, the shed's north end, one keg), what he cedes (the tubs, the trade, the tides), and what he salts down against a future that has no use for it (the wedding keg — which turns out to have a use after all) carry the spine.

- **Historical accuracy — verified spine** (checked this session against the standard record via web search): Zuiderzeewet adopted 14 June 1918 (Lely's plan, impelled by the 1916 flood); Zuiderzeesteunwet of 1925 compensating/supporting the fishery, with its assessments and its administering Rijksdienst stood up in 1932; Amsteldiepdijk connecting Wieringen to North Holland completed 1924; main Afsluitdijk works 1927–1932 under the MUZ, the Maatschappij tot Uitvoering van Zuiderzeewerken, the 1926 contractor combine of the country's big dredging/construction firms; boulder clay (keileem) as the closing material and brushwood sinking-mattresses (zinkstukken) as the foundation technique; the final gap, De Vlieter, closed 28 May 1932 at 13:02, worked at slack water by converging columns of clay barges, the last opening in the order of tens of meters; the enclosed water officially renamed IJsselmeer 20 September 1932; the salinity falling over the following years, the anchovy and herring fisheries dying with the salt while eel and pike-perch rose; the Dudok monument (1933) at the closing point, whose Hildo Krop bronze relief carries "Een volk dat leeft bouwt aan zijn toekomst." Because the bronze relief itself was cast later (foundry record 1935), the 1933 coda handles the inscription prospectively — Dirk reports "there were to be words set on it" — rather than placing the plaque on the tower that year.
- **Historical accuracy — marked inventions** — All named characters are fictional (the Rotgans family, Froukje Hoekstra and her father, Klaas Lont, Simon Zwart the cooper, the Snoek family, the clerk, the Enkhuizen buyer); no real person appears or speaks anywhere in the text (Lely is not staged; the ministry acts only through paper). The WR 43 *Twee Gebroeders*, the Rotgans shed, and every scene in them are fiction. The anchovy-curing craft (courses laid header-wise, the egg-proof of the brine with its stuiver-sized cap, the two-year keg-red cure, tally-heading by women paid by the thousand) is a period-flavoured reconstruction from general Zuiderzee salting practice, not a documented Wieringen procedure. The 1930 concentration of fish in the closing basin and the strange catches (including the returned sturgeon) dramatize a directionally true situation (record catches in the final years; species displacement) without documentary basis for the specific events. The Hippolytushoef polder meeting, the leasehold "queue," the tipping-gang competition, the gale-night turnout at the works, the 1916 floodwater in a Makkum kitchen, and the Harlingen salter buying the gear are plausible inventions consistent with, but not documented as, specific cases. Wieringermeer particulars (fallen dry 1930, ~20,000 hectares, drainage gangs, a co-operative store) are used at newspaper precision, not archival precision.
- **Sensitive-history care** — No real named individuals except institutions and public facts; the fishing communities' loss is carried by work, objects, and ledger entries, never nostalgia-kitsch; the dike is never mocked — the book's stated point is that the antagonist was right, and the closing chapter gives the works the fisherman's own highest word, recognition. No violence anywhere; the only deaths are offpage, historical, and prior. The content note declares the register.

Although Paper Orange is also 20th-century Dutch, nothing here touches occupation, war, Amsterdam, or the 1940s; the settings, decades, and search phrases are disjoint by construction.
