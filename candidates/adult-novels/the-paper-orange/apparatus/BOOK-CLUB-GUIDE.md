# The Paper Orange — Reading-Group Guide

*Book-club and classroom discussion guide for* **The Paper Orange** *(A Novel of
the Hunger Winter). Additive back-matter apparatus — a reader-facing guide the
author or publisher can print after the story or hand to a reading group. It
references the actual story; it changes no word of it. Page-anchoring is by
chapter, since editions repaginate.*

**Spoiler note for group leaders:** the questions below discuss the whole book,
including the ending. Read the novel first.

---

## Thematic overview

**The Paper Orange** is a close, quiet novel about a printer's widow, Mies
Verhoeven, who survives the Dutch Hunger Winter of 1944–45 by forging bread and
potato ration coupons on her dead husband's treadle press to feed people in
hiding — while behind her wallpaper waits a small orange folded from paper, the
forbidden royal colour, kept "for the day the lights come back."

Its largest theme is stated by the book's own framing: *forgery as mercy, and
what people feed each other when there is nothing left.* Around that spine the
novel turns over several others:

- **Grief that hides inside work.** Mies expects the shop to be where her
  husband Hendrik is "most gone"; it turns out to be where he is "least gone"
  (Chapter Three). The trade she inherits is "made entirely of habits, and the
  habits were made of him." Mourning, in this book, is not a pause from the
  work — the work *is* the mourning.

- **The ration as a leash.** Vos's argument in Chapter Two — "they have made
  the food into a register, and everyone the register cannot see, starves" — is
  the moral hinge. Bureaucracy, not only cruelty, is what starves the hidden;
  and a forged scrap of grey-green paper is the leash "cut in a few places."

- **What the living owe the taken.** Chapter Five's refused staircase, and
  Fien's demand that her mother "write it down somewhere that she was owed the
  orange" (Chapter Seven), build the "list of the owed" that the novel pays out
  at the end — a private ledger of debts to the dead.

- **Childhood rationed quietly.** Fien keeps the winter "into the ledger… at
  true"; Dirk strikes the question *what's for supper?* off his own ration as a
  courtesy to his mother (Chapter Seven). The book watches the war take
  "childhood the most quietly."

- **Being beneath notice as a survival skill.** The forged coupons succeed by
  being dull — "grey, mean, dull, tired, real" (Chapter Three) — and so does
  Mies, who becomes "her own dullest sheet" during the house search (Chapter
  Eight). Excellence, here, is the art of not being seen.

- **Waiting, and knowing when to stop.** "*Not yet*," Mies tells the hidden
  orange every night, until "*Now*" (Chapter Eleven). The book's discipline is
  patience — including the hard patience of keeping the children home for three
  more days after liberation, until the danger has actually passed.

## Discussion questions

1. **The opening image.** The novel begins with Mies peeling tulip bulbs "like
   onions," which "like onions… made you cry, though not for the same reason"
   (Chapter One). What does this first paragraph tell you about the world of the
   book — and about how it intends to handle suffering — before any character
   is even named?

2. **"An excellent liar."** When Vos relays Hendrik's line that his wife is
   "the better half of this shop at everything but lying," Mies answers, "He was
   wrong… I've told two children every night since March that things will be all
   right" (Chapter Two). How does the novel treat lying — forgery, the lies she
   tells her children, the "doorway face" she wears for the police? When is a
   lie a mercy in this book, and when isn't it?

3. **The third drawer.** Mies discovers that Hendrik had secretly begun forging
   before he died, teaching himself and hiding it from her, leaving a proof
   marked *Beter* — "Better" (Chapters Two and Three). Why does she find his
   secret both a wound and a mercy — "which somehow made it worse and made it
   holy at the same time"? What does it mean that he tried to keep the danger in
   his half of the shop, "still deciding for her" even in death?

4. **The refused staircase.** In Chapter Five, Mies will burn her own family's
   shop furniture — three generations of it — rather than take the staircase of
   the deported bookbinder Sal Winkler, even though she can "hear exactly what
   Sal Winkler would say — *take it, Mies, wood is wood.*" Is this a rational
   act? Why does the narrator say it "kept something in her upright that winter"
   — the one act she "was never once sorry for"? What would you have done?

5. **Mevrouw Aalders.** The old woman who "did better than most, having been
   loved by a street with one carpenter left in it" says, of the forged card
   slipped under her plate, "Thank God the standards of the house have slipped"
   (Chapter Four). What does Aalders — her canaries gone, her complaining gone
   quiet — let the novel show about how the very old experienced the winter?
   Why does Fien insist she was "owed the orange"?

6. **The checkpoint.** At the Landwacht checkpoint on the way home from the
   hunger trek, Mies has eight of her own forged coupons sewn into her coat hem
   — "eight small rectangles of evidence sufficient to take her from her
   children forever" (Chapter Six). She gets through by being flatly, dully
   unremarkable. What does the scene say about power, luck, and the "argument
   [that] was also on the ration"? How much of her survival is skill and how
   much is a bored young man's mood?

7. **The invisible commonwealth.** Chapter Seven describes Mies joining "the
   invisible commonwealth of the fed and the feeding," whose "whole creed was:
   today also, nobody vanished." How does the novel portray the resistance — not
   as heroics but as gloves, counters, silence, and a greengrocer slipping
   coupon-halves into his boot? Why might the author have chosen to keep it this
   quiet?

8. **The house search.** During the Sunday raid, the Dutch policeman in grey
   looks "*straight at*" the wallpaper seam hiding the orange, and it "just —
   waited" (Chapter Eight). Fien says, "It's good at it now. It's had the
   practice." What has the family learned about hiding, endurance, and patience
   that lets the seam — and Mies — survive that look? Why does the policeman
   write nothing?

9. **Vos.** We only ever see Vos "from outside," and learn late that he had
   already been taken and "sent east" weeks before his last parcels arrived
   (Chapter Nine). His final relayed message is "the ledger balanced, and the
   winter is the enemy's last." Why does the novel refuse to resolve his fate?
   What is gained — or lost — by keeping him at this distance?

10. **"HERE."** When the food-drop bombers come over at rooftop height, Dirk
    stands on the roof with "both arms, stretched to their utmost," shouting
    "*HERE. HERE. HERE.*" (Chapter Ten). Why is this the emotional peak for many
    readers? What does it mean that the mercy still "took days… to become actual
    bread," that people died "with the sacks already stacked on Dutch ground"?

11. **"Now."** After telling the orange "*not yet*" for four and a half years,
    Mies finally says "*Now*" and pins it in the window on 8 May — and the whole
    canal answers "by dusk… in glass after glass after glass" with oranges of
    its own (Chapter Eleven). Why does the book withhold this moment for so
    long, including three deliberate extra days after the capitulation? What
    makes the street's echo land the way it does?

12. **The keeping drawer.** In the final chapter Mies folds three oranges "that
    did not go to strangers" — for the hidden boy Joop, for the dead Aalders's
    windowsill, and one "paid into the drawer… to be delivered" for Vos, who may
    never come. The drawer "had been the most dangerous place in the house all
    winter, and it was the house's reliquary now… the same trade." What does the
    novel finally say about danger and keeping, forgery and remembrance, being
    the same act? And why does Mies choose the *colour* alone, "no text," for
    the oranges — "you do not gild an orange"?

13. **(Optional — form.)** The novel is told in close third person on Mies,
    past tense, single POV. The dead husband exists only through "memory, habit,
    and the objects he left." How would the book change if we heard from Vos, or
    from Fien, directly? What does the tight focus on one widow, in one house,
    let the author do that a wider lens could not?

14. **(Optional — title.)** By the end, the street's smooth answer to why the
    printer's house flies "a paper orange instead of a flag" is: "*because a
    flag says a country, but an orange says the lights came back.*" How does the
    novel earn that distinction? What does the paper orange mean by the last
    page that it did not mean on the first?

## Topics to research

Groups who want to bring the history into the conversation might read up on:

- The **Dutch Hunger Winter (*Hongerwinter*) of 1944–45** — its causes (the
  September 1944 railway strike and the German food embargo), the country cut in
  two at the great rivers, and the death toll in the occupied west.
- **Ration cards (*bonkaarten*) and the *persoonsbewijs*** — how the wartime
  Dutch ration and identity system worked, and why people in hiding
  (*onderduikers*) could not draw rations without them.
- The **Dutch resistance aid networks** that fed people in hiding, especially
  the *Landelijke Organisatie voor Hulp aan Onderduikers* (LO) — the real-world
  model for the novel's fictional "friends of Sint Nicolaas."
- The **deportation of Amsterdam's Jews** and the **Hollandsche Schouwburg**,
  the history that stands behind the emptied houses of Chapter Five.
- The **hunger treks (*hongertochten*)** and the role of the collaborationist
  **Landwacht** at the city approaches.
- The **Swedish Red Cross white bread** (February–March 1945) and **Operations
  Manna and Chowhound** (the Allied food drops of late April–May 1945).
- The **liberation of the Netherlands**, the 5 May 1945 capitulation, the
  Canadian entry into Amsterdam, and the **7 May shooting on the Dam**.
- The **House of Orange** and why orange was a forbidden colour under the
  occupation — and a colour of defiance and celebration at liberation.
