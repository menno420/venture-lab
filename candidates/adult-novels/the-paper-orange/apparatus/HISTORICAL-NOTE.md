# The Paper Orange — A Historical Note

*Reader's and book-club context note for* **The Paper Orange** *(A Novel of the
Hunger Winter). This is back-matter apparatus — a reader-facing note the author
or publisher can print after the story or paste into the book's supporting
material. It grounds the novel's setting in the historical record and marks,
honestly, where the record ends and the fiction begins. It does not alter one
word of the story.*

---

## The winter the book is set in

**The Paper Orange** takes place in Amsterdam between November 1944 and May
1945, in the months the Dutch remember as the *Hongerwinter* — the Hunger
Winter. It is not an invented catastrophe. The novel opens with Mies Verhoeven
peeling tulip bulbs "like onions" at her kitchen table and boiling out the
bitter yellow heart the food-office pamphlet says is "not suitable for
consumption" (Chapter One). That detail is drawn straight from the record: in
the last winter of the German occupation, tulip and other flower bulbs were
officially distributed as food in the occupied west of the Netherlands, with
printed advice to remove the toxic core.

The chain of events the book assumes, and dramatizes obliquely rather than
lectures on, is the real one:

- **September 1944 — the railway strike and the food embargo.** Chapter One
  tells it plainly: "the trains had stopped in September when the railwaymen
  struck… the Germans had shut the food off from the west like a man closing a
  tap." The Dutch government-in-exile called a national rail strike from London
  to hinder German troop movements after the Allied landings; the railwaymen
  answered it; in reprisal the German authorities embargoed food transport to
  the still-occupied west. The Allied advance then stalled after Arnhem, and
  "the country had been cut in two at the great rivers since the autumn"
  (Chapter One) — the liberated south below the great rivers, the starving west
  above them, through the coldest winter in memory.

- **The collapse of the ration.** The book charts the fall by the calorie. In
  Chapter Six the official ration is "down to two slices of bread a day"; in
  Chapter Seven it "touched five hundred calories that month"; by Chapter Ten
  it is "four hundred grams of bread a week." These figures track the
  historical decline in the western cities over the winter of 1944–45. Roughly
  eighteen to twenty thousand people are generally reckoned to have died of
  hunger and cold in the west that winter.

- **Cold as government (Chapter Five).** With gas and electricity cut off, the
  city burned itself to stay warm — first fences and sheds, then the canal
  elms felled "in a night," then the tarred wooden blocks pried up from between
  the dead tram rails, and finally the woodwork of houses standing empty across
  the Amstel. People cooked on a *noodkacheltje*, the little tin emergency
  stove "the size of a hatbox" (Chapter One), and read by hissing carbide
  bicycle-lamps (Chapter Five). All of this is documented Hunger-Winter daily
  life.

- **The emptied houses (Chapter Five).** The novel's most carefully handled
  history is the one it refuses to make into scenery. Mies takes a handcart
  across the Amstel to salvage firewood and cannot bring herself to take the
  staircase of the bookbinder Sal Winkler, "taken with his whole family in the
  June of 'forty-three." The houses of the old Jewish quarter stood open that
  winter because their inhabitants had been deported — through the
  Hollandsche Schouwburg (the theatre on the Plantage Middenlaan) and the
  eastbound trains — and the living city stripped them for fuel. The book
  registers the deportation of Amsterdam's Jews not as backdrop but as an
  absence the survivors must answer to.

- **The hunger treks and the checkpoints (Chapter Six).** City women walked
  north into the farm country of Waterland and beyond with prams and handcarts
  to barter linen, silver and wedding rings for potatoes and rye — the
  *hongertocht* — and risked having it confiscated on the way home by the
  **Landwacht**, the Dutch collaborationist auxiliary, "checking papers at a
  trestle." Both the treks and the arbitrary confiscations are historical.

- **The hidden and the coupon economy.** The novel's engine is the real
  bureaucratic trap Vos names in Chapter Two: "No registration, no
  *stamkaart*; no *stamkaart*, no coupons; no coupons, nothing." People in
  hiding — *onderduikers* — could not draw rations because rations required
  registration, so aid networks fed them with stolen and forged
  *bonkaarten* (ration cards). Mies's refusal to forge the *persoonsbewijs*
  ("the PB has broken finer workshops than this one, the watermark alone,"
  Chapter Two) mirrors the real difficulty forgers had with the deliberately
  hard-to-copy Dutch identity card.

- **The mercies from outside.** Two late reliefs in the book are historical.
  The **Swedish white bread** of Chapter Nine — flour shipped by the Swedish
  Red Cross and baked into free, off-ration loaves in late February and early
  March 1945 — really happened, and really was, to children who had never seen
  it, an astonishment: "white bread. Real white bread, whiter than peace." And
  the **food drops** of Chapter Ten — Operations *Manna* (RAF) and *Chowhound*
  (USAAF), flown at rooftop height under a negotiated truce from late April
  1945, sacks dropped without parachutes onto marked fields — are the raid Dirk
  greets from the rooftop with his arms up, shouting "*HERE. HERE. HERE.*" The
  novel is honest about the lag the record notes: the food took days to become
  bread, and people died with the sacks already stacked on Dutch ground.

- **Liberation, and its last wound (Chapter Eleven).** The German forces in the
  Netherlands surrendered effective the morning of 5 May 1945; the Canadians
  entered Amsterdam on 8 May. Between those dates, on **7 May**, German naval
  personnel opened fire from a building on the Dam into the crowd waiting to
  celebrate — the *Dam-schietpartij*, dozens dead on liberation's doorstep, no
  order for it ever established. The book keeps the family away from the square
  and hears the shooting "at distance," which is both true to the event and
  true to the mother's hard-won caution.

## The paper orange itself

The book's title object turns on one more piece of history. Orange is the
colour of the House of Orange, the Dutch royal family; under the occupation the
colour and the celebration of the Queen's birthday (31 August) were forbidden,
and in the first summer of the occupation, in 1940, people found small ways to
wear it anyway — "a marigold in a buttonhole, a carrot sticking foolishly out
of a shopping bag" (Chapter One). Hendrik Verhoeven's paper orange, inked and
folded on 31 August 1940 and hidden behind the wallpaper "for the day the
lights come back," is the novel's invention — but the small orange defiances of
1940, and the flags and orange that erupted "out of the linings of settees" at
liberation (Chapter Eleven), are not.

## Where the record ends and the fiction begins

This is a novel, and it is careful to say so. All of its named people are
invented — Mies, Hendrik, Fien and Dirk Verhoeven; the network man "Vos"; old
Mevrouw Aalders; the barber De Wit and his hidden son Joop; the farmwife Trijn;
the bookbinder Sal Winkler. No real historical person appears in a speaking
role. The aid network called "the friends of Sint Nicolaas" is a fictional name
modelled on the real Dutch resistance-aid networks (such as the *Landelijke
Organisatie voor Hulp aan Onderduikers*, the LO) that fed people in hiding with
illicit coupons. The specific shop on the Egelantiersgracht, the paper mill
"loyal to the friends," the exact appearance of the forged coupons, and the
wallpaper cache are all fiction. One real name appears only as a fact of the
trade, never as a character: the typeface *Hollandsche Mediaeval*, designed by
S. H. de Roos (Chapter Four), is a genuine Dutch book face.

The famine, the forgery, the treks, the deportations, the drops, and the
liberation are the history. The family living through them is the story.

## Further reading (public history)

Readers who want the documented record behind the novel can start with
well-known, widely available public history. The following are pointers to the
standard literature and public reference on the period, not sources the novel
quotes:

- Henri A. van der Zee, *The Hunger Winter: Occupied Holland 1944–1945* — the
  best-known English-language narrative history of the winter the book is set
  in.
- The general encyclopedic entries on the **Dutch famine of 1944–1945
  (Hongerwinter)**, the **Dutch railway strike of 1944**, and **Operations
  Manna and Chowhound** — each a reliable overview with onward references.
- On the deportation of Amsterdam's Jews that stands behind Chapter Five: the
  history of the **Hollandsche Schouwburg** (now a national memorial) and the
  wider record of the persecution of Dutch Jewry, of which Anne Frank's diary
  is the most widely read personal document.
- For the postwar reckoning, the multi-volume official history of the
  Netherlands in the Second World War by **Loe de Jong** is the standard Dutch
  scholarly account.

Any one of these will confirm that the arithmetic of *The Paper Orange* — what
a stove would burn, what a bulb would yield, what a checkpoint could take — is
the arithmetic people actually did.
