# THE PAPER ORANGE

*A Novel of the Hunger Winter*

> Amsterdam, the winter of 1944. The presses are cold, the canals are ice, and Mies Verhoeven — a printer's widow with two children and half a sack of tulip bulbs — discovers that the one thing her husband's workshop can still produce is the difference between eating and not: ration coupons good enough to fool a checkpoint. And behind the wallpaper, folded and waiting, is one small paper orange, for the day the lights come back.

---

**Content note:** Adult literary-historical fiction set during the Dutch Hunger Winter of 1944–45. Contains wartime famine and its effects on children and the elderly, deaths from hunger and cold (restrained, largely offpage), bereavement and grief, references to the persecution and deportation of Amsterdam's Jewish community, occupation raids and one distant shooting. No graphic violence; no explicit content. For adult readers.

---

# Chapter One — Half a Sack

The bulbs had to be peeled like onions, and like onions they made you cry, though not for the same reason.

Mies Verhoeven did them at the kitchen table in the last of the daylight, because light was a ration now like everything else. You took off the brown papery skin, and then you halved the bulb and dug out the yellow heart, which was the bitter part, the part the pamphlet from the food office said in its schoolteacher voice was *not suitable for consumption*, as though the rest of it were. What was left you sliced thin and boiled twice, first water thrown away, and it came to the plate grey and sweetish and mealy, tasting of pond and of chestnut and faintly, insultingly, of flowers.

"It's tulip," Dirk said, the first evening she served it, back in November. He was six and had announced it as a discovery, pleased with himself. "We're eating tulip."

"Hyacinth makes you ill. Tulip doesn't," Fien had said, ten years old and reading the pamphlet over her mother's arm, in the flat voice she used now for facts. "It says so."

That had been November, when there was still gas for an hour in the evening and the bulbs had been a strangeness, almost an outing. Now it was the middle of December, the gas was gone, the electricity had followed it the way one goes after a friend, and the half sack under the stairs had a slack look that Mies knew better than to squeeze. She cooked on the noodkacheltje, a little tin emergency stove the size of a hatbox that stood on the big cold range like a child in its father's chair, and it burned whatever she fed it — fence laths, a chopped-up drawer, yesterday a wooden coat hanger that had gone through the flames still wearing its wire hook — and it boiled a pan if you stood over it and gave it your whole attention and half your morning.

Below her, under the floor, the shop kept its own cold. She felt it through her clay-cold feet, the way you feel a cellar. VERHOEVEN — DRUKKERIJ, it still said on the fascia over the shuttered window, gold letters going grey: Verhoeven, printers. The shop had been shut since the paper permits stopped in the autumn, and her husband had been dead since the spring, and some evenings she could not have said which silence came up through the floorboards, the machine's or the man's.

Hendrik had run the press and she had set the type; that had been the shape of the marriage, or one of its shapes. Fifteen years at the type cases with her back to the treadle press's talk, thumb and stick, nicks up, mind your p's and q's, the letters upside down and backwards and her hands understanding them long after her eyes stopped bothering. She could still read a locked-up forme faster mirror-wise than most people read a newspaper. There was no work in that now. There was no work in anything. The last job the shop had done was in March, mourning cards, fifty of them, a heavy black border and HENDRIK VERHOEVEN and the dates, and she had set them herself because there was nobody else to do it, and she had stood at the case with the composing stick in her hand and thought: these are the last words I will ever set for him, and had gone on picking the letters, because the hands go on. Pneumonia, in the end. Three winters of war had worn his chest down to a door with no lock in it, and the fourth had walked in. No drama in it, no story; the doctor came twice and there was nothing to buy that would have helped even if there had been anything to buy. He was forty-six. It was the one death that winter that had nothing to do with the war, and it had hollowed her out more thoroughly than the war had yet managed, and she was ashamed, sometimes, at how the great catastrophe stood respectfully behind the small one.

At the window, the afternoon was giving up. Across the Egelantiersgracht the houses stood in a row with their shoulders up, no smoke from the chimneys, or almost none; you could read the whole street's arithmetic in its chimneys now. The canal had a grey skin forming on it. Under the window, on the side wall where the damp had been getting in since before the war, the wallpaper had lifted along one seam, a hand's width of it, curling away from the plaster like a page somebody had started to turn.

She did not touch it. But she looked at it, the way you look at a sleeping child when you pass the door: counting it, making sure.

Behind that seam, flat against the plaster, lay an orange. Folded out of paper, the size of a small fist if a fist could be flat: Hendrik had made it in the first August of the occupation, the thirty-first of August, the Queen's birthday, when the flags were forbidden and the orange was forbidden and half the city found some small way of wearing it anyway, a marigold in a buttonhole, a carrot sticking foolishly out of a shopping bag. He had stood at the stone in the shop that Sunday and mixed a colour by hand, chrome yellow and vermilion worked together with the palette knife, tint after tint, frowning at it, saying: too red and it's a tomato, too yellow and it's a lemon, there — *there* — that's her colour. He had inked a sheet of good wove paper edge to edge with a hand brayer, orange as the inside of a fire, hung it on the drying line like one square of another country's weather. And in the evening he had folded it, his big presswork hands going small and patient, crease by crease, while Fien, five then, hung on his elbow — into an orange: a plump segmented round thing with a little pursed nub of a stem, cunning as a chestnut, glowing in the lamplight.

*And now?* Mies had asked, meaning: now that you've made the one thing we're not allowed to have.

And Hendrik had walked to the window, where the seam had already begun to lift that year, and slid the flat side of it in against the plaster, and smoothed the paper down over it with his thumb the way he smoothed a sheet onto the tympan, and said: *Now it waits. When the lights come back, it goes in the window.*

The seam had held it for four years. It had become a household object without ever being seen, the way a saint lives in a church wall. On the worst nights — the night the radios had to be handed in, the night in the spring when the men were marched down the Rozengracht and the whole street stood behind its curtains not breathing, the night Hendrik died — she had caught herself, and once caught Fien, resting two fingers on the wallpaper seam in passing, lightly, the way you touch wood.

"Mama." Dirk, at her elbow, materialised the way he did. He had Hendrik's colouring and lately, this month, a new fineness to his face that she did not let herself look at directly, the skin gone thin over the bridge of the nose. "Is it bulbs?"

"It's bulbs with fried onion tonight."

"Real onion?"

"Half a real onion."

He took this the way a man takes the racing results, gravely, weighing it. "Fien says in the south they're liberated and they have white bread and the Canadians give the children chocolate out of the tanks."

"Fien says," Mies agreed. The south. The country had been cut in two at the great rivers since the autumn, the war standing still along them like a man asleep on his feet: below the rivers, Brabant, freedom, other people's luck; above, themselves, the winter. No post crossed. Her sister was in Eindhoven and might as well have been in Canada herself.

"When they come here," Dirk said, "I'm going to ask for the chocolate but I'm going to *share* it."

"That's the right way to do it," she said, and turned the bulbs in the pan, and did not tell him any of the true things, that the trains had stopped in September when the railwaymen struck, that the Germans had shut the food off from the west like a man closing a tap, that the strike had been called from London on the radio and the railwaymen had answered it and the country above the rivers was being made to starve for it now, tap shut, barges few, and the winter coming down early and hard as a shutter. He was six. His war was chocolate, at the far end of a dark street. Let it stay chocolate as long as it could.

They ate at half past five by candle-end, the three of them at the corner of the table nearest the little stove. Fried in the fat there was more onion than bulb, she had seen to that, and even so Fien ate the way she had taught herself to eat this autumn, slowly, in small bites, making the plate last, watching her brother's plate sideways to make sure his was bigger. Ten years old. Mies looked at her daughter doing arithmetic with a boiled tulip and thought, not for the first time, that of everything the winter was taking, it was taking childhood the most quietly.

"Mama," Fien said. "There was a man at the shop door today. At the shutter. When I came back from the soup queue."

Mies kept her fork moving. "A man."

"He knocked at the shutter and waited and then he wrote something and put it through the letter flap. I watched from the bridge." A pause, exactly the length of a child deciding to say the rest. "He looked at the upstairs windows first. Like he knew which house it was."

The letter flap of the shop dropped into a wire basket on the back of the door; she had not emptied it in a week, because nothing came. Mies laid down her fork, went down the inside stairs with the candle — the shop rose around her, the smell of it, ink and lead and machine oil and cold, Hendrik's smell gone mineral — and took the single folded paper out of the basket. No envelope. A small, careful hand, pencil.

*To the widow of H. Verhoeven,* it said. *Your husband once did fine work for the friends of Sint Nicolaas. The friends have need of fine work again, and pay in what matters. If the shop is willing, leave the shutter hook undone tomorrow. If it is not, burn this and forgive the intrusion. — V.*

She stood with the candle in one hand and the note in the other, in the dark shop, between the type cases and the shrouded treadle press, for a long time, until the wax ran warm onto her thumb.

Fine work. *Once did fine work.* She was the compositor of this house. She had set every job that had gone through it for fifteen years, every auction bill, every hymn sheet, every butcher's bag.

She had never set anything for any friends of Sint Nicolaas in her life.

# Chapter Two — Meneer Vos

She burned the note. That was simply sense; you burned everything, now, that was not food or fuel or proof of who you were. But she left the shutter hook undone.

She told herself, wedging the hook open with its own staple the next morning, that it was because a question had been left in her house overnight like a lodger, and she wanted it gone. That was true as far as it went. It did not explain why she had put on Hendrik's grey work coat over her dress, or why she had spent the morning in the shop, uncovering the treadle press and wiping down the bed of it as though the machine were expecting company.

The man came at dusk, through the shop door with the light behind him and his hat already off, which she noted. Fifty or so, narrow, with a clerk's stoop and a good coat gone shiny at the seams, and a way of standing that took up as little of the world as possible. He shut the door behind himself softly and stood on the mat.

"Mevrouw Verhoeven. I'm called Vos." A pause acknowledging that he was called Vos the way the winter was called weather. Fox. Half the country was called Fox now, or Miller, or de Groot. "I knew your husband a little. I was sorry to hear."

"Everyone knew my husband a little," she said. "It was a shop."

"Just so." He looked around it, at the cases, the imposing stone, the press under its regreased shroud, and she watched him look, and saw that he was not browsing; he was checking things off. "You'll forgive me if I don't take up your evening. You read my note. I'll say the rest plainly, because you're not a woman to be courted sideways, and because it's nearly curfew." He set his hat on the counter, crown down, like a man putting down a stake. "There are people in this city who do not exist. You understand me. People under floors and behind cupboards, people whose names went out of the registers, or had better have. A person who does not exist is not fed. No registration, no stamkaart; no stamkaart, no coupons; no coupons, nothing — the soup kitchen wants a coupon, the baker wants a coupon, the central kitchen wants the little numbered scrap or you may die politely in the street. The ration is the leash, mevrouw. They have made the food into a register, and everyone the register cannot see, starves."

"And the friends of Sint Nicolaas feed them," Mies said.

"We try. There are more of them every month — every razzia fills another floor and empties another larder. Some months we get genuine cards. Offices are raided; a man inside a distribution office grows a conscience, or a price. I'll not pretend to you: stolen is better than forged, stolen is *real*. But the offices have grown careful, and the couriers thin, and last month we fed our people on two-thirds of what we needed, and this month is worse. So." He looked at her directly for the first time, and his eyes in the ruined clerk's face were steady and perfectly serious. "Bonnen, mevrouw. Bread and potato coupons of the current period, good enough to pass a tired grocer at four in the afternoon in a dark shop. Not the persoonsbewijs — I know better than to ask; the PB has broken finer workshops than this one, the watermark alone, no. Just the little scraps. The leash, cut in a few places."

The shop was very quiet. Upstairs she could hear the children's feet, Fien's then Dirk's, crossing the ceiling, and the winter ticking in the windowpanes.

"You said my husband did fine work for you."

"Twice," Vos said. "In 'forty-three. Inserts for identity work — not the card itself, the inner leaves. Beautiful work. He was slow and he swore at us and he charged nothing." A small dry smile. "He said — I'll give it to you as he gave it, mevrouw, you'll know if it's true coin — he said: *my wife is the better half of this shop at everything but lying, so she doesn't hear of it.*"

That was the coin, minted true, Hendrik to the last serif; and it went into her like a blade so exactly shaped for the wound that for a moment she could not breathe around it. Eight months dead, and still arranging matters so that the danger stood in his half of the shop and not in hers. Still deciding for her. Still protecting her from her own house.

"He was wrong," she heard herself say. "I'm an excellent liar. I've told two children every night since March that things will be all right."

Vos inclined his head slowly, as though she had shown him a credential.

"What you're asking is death," she said. "Let's have that on the counter with your hat. If they take a woman for printing false papers, they shoot her or they send her east, and my children have no father to fall back to. That's the sum on my side of the ledger. Two children, no net under them but me."

"Yes," said Vos simply. "I won't insult you with less. That's the sum. Against it I can put only what's in the other column: that the people we feed are also somebody's sum. I had a list read to me on Tuesday — I won't carry paper, so I carry lists in my head, and this one recites badly." He looked at the shuttered window while he said it, flatly, a man reading off a wall she couldn't see. "A boy of seventeen under a floor in the Kinkerbuurt since the September razzia; his mother queues at two kitchens under two names, that's how he's alive. A family of four behind a false wall no wider than that stone. An old man who has not been outdoors since 'forty-two, mevrouw, whose whole nation is a back room, and who the register says went east with the rest of his street." The flat voice stopped being flat, one degree. "The friends ask. That's all the friends do. And if the answer is no, I take my hat and you never see me again, and no one will ever know the question was put — which is another thing I can promise that the other side can't."

She thought about it standing up, in the cold, in her husband's coat, which was how, she would think later, all the real decisions of that winter were taken: on your feet, in the cold, quickly, because the cold did not let anything take long.

"Come back Thursday," she said. "I'll want a sample of what I'm to match — a real card of the period, front and back. And paper. Coupon stock is nothing like jobbing stock; if you bring me newsprint I'll send you home with it. And then I'll tell you whether it can be done at all, and you'll take my answer as professional, not as patriotic."

"Professional," said Vos, picking up his hat, and for the second time there was the small dry smile, deepened now by one exact degree. "Mevrouw, in four years, do you know, that is the most reassuring word anyone has said to me in that doorway."

When he had gone she stood a while, then took the candle to the back of the shop, to Hendrik's cabinet under the window where the finishing tools lived. Third drawer. It had stuck since always; you lifted the left corner. She had not opened it since March. She was looking, she told herself, for the fine tweezers.

The drawer was shallow, and it had been re-lined since she had last known it, with a sheet of grey blotting paper cut to fit. Under the blotting paper, flat and orderly as type in a case: a rotary numbering box she had never bought, its wheels cleaned and oiled and set to a number that meant nothing to her. Two fine sable pens. A shallow tin of stamp-pad inks in three greens and a violet, official, ugly, unmistakable colours, not one of which had ever been used on a job that went through the books. And underneath all of it, folded once, a proof sheet: rows of small rectangles, grey-green, dense with tiny numbered type, the impression a hair too heavy on the left — he had never quite trusted the packing on that side of the platen — and in the margin, in soft pencil, in the hand that had written her name on fifteen years of parcels and one wartime will, a single word with a line under it.

*Beter.* Better.

Not good. Better. He had been teaching himself, in this drawer, at night, in the shop under her bed, and he had died before he got to *good*, and he had told her none of it.

Mies sat down slowly on the stool at the imposing stone, in the cold, among her husband's secrets, which were all of them mercies, which somehow made it worse and made it holy at the same time; and she put her face into the grey coat's sleeve, which no longer smelled of him, and she wept for the first time since the funeral — not long, the cold did not let anything take long — and then she blew her nose, and lit the second candle she could not afford, and began, mirror-wise, professionally, to read her husband's last proof.

# Chapter Three — The Drawer

Thursday brought Vos, and Vos brought a parcel done up as if from a butcher, in coarse paper with a string, with a smear of actual blood on one corner, which she chose to find thorough rather than theatrical. Inside: a genuine bonkaart of the current period, whole, unclipped — bread, potatoes, the little squares ranked and numbered like an army; a second card clipped half-empty for comparison; and forty sheets of blank stock.

She took the stock to the window light and knew inside three seconds, by the feel alone, that it was real. Coupon paper had a hard mean rattle to it, sized heavily so the shopkeeper's pencil wouldn't feather, greyish in the body with a faint machine texture like a twill; you could no more counterfeit that in a jobbing shop than you could counterfeit weather. "Where does this come from?"

"A mill loyal to the friends. Ask me nothing else about it."

"Then it can be done," Mies said. "Or it can be failed at properly. Come back in six days."

What she did in the six days was learn the size of her inheritance.

The type was there — that had been Hendrik's first smuggling, done in plain sight in the fount cases, the small sizes of the coupon face standing in among her everyday jobbing founts like deserters in borrowed uniforms; once she knew to look, she found he had even cased the special figures, the squat wartime numerals with the blind 6 and the flat-topped 3. Her side of the trade, at least, was only work. She stood at the case with the composing stick cold in her left hand and set the little rectangles line by line, BROOD 125 GRAM, one period-letter, one serial, the state's own mean grey grammar, and found that her hands still knew everything and cared about nothing, which was the correct professional arrangement of hands and heart, and had been since March.

The press was another matter. The press had been Hendrik's country. It stood under the window on its cast-iron frame like a black stork, the treadle below, the flywheel at its side, and it wanted no electricity — there was none to want — only a leg and a rhythm and a temper it would set itself against yours. She fed it her first forme on the Friday night with the children asleep and the blanket over the window and one candle, and it bit the sheet crooked, and creased the second, and printed the third so grey it looked exhausted. The ink was four years old and stiff as tar with the cold; she warmed the tin against the stove upstairs like a baby's bottle, worked it soft on the stone with the knife, took it down again, tried again. Too much ink and the little letters filled in and went fat: false at arm's length. Too little and they went starved and broken: false at a glance. The state's printing had exactly one virtue, dullness, and dullness, she was discovering, was a colour you had to mix and a weight you had to earn.

*Beter,* said the pencil in the drawer, every night, under the line.

She got it — the evening of the fifth day — by stopping trying to print well and starting to try to print *badly in precisely the government's way*: a fraction over-inked on the left, because the proof in the drawer ran heavy on the left, because the state's own platens were old and their packing tired and nobody at a state works cared, and the tiredness was the signature. She pulled a sheet, and laid it beside the genuine card in the candlelight, and her breath went out of her slowly.

You could see it, if you were the one who had made it. The 6 in her serial sat a half-hair high; hand numbering would fix that. Otherwise: grey, mean, dull, tired, real.

"Well," she said aloud to the press, to the shop, to the third drawer, to the general dark. "Better."

The word came out in his intonation. She heard it do it. That was the other thing about the trade she had married into and now inherited whole: it was made entirely of habits, and the habits were made of him, thumb on the tympan, the little settling shrug before a run, tap the quoins twice, and there was no way to do the work without walking around inside the man all evening. She had expected the shop to be where he was most gone. It was where he was least gone. Grief, which she had thought by now she knew the whole shape of, had this one last room in it, and the room was warm, and she stood in it with her forgeries drying on the line where the orange sheet had dried in another August, and could not have said what she felt, except that it was enormous, and that she could feed people with it.

Vos came on the sixth day and she gave him the trial sheets, cut and numbered, and made herself say it like a tradesman: "Twelve cards' worth. This period only — when they change the design in the new period, I start over, so bring me the new card the first day you see one. The numbering follows the genuine series; here's where my range starts and ends, keep your people inside it and spread them thin. Never two of mine in one shop on one day. And they pass at dusk, in busy shops, with tired grocers, as you said. They do not pass at a bank window and they do not pass a control with good light, and anyone who tries is spending my children's lives to find out what I've just told him for nothing."

Vos looked at the little grey rectangles for a long time. He did not say they were beautiful, which she was grateful for, because they were bread coupons and their whole ambition was to be beneath notice.

"There's a boy in the Kinkerbuurt," he said finally, "who eats this week because a widow on the Egelantiersgracht is a professional." He put on his hat. "Print, mevrouw. The friends will keep the paper coming as long as God and the mill allow."

That night she went up at one in the morning, dead on her feet, hands smelling of ink and turpentine substitute, and stood as she now stood every night for a moment by the window, two fingers on the lifted seam of the wallpaper. Under the paper, under her fingers, the flat crackle of the folded orange, patient as a bulb under snow.

"Not yet," she told it, which is what Hendrik used to tell it, on the bad nights, as though the orange were the impatient one, as though it were the orange that had to be held back from the window, and not the whole starving, waiting, iron-shuttered street; and she checked the blanket over the glass, and went to bed in her clothes, and slept, for the first time since March, the whole night through.

# Chapter Four — Grey-Green

December set into the city like ink into paper.

The work found its rhythm, and the rhythm was this: composing by daylight, upstairs, at the kitchen table, with the small formes carried up in her apron pocket, because type can be sorted where children do their lessons and no one thinks anything of a printer's widow fiddling with metal; presswork by night, downstairs, two nights a week, never the same two, the blanket over the shop window and a felt pad under the platen's kiss to soften the clack of it, the treadle worked in stockinged feet. A press cannot be silenced, only domesticated. Its sound, damped, was like a big dog turning around in its sleep, twice a second, for three hours; she learned which of the neighbours' walls it carried to, and it carried to Mevrouw Aalders's, and Mevrouw Aalders was eighty-one and deaf on exactly the correct side, which Mies chose to take as the winter's one act of courtesy.

Aalders lived above the empty tailor's next door, one flight up, on tea she no longer had and pride she would not ration. Before the war she had kept three canaries and complained about everyone's children from her window with great enjoyment; the canaries were gone — she did not say where, and no one asked, that winter, where a birdcage's occupants had gone — and the complaining had gone quiet, which frightened Mies more than any siren. Twice a week Fien carried up a covered plate, bulbs done as nicely as bulbs went, once a slice of real bread, and came down each time with the plate refused-then-accepted and some small former treasure pressed on her in payment — a thimble, a tortoiseshell button, once an entire cake of pre-war soap, pink, scented, absurd, holy.

"She says she isn't hungry in the mornings anymore," Fien reported, frowning, on the shortest day of the year. "She says it as if it's good news."

Mies had begun, by then, to know what that meant. Everyone in the street was learning that winter's particular medicine: that hunger, after enough weeks of it, stopped shouting and turned polite, and that the politeness was the dangerous stage; that the swelling at the ankles was called oedema by the doctor and called *the water* by the queue; that old people did not so much die of it as agree with it.

So an extra card went up the stairs under the covered plate on New Year's week. A genuine-looking, freshly numbered, grey-green lie: and there it was, the whole moral economy of her nights, folded into a square under a plate of tulip bulbs. Aalders, who was not a fool, who had watched this street through two wars from her window, held the card to the light, and then looked at Mies for a long moment with her little bright bird eyes.

"My late husband," said Mevrouw Aalders, "always maintained that Verhoeven was the last honest printer in the Jordaan."

"He was," said Mies.

"Mm," said Aalders, and put the card in her sleeve. "Thank God the standards of the house have slipped."

Across the canal at the barber's, De Wit had stopped being able to meet anyone's eyes in October, which in that street, in that year, was as good as an announcement. His boy Joop was nineteen and had not answered the labour call-up in the autumn, and there was a raised floor in the back room where the towels were laundered; everyone knew, no one knew, the whole street held its knowing the way you hold your breath in church. De Wit still shaved half the neighbourhood — cold water, weekday queues, a sliver of that pink soap doing the rounds like a relic — and when Mies came in on the last Saturday of the year to have her scissors ground, he took her hand in both of his under the pretence of taking the scissors, and left in it, folded very small, three genuine coupons somebody had given him, and would not take them back.

"For the children," he said, to the strop, not to her. And then, so quietly it hardly moved the air, still to the strop: "There are more of us fed by this street than the street thinks. I don't ask how. I cut hair. But there's a household on this canal that God watches specially, mevrouw, and it isn't mine."

She walked home over the bridge with her ground scissors and her three coupons, past the shut shops, under the swept sky — the one abundance that December was stars, the city blacked out and starving under the most magnificent skies of the century — and found that she could not tell whether she wanted to laugh or cry, and settled, being her mother's daughter, for neither.

Sint Nicolaas came on the fifth and there was nothing in the shoes. Or nearly nothing: she had saved two real sugar cubes from an ancient tin, and Fien got Hendrik's brass line-gauge, which she had coveted since she was seven, and Dirk got the paper boat his father had folded him one Sunday on the Prinsengracht — Mies had kept it in the linen press three years for no reason she could have defended before this one — and a poem, because the poem was the law of the feast. She had set the poem in eight-point Hollandsche Mediaeval and pulled a proof of it like a proper job, DEAR DIRK, CAPTAIN OF THIS HOUSE, and the boy, who had been six days sulking that the Sint could not get through the Germans, read his own name in real print and looked up at her with his whole face open, and said: "He *came*. He came in the *shop* way," — and Fien, ten going on forty across the table, met her mother's eye and said nothing at all, and turned her line-gauge over in her fingers, and set it, precisely, at true.

Vos came the Thursday after Christmas — she had fed him bulbs and he had eaten them like a man at a banquet, out of courtesy so old it had become sincerity — and brought the new period's card, as instructed, on the first day of it. He also brought, wrapped inside the butcher paper against the stock, without comment, a quarter pound of real coffee.

"I won't ask how the friends came by this," Mies said.

"The friends," said Vos, with dignity, "looted it back." He turned his hat in his hands, once, which she had learned was the whole of his visible register of feeling. "The list I carry got shorter by one this week, mevrouw. Not the bad way. The boy in the Kinkerbuurt — his mother got him moved out to a farm placement, north of the city. He went out of town on a genuine card, but he ate to the city limits on one of yours." He put his hat on. "I thought the shop should hear a delivery confirmed, once, out loud. It won't become a habit. Good night."

She set the new period that same night, the state's grammar changed in six small mean ways, and her hands took the changes in without complaint. Outside the frost deepened; the canal shut over like an eye; the year turned. She stood at the window a moment before bed with the coffee tin in her hand — they would drink it, she decided, one cup at a time, on days that earned it — and touched the seam once, lightly, and told the wall's other lodger, *not yet.*

# Chapter Five — What Burned

In January the cold stopped being weather and became government. It ruled everything; it had a policy on everything; it stood in the room with you. The city's gas had gone, the electricity had gone, and now the city itself began to be fed to the stoves. First the fences went, and the garden sheds, sawn up almost lawfully, each street eating its own extremities like a starving body drawing blood in from the fingers. Then the trees — the elms of a canal born before Napoleon, down in a night, worked over by families with bucksaws until by morning there was nothing at the kerb but a fresh pale stump like a pulled tooth. Then the tram blocks: the trams had stopped running when the power died, and between the rails the road was paved with tarred wooden blocks, and the blocks came up by crowbar in the dark, street by street, so that the tramways stood stripped down their middles like filleted fish. The blocks burned hot and filthy, spitting tar. Everyone's ceilings went grey that month. It was, people said in the queues, with the specific gallows courtesy of that winter, the first time in living memory the trams had warmed anybody.

And then the houses. There were, by that January, whole streets across the river Amstel, in the old Jewish quarter, standing empty — and the word *empty* did the same work in every mouth that winter, the same small cowardice, and Mies used it too, and knew it each time: not empty. Emptied. The people of those houses had been taken away, street by street, over two years, in the trams that now ran nowhere, to the theatre on the Plantage Middenlaan and from the theatre to the trains, and the trains east; and their houses had stood since with the doors forced and the winter walking in and out. Now the living city was coming across the bridges with sledges and prams and taking the woodwork. Stairs, doors, floorboards, window frames, roof beams: by spring some of those houses would stand open to the sky, holed like teeth, gutted down to brick by their neighbours' stoves.

She went. That has to be said plainly, because she said it plainly to herself for the rest of her life: on the seventeenth of January, with the children's room at four degrees and the last fence lath gone, Mies Verhoeven took the handcart across the Amstel like everyone else, to take a dead man's staircase to burn under her children's supper.

The street she came to was one she had known. A bookbinder Hendrik had traded work with had lived and worked there — Sal Winkler, a small precise laughing man who bound hymnals for half the churches of the city and could gild a spine blindfold; he had bound their wedding album as a gift, grey linen, her maiden name and Hendrik's in gold on the cover, and he had been taken with his whole family in the June of 'forty-three, and the shop had been sealed, and that was all anyone knew, which was its own sentence in the language of that time: *that was all anyone knew.*

The door of the Winklers' building stood open on darkness. Up and down the street came the sounds of quiet demolition, wood squealing off nails, no voices; nobody talked at that work. On the doorpost, where two years of weather had scoured the paint, was a small oblong shadow, darker paint in the shape of an absence, two nail holes, where the little case with the scripture in it had been fixed and had been torn away. She stood in front of it with the handcart's rope in her fist. Through the doorway she could see the bottom of the staircase, the handrail Sal Winkler's hand had worn pale, going up into the dark.

It was not that she thought the dead would grudge it. That was the terrible thing, she thought afterwards; it was the opposite. It was that she could hear exactly what Sal Winkler would say — *take it, Mies, wood is wood, warm those children* — could hear the laugh in it, the generosity that had been the whole man; and that to take his staircase on the strength of his imagined kindness, while he could not stand in his own doorway to offer it, was a transaction her hands would not close. The city could call it salvage. Her hands called it what you did to people once the trains had made sure they could never mind.

She stood there long enough for the cold to make the decision procedural, took the handcart home over the bridge empty, and that evening went down to the shop and started on its furniture instead. The paper cupboard's doors. The compositor's high stool that had been her own throne for fifteen years — it burned an entire evening, seasoned beech, lovely. The wide pine shelving Hendrik's father had put up in nineteen-oh-six. She fed the shop of three generations of Verhoevens to the tin stove board by board and spared only what worked: the press, the cases, the stone, the third drawer. And if it was madness to burn your own house rather than a dead man's — and the street, had it known, would have called it exactly that — it kept something in her upright that winter, some beam the frost never got, and she came to think, in the years after, that of everything she did in the Hunger Winter this was the act she was never once sorry for.

The children did their lessons that month by carbide light. The lamp was a squat brass thing off a bicycle, fed with grey stones and water, and it hissed and stank like a small dragon and gave a light whiter than the electric had ever been, and Dirk loved it with his whole heart and was allowed, as captain of the house, to drip the water in. Aalders came down on the coldest nights and slept in the kitchen by the banked stove, complaining magnificently, restored to complaint by cold as some invalids are restored by sea air. And in the third week of January, Vos's knock changed: one soft, two quick — paper — and she went down and found the butcher's parcel on the mat and the street empty, both ways, in the white starlight; and pulled that period's run with her breath smoking over the platen, in fingerless gloves Fien had knitted her from an unravelled pullover, grey-green lies by dragon-light, while the cold stood at her shoulder all night like a foreman.

*Not yet,* she told the wall, going up at two.

The seam had frozen. Under her two fingers, for the first time, the paper over the orange was stiff with frost, and did not give at all — as if the wall itself had joined the occupation; as if even the promise had been put on the ration.

# Chapter Six — The Barrow

By the end of January the arithmetic stopped working. The official ration was down to two slices of bread a day and the potatoes had stopped coming altogether; the central kitchen's soup had become a broth in which, as Aalders put it, the vegetable had died leaving no relatives. The half sack under the stairs was down to its corners. Dirk had stopped growing — the doctor said it kindly, as though pausing were something children did, like sulking — and there were evenings now when he did not ask what was for supper, and those evenings were worse than any siren, worse than boots in the street, worse than anything except the mornings after them.

So Mies did what the whole city was doing. She joined the hunger trek.

They went out from Amsterdam every day that winter in their thousands — women, mostly, because a man on the road was a man offering himself to the labour round-ups — walking north into the farm country with prams and handcarts and bicycles with wooden tyres, and walking back days later, or not walking, some of them; there were people that winter who died on the dykes with a bag of rye on their backs, within sight of the city. The trades ran one way: linen, silver, wedding rings, wristwatches, going out; potatoes, rye, a piece of bacon if God smiled, coming back. The farms nearest the city had been traded bare by December. You walked further now. Everyone had a story of a cousin who had walked to Friesland over the frozen sea-dike, and everyone had the other story too, about the checkpoints on the way home, where what you had walked forty hours for could be taken out of your hands in forty seconds.

She left on a Tuesday before light, Aalders installed in the kitchen with the children and the last of the real coffee, and walked north out of the sleeping city with the handcart: over the IJ by the ferry, which still ran, crowded even at that hour with barrows and prams and grey faces all pointed the same way, and then out along the dyke roads into Waterland, into the wind. In the cart, under sacking: Hendrik's Sunday suit, good English cloth bought in the fat year of 'thirty-eight and worn nine times; two linen sheets of her trousseau with her initials embroidered at the hem; the tortoiseshell buttons; the pink soap. She had lain awake deciding about the suit and taken it in the end almost angrily — *he would sell you for potatoes, Mies, and be right* — and all the first hours of the walking it rode behind her with its arms folded, like a passenger with opinions.

The country was iron. The great polders ran flat and white to the ring dykes, the windmills stood bare-armed, the farmhouses sat far apart in their windbreaks like ships anchored offshore, showing no lights. At the first two farms the dogs were loose in the yard and no one came out, and a printed card was nailed to the gatepost — NO TRADING, EVERYTHING GONE, GOD HELP US ALL — that she read with a compositor's eye even then, noting the worn face and the amateur spacing, thinking: everyone's at some press this winter.

At the third farm, in the shadow of the Beemster dyke, a woman her own age came out, looked a long moment at the handcart, and said: "Come in out of the wind, then. It goes quicker warm."

The kitchen was the first truly warm room Mies had stood in since November, and the warmth nearly undid her; that was the humiliation the city no longer had the food to feel, and it caught her at a farm table instead, her eyes filling stupidly at the sight of a side of bacon hanging from a beam like a hymn. The farmwife — Trijn, she said, no surname offered or wanted — traded hard but not cruelly, which by that winter was the countryside's highest available virtue. The sheets she turned in raw red hands, and priced fairly; the buttons she pushed back across the table, *keep those, they're your mother's or I'm a Frenchman*; and then the suit came out of its sacking, and the kitchen went quiet.

"That's a good suit," Trijn said at last.

"He was a good printer," Mies said. It came out before she knew it was coming, the tense doing all the work, and the farmwife looked at her once, sharp and complete, the whole file read in a glance: widow, city, two children or three, come forty kilometres in ice with the wedding sheets.

"Then it'll be for something," Trijn said, obscure and final, and stood, and got weighing.

Mies walked back next day with sixty pounds in the cart: potatoes, a small sack of rye, brown beans, a piece of bacon the size of a psalm book, six eggs packed in the rescued buttons' tin, and, wedged in by the farmwife at the last with no negotiation permitted, a stone jar of dripping. She had slept in the hay barn with eleven other women from the city, all of them lying awake in the dark doing the same arithmetic, listening to the wind off the Zuiderzee try the doors, and had set off before light on legs that had opinions of their own now; and it was late afternoon, in the last grey light, at the very lip of the city — the north bank of the IJ in sight, the ferry pier a black comb in the water — when she came round the tail of a line of barrows and saw the checkpoint.

Landwacht. Not even Germans: the home-grown kind, Dutchmen in black with shotguns on their shoulders and armbands, the occupier's Dutch help, checking papers at a trestle and going through the carts of their own starving countrywomen with a thoroughness the queue endured in a silence you could have skated on. They were confiscating by mood. That was the truth of those posts and everyone in the queue knew it: there was a regulation quantity you were allowed to bring in, and then there was the man in front of you and what kind of morning he'd had. Ahead of her a woman argued over a sack of rye — her own voice climbing, then breaking off short at something said quietly across the trestle — and stood after with her face wiped blank, watching the sack go under the table, learning the winter's oldest lesson: that the argument was also on the ration.

Mies stood in the queue with her heart doing its work high in her throat, and her papers in her glove. Her papers were genuine. Her cart was over the limit; everyone's cart was over the limit; the limit was designed to be over. In her coat's hem, sewn flat between cloth and lining at the left seam, rode eight of her own grey-green coupons, the last of the period's run, kept back for Aalders and the De Wits — eight small rectangles of evidence sufficient to take her from her children forever, pressed against her hip like a hand.

The Landwachter who turned over her load was twenty-two or -three, with a farm face and cold-cracked lips, and he did it lazily, tipping the sacking back, weighing the bacon in his hand while he looked at her, the way they did, waiting to be given a reason of any kind, or none.

"Whose suit did this buy, then?" he said. Amused. The suit's sacking was still in the cart, with Hendrik's careful chalk mark on it from some job of years ago.

"My husband's," Mies said. And then — she heard herself do it, the flat professional voice of six weeks at the press, dull, mean, grey-green, beneath notice — "He has no further use for it. If you take the bacon, take it. It's a long queue and my children eat at six."

Whatever the reason was that he had been waiting to be given, that was not it; boredom, or the next cart, or some tiny leftover ledger entry of shame, tipped him the other way. He took the eggs — all six, the tin too, buttons and all, because taking something was the trade's grammar and eggs travelled — and waved her through, already looking past her; and Mies Verhoeven walked the last kilometre onto the ferry very upright, with sixty pounds of winter behind her and eight coupons burning cold at her hip, and did not begin to shake until the water was moving under the deck.

They ate that night at half past six, eggless, like kings: real potatoes with dripping, and the psalm-book bacon rendered into a fragrance that brought Dirk to the table before he was called, on the run, shouting the menu back into the flat like news of a landing. Aalders wept two entire tears over the brown beans and blamed the onions, of which there were none. And when the children were asleep Mies unpicked the coat's hem stitch by stitch by carbide light, laid the eight little lies flat on the kitchen table, and looked at them a long time.

Then she got the sewing box out again, and sewed the seam back, empty, ready.

*Not yet.*

# Chapter Seven — The Shortest Month

February was the month the winter stopped pretending there would be survivors of every house.

It was the shortest month and the longest. The thaw came mid-way and made everything crueller, the canals rotting open, the streets in slush, the cold exchanging its clean knife for a wet rope; hunger does worse work at two degrees above freezing than at ten below, the doctor said, because the damp gets the weak ones' chests, and the doctor was run so ragged by then that he said such things aloud, on stairs, forgetting who was listening. The official ration touched five hundred calories that month. The queues outside the central kitchens began forming at half past five in the morning, in the dark, and the queue itself became one of the places the winter did its killing: there were mornings that February when someone at the far end sat down against a wall, out of turn, quietly, the way Amsterdammers did everything now, and did not get up, and the queue would part around the fact of it and close again, holding its pans.

Mies saw the city that month with a terrible double sight. On top, the streets she had known her whole life, going about a starving parody of themselves. Underneath, the other city, the one she was a citizen of now: the network. A courier girl on a wooden-tyred bicycle who crossed her path twice a week and never looked at her. The greengrocer on the Westerstraat who took her grey-green coupons at dusk with a hand quicker than his face, and slipped the clipped halves not into the drawer but into his boot. De Wit's strop going all day behind his steamed window, and under his laundry-room floor a nineteen-year-old shape that had not stood upright since September. She had joined the invisible commonwealth of the fed and the feeding, and its whole liturgy was performed at counters, in gloves, in silence, and its whole creed was: today also, nobody vanished.

Mevrouw Aalders died on the ninth of February, in the kitchen, by the banked stove, in her sleep, in the night between one soup day and the next. There was no drama in it; the winter's deaths had given up drama entirely by then, as if drama too were rationed. She had complained at nine o'clock, with vigour and specificity, about the tepidness of the ersatz tea, the amateurishness of the RAF, who had kept her awake crossing to Germany, and the moral decline of a street that let its children skate on thaw ice; and at seven in the morning she was still in the chair with her hands folded and her face set in the mild professional scepticism with which she had watched the twentieth century conduct itself under her window, and was cold.

The water was off in the pipes and the ground too wet for digging and the city had run out of coffins in January. That was simply the state of things, that February, and everyone knew it the way they knew the ration: the dead waited now. They waited in side rooms and in cold churches — in the Zuiderkerk across town, people said, they lay in rows under the whitewash where Rembrandt had buried his children, numbered, patient, waiting for wood and for ground and for men with the strength to dig — and the living stood in the soup queue with that knowledge tucked in under their scarves, and held their pans, and shuffled forward. Aalders did better than most, having been loved by a street with one carpenter left in it: the coffin was fence-plank and shelf-board — a plank of the Verhoeven paper cupboard went into it, which Mies found fitting past all expressing — and she went to the Noorderkerk on the neighbours' shoulders, and waited there ten days for her ground, in good company, with the psalms said over her twice for safety.

Fien took it the way she took everything now, which was into the ledger, silently, at true. But that evening she came and stood at the wallpaper seam, openly, both fingers on it, the first time in her life she had ever done it in front of her mother — the touching had always been a private commerce, each of them pretending not to see the other — and she said, in the flat voice, not turning round: "She waited four and a half years. She only needed a few months more. It isn't fair," and then, with the terrifying precision of ten: "Write it down somewhere that she was owed the orange."

"I'll write it down," Mies said, and did, that night, in the shop, in soft pencil, in the margin of Hendrik's proof sheet in the third drawer, under his one underlined word, the second entry of what became, before the winter was done, a list: *A. Aalders, owed the orange, 9 Feb.*

And Dirk — Dirk that month became the arithmetic she did in the dark. He was not ill. The doctor, rubbing his eyes, confirmed it in the only grammar left to him: not ill, only hungry; there is nothing wrong with this child that a liberated country would not mend in six weeks. He had gone translucent and slow, that was all, a boy done in watercolour where there had been a boy in oils; he played sitting down now, moving the lead soldiers through campaigns of great intricacy and no motion, and he had stopped, entirely, asking what was for supper — and once, watching him not ask, she understood with a clarity the cold made possible that his not-asking was courtesy: that the captain of the house had privately concluded that the question hurt his mother, and had struck it off his ration.

That was the night the last restraint went out of her forging. Until February she had run Vos's paper and no more, the friends' stock for the friends' mouths, her own eight coupons the one private levy. After the ninth — Aalders in the Noorderkerk, Dirk's courtesy, the queue parting around its quiet fact and closing — Mies went down, and unlocked the chase of the current period, and printed to the very last sheet of the month's stock, edge to edge, no margin kept for scruple; and when Vos's knock came she met him at the door with the count and with a flat new sentence she had set in her mind all day like a line of pica:

"Bring more paper. Whatever the mill can lose. The design changes again in three weeks and I can hold the new period in four days — I've got his hand now as well as my own." She heard the plural and let it stand. "We're quicker than we were, and the winter isn't. Bring more paper."

Vos looked at her a moment — at the widow in the grey work coat in the doorway of the last honest print-shop in the Jordaan — and what moved in the ruined clerk's face was carefully put away before it could be named.

"Mevrouw," he said, "the friends have been waiting all winter for someone to say that," and was gone into the dark; and the parcels, from that week, came heavier.

# Chapter Eight — Locked Formes

The trouble, when it came, came the way she had always known it would: not through the work, but through the street's own goodness.

On the last Tuesday of February the courier girl did not cross her path. No bicycle Thursday either. On Friday the greengrocer's shutter stayed down on a weekday, and the queue outside it dispersed with the studied slowness of people declining to have seen anything; and that evening De Wit crossed the bridge in the dusk, hatless, to return — loudly, on the doorstep, for the whole street's benefit — a pair of scissors he had never borrowed.

"They took Meneer Blom Wednesday," he said through it, barely moving the air. "The greengrocer. Coupons in the boot, they say, a whole winter of them. And there's a black car been down the Westerstraat twice today, and men in it writing." The strop-and-lather hands, wringing each other flat on her doorstep. "Blom's a rock, but every man's a rock till they start on him, mevrouw. If there's anything in any house on this canal that shouldn't be in it — tonight's the night houses get tidy."

Vos had a protocol for this; Vos had a protocol for everything; she had taken it in with the first parcel like a catechism. *Nothing sleeps in your house that can testify. Type is innocent until it's standing in coupon order — so the formes stay locked only while the press runs, and the instant the run ends, they're dissed back into the cases, letter by letter into the alphabet's honest crowd, where no policeman born can tell a false 6 from a true one. Stock and finished work leave the same night they're made. The numbering box is a numbering box; every shop has one. The inks are inks. Your hands are a widow's hands. And the one thing that can hang you is the thing you keep for sentiment — so keep nothing.*

She worked until two that night, and was correct by dawn. The February run was gone already, gone with the last parcel; the blank stock — nine sheets, the winter's whole remaining treasury — could not leave, there being nowhere and no one to carry it, so it went where Hendrik's ghost suggested: interleaved singly into the middle of the shop's dead-stock ledgers of 'thirty-one to 'thirty-eight, one mean grey sheet between fat honest pages of invoices for chapel notices and cheese-market bills, on the open shelf, in plain sight, dusty, boring, beneath notice. The little coupon founts were dissed back into the cases and stirred deep. The third drawer she did last, at two in the morning, by one candle, and stood over it, and had it out with herself and with him.

The numbering box: stays, is innocent. The pens, the pad inks: stay, are innocent. The proof sheet — the grey-green rows a hair heavy on the left, the soft pencil, *Beter*, and beneath it now her own small column, the list of the owed — was evidence and only evidence, of no use to any living soul, worth a bullet, keepable for not one reason on earth except that his hand had moved across it and that burning it was burning the last unspent thing he had ever said to her.

*The one thing that can hang you is the thing you keep for sentiment.*

"You'd burn it," she said to the shop, and the shop did not disagree; and she put the proof in the tin stove upstairs and watched her husband's last lesson go the colour of the winter and then the colour of the orange and then go nothing at all; and wrote the list of the owed out fresh, from memory, in her own hand, on the back page of her household book between the turf account and the christening dates, where a list of names and small sums is the most innocent object a Dutch kitchen contains. If they came now, let them come. The house was all wool and alphabet.

They came Sunday, mid-morning, with the bells of the Westertoren going for the second service — Grüne Polizei, green as their name, four of them and a Dutchman in civilian grey who did the talking, working down the even numbers of the canal, methodical, unhurried, a routine control: which was the worst kind, De Wit always said, routine being merely a raid that hasn't found anything yet. She heard them at the tailor's. Then the knock.

What she learned about herself in the following fifty minutes she kept for the rest of her life, in the same pocket as the checkpoint on the IJ: that terror, professionally accommodated, is only attention. She stood in her kitchen in her apron with her genuine papers in her hand and became her own dullest sheet: the widow Verhoeven, printer's relict, shop shut for want of permits — the permits' lapse documented in that folder there, meneer, in order, initialled — two children at the table doing sums; and the grey Dutchman turned over the house with a boredom that never once reached his eyes. He was not a fool. That was the fifty minutes' work: being nothing, in front of a man who was not a fool.

In the shop he woke the press with one finger on the flywheel, spun it, watched it die. Ran a thumbnail down the dead-stock ledgers' spines — along them, not into them — while Mies stood holding her elbows like any woman watching mud walked into her floors. Opened the third drawer; picked up the numbering box; turned it; put it back. In the kitchen, upstairs, at the last, he stood by the window a moment in the winter light, and his glance went over the walls the way glances do, and stopped, and Mies felt the whole winter come to a point no bigger than a nailhead — the seam. The lifted seam, the hand's width of paper standing away from the damp plaster, curling like a page. He looked at it. A house going to seed around a widow; damp getting in; wallpaper lifting; the whole shabby grammar of the ration years, written up one wall. He looked at it the way you look at the hundredth sad wall of your Sunday shift.

"You heat with what, mevrouw?" he said, and under the words she heard the true question — *why is this house still standing and its neighbours' furniture smoke?* — and she answered the true question, flatly, holding her elbows: "My husband's shop, meneer. Shelf by shelf. The dead don't feel the draught."

The Dutchman in grey looked at her for a moment with something that had been, at some earlier stage of its career, shame; and wrote nothing; and touched his hat — that reflex, absurd, surviving in him like an appendix — and took his green men down the stairs and on to the odd numbers over the bridge.

She fed the children. She sat through the afternoon. And at dusk, when her hands had agreed to be hers again, she went and stood at the seam and put two fingers on it, over the flat hidden crackle, and stood a long time, until Fien, from the table, without looking up from her sums, said what both of them had spent the whole day not saying:

"He looked *straight at it*."

"Yes."

"And it just — waited." The pencil, moving again, steady. "It's good at it now. It's had the practice."

# Chapter Nine — White Bread

The bread came at the beginning of March, and it came, of all the winter's mercies, from the sea.

The rumour ran ahead of it for weeks, the way rumours of food did, half-believed, hoarded like the food itself would be: ships, people said in the queues. Swedish ships, come through the war to Delfzijl with flour and margarine, a gift of the Swedish Red Cross, and the flour milled and the loaves to be baked here, in the city's own cold ovens, and given out — *given*, that was the word the rumour could not stop turning over — one loaf to every soul on the register, free, off ration, off the books of hunger entirely. The city, which had learned to believe in nothing on principle, did not believe in it right up until the Wednesday morning it stood in its thousands outside the bakers' shops and watched the shutters go up on shelves stacked with it: white bread. Real white bread, whiter than peace, whiter than anything the children in the queue had ever seen; there were children of six that morning who did not know what it was.

Mies queued the full four hours with both children, because the loaves went by heads and every head on her card was coming with her, and she watched Amsterdam take its gift. It was not joyful, quite. It was too serious for that. People took the loaf into their hands like a christening, and stepped out of the queue, and a great many of them, men as well, old dockers as well, wept — standing quite still, in the March wind, holding bread — and nobody looked away from anybody, that morning, there being nothing to hide that everyone was not doing too. Dirk carried the household's third loaf home himself, both arms, chin on the crust, walking with the extreme ceremonial care of a boy bearing the regalia of a kingdom; and at the table he ate his first slice so slowly, in such a silence, that Mies had to go and be busy at the stove, because six years old is too young to have learned that of the two available kinds of unbearable, plenty is the sharper one.

"It tastes like the inside of the seam," Dirk said at last, incomprehensibly, and Fien, who alone held the concordance of her brother's tongue, glossed it without looking up: "He means like something kept for after. He means it tastes like the orange."

That was the week hope changed sides. Nothing else changed — the ration after the Swedish loaf sank straight back toward its floor, the guns still stood along the rivers, the razzias went on, March was hungrier than February by the numbers, and the numbers were on the kitchen wall of every house in the west — but the bread had been a fact, and it was a new kind of fact: the outside world had reached in through the wall of the war and touched the city with a white finger, once, deliberately, as if to say: *hold on; you are seen; it is a matter of shipping now, and shipping is a matter of weeks.* The city put the fact under its scarf with its other knowledge and shuffled forward. Holding on, from that week, had a direction to it.

Vos never saw the white bread.

The knock that came on the second Thursday of March was the wrong knock, and it was a boy of maybe sixteen with a scholar's stoop to him and the parcel done up wrong — done up lovingly, in fact, which was itself the error, no butcher on earth having wrapped anything lovingly since 'forty-three — and he stood on the shop mat where Vos had stood and delivered the message in the manner of the network's boys, eyes at her shoulder, voice at dictation speed, nothing written anywhere in the world:

"The friends greet the shop. The gentleman who called on Thursdays was taken on the twenty-third of January, in the south of the city, on other business, not" — the boy's voice laying the next words down with great care, a message inside the message — "*not on shop business. Nothing that walked to this door ever sat in his pocket.* The friends have confirmed he crossed no one's path who matters here." A breath, the dictation resuming. "He is believed to have been sent east with the February transport from the Weteringschans. Further than that, the friends do not know, and the friends have learned not to invent what they do not know. He asked, before the transport — this came out with a released man — that the shops on his list be told the following, in these words: *the ledger balanced, and the winter is the enemy's last.*" The boy blinked, done, and became sixteen again. "There's paper in the parcel. The friends ask if the arrangement continues."

The twenty-third of January. She stood in the doorway doing the terrible reckoning even as her face went on being a doorway face: the January parcels, the February parcels, heavier, on time, the knock one-soft-two-quick in the starlight — six weeks of paper delivered by a man already six weeks taken. The network had closed over its wound and pumped on, and had not told her, and she understood without being told why it had not told her: because she would have looked, at some counter, in some glove, with some fraction of a grief the street could not afford. The invisible commonwealth kept its dead as it kept everything else, off the books and on the list; the work was the mourning. There had been a man whose hat came off before the door was fully open, who ate boiled tulip like a guest of honour, who carried lists of the hidden in his head because paper betrays and memory only breaks; and the sum of what the daylight world would ever hold of him was a false name, a hat's courtesy, and a sentence about a ledger, relayed east to west by a released man like a coin passed cell to cell.

"Tell the friends the arrangement continues," Mies said. "Tell them the shop said: as long as God and the mill allow."

Then she went in and shut the door and stood in the cold shop among the cases, and took down the household book, and opened it to the back page, between the turf account and the christenings, and wrote, under *A. Aalders*, in her own steady compositor's hand, the third entry of the list of the owed:

*V., taken 23 Jan, sent east. Owed the orange. The ledger balanced.*

The March run took her four days, as promised. The new period's changes were petty and she had his hand now as well as her own; the press talked its damped double-knock into the felt, the grey-green scraps came off the platen mean and dull and tired and perfect, and upstairs above her head slept the two reasons, and behind the wallpaper waited the third; and on the twenty-first of March, the first day of a spring no one could yet see but the light itself, Mies Verhoeven marked one year a widow at the type case with the composing stick in her left hand, setting bread for the hidden of the city in her dead husband's stolen typeface, which was, she had come at last to understand, the only wreath either of them would have wanted laid.

*Not yet,* to the seam, going up at two.

But for the first time, that night, it came out sounding like arithmetic. Like something with a remainder growing small.
