# Decisions — The Paper Orange

Run under ORDER 008 (owner night run 2026-07-13, BOOKS clause). First complete manuscript for the concept vetted in `docs/publishing/vetting/the-paper-orange.md` (concept-stage packet, PR #105 wave; packet verdict: "PARKS at 'no manuscript'"); this is the write-slice that packet parks on.

- **Premise fidelity** — Delivers the vetted packet exactly: Hunger Winter, Amsterdam 1944–45; Mies Verhoeven, a printer's widow with two children and half a sack of tulip bulbs, forges ration coupons on her late husband's treadle press to feed people in hiding; the paper orange (the forbidden royal colour, folded by her husband on the Queen's birthday 1940) waits behind the lifted wallpaper seam "for the day the lights come back" and carries the emotional spine to the liberation window scene. Register is the packet's: literary, close interiority, restrained — "forgery as mercy, and what people feed each other when there is nothing left." House of Orange resonance handled by object and colour, never by flag-waving; no royal cameo, no invented royal dialogue.
- **Scope/length** — One complete, standalone literary-historical novella, English. Honest count: `wc -w` = **15811** on `en/the-paper-orange.md` (includes front matter and chapter headings), inside the coordinator brief's 13,000–17,000 target. Note: the packet's §3 planned length was ~25k–35k; this run's brief set 13k–17k and the shorter target won — flagged honestly rather than padded.
- **POV/tense** — Close third person on Mies, past tense, single POV throughout. The dead husband (Hendrik) is present only through memory, habit, and the objects he left; the network man (Vos) is seen strictly from outside, which is period-true (compartmentalisation) and keeps his offpage fate honest.
- **Structure** — 12 chapters, November 1944 → May 1945: I (ch. 1–3) widowhood, the note through the shutter, the recruitment, the discovered drawer (her husband forged before her and hid it from her), first successful forgery; II (ch. 4–7) the working winter — deliveries, the fuel crisis and the refused staircase, the hunger trek and the Landwacht checkpoint, February's deaths (Aalders) and the boy fading; III (ch. 8–12) the house search near-miss at the wallpaper seam, Swedish white bread and Vos's fate (taken, sent east, never resolved — an honest period null), Manna food drops, capitulation/Dam shooting/Canadians, the orange into the window, and the coda of printed orange sheets folded by the street's children. No placeholders; every line finished prose.
- **Historical accuracy — verified spine** (checkable against the standard Hunger Winter record): September 1944 railway strike and the German food embargo on the occupied west; the country cut in two at the great rivers; rations collapsing to a few hundred calories with ~400 g of bread a week by April 1945; official distribution of sugar beets and tulip bulbs, with pamphlets advising removal of the bitter core; gas and electricity cut off during the winter; noodkacheltjes, carbide lamps, felled canal trees, pried-up wooden tram blocks; the stripping for firewood of houses emptied by the deportation of Amsterdam's Jews; hongertochten (hunger treks) into the farm country north of the IJ and confiscations by the Dutch collaborationist Landwacht at city approaches; central kitchens; ~18,000–20,000 famine deaths in the west, coffin shortage, churches (the Zuiderkerk among them) used as temporary morgues; onderduikers cut off from rations because coupons required registration, fed by aid networks using stolen and forged bonkaarten; the persoonsbewijs (Lentz design) as near-unforgeable — the heroine accordingly refuses PB work, which is true to the historical forgers' record; radio confiscation of May 1943 and clandestine sets; Radio Oranje; Weteringschans transports east; Swedish Red Cross flour and the free white-bread distribution of late February/early March 1945; Operations Manna/Chowhound, 29 April–8 May 1945, truce-protected low-level drops without parachutes and the several-day distribution lag; capitulation announced the evening of 4 May, effective 8:00 on 5 May; the 7 May Dam square shooting by German naval personnel into the celebrating crowd (no order ever established — the text says exactly that and keeps the family away from the square); Canadian entry into Amsterdam on 8 May 1945; the ban on orange and on the Queen's birthday (31 August), and the small orange defiances of 1940. The typeface named (Hollandsche Mediaeval, S.H. de Roos) is real; De Roos appears as a name on a fount, not as a character. Church-bell losses to wartime requisition are acknowledged in-text with a hedge ("what bells the war had left").
- **Historical accuracy — marked inventions** — All named characters are fictional (Mies/Hendrik/Fien/Dirk Verhoeven, Vos, Mevrouw Aalders, the De Wits, Blom, Trijn, Sal Winkler); no real person appears in a speaking role, and no dialogue is attributed to any real person. The "friends of Sint Nicolaas" network name is invented (modelled on LO-style aid networks, which really did feed onderduikers with illicit coupons). The loyal paper mill supplying genuine blank coupon stock is a plausible invention consistent with, but not documented as, a specific case. Coupon appearance details (grey-green ink, the layout, the wartime numerals, serial ranges, "BROOD 125 GRAM") are period-flavoured invention, deliberately NOT a facsimile of any real bonkaart. The specific shop on the Egelantiersgracht, the eleven folding steps, and the wallpaper cache are fiction. Exact ration figures spoken by characters are kept directional where the record varies week to week.
- **Sensitive-history care** — Dignity throughout: famine deaths restrained and largely offpage; the deportations are present as an absence the living must answer for (the refused staircase in Chapter Five — she burns her own shop's furniture rather than a deported bookbinder's stairs) rather than as backdrop texture; no atrocity rendered for effect; the Dam shooting is heard at distance, not staged. The content note in the front matter declares all of this to the reader.
- **Keyword discipline** — Register held to the packet's reserved phrases (Hunger Winter / WWII Netherlands / occupation 1944 / Liberation of Holland / wartime widow). The watched adjacency from the packet §6 (`Amsterdam occupation 1944 story` vs Weigh House's `Amsterdam crime novel`) stays disjoint: nothing in this text drifts into modern-Amsterdam-crime phrasing; the setting was deliberately placed in the Jordaan, away from the Weigh House book's Nieuwmarkt ground.

- **Cover** — Cover-only production gate per packet §5 (a single orange folded from newspaper against winter-grey wallpaper; quiet lit-fic register, no war-kitsch; 1600×2560 px min); any art spend is an owner decision.

Needs its own vetting row (collision scan + keyword rows are language-specific) before any write-slice.
