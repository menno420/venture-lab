# THE GLASS RECTORY

*A Ghost Story*

> When Honor Vane comes to keep house for her brother, the new curate of a moorland parish, she is told two things about the rectory: do not mind the cold, and do not go into the glass wing after dark. The conservatory the last rector built is warm all winter, green all year — and empty since the night he walked into it and never came out. A ghost story in the classic register, about the things we grow in place of grief.

---

**Content note:** Adult gothic fiction. Contains bereavement, a long-past death from illness, and a disappearance; all deaths occur off the page and in the past. No violence, no gore, no explicit content. A quiet, cold read for adult readers.

---

# Chapter One — The Two Instructions

It is a long time now since I kept house for my brother, and longer still since anybody has asked me to tell what happened at Wythershaw in the winter of 1893; but the year has come round again to the dark end, and the fires are lit, and I find that the story asks itself. I set it down plainly, because it was a plain thing, in its way. Nothing in it shrieks. Nothing in it bleeds. It is only that once, for a season, I lived in a house where grief had been given a room of its own, and glass walls, and a gardener; and I learned what such a room costs, and who pays.

My brother Edward was twenty-six that autumn, and newly priested, and the Bishop of Aldergate had done him the doubtful kindness of a moorland parish that two incumbents had already left inside of ten years. Edward called it a beginning. Our aunt, who read the church papers the way other women read the assizes, called it Wythershaw, and said the name as if it closed the subject. I was twenty-nine, three years past the death of the man I was to have married, and I had discovered in myself by then a talent that unmarried women of my kind were expected to have, and which I exercised, I confess, the way some men drink: I kept house. I had kept my aunt's house, and my cousin's through her confinement, and the house of a school in Harrogate through a scarlatina winter. I was very good at it. If I did not sleep much, the linen never knew.

So when Edward wrote — *the living is vacant, the Bishop sends me as curate-in-charge, the rectory is large and I am told peculiar, will you come* — I had my boxes corded within the week.

We went up from Aldergate by carrier's cart on the eighth of October, the last four miles at a walking pace with the road climbing all the way. I remember that the moor came at us by degrees, the walls giving out first, then the trees, until there was nothing on either hand but heather going over from bloom to rust, and a wind with no gossip in it, that had touched nothing since the sea. The village, when it came, was a grey lane of houses pitched down a fold of the hill as if for shelter, a church tower above them with a fog of rooks about it, and beyond the church, alone where the moor began again, the rectory.

I saw the glass before I saw the house. That is the first thing to set down honestly, because everything afterwards is in it. The light was failing, and the house itself was a black shape with its chimneys against the last of the sky — a long stone rectory of the plain old sort, rain-darkened, shouldered up against the weather. And at its southern end, where in such a house you would look for a dull wall and a laurel hedge, there stood instead a great wing of glass, high as the eaves, long as the house itself was deep — and it was lit. Not lamplit; there was no flame in it. It held the western light the way a pond holds it after the fields have gone dark, a standing shape of pale gold and green, and inside the glass, black against that shining, I could see leaves. Great fronds and fans and towers of leaf, crowded as a jungle, motionless in the wind that was making the cart-horse lean.

"Somebody's grand notion," said the carrier, not looking at it.

"Is it the rector's?" I asked, and he said, "It were," in the tone the country keeps for closed subjects, and clicked at the horse.

Mrs. Slee was waiting for us in the porch with a lamp. I will give her to you at once, because without her we should neither of us have lasted the month: a widow of the village, sixty or thereabouts, flat-backed, wide-handed, with the local manner that strangers take for coldness and that is in fact a kind of mercy, since it promises you nothing it will not perform. She had aired what could be aired and lit what could be lit. She showed us the kitchen, the pump, the lamp-oil, my brother's study with the parish chest, the bedrooms — hers was not among them; she would come days, she said, and go home nights, and something in the way she said it had the smoothness of a stone often handled.

It was on the stairs, with the candle in her hand, that she gave me the two instructions. She gave them without drama, as she might have told me which chimney smoked.

"You'll not mind the cold, miss," she said. "The house runs cold, do what you will with it. It's not the sashes nor the doors, and it's no fault of the coal. You'll take it as you find it and not wear yourself out against it. That's the first thing."

"And the second?"

She was three steps above me, and the candlelight came down over her face so that I could not properly see it.

"You'll not go into the glass wing after dark," she said. "Days, if you must; Gathercole will let you in when he does the stoking, and there's no harm in a garden in the daylight, though I don't go in myself and never shall. But not after dark. Not for a dropped thimble. Not if you hear —" and there she stopped herself, with the neatness of a woman shutting a drawer, and finished, "Not for anything, miss. That's all it is."

"Not if I hear what, Mrs. Slee?"

"Wind in an ill-made building," she said, "is all you would hear," and went on up with the candle, and left me to follow or stand in the dark.

Edward, when I told him over supper, was delighted, in the way of a young man whose education has cost a great deal and must be used. A cold house was ventilation; a forbidden conservatory was superstition; the village had made a bogey of a dead man's hobby, and it would be our work — he said *our* very kindly — to be cheerful and regular and unafraid, until the parish learned that the rectory was only a house. He said grace over the cold mutton with real gladness. He was, I should say here, the best man I have ever known, and nothing in this account is meant otherwise; a lantern is not to blame that there are places its light puts deeper into shadow.

I went up at ten. My room faced south. I mention it as flatly as Mrs. Slee might: the moon had risen over the moor, and below my window the glass wing lay long and pale under it, like a church built of ice, or a hothouse for growing the moonlight; and the panes of it were bright and dry and black with leaves; and though the night had turned sharp enough to sting the hand I rested on my own windowsill, and though my own window was fogged grey with the warmth of my candle and my breath — every pane of that great cold-lit roof below me was clear. Clear as still water. Clear as if nothing on either side of it had any breath at all.

I drew my curtain, and minded the cold, and slept.

# Chapter Two — The Green Wing

Gathercole did the stoking at eight and at four. I met him the first morning, coming round the frost-whitened gravel with my shawl about my head, and found the southern end of the house even stranger by daylight than it had been under the moon.

The wing was built out from the gable in the grand manner of thirty years before: a spine of white-painted iron and a ridge of ornamental cresting, curved lights at the corners, and pane upon pane upon pane, hundreds of them, set in glazing bars fine as the veins of a leaf. Against the grey of the house and the browns of October it did not look wicked. I want that understood. It looked expensive, and mistaken, the way a ball-gown would look mistaken hung on a scarecrow; and it looked warm. Its stone footing ran down to a sunken furnace-house at the far end, with a low door and an iron chimney, and out of that door, stooping, backing, drawing a rake after him, came a man built like a thorn tree, all angle and weather, with white hair under a black-brimmed hat. He must have been past seventy. He looked at me the way the carrier had looked at the glass.

"You'll be the sister," he said.

"Honor Vane," I said. "You keep the furnace, Mr. Gathercole?"

"I keep the grave-yard," he said, "and the church stoves, and yon." He nodded at the wing without turning his eyes to it. "Four o' the clock and eight o' the clock, every day the Lord sends, twelve year now. There's money left for it. You'd best see inside, I reckon, and get it over."

That is exactly what he said: *get it over*. He took me up the path to the wing's own door — a proper door of oak and glass, set in the end elevation, with a bright-worn brass handle — and he opened it, and stood aside with his hat off, as I have seen men stand aside at the door of a church they no longer worship in. He did not go in. I went in.

I had been in glasshouses before. My cousin's husband forced peaches; the school in Harrogate had a fernery, six feet by ten, that dripped. I had even once, as a girl, been taken through the Palm House at Kew, and carried away from it chiefly a memory of my own collar wilting. So I knew, or thought I knew, what a warm house of plants would be — the wet heat, the green smell, the breathing quiet — and some of that was there, and if it had all been there I should have had nothing to write.

It was warm. It was June-warm, gentle-warm, the warmth of a walled garden at four o'clock on a good day; and the air was sweet — not heavy, but sweet, camellia and clean earth and something underneath like oranges, for there were orange trees, four of them, in great slatted tubs down the centre walk. There were camellias in bud along the whole southern side, budded thick as a hawthorn, in October. There was a fig trained black and ancient against the house wall, a stand of tree-ferns unrolling new fronds fresh as green wax, heliotrope in a raised bed breathing out its cherry-pie sweetness, and down at the far end, where the walk widened to a little paved circle with a bench, a plumbago in full flower, that blue that has no other name. Everything flourished. Everything was clean. Not a fallen leaf on the flags, not a spotted frond, not a snail-track; the water in the slate tank in the corner stood full and bright, though I had seen no pipe and no pump.

And it was silent. A glasshouse in the sun ticks and drips and stirs; the lights creak in their bars; a bee blunders. Here the sun stood in through the south panes in long clean lozenges, and the leaves hung in it without one tremor, and my own boot-heels on the flags were loud as a stranger's in an empty church. I remember I stood in the middle of the walk and heard my own heart, and was ashamed of hearing it, in a garden, at nine o'clock on a bright morning.

I said, because saying things aloud is what we do in empty rooms that do not feel empty, "Well. You are very fine." And the leaves stood, and the light stood, and the plumbago at the far end was so blue against the moor beyond the glass that it hurt, and nothing answered, and I have never in my life felt more distinctly that something had heard.

Gathercole was still at the door, hat in hand, when I came out — quicker, I think, than I had meant to come.

"Who does the gardening?" I asked him. "You lay the fires, you said. But someone prunes; someone waters; someone sweeps."

"There's money left for the stoking," he said. "There's no money left for aught else, because there's no need of aught else. I've not watered in yon house in twelve year, miss. It doesn't dry. Nothing drops and nothing spoils and nothing dies, and if you can bide the thought of that, you're welcome to sit in there of a morning, warm as a cat, like the last rector's lady used to do." He set his hat back on. "And if you can't bide it, you're in your right wits."

I asked him the question I had been circling all night. I asked who had built it, and he told me the shell of the story standing in the graveyard he kept, wind at us over the wall, and the glass throwing us back the whole width of the morning sky: that the Reverend Doctor Ambrose Snow, rector of Wythershaw for one-and-thirty years, had built the wing for his wife's sake in the year 1870, she being then dead; that he had tended it himself, nine years, ten, no man else so much as carrying a can into it; and that on a January night in 1881 he had risen from his bed and gone down into his garden in the dark, in his nightshirt, in the frost, and had never come out of it, nor been found in it, nor been found anywhere in this world since.

"The door was fast on the inside," Gathercole said. "Snow on the ground that night, and never a mark in it, all round the wing, and I'll take my oath on that before any bench in England, for I was the one they had walk it. Church court sat on it after. Misadventure, they wrote. And his boots" — he looked at me for the first time since he had opened the door — "his boots were on the mat inside, miss, set together tidy, the way a man sets them when he means to walk on grass."

I looked back down the length of the wing. The sun had come round a point further; the lozenges of light had shifted on the flags; and it came to me, with the particular coldness that attends small facts, that from where I stood every pane of the southern face held the picture of the moor and the racing October sky, bright and dry and perfect, and that my breath, and Gathercole's, went up between us in two plumes of grey — and that in all that acreage of cold morning glass there was not one square inch of mist.

"Why does it never fog?" I said. "Warm within, cold without — it should stream, Mr. Gathercole. Any window in the village —"

"Any window in the village has folk breathing behind it," said Gathercole, and shot the brass bolt of the oak door, and that was the end of the first lesson.

# Chapter Three — Doctor Snow

A house tells you its dead slowly, and a village faster, and a parish chest fastest of all, if you can read its hand. Between the three of them, by All Souls' I had the story of Ambrose Snow as whole as anyone living was likely to have it; and because everything that came after stands on it, I will set it out here in order, though it came to me in rags — a sentence from Mrs. Slee over the bread-trough, a page of the register under Edward's lamp, a word let fall at a cottage door and regretted before it finished falling.

He came to Wythershaw in 1850, a scholar's son, a Doctor of Divinity at an age when most men are still copying sermons, and by every account a cold preacher and a warm man; the parish, which will forgive anything sooner than the reverse arrangement, loved him. In 1859, being past forty, he went down to Aldergate and came back married, to a woman twenty years his junior — Eugenie, her name was, a fair, light, laughing person by all telling, a doctor's daughter from the south, who filled the rectory with music no one up here could name, and who was, from her first winter on the moor, dying. Not quickly. The consumption in her family took its time, Mrs. Slee said; it had taken her mother twenty years. It played with her: good summers, bad Januaries, a bloom on her some seasons that deceived everyone but her husband. The doctors said what the doctors of that day said — warmth, soft air, the south. Madeira, one of them wrote, or Mentone; and there is a letter of Snow's in the parish chest, a draft or a copy he kept, I do not know which, to a college friend, asking — the hand quite steady — whether a man might decently resign a cure of souls to lengthen one life, and if he might, on what they should live, for he had nothing but the living, and she had nothing at all.

He did not resign. I have thought about that as much as about anything in this history. He stayed, and she wintered on the moor, ten winters, wrapped and carried and cheerful — everyone insists to me she was cheerful — planning aloud, Mrs. Slee remembered, the garden they would have when they went south at last: orange trees, she would say, and camellias, and heliotrope, and a blue plumbago over a white bench, *and no wind, Ambrose; a garden where the air stands still*. She died in the January of 1869, in the tenth winter, in the north-west bedroom of this house — my room; I had guessed it, somehow, before Mrs. Slee's silence told me — with the moor wind, they say, so loud on the gable that night that the watchers could not hear her breathing stop.

The wing went up the year after. He sold his library to a college — that is on record — and his mother's silver, and it is thought he spent besides the whole of what had come to him at his marriage; the ironwork came from Glasgow and the glazers from York, and the village stood at the churchyard wall the week the glass went in and did not know what to make of it, a palace of light rising off the gable-end of that grey house, eighteen months after they had carried its only brightness down the path below it. He was not mad; I must not let you think the village thought him mad. He did his duties to the end, christened and married and buried, sat on the school board, walked his bounds. Only every daylight hour that was left over went in under the glass, and no other soul with it; and by the second year the parish had seen, through those clear walls, what he was growing there, and understood it without a word said, in the way a village understands: orange trees. Camellias. Heliotrope in a raised bed. A white bench at the south end, and over it, trained along wires he strained himself, a plumbago, for the blue.

He grew her garden. The garden of the south they had never gone to, pane by pane and root by root, on the exact spot of England she had found hardest to bear; and he kept it at the warmth of the coast she never saw; and he walked in it every evening of the last decade of his life, up and down, up and down, between the standing leaves. Waiting — the village finished the sentence variously, according to the teller's faith. Mrs. Slee would not finish it at all. "He kept it perfect," was all she would say, kneading. "Whatever else, miss, it was perfect. There was never a dead leaf in it while he lived, and God knows there's been none since."

Of the night itself — the twenty-second of January, 1881, twelve years to the night, I did not fail to notice, after his wife's dying — I have told what Gathercole told me, and the church court's finding, and there is nothing to add to it but one small thing from the register, which I found myself, and wish I had not. The register of burials for 1869 is in Snow's own hand, of course; he buried his own wife, there being no other clergy nearer than Aldergate and the roads being what they were. The entry is perfectly formal, perfectly regular. Eugenie Snow, aged 39 years. But low in the margin, in the same ink, very small, where no rubric asks for anything, he wrote four words. *The glass never fogged.*

I understood it, sitting in Edward's study with the register open under the lamp, quicker than I can make you understand it, and I sat for some time before I called Edward to see. In the last nights, you comprehend, they will have kept her window a little open even in January — it was thought healthful — and a screen about it, and the room breathing its warmth out at the gap; and a man who cannot look any longer at the bed will look at the window; and the pane above a dying person's bed clouds and clears, clouds and clears, with a rhythm you cannot help but watch, until it is the only clock in the room. And at some hour of that night the mist on the glass had smoothed away and not returned, and that is how he knew — before the watchers, before the doctor, in the perfect silence of that room — that the breathing had stopped. The window told him. *The glass never fogged.* He had written down, in the driest hand in the world, in the margin of a public book, the exact shape in which his grief had come to him; and then he had gone out and built that shape, eighty feet long, in iron and glass, and filled it with the south, and kept it seven-and-twenty degrees above the frost for the rest of his life.

Edward, when I showed him, was quiet for a long while, and then said, gently enough, that it was very sad, and very morbid, and that the sooner the diocese let him take the wing down the better; the heating of it was a scandal in a parish with cold cottages — there was a bequest, but a bequest could be diverted — and the thing itself was a monument to a man's refusal of the will of God. That is doctrine, and I cannot fault it. But I had kept more sickrooms than Edward, and I said nothing; because I had been that year three winters in the practice of a smaller art of the same kind — three winters of keeping other people's linen presses in an order Stephen would never see, of being so good at houses, so warm, so lit, so ceaselessly, cheerfully busy that nothing in me was ever left alone in a quiet room with what had happened; and I knew the rector's garden from within, as a native knows a country. I did not say this to Edward. I have never said it to anyone until now.

The first tapping came eight days later, on the fourth of November, a little after ten o'clock at night; but you must have one more thing first, because I promised to set this down in order, and in order it stands: on the last day of October, dusting where Mrs. Slee's writ did not run, I found on the sill of the landing window, which looks south down the roof-ridge of the glass wing, a small brass thimble, a woman's, very old, green with sitting; and when I mentioned it at the bread-trough, Mrs. Slee went still for exactly one breath, and then said, in her flattest voice, that there had never been a child in this house in her time, and that I was to leave it where it lay.

I left it where it lay.

# Chapter Four — The First Tapping

The fourth of November was a Saturday, and wild. The wind had been getting up all day out of the north-west, and by supper it was at the house like a creditor, trying every door and going away and coming back. Edward was at his sermon. I was at the mending, in the small sitting-room that shared its chimney with the kitchen and could therefore be brought, with management, to something near comfort; and at a little after ten, in a lull between two gusts — in the lull, this matters, in the flat silence while the wind drew breath — I heard, from away through the house, southward, very small and clear:

*Tap. Tap. Tap.*

Three, and done. The gust came back and buried everything. I sat with Edward's shirt in my lap and told myself the truth as briskly as I could: that in a gale a house is an orchestra of nonsense; that iron cools and cracks its knuckles; that the wing stood full of eighty feet of heated air in a freezing night, and must tick along every bar of it. All true. And I sat on, not sewing, through gust and lull, gust and lull; and in the fourth lull it came again, three taps, deliberate, spaced like a man rapping a door with one knuckle when the house is asleep and he does not wish to wake it — a small sound meaning to be small — and it came, I could no longer pretend otherwise, from glass. Nothing else in the world sounds like glass. It rings under the finger-nail, one grain of a ring, the ghost of a bell in every pane.

I did not go south along the passage. I record that without shame. I banked the fire, and went up to bed, and lay listening to the gale until it was only weather, and slept.

But in the morning, which was bright and still and white with the first hard frost, I went out before Gathercole came, and walked the whole southern face of the wing on the crisped grass, and made myself look, pane by pane. I knew what I should find, because I had settled it overnight: the old fig. Its trained boughs ran within a foot of the end elevation; a straying shoot, wind-worked, would peck at the glass all night. That was my branch, and I meant to have it.

There was no branch. The fig lay flat and close along its wires as a ship's rigging, a foot of clear air between its outmost twig and the panes, and nothing else grew within three yards of the glass anywhere, and there was no ivy and no rose and no wire loose, and the frost on the grass where I walked was printed with nothing but my own boots and the stitching of the birds. I stood at the end elevation with my breath going up in plumes, before the tall clear panes beside the oak door, and the garden stood inside in its June-green, drinking the winter light, the plumbago blue as noon at the far end; and the pane in front of me held the moor and the sky and my own shape, dry and bright and perfect; and I was seized — I have wondered since how much of everything after hangs from this one moment of temper — I was seized by a plain, furious, housekeeping refusal to be managed, by a building; and I pulled my glove off and rapped the glass twice with my knuckle. *Tap. Tap.* "There," I said aloud. "Now we have both done it."

The pane rang its grain of a ring. The garden stood. Nothing moved but my own breath, and I went in to my work, feeling a fool by the time I reached the porch, and said nothing to anyone.

That night the wind had gone and the frost held the house in a great stillness, the kind of night when the beams tick and the moor is so quiet you can hear the beck. I heard the church clock strike ten across the graveyard. And as the tenth stroke faded, from away south through the sleeping passages, small and clear and unhurried, came the answer I had asked for.

*Tap. Tap.*

Two. Two only; my own two, returned; and the interval between them was my interval, as a voice you know is a voice you know, and I sat up in the dark with my heart going like a sparrow's, and heard the stillness close over it, and heard nothing more, that night, but my own listening. Which does not sleep.

# Chapter Five — The Cold House

I have kept houses cold and houses warm, and I know the price of both to the last hundredweight, so you may take it from me as a professional matter: the cold of Wythershaw rectory was not honest cold. We burned, that November, half as much coal again as the house I had kept in Harrogate, which was bigger, and older, and held a school. It made no difference. Fires that should have roasted a parlour made a red glow and a ring of warmth a cat could have circled, and beyond that ring the air stood chill and sweet as a dairy. The kitchen with its range going all day was warm the way a candle is warm in a shed. And all the while, eighty feet of glass at the gable-end stood green and gentle as a Madeira noon, on two rakings a day of Gathercole's coke.

I said something of the sort to him — it was economics I meant, nothing stranger — one four-o'clock as he racked his tools, and he heard me out and then said, in his flattest way: "You've been cold in your time in good houses, I reckon, miss, wi' the fire banked high." I said that everyone had. "Aye," he said. "And where's the warmth gone, those nights? Up the flue, you'll say. Maybe. And maybe houses are like folk, and there's them in a house that can eat all it has and leave the rest hungry, and never mean harm by it, no more than the babby means harm at the breast." He swung his bag up. "Doctor Snow's coke were the same money as ours, miss. His study fire wouldn't cast to the hearth-rug, last years. He wore his greatcoat to write his sermons in, and yon fig put on four foot a summer. I've been stoking longer than you've been cold. That's all I'll say."

It was Mrs. Slee's first instruction over again — *the house runs cold, it's no fault of the coal* — and I took it the way I was learning to take everything at Wythershaw, which was to say into a drawer of my mind I did not often open, because the drawer beneath it held the tapping, and the drawers must not be allowed to touch. Edward meanwhile had opened a correspondence with Messrs. Pursey & Hart of Aldergate touching the Snow bequest, and was learning what a careful man the Doctor had been. The annuity for the stoking was tied to a trust, and the trust to a condition, and the condition was one line long: *so long as the glass wing at the rectory of Wythershaw be kept warm and whole.* Warm, and whole. Not tended; it needed no tending. Not entered; he made no provision for anyone to enter. The money died the day the wing went cold or the glass was broken, and reverted to a missionary society, and the diocese, Edward was given to understand, saw no cause to hurry a delicate matter. My brother preached that Advent on the sin of making monuments of our sorrows, to a full church, every soul of which had walked past the lit glass in the dark to hear him; and not one of them, coming out into the churchyard after, so much as turned a head toward it. You could measure the parish's knowledge by that not-turning. It was exact, like everything at Wythershaw.

And I — I will deal honestly, this account being good for nothing otherwise. That November I began going into the wing. In the mornings, in the daylight, within the law; Gathercole unbolted at eight, and I found that my mending, and the parish sewing, and Edward's accounts, and my letters, all of them somehow went south by ten o'clock, to the white bench under the leafless-blue plumbago, in air like the third week of June. Do not be quick with me. You have not kept a cold house on a moor with all your dead about you. It was warmth without asking, company without speech; the stillness that had frightened me on the first morning I now sat inside of, the way you sit inside a church when the service is long over; and if the glass never fogged and the leaves never fell, why, neither did anything else in that garden make any of the demands the living make. It asked me only to be there. I did my work well and my sleep improved and my temper, Edward said, was sweeter than the butter; and some mornings I looked up from the sewing at the long walk and the standing leaves and could not entirely remember having wound the two hours since I sat down.

It was on one of those mornings, near the end of the month, that I caught the thing I should have caught in October, and my drawers, as it were, all came open at once. Gathercole had come through at his stoking, hat off as always under the glass, and paused by the bench as always to see if I wanted aught, and I, threading a needle, said idly — *idly*, after four weeks of that bench — "You told me once the last rector's lady used to sit in here of a morning, Mr. Gathercole. She never did, though. She died the year before ever the glass went up."

The needle took its thread. The garden stood green. Gathercole looked down the long walk, and his face was the face of a man doing sums he has done before and getting the answer he got before, and at last he said:

"Aye, miss. I did say that."

And put his hat on, which he had never once done under that roof, and walked out, and did not come to the four-o'clock stoking that day, but sent his grandson.

# Chapter Six — The Garden-Book

I found the garden-book on the second of December, and I say *found*, though the truer word is the one Mrs. Slee used of the thimble and I have been avoiding since October: it was left where it lay. It lay in the potting lobby — the little slated ante-room between the oak door and the garden proper, where the tools of a gardener who never came hung in their oiled ranks — in the drawer of the potting bench, which I had opened in November and found full of sieves; and on the second of December, hunting a ball of tarred twine for Mrs. Slee's parcels, I opened it again and the sieves were there, and lying on them, square and quiet, a ledger-book in green cloth, much worn at the corners, with a brass thimble sitting on its cover like a paperweight; and I stood a long time looking at that arrangement, which had the exact quality of a table laid for one; and then I took up book and thimble both, and carried them to the white bench, and read until the light went and Gathercole's grandson came rattling at the furnace door and I remembered where I was.

It was the rector's, of course. Thirty years of it, in the little upright scholar's hand I knew from the registers, begun the spring the foundations went in, and for the first years it is nothing but a gardener's day-book, and a good one — heats and blights, Glasgow iron and York glass, the tale of coke burned against the tale of frosts kept out, the orange trees fetched as far as Hull by steamer and the last stage on a wool-wain, wrapped, he notes, *as carefully as anything is ever wrapped that goes up this road*. He grew what I had been told he grew, and the book gives the reason in one line, early, without decoration: *E. spoke of camellias, Jan. 12th 1866, and of the blue plumbago at Funchal that her mother had; this list is hers, and is not to be added to.* And it never was. Thirty years, and not one plant in that ledger she had not named.

You will want to know when it turns strange, and the honest answer is that it never does, which is the strangeness. The hand never wavers; the entries never rave; there is not a line in it he could not have read aloud to the church court. It is only that, somewhere about the third year, the grammar begins to keep her. *The heliotrope does better since it was moved; she always held it wanted morning sun.* Held, not had held. Then, in the sixth year, among the frosts: *Jan. 22nd. Twelve degrees of frost by dawn. Kept the house at her degrees through the worst of it, and sat up with it. The glass clear all night.* Her degrees — sixty-one by day, he sets down once, fifty-four by night, and I have wondered at the parlour thermometers of their courtship behind those numbers. And from the ninth year, this, which I copy exactly: *The house has ceased to fog these two winters, whatever weather. I do not now scrub the panes, and the panes do not now need it. A garden should breathe of itself; I have built better than I knew. She was always for the air standing still.*

He knew, you see. That is what I brought away from the green book, with the dark coming on and the leaves standing round me: he was not deceived, not for one hour of those nine years, about what he was doing under his own glass. There is an entry — I have it by heart — from the seventh spring: *A man may not keep what he was not given to keep. But a man may keep the garden of it. I am persuaded there is no sin in the garden of it, so it be kept perfect; the sin would be a dead leaf.* The sin would be a dead leaf. He wrote that, and dated it, and beneath it, the next day, in the same even hand: *Dressed the camellias. Wren in the house all morning; let it stay.*

I sat with the book shut in my lap while the moor went violet through the southern panes, and I thought of linen-presses in Harrogate, and of three years of my own even hand in other people's account-books, and of how there was no sin in any of it, so it be kept perfect; and I was as near to weeping as I had been since they folded Stephen's things into his sea-chest — near it, mind; I did not weep; I had not wept yet, you understand, not once in three years, and the garden stood about me in its great warm stillness like a hand laid on my shoulder saying *there is no need, here, ever* — and it was at that exact moment, out of the tail of my eye, northward up the long walk in the last of the light, that I saw the figure among the camellias.

I will set down only what was there. A tall stooped darkness, the height of a tall man stooping, at the far end of the walk between the camellia stand and the glass; motionless as everything in that garden was motionless; and where its face should have been, nothing I could name — but its arm was raised, crooked high, the hand flat to a pane above its head, in the attitude of a man who has been knocking at a door through a long night, and has stopped knocking, and leans, and waits, past hope, to hear the house wake.

The furnace door rattled at the south end; the grandson, with the coke. Between one beat of my heart and the next the walk stood empty, and the garden was a garden, green and dim and sweet; and high on the end elevation, above the camellias, in the first winter dark, one pane — one pane only, of all those hundreds that had not misted in twenty years — showed grey, fogged whole and even from edge to edge, as a pane fogs above a bed where someone is breathing still.

I took the book, and the thimble, and I walked out of the glass wing, not quickly, my heels loud on the flags the whole length of that walk with the dark standing in the leaves on either hand; and Mrs. Slee, when I came into the kitchen, looked once at my face and once at the green book under my arm, and put the kettle to the fire without a word being passed; and it was not until I was warm, and the lamp trimmed, and the bread cut, that I understood the sound I had been listening for behind me all down the walk, and had not heard. There had been no bolt shot that morning. The oak door had stood unlocked all day. Whatever waited at the north end had waited with the way open; and it had let me go by it, out of the dark of its house, as courteously as grief lets go the guest who is not yet ready; and I knew — this is the last thing I will set down of that day, and the first thing of all the rest — I knew that I had been asked for something, and that I would go back, after dark, before the winter was out, and answer.

# Chapter Seven — What the Village Saw

I did not go back to the white bench. Understand the shape of that December rightly: it was not courage, and it was not obedience to Mrs. Slee; it was the plain sense of a woman who has caught herself at the top of her glass of an evening and puts the decanter in a locked press. I knew now what the warmth was made of. So I sat my mornings out in the cold sitting-room with my back to the south passage, and did my work with a blanket over my knees like an invalid, and was short with Edward twice in one week, which he bore so mildly I could have shaken him; and every night, a little after the church clock struck ten, from away down the sleeping house, patient as water, the tapping came.

It was never loud. It never once, in all that winter, did anything so vulgar as to grow louder. Three taps, and a waiting; three, and a waiting; sometimes for a quarter-hour together, sometimes for an hour; a small sound meaning to be small, a sound with manners — and that was the horror of it, if I am to use the word at all, for you can bar a door against a battering, but there is no bar in the world against a courtesy that simply waits. Edward heard it too. I know he did, though we had somehow, without one word of arrangement, come to a treaty never to hear it in the same room at the same time: he took to reading prodigiously late that Advent, and I would see the line of lamplight under the study door as I went up, and hear him not-hearing, as he must, going up in the dark past the landing window, have heard me not-hearing, night upon night, all down that long month. Of the two of us I believe it went harder with him. I at least knew what I was not listening to. He had only his good sense, and a parish that watched him kindly and told him nothing, and a sermon to write every week on the mercy of God.

It was Mrs. Slee, at last, who gave me the rest of the story — the part Gathercole's oath before the bench had not covered; and characteristically she gave it me not at the fireside in the accepted manner of ghostly tales, but at the copper on washing-day, elbow-deep, with the plain speed of a woman clearing a debt she had decided to pay. I had asked her nothing. She began as if answering.

"You'll have wondered why it's *after dark* and not plain *keep out*," she said. "I'll tell you the once, and then I've told you. The night the old Doctor went, the whole village saw the wing lit. Two in the morning, black January, and it stood down there at the gable like a church window at evensong, miss — a light with no lamp in it, gold-like, the length of the garden, and every soul that saw it will tell you the same thing, that it was a light as if a door stood open somewhere past the far end of yon walk, and what light it was leaked round the edges. My own mother saw it, from this yard. Nobody went down. You'll not blame us that nobody went down. And at eight in the morning Gathercole found the wing dark and green as any morning, and the oak door bolted on the inside, and the snow lying all about it like a fresh sheet, and his boots on the mat, and the Doctor gone. That's the first thing.

"And the second thing is Mr. Pordage, that had the living after. Three winters he stood it. His lady took to sitting in the glass of a morning — as it might be anybody," and she did not look at me, which was her way of looking at me, "and she went down like a lamp turned low, that third winter, and he took her away to her sister's at Whitby and resigned the cure. And after him Mr. Casson, that was a hard-headed Cambridge man like your brother, God keep him, and Mr. Casson heard the tapping his second winter, and one night between Christmas and Epiphany he took his lantern and his dog and went in to have it out. The dog wouldn't cross the sill, and the dog was right. He was in there, by his own clock, four minutes. He came out and locked the door and sat in this kitchen till morning with the Book open in front of him, and he never read a word of it, and he never said a word of what he saw, then nor after, only one thing, to my mother, the day the vans came for his furniture. He said: *It is not a wicked thing, Mrs. Slee. If it were wicked a man could fight it. It is a faithful thing, and it wants company in its keeping, and I will not let it make a keeper of me.* And he was gone by Candlemas, and the living stood empty four year, till the Bishop hit on sending a man too young to have heard aught, with a sister to do his housekeeping."

She wrung the sheet as if it had personally misled her, and added, in exactly the same voice: "You've been better this fortnight, miss, stopping out of yon place. But you sleep with your door open now, nights. I'd shut it, if I were you."

I had not known that I slept with my door open. I shut it, after that, every night; and every morning, rising, I found it ajar, and the house cold, and the passage empty; and I want it written down that I speak of a door I had shut myself, with a good iron latch, in a house where only two people slept, one of whom was reading Hooker till two in the morning behind a closed study door for reasons neither of us named. On the fourth morning, the twenty-first of December, the shortest day, I found the door ajar and the landing window beyond it standing grey with frost-fern, every pane of it flowered thick — save one. One pane, breast-high, mid-window, was clear and black and dry, a neat blank in all that white lacework, exactly as though a warm palm had rested against it through the night, patiently, from the outside; and my room, I remind you, is on the upper floor, and below that window is nothing but forty feet of moorland dark and the long glass roof of the rector's garden, shining under the last stars.

I said nothing to Edward. It was two days short of Christmas, his first, and the church was dressed in holly, and I would not. But I wrote that day to nobody, and burned the letter I did not write, and took the green book out of my linen-drawer where I had buried it beneath the lavender, and set it on my table, plainly, beside the thimble; because pretending not to keep a thing is the very manner of keeping I had learned that autumn to be afraid of; and if the garden and I were to understand one another, we should at least do it honestly.

# Chapter Eight — The Keeper's Confession

Christmas passed, and was even gentle: a full church twice, the farm suppers, Edward shining like his own altar brass, and for six nights together — I noted them in my pocket-book like frosts — no tapping at all. I had almost persuaded myself that the wing kept the feast, or that some tide in it had turned, when Gathercole appeared at the kitchen door on the morning of the thirtieth and asked, hat in hand, awkward as a boy, whether I would walk up to the church with him and give my opinion of the stove-flues; which piece of diplomacy deceived nobody, Mrs. Slee least of all, who looked from him to me and went out to the hens with a face like the lid of a box.

We got no nearer the stove-flues than the churchyard wall. He stood himself where he could see the wing below us — he always placed himself so, I realised that morning; in twelve years I doubt the man had ever once let it stand behind him — and he paid his debt as Mrs. Slee had paid hers, straight out, in order, like a man reciting evidence.

"You'll hear it plain, miss, for it's you it concerns. Twelve year I've stoked yon furnace, and till this October there was never a sound out of the place but weather. The tapping began the fourth night after you slept your first night in that house. I heard it first, doing the eight-o'clock in the dark o' the year — I hear it most nights I'm late at the coke, and it's never once sounded while the Doctor's garden had nobody to hear. It knows its own, does yon place. It knew the Doctor. It knew Mrs. Pordage — she'd buried three bairns, miss, before ever she came here; folk didn't tell you that; it was her it warmed first and her it wanted. It let Mr. Casson alone till it learned him, and him it couldn't warm, so him it showed summat instead, and shifted him that road. And it knows you. I'd sooner have said nowt, and seen you leave at the spring wi' your brother none the wiser. But you've stopped going in, and it's begun coming out — I've seen the landing window, miss, from the yard, of a frost morning — and there's a thing now I mun say that I've said to no living soul, and you'll do me the kindness of hearing it looking at the flues, for I can't say it and be looked at."

So I looked at the flues, or at the tower, or at the rooks; and beside me, in a voice gone carefully flat, the old man said:

"I've seen her. Over the years. Of a morning, early, in the good light — never in the dark; whatever's in there after dark, it's not her, and you'll mind that distinction, for your life you'll mind it. A little fair lady on the white bench at the far end, sewing, wi' her head bent, the way a lady sits that's waited so long she's forgot she's waiting. Five times in twelve year, maybe six. And every time, miss, every time, so help me — the plants had grown that week. And I'll tell you what I hold, that I've worked out in that yard wi' a rake in my hand while better men made nothing of it in committees. It's not her ghost, and it never were. Her soul's with God and has been these five-and-twenty year, and whatever sits sewing down yonder no more knows it's not her than a picture knows it's paint. He grew it. D'ye see me, miss? Thirty year a scholar of one woman, and nine year pouring the whole of his remembering into eighty foot o' glass at her degrees — he grew the *shape* of her, the way you'd grow a rose from a cutting, and the shape of his own grieving round it like the glass round the garden; and a shape like that wants tending, miss, that's its whole nature, it were made out of tending; and when he came to see what he'd done — and he did see, at the end; I stoked for him; I watched the seeing come on him like a winter — it were stronger nor him, and it kept him. It keeps him yet. Yon tall one at the panes in the dusk, that taps — that's no garden-grown thing, miss, that's the Doctor himself, that went in o' the anniversary night twelve year since to do a thing he'd not the strength left to do alone; and what he's tapping for, this winter and never before, is that for the first time in twelve year there's somebody the far side of the glass that's of his own trade, and might" — the flat voice went, all at once, like ice going, and he was an old man by a wall — "might have the mercy to help him finish it."

The rooks went round the tower twice while I stood and took this in, the whole of it, in silence; and I found that what I had to say back was not any of the questions a sensible woman would have brought away from such a speech, but one thing only, that came up out of my own three years like a bubble out of a pond.

"Mr. Gathercole," I said, "why have you kept the furnace going? Twelve years, twice a day, knowing all this — the money never mattered to you, I think. Why did you keep it warm?"

He was quiet so long that I thought the confession done, and had half turned for the porch; and then he said, to the wall, to the wing shining below us in the short-day light:

"Because he'd have felt the cold, miss. Wherever he is in yon place. He were my rector one-and-thirty year, and he christened all mine, and buried two; and I couldn't fetch him out, and I couldn't get shut of it, so I kept him warm. Same as you'd keep a lamp in a window. You'll know summat of that, I reckon."

And that is the whole of what happened at the churchyard wall on the thirtieth of December; and if in all this account there is one place where the reader should stop and be quiet a moment, I believe it is here, with the two of us looking down at that bright and terrible building: the woman who could not weep, and the man who had stoked a dead man's grief against the frost for twelve winters, twice a day, out of pure unpaid faithfulness; and neither of us knowing yet that between us we already held everything that was needed — the book, the thimble, the coke-rake, and the knowledge — to bring the winter in.

# Chapter Nine — The Last Entry

The snow that had held off all season came in the third week of January, and with it, as if the weather kept the parish accounts, came word down from Scarhead — the far, high end of the parish, five miles over the tops — that old Metcalfe the shepherd was going at last, and going hard, and asking for the church. Edward did not hesitate; I will have nothing thought against him on this head; a dying man outweighs a living building in any arithmetic a priest may decently keep, and neither he nor I said aloud what the date was. He went up on the Friday with the carrier as far as the road served and walked the rest, and the snow began that night in earnest; and on the Saturday came a boy over the tops with word that Metcalfe was lingering, that the road was filling, and that Mr. Vane bade me keep the fires in and expect him when the weather chose. The boy went back before noon. The snow came on. And so it fell out, with no one's contriving and everyone's, that on the twenty-second of January, the anniversary night, I was alone in Wythershaw rectory from four o'clock, when Mrs. Slee — who fought me over it at the door, and whom I ordered home at last in words I am glad of, because they left her blameless — went down the lane into the falling white.

I make no mystery of what I did with the evening. I had known for three weeks what I should do with it, ever since the churchyard wall; known it, I daresay, since the potting lobby in December. I banked the kitchen fire and trimmed two lamps. I ate bread and cheese standing, like a traveller. And then I sat down at the kitchen table with the lamps before me and read the end of the garden-book — the last leaves, which I had never let myself read, the way you will keep one letter back — because I would not go into that place ignorant of what its keeper had purposed on the night it kept him.

They are dated through the December of 1880 and into the January, and the hand never changes, and I will copy here only what is needful. *Dec. 14th. The camellias never better. I find I have not been down the village a fortnight. Gathercole does all. It is a strange thing for a man to discover at threescore-and-eight that he is the pane, and not the gardener; that he has been these many years the thing looked through, and what looks is the garden. I do not say this is madness; I have examined it as I was taught; it is worse, it is horticulture.* Then, some days on: *I have tried three times this winter to let one leaf lie where it fell, as a trial of my governance, and three times have not. The sin would be a dead leaf — I wrote that once, in this book, and believed it. I ask now, before God, what it is that so believes in me.* And then the entry I had come for, dated the twentieth, two days before the night, and set down as evenly as a planting-note:

*I bury the parish's dead and cannot bury my own; that is the account in one line. She asked me once, near the end, whether I would be able to let her go, and I told her a lie, and she knew it for one, and spared me; I have been nine-and-twenty years spending that mercy. What I keep under this glass is not Eugenie. It is the lie, grown very fine. A man may not keep what he was not given to keep — I knew it when I wrote it, and built on. On the two-and-twentieth, at her hour, I mean to put the garden to bed: rake out the fire, open the lights, and let January do what I cannot. If I come to the end of that night's work, the parish shall have glass for its cold frames and I shall have a bare wall and the honest weather, and shall go down to the village and be an old man there, which is a thing I have never tried. It will go hard. It has had my whole strength, and knows my whole mind, and it is faithful, which is the terrible thing in it, as I have cause to know, having made its faith myself, out of the best of me. If any hand but mine reads this, the fault was not in the trying. Finish it kindly. It was grown out of love, every leaf, and has done nothing, in all its nature, but keep what it was given.*

There is one line more, small, beneath, the last writing of his life: *Her degrees tonight, one hour more. Then the frost.*

I sat for a long time when I had done, and the house sat with me — cold, and listening; it had listened all winter; I had simply learned its posture. You will understand now, as I understood at that table, the whole grammar of the thing: the tapping that began when a griever came; the door left unbolted all a December day; the book and the thimble laid out on the sieves like a place set at table. Twelve years past, at her hour, an old man had gone in to let the garden die, and the garden — being made of his own faithfulness, and stronger — had kept him instead, as it kept everything, perfectly, and would keep him warm and unfinished under its clear glass until someone of his own trade came and did his night's work for him. It was not haunting the house. It was asking it. Three taps at a time, with manners, for twelve years, in the only language a garden has — sound against glass; *the pane rings, someone is there; the pane fogs, someone is breathing* — asking, of whoever might at last be listening, the one mercy it could not perform upon itself and had been built never to allow: *Finish it. Kindly. Then the frost.*

The church clock struck ten across the snow. And out of the south passage, small and clear as the first night, but not three now — two; my own two, my answer rendered back to me across eleven weeks, precise as an echo, courteous as an invitation —

*Tap. Tap.*

I rose, and dressed warm as for a journey, and filled the little hand-lamp; and I took the green book under my arm, and the thimble in my left hand, and the kitchen matches in my pocket; and I went down the south passage to the oak door, and drew back the bolt that Gathercole — who had done his four-o'clock stoking that day, I had heard him at it, twelve years faithful and the snow coming on — had left, for the first time in all those years, unshot; and I opened the door of the glass wing, after dark, and went in.

# Chapter Ten — After Dark

I want to be exact here, more than anywhere, because this is the part of my account that will be doubted, and I have nothing to set against the doubting but exactness.

The first thing was the warmth, and the wrongness of the warmth. By day the wing was June; that night it was August — a thick, sweet, breathing blood-heat, the heat of a sickroom kept too kind, and it met me at the door like something that had been standing just inside it. The second thing was the light. My hand-lamp made its little ring, but the garden did not need it: the snow outside lay bright under a clearing sky, and all that whiteness came in through the black panes and stood among the leaves as a kind of grey shining, so that the whole eighty feet of the walk lay visible, dim and silver, like a garden at the bottom of a lake. And the third thing — I set it down flatly — is that the garden was awake. There is no argument I can offer you; there is only the difference, which every human creature knows and no instrument measures, between a room that is empty and a room in which someone has just stopped speaking. All those weeks of daylight the wing had been still as a picture. Now every leaf down both sides of the walk stood in the stillness of *attention*, the way a congregation is still; and my heels on the flags rang loud, and I would not tiptoe, and said so, aloud, to the leaves — "I am asked here, and I'll walk like it" — and my voice went down the walk and was received. That is the only word. Nothing echoed. It was received, as a church receives.

I had meant to go straight down to the furnace-door at the far end. I did not get ten yards. The heliotrope bed lies ten yards in, on the left, breast-high, and as I came abreast of it the scent rose up round me in the heat — cherry-pie, they call it, that heavy almond sweetness — and inside the sweetness, folded in it the way a letter is folded in an envelope, was pipe-smoke. His tobacco. The cheap ship's twist Stephen smoked through every examination and swore he would give up when the wards allowed him leisure to suffer; the smell that lived in his coat-collar, that I had breathed on the platform at Leeds the last morning of my life that was ever going to happen to me; a smell that has no business inside any glasshouse on this earth, and that no other soul in Yorkshire could have compounded, because the recipe of it — twist and carbolic and cold railway air and the wool of one particular coat — existed nowhere in the world any longer except in me. I stood there, and the garden held it round me, gently, the way you hold salts under the nose of a woman who is going down; and the warmth came in close, and the stillness said — no words; I will not pretend to words; the stillness *conducted itself toward me*, as manner does — the stillness said: *It is all here. It keeps. Everything they let fall, everything they will not let you speak of at the dinner-tables, the whole of what you carry and may not put down — bring it in under the glass, and it will be kept perfect, and nothing here will ever ask you to be finished with it. Sit down. There is work in plenty. The bench is white, the air stands still, and grief kept at its own degrees is only another name for love with a roof over it. Sit down, keeper, and be warm.*

And God forgive me, for one whole swing of the church clock's pendulum I saw it: saw myself at the white bench with my needle going, mornings, years of mornings, the parish sewing done to a miracle and my face sweet as October butter; saw the twist-smoke folded round me fresh as 1890 every day of my widowed spinsterhood; saw, at the rim of the picture, dim and dear, a coat-collar, a shape learning to sit where shapes are grown. I had built smaller versions of that bench in four counties. I was of the trade. It had judged its woman to a hair.

What saved me — and I have turned this over for thirty years, and it is either the plainest thing in the story or the deepest — was the thimble in my left hand. My hand had closed on it, hard, of itself, and the rim of it bit my palm; and a thimble, you know, is a housekeeper's whole science in one brass acorn: it exists because the needle must go *through*, and the stuff resists, and the finger is mortal and must be armoured for the passage. It is a tool for going through with things. She had owned it, that little fair lady who was five-and-twenty years with God, who had asked her husband on her deathbed whether he could let her go, and had known he lied, and had spared him — she had pushed her needle through the resisting stuff of ten moorland winters with it, dying all the while, cheerful all the while, planning a garden she knew as well as anyone she would never see; she had been, of all the souls in this history, the one who was never once deceived; and holding the weight of her thimble I heard, not in the garden's voice but clean across it, in the dry small voice of the green book on my arm: *What I keep under this glass is not Eugenie. It is the lie, grown very fine.*

"No," I said, to the heliotrope, to the pipe-smoke, to the warm attending dark. "No. He is not in your keeping, my dear, and he never will be. He is dead. He died at half-past four in the morning, the eleventh of March, 1890, with his hand in mine, and it was the worst thing that will ever happen to me, and it is *mine* — it happened, and you offer me a world where nothing has finished happening, and I would rather freeze." And I walked on, and the scent let me go — it did not snatch, it *let go*, and the letting-go was worse than any clutching, for it was done with the sorrowing patience of a thing that had heard *no* before, once, in January of 1881, from a better keeper than I — and I went down the length of the silver walk, past the standing camellias, to the far paved circle where the white bench stood; and there, between the bench and the end elevation, black against the snowlight, at the one pane in all those hundreds that was fogged grey from sill-bar to glazing-bar, stood the tall stooped figure, its hand against the glass; and I was no longer in any degree afraid; and I said, as you would say to any parishioner kept waiting shamefully long in a cold porch:

"Doctor Snow. I have read your book, and I am come to finish your work. Show me how."

The hand came off the pane. Where the palm had rested, the fog cleared in its shape — five fingers and the heel of a hand, printed in clear glass on the grey, exactly as such a print stands on a breathed-on window; and then the whole figure turned — I never saw its face; I am glad past telling that I never saw its face; it was spared me, I believe, deliberately, as a kindness, by whatever of the man was left to be kind — and it went before me, down the last yards of the walk, through the leaves that bowed nowhere and stirred nothing, to the low iron door of the furnace-house; and it stood aside, stooped, courteous, its arm out toward the latch: a host, at the end of a long evening, showing the last guest where the lamps are put out.

# Chapter Eleven — Letting In January

The furnace-house was small and red and roaring — Gathercole's four-o'clock charge, banked to see the anniversary through at her degrees, one hour more. The iron rake stood against the jamb. I hung my lamp on the nail above it, laid the green book and the thimble on the sill of the little sooty window where I could see them, took the rake in both hands, and did what I had watched the grandson do in December: opened the fire-door, and raked the burning heart of the rector's garden out of the grate, hissing, on to the wet stone floor, and spread it thin, and watched the red go grey rib by rib; and then I stopped the dampers, and shut the fire-door on the cold, and stood up straight in the sudden dark with my shoulders cracking; and from the long glass house above me — I was half below its floor, you understand, at the roots of it — there came through the brickwork a long, single, settling sigh, such as a ward gives at the turn of the night, when the fever breaks all down the row at once.

The vents were the harder labour, and I was the better part of an hour at them: the winding-gear for the ridge-lights stood at the north end, of course, back the whole length of the walk, and the handles had not been turned in twelve years, and I am not a large woman. I record, all the same, that not one wheel resisted me beyond its rust. I cranked the ridge open first, the whole spine of the roof, a black yard-wide slot of January sky the length of the building, and the cold came down into that August heat like clear water into wine; then the side-lights, bay by bay, working south, my breath blooming grey round my head — and here is a thing I did not notice until it had been true for some minutes, and when I noticed it I had to stand and hold the winding-gear a moment with my eyes shut: the panes were fogging. All of them. Everywhere. The warm wet breath of the dying garden was striking up against glass with true winter on the other side of it at last, and the whole vast clerestory of the rector's keeping was going grey, softly, evenly, pane by pane by pane, the moor and the snow and the stars blotting out all down its length — the house doing at last, in one night, all the breathing it had been forbidden for twenty years. The glass fogged, and I stood in the middle of it and watched it fog, and if you have followed this account at all you will know that of every strange thing I saw at Wythershaw, this, which any scullery window does of a Monday, was the one I could hardly bear to look at, for the mercy in it.

The cold came down. The garden took it — I will say it so, and let it stand — the garden took it the way the brave take bad news. Nothing writhed; nothing, God be thanked, cried out; that faithful place had been asked at last, by a keeper with the strength to ask it, for the one obedience it had been grown never to give, and it obeyed. By the time I had the last light open the fronds of the tree-ferns had begun to drop, slow as minute-hands; the heliotrope went while I watched, its whole bed sinking on itself with a smell — the last it ever gave — not of cherry-pie nor of any tobacco, but plainly and sweetly of cut hay, which is the smell of things that are finished properly; the orange trees let go their leaves in ones and twos, without grudging, a soft irregular patter down the dark walk like the first big drops before rain. And the camellias — the camellias, that had been budded fat since October and had held those buds shut all winter like fists at a funeral — the camellias, in the frost of their first and last January, bloomed. Every bud on every bush, wide open, white and red, in the falling cold, in the dark: one flowering, entire, unwitnessed by any soul in the world but me and the two I did it for. I make no apology for the sentence I must write next. It was the most beautiful thing I have ever seen, and it lasted perhaps a quarter of an hour, and it was a death; and all three of those clauses are the truth, and any account of that winter which cannot hold all three together is a lie.

Of the end I have only this to tell. As the cold settled toward its work — the church clock had gone two, I think; the fog on the panes had begun its silent turning into frost-fern, the grey lace going white and crystal, the whole long house flowering on the inside of its own glass now that nothing under it would flower again — I saw, down at the far paved circle, on the white bench, where the snowlight came thinnest through the furring panes: two shapes. The tall stooped one, seated, at rest, its long hands on its knees at last, like a labouring man at the end of the day's darg; and beside it, close beside it, small and fair in the grey, head bent, a shape that had waited out its own image's long imposture with whatever patience the dead are given, sewing — or seeming to sew, the elbow moving small and steady — and the tall one's head bowed down toward the bent head, in the attitude, past all misreading, of a man who has been a long time forgiven and is only now able to believe it. I did not go down the walk to them. It was their hour, bought very dear, and no housekeeper worth her wages carries a lamp into it. I turned my back, as you turn from a lit window that is none of yours, and walked out through the falling leaves of the orange trees, and drew the oak door to behind me and did not bolt it; and as the latch went home I heard, deep in the dark behind me, one knock — a single stroke against glass, unhurried, final, neither plea nor summons any longer but the plain punctuation of a finished sentence: the sound, I have thought every winter of my life since, of a door being gently shut somewhere by somebody going, at very long last, home to bed.

I slept in the kitchen chair, and woke grey and stiff to the sound of Gathercole's boots in the yard at eight — twelve years faithful, and the snow stopped in the night — and we went in together, he and I, through the unbolted door, into the honest ruin of a glasshouse in winter: black leaves on white frost, burst pipes' silence, our breath before us, every pane furred blind with fern, cold as any church and emptier than any I ever stood in, for there was nothing in it now, nothing at all, but a garden that had died and two long-dead mortal names; and down at the far end, at the foot of the great camellia by the white bench, where the falling frost had shrunk the leaf-mould of two decades — I had walked past the spot at midnight within a yard of it — lay quiet in the black earth, and I set it down here as reverently as I am able, all that January had been asked to come in for: a longer shape and a smaller, side by side; the smaller five-and-twenty years dressed in lead and oak, translated out of the churchyard earth beneath, by what gardening no inquest was ever going to name, into the root-heart of the tree she had chosen at Funchal with her mother, half a century and a whole world of frost away; and the longer shape in a nightshirt gone to lace, its bare feet crossed at the ankle, its hands folded on its breast by no possibility of accident — an old man laid out by loving hands upon the threshold of the room where his one lie was kept, at the exact place where the earth had opened to show him the truth it held, and, holding it, had let him stay.

Gathercole took his hat off, that last morning as every morning; but he had gone in bare-headed to a garden, and he stood now in an unroofed grave with his hat against his breast; and the old man looked a long time, and then said, in his flattest voice, the truest funeral sentence I have yet heard from priest or layman: "Well, Doctor. Frost got in, seemingly." And then we both wept — he like a beam breaking, in three great cracks and done; and I — 

I wept, at last, is all. For the four of them, first, the two under the camellia and the two the garden had shown me on the bench; and then, the sluice being open and the science of it past my mending, for my own: for half-past four in the morning of the eleventh of March, 1890, and a coat-collar on a cold platform, and three years of linen kept perfect in four counties by a woman frozen at her own degrees; I sat down on the frosted flags of that dead paradise in my out-door coat and wept myself soft again, while the fern-work whitened on the panes above me with the rising sun behind it, every crystal of it built out of breath; and Gathercole the sexton, keeper of the graveyard and the stoves and yon, stood by me bare-headed in the ruin with the patience of his whole faithful life, and let me.

# Chapter Twelve — Things That Die in Their Season

There is not much more, and what there is, is daylight.

Edward came down off the tops that afternoon, having buried old Metcalfe at Scarhead in the morning, to find his sister with a face like a woman new over an illness, his conservatory dead, and his parish — the news having gone up the lane before breakfast in the way of villages — standing about his gate in the snow, not curious, but the other thing, the thing a moorland parish will die before it names: moved. What passed between my brother and me across the kitchen table that evening belongs to the two of us; I will say only that he heard the whole of it, gravely, his tea going cold, and that where I had braced for doctrine I got instead a long silence, and then: "I preached on monuments at Advent," he said. "To a church full of people every one of whom knew more of the matter than I did. They came, Honor. They came and sat under my sermon like folk sitting out a hailstorm, because they liked me, and they knew." He turned his cup, in the way people do. "I shall be ten years learning what they were being patient about. I had better make a start." He has made, I may say here, across these thirty years, the best rector Wythershaw ever had, not excepting Doctor Snow; but that is his story, and he is too modest to write it.

The inquest — for there had to be one, twelve years late — sat at Aldergate in February, viewed the evidence of the church court of 1881, heard Gathercole's oath over again word for word, heard mine with the nighttime third of it decently omitted (Mrs. Slee schooled me in the omitting, at the copper, with the severity of a Queen's Counsel), and found what such bodies are for finding: that the Reverend Ambrose Snow, Doctor of Divinity, had died on or about the 22nd of January 1881, by misadventure, having fallen or been overcome while at work in his conservatory, and his body been concealed by the subsequent subsidence of a deep planting-bed. Of the other matter — the smaller shape, and how it had come from consecrated ground through undisturbed earth into the roots of a camellia — the inquest said nothing whatever, having taken very good care not to be told; the diocese, with a speed that told its own tale, issued a faculty for reinterment; and on a hard bright morning at the end of February they were buried together in the churchyard, in the one grave under the wall where she had lain since 1869, my brother reading the service, the whole parish standing out in the cold, and Gathercole, who had dug every grave at Wythershaw for forty years, digging this one as his last and then going home and hanging up the spade, as he said, "while I'm ahead."

The wing came down at Easter. The Snow trust, its condition failed — the wing being neither warm nor, by then, whole, for the February gales had taken the ridge — passed quietly to its missionary society; but the glass, by the terms of a memorandum in the green book which Messrs. Pursey & Hart allowed would have force, went to the parish: and that is why, to this day, if you walk through any cottage garden in Wythershaw in March, you will see the spring lettuce coming up early under lights of curious curved glass, in frames knocked together thirty years ago out of white-painted iron; and why the old people, tapping a loose pane home, will still say, without a smile, that they must keep the Doctor warm. The bare gable stood raw a summer, and then Edward had it ivied; the house, with the wing gone, ran warm — I state the fact and leave it — and the coal bills of Wythershaw rectory declined that next winter to the great satisfaction of the church-wardens, who had, of course, their own explanation, as sound people always do.

I kept my brother's house four years more, until he married — a person so exactly right that I take some credit, having introduced them — and then I did the thing the garden had shown me I was in danger of never doing: I stopped. I did not stop keeping house; one is what one is, and the linen of this world does want somebody who understands it; but I stopped keeping it as the drink, and kept it thereafter as the trade, for wages, in houses I could leave. In one of them, in time, I met a man — a land-agent, a widower, no part of this story except that he is the whole of the rest of my story — who asked me, the third spring I knew him, why I planted nothing in my window-boxes but annuals; sweet peas, mignonette, heliotrope even — I had made my peace with heliotrope by then — things that must be sown again every year from nothing. And because it was March, and the panes of the potting-shed where we stood were streaming grey with our two breaths and the seed-trays' warmth, streaming and fogging as the windows of the living properly do, I found I could tell him: that I had once seen the other kind of garden, the kind that is never allowed to die and so is never allowed to have lived, and that I had learned there to trust no green thing that cannot make an end. *Things that die in their season,* I told him, *come up again in their season. It is only the things we will not let die that never come back.* He considered this for a while, in the way he has. Then he said that it sounded like sense, and that the mignonette wanted thinning, and we thinned it; and I set down that small conversation here at the end of a ghost story deliberately, because it is the resolution of the ghost story, more than the inquest, more even than the grave under the churchyard wall: two middle-aged people thinning seedlings in a fogged shed, in the spring, in the ordinary mortal weather.

Eugenie's thimble I did not keep; I have kept nothing of Wythershaw's, on principle, but the principle itself. It went into the grave at the February burying, into the new-turned earth at the head, privately, between Mrs. Slee's arm and mine, the two of us stooping together as if at the same weed. But every year while I lived in that country I planted the grave for them, with the rector's leave, which was never once withheld: no camellias, no evergreen; sweet peas, and mignonette, and one row of heliotrope, sown fresh each spring, blooming their little summer over the two names, and dying, root and all, every year, at the coming of the honest frost — being mourned, and missed, and sown again; which is what we owe the dead, and all we owe them; and the glass of that house is raising lettuces; and the cold of it is thirty years thawed; and my window this evening, as I write the last of this by lamplight with the year at its dark end again, is fogged grey from sash to sash with the warmth of a living room, and I have breathed on the pane, and written in the breath, as I finish, the same two taps I gave a garden once — *there; now we have both done it* — and watched the mist close gently over them, the way the well-kept griefs of this world, if we will only consent to let them die, close over, and soften, and come back kind.
