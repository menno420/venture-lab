# THE GLASS RECTORY

*A Ghost Story*

> When Honor Vane comes to keep house for her brother, the new curate of a moorland parish, she is told two things about the rectory: do not mind the cold, and do not go into the glass wing after dark. The conservatory the last rector built is warm all winter, green all year — and empty since the night he walked into it and never came out. A ghost story in the classic register, about the things we grow in place of grief.

---

**Content note:** Adult gothic fiction. Contains bereavement, a long-past death from illness, and a disappearance; all deaths occur off the page and in the past. No violence, no gore, no explicit content. A quiet, cold read for adult readers.

---

# Chapter One — The Two Instructions

It is a long time now since I kept house for my brother, and longer still since anybody has asked me to tell what happened at Wythershaw in the winter of 1893; but the year has come round again to the dark end, and the fires are lit, and I find that the story asks itself. I set it down plainly, because it was a plain thing, in its way. Nothing in it shrieks. Nothing in it bleeds. It is only that once, for a season, I lived in a house where grief had been given a room of its own, and glass walls, and a gardener; and I learned what such a room costs, and who pays.

My brother Edward was twenty-six that autumn, and newly priested, and the Bishop of Aldergate had done him the doubtful kindness of a moorland parish that two incumbents had already left inside of ten years. Edward called it a beginning. Our aunt, who read the church papers the way other women read the assizes, called it Wythershaw, and said the name as if it closed the subject. I was twenty-nine, three years past the death of the man I was to have married, and I had discovered in myself by then a talent that unmarried women of my kind were expected to have, and which I exercised, I confess, the way some men drink: I kept house. I had kept my aunt's house, and my cousin's through her confinement, and the house of a school in Harrogate through a scarlatina winter. I was very good at it. If I did not sleep much, the linen never knew.

So when Edward wrote — *the living is vacant, the Bishop sends me as curate-in-charge, the rectory is large and I am told peculiar, will you come* — I had my boxes corded within the week.

We went up from Aldergate by carrier's cart on the eighth of October, the last four miles at a walking pace with the road climbing all the way. I remember that the moor came at us by degrees, the walls giving out first, then the trees, until there was nothing on either hand but heather going over from bloom to rust, and a wind with no gossip in it, that had touched nothing since the sea. The village, when it came, was a grey lane of houses pitched down a fold of the hill as if for shelter, a church tower above them with a fog of rooks about it, and beyond the church, alone where the moor began again, the rectory.

I saw the glass before I saw the house. That is the first thing to set down honestly, because everything afterwards is in it. The light was failing, and the house itself was a black shape with its chimneys against the last of the sky — a long stone rectory of the plain old sort, rain-darkened, shouldered up against the weather. And at its southern end, where in such a house you would look for a dull wall and a laurel hedge, there stood instead a great wing of glass, high as the eaves, long as the house itself was deep — and it was lit. Not lamplit; there was no flame in it. It held the western light the way a pond holds it after the fields have gone dark, a standing shape of pale gold and green, and inside the glass, black against that shining, I could see leaves. Great fronds and fans and towers of leaf, crowded as a jungle, motionless in the wind that was making the cart-horse lean.

"Somebody's grand notion," said the carrier, not looking at it.

"Is it the rector's?" I asked, and he said, "It were," in the tone the country keeps for closed subjects, and clicked at the horse.

Mrs. Slee was waiting for us in the porch with a lamp. I will give her to you at once, because without her we should neither of us have lasted the month: a widow of the village, sixty or thereabouts, flat-backed, wide-handed, with the local manner that strangers take for coldness and that is in fact a kind of mercy, since it promises you nothing it will not perform. She had aired what could be aired and lit what could be lit. She showed us the kitchen, the pump, the lamp-oil, my brother's study with the parish chest, the bedrooms — hers was not among them; she would come days, she said, and go home nights, and something in the way she said it had the smoothness of a stone often handled.

It was on the stairs, with the candle in her hand, that she gave me the two instructions. She gave them without drama, as she might have told me which chimney smoked.

"You'll not mind the cold, miss," she said. "The house runs cold, do what you will with it. It's not the sashes nor the doors, and it's no fault of the coal. You'll take it as you find it and not wear yourself out against it. That's the first thing."

"And the second?"

She was three steps above me, and the candlelight came down over her face so that I could not properly see it.

"You'll not go into the glass wing after dark," she said. "Days, if you must; Gathercole will let you in when he does the stoking, and there's no harm in a garden in the daylight, though I don't go in myself and never shall. But not after dark. Not for a dropped thimble. Not if you hear —" and there she stopped herself, with the neatness of a woman shutting a drawer, and finished, "Not for anything, miss. That's all it is."

"Not if I hear what, Mrs. Slee?"

"Wind in an ill-made building," she said, "is all you would hear," and went on up with the candle, and left me to follow or stand in the dark.

Edward, when I told him over supper, was delighted, in the way of a young man whose education has cost a great deal and must be used. A cold house was ventilation; a forbidden conservatory was superstition; the village had made a bogey of a dead man's hobby, and it would be our work — he said *our* very kindly — to be cheerful and regular and unafraid, until the parish learned that the rectory was only a house. He said grace over the cold mutton with real gladness. He was, I should say here, the best man I have ever known, and nothing in this account is meant otherwise; a lantern is not to blame that there are places its light puts deeper into shadow.

I went up at ten. My room faced south. I mention it as flatly as Mrs. Slee might: the moon had risen over the moor, and below my window the glass wing lay long and pale under it, like a church built of ice, or a hothouse for growing the moonlight; and the panes of it were bright and dry and black with leaves; and though the night had turned sharp enough to sting the hand I rested on my own windowsill, and though my own window was fogged grey with the warmth of my candle and my breath — every pane of that great cold-lit roof below me was clear. Clear as still water. Clear as if nothing on either side of it had any breath at all.

I drew my curtain, and minded the cold, and slept.

# Chapter Two — The Green Wing

Gathercole did the stoking at eight and at four. I met him the first morning, coming round the frost-whitened gravel with my shawl about my head, and found the southern end of the house even stranger by daylight than it had been under the moon.

The wing was built out from the gable in the grand manner of thirty years before: a spine of white-painted iron and a ridge of ornamental cresting, curved lights at the corners, and pane upon pane upon pane, hundreds of them, set in glazing bars fine as the veins of a leaf. Against the grey of the house and the browns of October it did not look wicked. I want that understood. It looked expensive, and mistaken, the way a ball-gown would look mistaken hung on a scarecrow; and it looked warm. Its stone footing ran down to a sunken furnace-house at the far end, with a low door and an iron chimney, and out of that door, stooping, backing, drawing a rake after him, came a man built like a thorn tree, all angle and weather, with white hair under a black-brimmed hat. He must have been past seventy. He looked at me the way the carrier had looked at the glass.

"You'll be the sister," he said.

"Honor Vane," I said. "You keep the furnace, Mr. Gathercole?"

"I keep the grave-yard," he said, "and the church stoves, and yon." He nodded at the wing without turning his eyes to it. "Four o' the clock and eight o' the clock, every day the Lord sends, twelve year now. There's money left for it. You'd best see inside, I reckon, and get it over."

That is exactly what he said: *get it over*. He took me up the path to the wing's own door — a proper door of oak and glass, set in the end elevation, with a bright-worn brass handle — and he opened it, and stood aside with his hat off, as I have seen men stand aside at the door of a church they no longer worship in. He did not go in. I went in.

I had been in glasshouses before. My cousin's husband forced peaches; the school in Harrogate had a fernery, six feet by ten, that dripped. I had even once, as a girl, been taken through the Palm House at Kew, and carried away from it chiefly a memory of my own collar wilting. So I knew, or thought I knew, what a warm house of plants would be — the wet heat, the green smell, the breathing quiet — and some of that was there, and if it had all been there I should have had nothing to write.

It was warm. It was June-warm, gentle-warm, the warmth of a walled garden at four o'clock on a good day; and the air was sweet — not heavy, but sweet, camellia and clean earth and something underneath like oranges, for there were orange trees, four of them, in great slatted tubs down the centre walk. There were camellias in bud along the whole southern side, budded thick as a hawthorn, in October. There was a fig trained black and ancient against the house wall, a stand of tree-ferns unrolling new fronds fresh as green wax, heliotrope in a raised bed breathing out its cherry-pie sweetness, and down at the far end, where the walk widened to a little paved circle with a bench, a plumbago in full flower, that blue that has no other name. Everything flourished. Everything was clean. Not a fallen leaf on the flags, not a spotted frond, not a snail-track; the water in the slate tank in the corner stood full and bright, though I had seen no pipe and no pump.

And it was silent. A glasshouse in the sun ticks and drips and stirs; the lights creak in their bars; a bee blunders. Here the sun stood in through the south panes in long clean lozenges, and the leaves hung in it without one tremor, and my own boot-heels on the flags were loud as a stranger's in an empty church. I remember I stood in the middle of the walk and heard my own heart, and was ashamed of hearing it, in a garden, at nine o'clock on a bright morning.

I said, because saying things aloud is what we do in empty rooms that do not feel empty, "Well. You are very fine." And the leaves stood, and the light stood, and the plumbago at the far end was so blue against the moor beyond the glass that it hurt, and nothing answered, and I have never in my life felt more distinctly that something had heard.

Gathercole was still at the door, hat in hand, when I came out — quicker, I think, than I had meant to come.

"Who does the gardening?" I asked him. "You lay the fires, you said. But someone prunes; someone waters; someone sweeps."

"There's money left for the stoking," he said. "There's no money left for aught else, because there's no need of aught else. I've not watered in yon house in twelve year, miss. It doesn't dry. Nothing drops and nothing spoils and nothing dies, and if you can bide the thought of that, you're welcome to sit in there of a morning, warm as a cat, like the last rector's lady used to do." He set his hat back on. "And if you can't bide it, you're in your right wits."

I asked him the question I had been circling all night. I asked who had built it, and he told me the shell of the story standing in the graveyard he kept, wind at us over the wall, and the glass throwing us back the whole width of the morning sky: that the Reverend Doctor Ambrose Snow, rector of Wythershaw for one-and-thirty years, had built the wing for his wife's sake in the year 1870, she being then dead; that he had tended it himself, nine years, ten, no man else so much as carrying a can into it; and that on a January night in 1881 he had risen from his bed and gone down into his garden in the dark, in his nightshirt, in the frost, and had never come out of it, nor been found in it, nor been found anywhere in this world since.

"The door was fast on the inside," Gathercole said. "Snow on the ground that night, and never a mark in it, all round the wing, and I'll take my oath on that before any bench in England, for I was the one they had walk it. Church court sat on it after. Misadventure, they wrote. And his boots" — he looked at me for the first time since he had opened the door — "his boots were on the mat inside, miss, set together tidy, the way a man sets them when he means to walk on grass."

I looked back down the length of the wing. The sun had come round a point further; the lozenges of light had shifted on the flags; and it came to me, with the particular coldness that attends small facts, that from where I stood every pane of the southern face held the picture of the moor and the racing October sky, bright and dry and perfect, and that my breath, and Gathercole's, went up between us in two plumes of grey — and that in all that acreage of cold morning glass there was not one square inch of mist.

"Why does it never fog?" I said. "Warm within, cold without — it should stream, Mr. Gathercole. Any window in the village —"

"Any window in the village has folk breathing behind it," said Gathercole, and shot the brass bolt of the oak door, and that was the end of the first lesson.

# Chapter Three — Doctor Snow

A house tells you its dead slowly, and a village faster, and a parish chest fastest of all, if you can read its hand. Between the three of them, by All Souls' I had the story of Ambrose Snow as whole as anyone living was likely to have it; and because everything that came after stands on it, I will set it out here in order, though it came to me in rags — a sentence from Mrs. Slee over the bread-trough, a page of the register under Edward's lamp, a word let fall at a cottage door and regretted before it finished falling.

He came to Wythershaw in 1850, a scholar's son, a Doctor of Divinity at an age when most men are still copying sermons, and by every account a cold preacher and a warm man; the parish, which will forgive anything sooner than the reverse arrangement, loved him. In 1859, being past forty, he went down to Aldergate and came back married, to a woman twenty years his junior — Eugenie, her name was, a fair, light, laughing person by all telling, a doctor's daughter from the south, who filled the rectory with music no one up here could name, and who was, from her first winter on the moor, dying. Not quickly. The consumption in her family took its time, Mrs. Slee said; it had taken her mother twenty years. It played with her: good summers, bad Januaries, a bloom on her some seasons that deceived everyone but her husband. The doctors said what the doctors of that day said — warmth, soft air, the south. Madeira, one of them wrote, or Mentone; and there is a letter of Snow's in the parish chest, a draft or a copy he kept, I do not know which, to a college friend, asking — the hand quite steady — whether a man might decently resign a cure of souls to lengthen one life, and if he might, on what they should live, for he had nothing but the living, and she had nothing at all.

He did not resign. I have thought about that as much as about anything in this history. He stayed, and she wintered on the moor, ten winters, wrapped and carried and cheerful — everyone insists to me she was cheerful — planning aloud, Mrs. Slee remembered, the garden they would have when they went south at last: orange trees, she would say, and camellias, and heliotrope, and a blue plumbago over a white bench, *and no wind, Ambrose; a garden where the air stands still*. She died in the January of 1869, in the tenth winter, in the north-west bedroom of this house — my room; I had guessed it, somehow, before Mrs. Slee's silence told me — with the moor wind, they say, so loud on the gable that night that the watchers could not hear her breathing stop.

The wing went up the year after. He sold his library to a college — that is on record — and his mother's silver, and it is thought he spent besides the whole of what had come to him at his marriage; the ironwork came from Glasgow and the glazers from York, and the village stood at the churchyard wall the week the glass went in and did not know what to make of it, a palace of light rising off the gable-end of that grey house, eighteen months after they had carried its only brightness down the path below it. He was not mad; I must not let you think the village thought him mad. He did his duties to the end, christened and married and buried, sat on the school board, walked his bounds. Only every daylight hour that was left over went in under the glass, and no other soul with it; and by the second year the parish had seen, through those clear walls, what he was growing there, and understood it without a word said, in the way a village understands: orange trees. Camellias. Heliotrope in a raised bed. A white bench at the south end, and over it, trained along wires he strained himself, a plumbago, for the blue.

He grew her garden. The garden of the south they had never gone to, pane by pane and root by root, on the exact spot of England she had found hardest to bear; and he kept it at the warmth of the coast she never saw; and he walked in it every evening of the last decade of his life, up and down, up and down, between the standing leaves. Waiting — the village finished the sentence variously, according to the teller's faith. Mrs. Slee would not finish it at all. "He kept it perfect," was all she would say, kneading. "Whatever else, miss, it was perfect. There was never a dead leaf in it while he lived, and God knows there's been none since."

Of the night itself — the twenty-second of January, 1881, twelve years to the night, I did not fail to notice, after his wife's dying — I have told what Gathercole told me, and the church court's finding, and there is nothing to add to it but one small thing from the register, which I found myself, and wish I had not. The register of burials for 1869 is in Snow's own hand, of course; he buried his own wife, there being no other clergy nearer than Aldergate and the roads being what they were. The entry is perfectly formal, perfectly regular. Eugenie Snow, aged 39 years. But low in the margin, in the same ink, very small, where no rubric asks for anything, he wrote four words. *The glass never fogged.*

I understood it, sitting in Edward's study with the register open under the lamp, quicker than I can make you understand it, and I sat for some time before I called Edward to see. In the last nights, you comprehend, they will have kept her window a little open even in January — it was thought healthful — and a screen about it, and the room breathing its warmth out at the gap; and a man who cannot look any longer at the bed will look at the window; and the pane above a dying person's bed clouds and clears, clouds and clears, with a rhythm you cannot help but watch, until it is the only clock in the room. And at some hour of that night the mist on the glass had smoothed away and not returned, and that is how he knew — before the watchers, before the doctor, in the perfect silence of that room — that the breathing had stopped. The window told him. *The glass never fogged.* He had written down, in the driest hand in the world, in the margin of a public book, the exact shape in which his grief had come to him; and then he had gone out and built that shape, eighty feet long, in iron and glass, and filled it with the south, and kept it seven-and-twenty degrees above the frost for the rest of his life.

Edward, when I showed him, was quiet for a long while, and then said, gently enough, that it was very sad, and very morbid, and that the sooner the diocese let him take the wing down the better; the heating of it was a scandal in a parish with cold cottages — there was a bequest, but a bequest could be diverted — and the thing itself was a monument to a man's refusal of the will of God. That is doctrine, and I cannot fault it. But I had kept more sickrooms than Edward, and I said nothing; because I had been that year three winters in the practice of a smaller art of the same kind — three winters of keeping other people's linen presses in an order Stephen would never see, of being so good at houses, so warm, so lit, so ceaselessly, cheerfully busy that nothing in me was ever left alone in a quiet room with what had happened; and I knew the rector's garden from within, as a native knows a country. I did not say this to Edward. I have never said it to anyone until now.

The first tapping came eight days later, on the fourth of November, a little after ten o'clock at night; but you must have one more thing first, because I promised to set this down in order, and in order it stands: on the last day of October, dusting where Mrs. Slee's writ did not run, I found on the sill of the landing window, which looks south down the roof-ridge of the glass wing, a small brass thimble, a woman's, very old, green with sitting; and when I mentioned it at the bread-trough, Mrs. Slee went still for exactly one breath, and then said, in her flattest voice, that there had never been a child in this house in her time, and that I was to leave it where it lay.

I left it where it lay.
