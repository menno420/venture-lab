# THE WEIGH HOUSE

*An Amsterdam crime novel*

> An Amsterdam police diver pulls a weighted body from a canal and recognises the killing — it is the same method that drowned her own husband two years ago, in a death the department ruled an accident.

---

**Content note:** Adult crime fiction. Contains murder, drowning, non-graphic violence, grief and the death of a spouse, criminal-finance themes, and brief strong language. No sexually explicit content; no gratuitous gore. For adult readers.

---

# Chapter One — The Recovery

The water at the Kloveniersburgwal was three degrees and the colour of nothing. Sanne went down the shot line hand over hand, counting her breaths in threes, and the city folded shut over her head. Cold came in at the seams of her drysuit the way it always did, patient, without malice, personal. It found the wrist first, then the throat, a thin line drawn along the skin by a finger that never hurried. Below her somewhere a man was waiting who would never be cold again.

They had put the tent up on the quay at half past five, blue and lit from inside like something that did not belong to winter. She had watched them raise it from the van, watched the frame go up in the dark and the skin snap taut against the sleet, and she had thought, as she always thought, that it was the wrong colour for what it held. The Waag stood across the empty market square with its towers and its old stones, the weigh house, the place where they had once cut open the dead to see how they were made. Sanne had passed it a thousand times and never thought about it. This morning she thought about it. She could not have said why.

The line ran down at an angle into the murk. Visibility was nothing, less than nothing — she worked by the line and by her hands and by the map she built behind her eyes as she went. Fifteen breaths down. The regulator gave her air in cold dry pulls and took it back as a rattle of bubbles that rolled up past her mask and were gone. The bottom of an Amsterdam canal was not a bottom in any clean sense. It was silt over centuries of silt, and in the silt there were bicycles and bottles and phones and once a safe and once, memorably, a car, and the mud held onto everything with a soft and total grip. She had learned to move above it, to disturb nothing, to keep her fins high and her body long, because the first careless kick raised a cloud that took twenty minutes to lie back down and could take a diver's day with it.

Tim's voice came thin down the comms. "You on it?"

"Not yet." Three breaths. "Give me slack."

The line loosened above her. She felt Bram let it go — she knew the hand on the other end of that rope the way you know a voice in the dark. Bram Verhoeven had tended her line for eleven years. He tended it like a man tending a fire, attentive, unhurried, never letting it go dead and never letting it flare. He read her through the rope, through the small pulls and the pauses, the way she read the water; he knew when she had stopped to think and when she had stopped to be afraid, and he never confused the two. She trusted the rope because she trusted the hand. That was the whole of diving, in the end. You went into a place that wanted to keep you and you trusted a hand you could not see.

The tug on the line had come from a dredging crew at first light, a routine sludge run for the winter, and the grab had come up with cloth in its teeth and they had stopped and called the police and the police had called the duikteam. She had done the descent the way she always did it, the way Bram had taught her before she taught it to herself: check the gauge, check the line, one long breath at the surface to set the count, then down. No hurry. The dead did not reward hurry and the water punished it. Now the grab hung idle against the far quay, dripping, and Sanne went down through the water it had stirred, water thick with what the bucket had torn loose, and at twenty-two breaths her glove found him.

Not a face. A shoulder, first — the give of it, wrong, too soft under fabric, and then the fabric itself, a coat, wool by the feel, buttoned. She held still and let the cloud of silt her own movement had raised drift off her, and she waited, and she counted. One two three. One two three. When she moved again it was slow.

He was on his back in the mud. That was the first wrongness, though she did not yet know to name it. Men who fell into canals did not lie neatly on their backs. They came to rest in the shapes that drowning left them, curled, turned, face into the silt, an arm thrown up as if against a light. This man lay like a man laid down. His arms were at his sides. His coat was buttoned to the throat.

"I have him," she said.

Topside went quiet. Then Bram: "Take your time, Sanne. He's not going anywhere and neither are you."

She ran her hands down him in the dark, reading him the way the water made her read everything, by touch and patience. The coat. A jacket beneath it. A shirt, a belt at the waist — a real belt, leather, ordinary, the buckle small and warm-shaped under her fingers the way a man's own belt is. And then, lower, across the hips and cinched hard over the coat itself, another belt, and this one was not ordinary. She knew it under her fingers before her mind would say it. Webbing, stiff and flat. A quick-release buckle, the wide steel kind, the kind she wore at that moment around her own waist. And threaded onto the webbing, flat and heavy and unmistakable, the lead.

Dive weights.

She stopped counting. That was how she knew it had reached her, the thing that would not let go — her breath fell out of its threes and she had to take it back deliberately, one two three, one two three, the way you take back a horse that has felt something in the field it cannot see.

Weight belts. Two of them, she found, working her hands around him in the cold and the black. One across the hips and one higher, under the arms, dragged tight so the body would not roll or rise. She counted the lead by feel, the way she counted her own before a dive, running her thumb over each block — four on the low belt, four on the high, the standard load, a diver's load, laid on a man who was not a diver. Whoever had put this man in the water had wanted him to stay. They had weighted him the way you weighted a diver, deliberately, with knowledge, with the correct and specific tools for holding a thing beneath the surface.

"Sanne." Bram's voice, gentle. "Talk to me. Your breathing changed."

"He's weighted," she said. "Dive belts. Two."

A pause on the line. In the pause she could hear the sleet on the tent skin far above, a dry whisper carried down through the water to her, and under it the deeper hush of the canal itself, the sound a body of water makes that is not quite silence.

"Rope him and bring him up," Bram said. "Don't work the scene down there. That's not our job. That's theirs."

He was right. It was not her job. Her job was recovery — to make the dead ascend, to hand them dry-side to the people whose job was the rest. She unclipped the recovery line from her harness and passed it under him, under the small of his back where the mud gripped hardest, and the mud fought her for him, sucking, reluctant, and she worked her fingers into the cold slurry and felt it give at last with a long soft reluctance like something changing its mind. She had done this forty times, fifty. It never stopped feeling like taking something from a hand that did not want to open.

She got the line around him and cinched it and clipped it off, and she checked the clip twice, because a body lost on the way up was a body recovered twice, and no one should have to be found twice. She signalled topside with two long pulls, the pull for a load made ready, and felt the answering pull come back down the rope, Bram's hand telling her it had reached him, that he had it, that she could let the surface take the weight. Then she stayed a moment with her glove flat on his chest, over the buttoned coat, over the belt that should not be there. She did not pray. She was not a woman who prayed. But she kept her hand there for three breaths, one two three, because it seemed to her that someone ought to, and there was no one else down here in the cold with him but her.

"Coming up," she said. "Tell them to bring the board."

They laid him on the quay under lights and the sleet came down onto him and nobody wiped it away because you did not touch what you did not have to. Sanne came up the ladder, the ladder biting cold through her gloves, and stood dripping on the stone while Tim unclipped her line and coiled it in the flat efficient loops he always made. She pulled her hood back and let the real cold, the air cold, take her wet head, and for a moment the world was very loud after the water — the generator, the sleet, a gull somewhere over the market, a man's radio, all of it rushing back in.

He was younger than she'd expected. Forty, perhaps. A soft, unfinished face, the kind that looked apologetic even now, even done. The water had been kind to him, or the cold had; he had not been down long enough for it to be otherwise. The coat was good, the shoes were good — office shoes, thin-soled, no shoes for the water, no shoes for a January quay. His hands were pale and clean, the nails neat. Not a labourer's hands. A clerk's hands, maybe. Someone who sat at a desk, who signed things, who kept his cuffs clean. She looked at him the way she made herself look at all of them, once, fully, on the stone, because it seemed to her the least you could do for a person was to see them plainly at the end, when everyone else was busy seeing the evidence. Then she stopped looking, because the rest was theirs.

And around him, dark against the wet stone, the belts.

The scene-of-crime people crouched over him with their bags and their cameras, working in the unhurried way of people for whom the worst has already happened, and one of them, a technician Sanne half knew, a woman with careful hands, turned one of the belts to photograph the buckle, lifting it just clear of the coat. The strobe went off. And in the white flash, in the half-second the light stood everything still, Sanne saw it.

Stamped into the steel of the buckle plate, small, official, the way a thing is stamped that belongs to a fleet, to a store, to a service: a number. A serial. And beneath it, worn but legible, three letters she had seen ten thousand times because they were stamped on her own gear, on Tim's, on every set of lead in the shed at Zeeburg, three letters she could have found blind.

Team issue.

The belts around the dead man were theirs.

Sanne stood very still on the quay with the sleet running down the back of her neck and into her collar, and she counted her breaths, and for the first time in eleven years the counting did not help. One two three. The number stayed. One two three. The number stayed, stamped now on the inside of her eyes the way the strobe leaves its ghost, and she understood without wanting to that she would carry it up off this quay and home and into the dark, and it would still be there in the morning.

Bram came up beside her and put a towel around her shoulders, the way he always did, warm from inside his own coat, and he did not look at the belts. He looked at her.

"Cold one," he said. "Go get in the van."

She went. But she had seen the number, and she could not make herself unsee it.

# Chapter Two — Ruling

The debrief room at Marineterrein had one window and it looked out on the harbour where the grey water met the grey sky with no line between them. Somewhere out there a barge was working; she could hear it more than see it, a low diesel patience under the double glazing. Sanne sat with her hair still damp at the roots and a paper cup of coffee going cold in her hands and listened to a room decide what it had already decided.

There were seven of them. Two from the recherche, one from the prosecutor's office — the Officier van Justitie sent a young one, a man in a good suit who checked his phone under the table — a police doctor, a press liaison who should not have been there, Bram, and Sanne. The heating ticked in the wall. The dead man had a name now. Victor Maas. Forty-one. A compliance clerk at a notary office in the Jordaan, a firm called Kanttekening Notariaat. He had not been reported missing. He lived alone. He had, someone said, a history.

"A history of what," Sanne said.

The young prosecutor looked up from his phone. "He'd been signed off work. Stress. There's a note in his file from the huisarts — low mood, that sort of thing." He said it the way you'd read a weather report, turning the page as he spoke. "It fits."

"Fits what."

"A man in a bad place who walks into a canal in January." He shrugged, a small clean movement that did not disturb the line of his jacket. "It happens. It happens more than people think."

"He was weighted," Sanne said.

The room did the thing rooms did. It went smooth, the way water goes smooth over something dropped into it, closing the surface. The police doctor, a heavy patient woman named Prins, folded her hands on the table. "People do weight themselves," she said, not unkindly. "It's not common but it's documented. Stones in pockets. Chains. A man who wants to be sure he won't lose his nerve and swim."

"With two dive belts. Cinched under the arms. From behind."

"We don't know from behind."

"I recovered him. I felt the buckles. They were dorsal — the release was at his spine, both of them. You can't reach your own spine and pull a two-belt release under water in the dark." She heard her own voice starting to climb and she took it back down, flatter, colder, because heat lost you the room and she had learned that a long time ago in rooms much like this one. "You physically cannot. Someone else put those on him. Someone who knew how to weight a body so it would stay down. That's not a man who wants to be sure. That's a man being made sure of."

The young prosecutor wrote nothing. The press liaison wrote something.

"Sanne." Bram's voice, from the end of the table, easy and warm, the voice he used on the boat when the weather turned. "Nobody's saying you're wrong about what you felt. You felt what you felt. But we don't build a case in a debrief, and we don't build it on the recovery diver's read of a buckle in zero vis." He smiled at her, and then at the room, spreading it around like a man spreading a coat over a puddle. "She's the best pair of hands I've got in the water. In the water. This part isn't hers."

It was said with such affection that it took her a second to feel it land. He had, in one soft sentence, moved her out of the room while leaving her in her chair. She looked at the cold coffee and did not answer, because there was no answer that would not sound like exactly the thing he had just, so gently, told them all she was. That was the trick of it, and she had felt it work on her before, in this same building, in a smaller room, two years ago: you could not argue against being cared for without seeming to prove you needed it. The kindness closed the door and stood in front of it, and you could not push past a man who was only trying to keep you warm.

The two from the recherche exchanged a look, brief, the look of colleagues who have worked together long enough to hold a whole conversation in a glance, and one of them started to talk about resourcing, about the caseload, about how the winter always did this — three floaters a month once the barges started their runs, and how each one ate a week you didn't have, a week of phone calls and next-of-kin and paperwork that led nowhere. Sanne understood then how it would go. She had watched it go this way before. She had watched it go this way exactly once before, and the memory of that time came up through the floor of her like cold through a drysuit seam, patient, personal, finding the same thin line along the throat.

She put her cold coffee down.

"There's a woman," said the recherche man, "coming in on it. Transfer from Rotterdam. Okonkwo. She'll pick it up, do the file, and if it's what it looks like—" he opened his hands, a small gesture of release "—it closes."

If it's what it looks like. Sanne knew what it looked like. It looked like a fast door closing on a slow question, and she had stood in front of that door before, and she had let it close, because the man who was closing it had put a towel around her shoulders warm from his own coat and told her to go get in the van.

The woman from Rotterdam was in the corridor when Sanne came out, reading the case tablet against the wall with the particular stillness of someone who has learned that standing still makes people forget you're there. She was younger than Sanne, mid-thirties, tall, with a tiredness in her that had nothing to do with the hour and everything to do with the years. She had not taken off her coat. She looked up.

"You're the diver," she said. Not a question.

"Sanne Kessler."

"Nadia Okonkwo." She didn't offer a hand; her hands were full of the tablet. She looked at Sanne the way Sanne looked at water, reading it for what was under it. "You said dorsal releases. In there."

"You were listening at the door."

"I was reading the door." She tapped the tablet with one knuckle. "It's already got a shape, this file. Misadventure. Somebody typed 'consistent with self-harm' before the man was dry." A thin, tired flick of the mouth that was not a smile and did not pretend to be. "I've been here four days. I don't have friends to spend yet. So." She lowered the tablet against her thigh. "Tell me why you're sure."

Sanne told her about the buckles. About the dorsal releases, both of them, riding the spine where no man could reach. About the neatness of him on his back in the mud, arms at his sides, when the water never left them neat. About the coat buttoned to the throat, and the office shoes, thin-soled, that no man wears to drown himself in a canal in the cold.

Nadia listened without writing, which Sanne respected. She had a way of holding still and letting the other person fill the silence, and when Sanne finished, the detective did not fill it herself, not at once. She let it sit. Then she said, "It's not enough."

"I know."

"A good defence eats every word of it. Zero visibility. No corroboration. A diver with—" she chose the word, turning it over first "—strong feelings." She held up a hand before Sanne could speak. "I'm telling you what they'll say, not what I think. What I think is I want to see the belts before somebody photographs them into a box and forgets them." She glanced back down the corridor toward the closed door of the debrief room. "Your supervisor. Verhoeven. He's very keen to keep you out of it."

"He's protecting me. It's what he does."

"Mm," said Nadia, and did not agree or disagree, and that small refusal — the sound a person makes instead of the word yes — stayed with Sanne longer than it should have. She thought about it on the tram home, turning it over. It's what he does. She had said it as a defence and it had come out sounding like an accusation, and she had not been able to take it back, because Nadia had already heard the shape of it, the way she herself heard the wrongness in a body's rest before she could name the wrong.

She did not sleep.

The houseboat was on the Kloveniersburgwal, a long low barge Daan had picked and loved past all sense, and at night the water threw the streetlight up onto the cabin ceiling in a slow moving grid that she had watched for two years and could not stop watching. The radiators knocked as they cooled. Daan had liked the water so much. It had seemed like a joke sometimes, a diver and a man who loved canals, him leaning on the rail of a bridge to look down while she stood back from the edge, and then it had stopped being a joke.

Daan Kessler had died in the Amstel two years ago, in March, in water not much warmer than this morning's. He had gone in off a friend's boat near the Magere Brug on a grey afternoon and he had not come up, and it had been Bram — Bram, who had been Daan's friend for twenty years before he was Sanne's supervisor for eleven — Bram who had gone down and found him and brought him up. A diving accident. Recreational. A regulator failure, they said, and a man alone in cold water, and the cold does what the cold does. The ruling had come fast. She remembered being grateful, at the time, for how fast it came, for how quickly they let her stop having to ask. Bram had handled everything. Bram had stood at the front of the crematorium and spoken about her husband in a voice that shook, and afterward he had driven her home and sat with her in the dark cabin while the water moved on the ceiling and he had said, you don't have to be anywhere, Sanne, you don't have to do anything, I've got the line.

She got up once in the night and stood at the window with her forehead against the cold glass and looked out at the canal, black and moving, the streetlight lying broken along it. A late tram went over the bridge and its sound came across the water muffled and faded, and the water took no notice, went on the way it went. She had loved this view once. She had stood here with Daan's arm around her and thought herself a lucky woman, warm in a cold city, a hull's thickness above the water she went down into for a living. She did not feel lucky now. She felt watched, though there was nothing to watch her but the canal, and the canal did not care.

She lay down again in that same light and did the arithmetic she did not want to do.

Daan had died on a Tuesday. The ruling — accident, closed, no further inquiry — had been entered on the Friday. Three days. She had thought, at the time, that this was mercy. She thought now that it was speed, and that speed was not the same thing as mercy, and that she had confused the two because she had wanted to. A grieving woman will take whatever hand is offered her out of the water. She does not ask, in the moment, whose hand it is.

Victor Maas had come out of the water this morning. She had heard the young prosecutor in his good suit already saying it fits. Already, before the man was dry, before his mother knew, before the mud was off him.

The same speed.

She turned onto her back and looked at the moving grid of light and counted her breaths in threes, one two three, one two three, and did not sleep, and somewhere below her the canal went on carrying the light up to her ceiling, patient, personal, the way it carried everything, the way it had carried her husband, the way it had carried a clerk named Victor Maas with two belts of police lead cinched at his spine by a hand that knew exactly how to make a thing stay down.

# Chapter Three — The Log

The dive shed at Zeeburg smelled of neoprene and salt and the particular chemical sweetness of the tank room, and at seven on a Sunday morning it was empty, which was why Sanne had come.

She let herself in with her own key. The lock was stiff in the cold and she worked it the way she always did, a lift and a turn, and the door gave with its familiar complaint. The base sat out on the water at the edge of the Marineterrein, low buildings and a floating dock and the masts of the harbour beyond, and the sleet had turned to a fine rain in the night and the rain came down on the corrugated roof in a soft continuous hush that filled the whole shed like breathing. She did not turn on the main lights. She went to the back, to the equipment room, and turned on the one bank of tubes over the gear racks, and they came up cold and blue-white and flickering, and she stood a moment letting the place settle around her, letting her eyes take the room.

The lead was racked along the far wall. Weight belts hung from a steel rail like a row of dark waiting things, each one tagged, each one stamped. This was the part civilians never understood about the work — how much of it was accounting. Every tank, every regulator, every metre of line, every kilo of lead was logged. Gear that could kill you was gear you kept count of. You signed it out and you signed it in, and if a set of weights did not come back you wrote down why, because the alternative was a diver reaching in the dark for lead that wasn't there, or reaching for lead that someone else had let go soft, and the water did not forgive an unkept account. She had been raised in the work on that discipline. Bram had raised her in it. Count everything. Trust the count.

The log lived where it had always lived, a fat ring binder on the shelf by the door beside the computer nobody used, because Bram was fifty-eight and did not trust the computer and kept the true record in his own square careful hand. Sanne took it down. It was heavier than it looked, swollen with years, the cover soft at the corners from handling, and she opened it on the bench under the light and the pages gave off the smell of a thing that has lived a long time in a damp room.

She had the serials. She had memorised them on the quay in the white of the strobe without deciding to, the way she memorised depths and headings, the way the numbers of the dead arranged themselves in her without permission. And she had written them on the inside of her wrist in the van, in ballpoint, pressing hard, because she did not trust even her own memory with a thing this heavy. Two belts. Two numbers.

She turned the pages slowly, running one finger down the return column, and she found the first serial on the fourth page she checked.

It was there in Bram's hand, signed out eighteen months ago, and beside it in the return column, instead of a date and a countersignature, three words.

Lost — Amstel exercise.

She looked at it for a long time. The rain went on above her. Somewhere in the building a pipe shuddered and was still.

The second serial took longer. It was on a different page, signed out on a different date, four months later, and the return column said the same thing in the same square hand, the letters small and even, a man's careful record of a small loss.

Lost — training. Not recovered.

Two team belts. Both signed out under Bram's name — as supervisor he signed out most things, that was nothing, that was procedure, half the entries on any page were in his hand. Both written off as lost. One of them lost, the log said, in the Amstel.

She stood in the cold room with the rain on the roof and made herself go slowly, because she knew what she wanted this to mean and wanting was how you drowned. Belts got lost. It happened. Lead came off in current, buckles failed, a diver in trouble dumped his weights to get to the surface and that was the whole point of a quick-release, you ditched the lead and you lived and the lead stayed on the bottom and you wrote it off. Two lost belts over eighteen months was not remarkable. In another unit, a busier one, it might not even be noticed; it might be a shrug and a signature and a requisition for more.

But lost belts stayed lost. That was the thing. That was the whole of it. Lead on the bottom of the Amstel stayed on the bottom of the Amstel. It did not climb out of the river and cross the city and cinch itself around the spine of a compliance clerk in the Kloveniersburgwal.

A civilian corpse was wearing two belts of police lead that the log said were lost. Written off. Gone. Which meant the log was wrong, or the log was a lie, and either way there was only one way for logged, written-off police weights to end up on a murdered man.

Someone with access to this room. Someone who could take a belt off the rail and write lost beside its number in careful square handwriting and walk out with the lead under his coat, and no one would ever count it again, because a lost thing is a thing no one counts.

Someone inside.

"You're in early."

She did not startle. She had trained a long time ago out of the reflex to startle, because underwater a startle killed you, cost you air, cost you the map. But something in her went very still and very cold, colder than the room, and she turned with the binder open in her hands and Bram was standing in the doorway in his old canvas jacket with two coffees, one in each hand, steam coming off them into the chilled air.

"I saw your bike," he said. "Thought you might want one of these." He came in and set a coffee on the bench, near her, not too near. He did not look at the binder. Or rather — he looked at it the way he had looked at the belts on the quay, which was to say he was very careful not to, and she knew that particular care, she had spent eleven years across a boat from it. "Couldn't sleep either?"

"No."

"No." He wrapped both hands around his own cup and leaned against the tank rack and looked at her with those warm tired eyes, and she felt, standing there, the full weight of eleven years of that look. The look that had tended her line in forty dives she should not have survived. The look that had sat with her in the dark cabin while the water moved on the ceiling. "You went and pulled the log."

There was no point in anything but the truth, or part of it. "The belts on Maas were ours. Team issue. I read the serials on the quay."

"I know they were ours." He said it easily, and it stopped her, that ease, the way a step that isn't there stops you. "I saw it too. I've already flagged it up the chain — where does a man like that get police lead, it's a proper question, and Okonkwo can chase it." He took a slow sip. "It's exactly the kind of thing that looks like a smoking gun and turns out to be a car boot sale. Half our old kit gets surplussed, Sanne. Sold off, donated, ends up in a diving club in Almere. Lead's lead. Someone buys a job lot of ex-police weights and years later—" he tipped the cup, spilling the thought out into the air between them. "It'll have an ordinary answer. They mostly do. You know that. Most of what looks like murder is just a sad man and cold water."

"The log says these two were lost. Not surplussed. Lost. In the water."

Something moved behind his face. It was very small and it was gone at once, folded back under the warmth before it had finished, but she had spent eleven years reading small movements in low light, on a heaving deck, through a fogged mask, and she saw it — a stillness, a fractional attention, the flicker of a man recalculating a sum he had thought was closed. Then he smiled, and the smile was sad, and the sadness was the worst of it because it was real, or looked so real she could not tell the false from the true, and that not-being-able-to-tell was itself a new cold in the room.

"You've been up all night," he said gently, "chasing two numbers in my handwriting, and now you're standing in a cold room at seven on a Sunday building a story out of them. I know you. I've watched you do this in the water — you get a wrongness in your head and you won't leave it, and mostly that's why you're the best I've got, because you don't leave things on the bottom." He set his cup down and looked at her, and his voice dropped, and it was kind, it was so kind. "But you're doing the thing you did after Daan. You're taking a bad morning and you're making it carry more than it can hold."

There it was. Daan's name, laid on the bench between them like a third belt.

"You buried him," Bram said, "and you never let yourself put it down, and I understand that, God knows I do, I was there, I brought him up—" his voice caught, or seemed to "—and I will not stand here and watch you tie yourself to another dead man and go down with him. Maas is not Daan. This is not that. Give it to Okonkwo. Let it be hers." He picked his coffee back up. The kindness settled over everything like silt settling after a diver passes, softening the shapes, covering what had been for a moment exposed. "Don't make this heavier than it is, Sanne."

She stood with the log open in her hands and the rain on the roof, and she looked at the man who had tended her line for eleven years, and she thought: heavier. He had chosen the word. Of all the words in the language, that one. A man does not reach for weight, for lead, for the language of things that hold you down, unless the water is already in his mind, unless he has been thinking, somewhere below his own knowing, about how much it takes to make a thing stay.

"You're right," she said. "I'm tired."

"You are." Relief moved through him, small and warm, easing his shoulders down from where they had been without his knowing it either. "Go home. Sleep. Let me close up."

She closed the binder and put it back on the shelf, exactly where it had been, square to the edge the way he liked things, spine out, the way she had watched him square it a thousand times. She picked up the coffee he had brought her and she drank some of it so he would see her drink it, and it was good coffee, and that was somehow the worst thing of all. She said something ordinary about the tide and he said something ordinary back, and she got her bike from the rack and rode home along the harbour in the fine grey rain.

But she had the two serials on the inside of her wrist, and she had seen the flicker behind his face, and she understood now — riding, counting her breaths in threes against the cold, one two three, one two three, the rain fine as breath on her face — that the thing she could not put down was not too heavy for her.

It was exactly as heavy as a man who did not want it lifted.

# Chapter Four — Nadia

The café was on the Nieuwmarkt, a brown bar with fogged windows and a floor of old tile, and from the table in the back corner Sanne could see the Waag across the square, its towers dark against a sky the colour of wet ash. The windows ran with condensation and the room smelled of coffee and wet wool and the faint tin of the radiator. She had chosen the seat without thinking and only afterward understood why: from here she could watch the water where they had brought Victor Maas up, forty metres away, under the place where they used to open the dead to learn how they were made.

Nadia Okonkwo came in shaking rain off her coat and sat down across from her and did not take the coat off, which told Sanne she meant this to be short.

"Off the record," Nadia said. "You said off the record on the phone. I want to be clear I heard you. Because I don't have an on-the-record yet, and if I did, this—" she gestured between them, the table, the two cups the waiter had not yet brought "—wouldn't be it. I don't meet witnesses in cafés. So whatever this is, it's two people talking."

"I understand."

"Then tell me why I'm drinking bad coffee on my day off."

Sanne turned her wrist over on the table. The two serials were there in ballpoint, faded now, half sweated away, the skin around them chafed pink from where she had washed and not been able to help scrubbing at them, but legible. She had copied them onto a card as well, in the night, in a steadier hand; she slid the card across the tile.

"The belts on Maas," she said. "Both team issue. I read the serials on the quay when SOCO strobed them. This morning I checked them against our equipment log at the base." She kept her voice flat, the debrief voice, the voice that had lost her the room once and would not lose her this one. "Both belts are logged. Both were signed out and then written off as lost. One of them was recorded lost in the Amstel. The other in a training exercise. Neither was ever surplussed, sold, or recovered. According to our own paper, both of those belts are on the bottom of a river."

Nadia looked at the card. She did not pick it up. "Belts get written off."

"They do."

"Lead comes off in current. Divers ditch weights to surface. Kit gets sold as surplus and turns up years later in a club in Almere." She was, Sanne realised, quoting the counterargument to herself, running it the way Sanne had run it in the cold room, the way she had heard Bram run it that morning over the steam of two coffees, testing it aloud for where it held and where it gave. "There are a dozen boring ways police lead ends up in the world."

"Surplus lead has a paper trail. Sold, dated, signed. These weren't sold. These were lost — and lost gear stays lost. It sits on the bottom. It doesn't come back up cinched around a man's spine with a dorsal release he couldn't reach." Sanne leaned in a fraction, no more. "For these two specific belts to be on Victor Maas, someone had to take them out of our racks, write 'lost' beside their numbers, and keep them. Someone with a key to that room. Someone who knew how to weight a body so it would stay down and who had a reason to reach for gear that the paperwork already said was gone. You don't write a thing off and then use it unless you needed it to be a thing nobody would ever count."

The café made its small warm noises around them, the hiss of the machine, the scrape of a chair, the rain steady on the glass. Nadia was quiet for a long time. She looked at the card and out the fogged window at the square and back at the card. Then she picked it up, finally, and turned it in her fingers, and Sanne watched the detective's tiredness sharpen into something else, something with an edge under it, because the detail was too clean to walk away from and they both knew it. A defence could eat a diver's feeling about a buckle. A defence could not so easily eat a serial number written into a police log in a supervisor's own hand.

"This is very tidy," Nadia said, "for a suicide."

"Yes."

"Suicides aren't tidy. That's the whole trouble with them, they're never tidy, they're the messiest thing I see. People think a decision that size makes a clean shape. It doesn't. It leaves the stove on and half a letter and one shoe by the door." She set the card down and pressed it flat under one finger, as if it might otherwise lift. "All right. I'm not saying you have anything. You don't. Two serials and a story is not a case. But it's a thread, and it's a thread that goes into your own shed, which is exactly the kind of thread everybody in that debrief would like me not to pull." She almost smiled, the tired flick again. "Which is a good reason to pull it."

"Then pull it from Maas's end," Sanne said. "Not the shed. Not yet." She had thought about this, riding home in the rain with the two numbers on her wrist, and she had decided the belts were the wrong door to go through first, because the belts led back into a building full of people who had watched a fast ruling written on her husband once already, people who closed smooth over a dropped stone. "Find out who he was. A compliance clerk doesn't get weighted with police lead for stress leave. Somebody did that to him. So somebody had a reason, and the reason is in his life, not on the bottom of the Kloveniersburgwal. Go at the life. The belts will still be there when you get back to them."

Nadia took out a notebook — a real one, paper, its corners furred — which Sanne liked her for. "Victor Maas. Forty-one. Kanttekening Notariaat, in the Jordaan. Compliance." She wrote it and looked up. "You know the firm?"

"No." Sanne heard herself say it and then heard herself go on, because it was true and because Nadia would find it anyway and better it come from her than from a file. "But I know the world. My husband worked compliance. Not at a notary — at a firm that did work for them. Anti-fraud, anti-money-laundering, the checks. He used to talk about it at the table, more than he should have, probably." She stopped. The words compliance and notaris sat in her mouth with a strange weight, an old weight, the weight of a man's voice across a kitchen two years dead, a man gesturing with a fork, warming to it. "He said the notary offices were where the money went to get clean. That a bent notaris could make anything look legal — a sale, a transfer, a company built to hold nothing but somebody else's money, all of it signed and stamped and true on paper. He said the whole city ran on it and nobody looked, because a notaris was a person you trusted by definition. That's the job. That's the whole job. To be trusted."

Nadia had stopped writing. "Your husband. He's—"

"Dead. Two years." Sanne kept her eyes on the Waag across the square, the wet stone of it, the towers. "A diving accident. In the Amstel. It was ruled fast, the way this is being ruled fast." She made herself say the rest of it flatly, the way you reported a depth, a heading, a fact into a comms line. "He was a compliance man who died in the water and got a quick clean ruling. Now there's a compliance man who died in the water and he's getting a quick clean ruling. And the belts that held the second one down are logged as lost in the same river that took the first."

She had not meant to lay it out like that. She had meant to keep Daan out of it, the way Bram had told her to, the way the sensible part of her knew she should — because the moment she tied the two together in front of a detective she became what the room already thought she was, a widow chasing a ghost through another man's death, a woman making a bad morning carry more than it could hold. But it was out now, on the tile between them beside the card, and she watched Nadia not dismiss it. That was all. That was everything. Nadia did not lean back, did not soften her face into the careful patience people used on the bereaved, the patience that is its own kind of door closing. She sat with it. She let it be heavy, and she did not set it down.

"Two years ago," Nadia said slowly. "The Amstel. What was his name."

"Daan Kessler."

Nadia wrote it under Victor Maas, and drew a line between the two names, a single deliberate stroke of the pen, and Sanne looked at the line and felt something in her chest that was not grief exactly, or not only grief — it was the cold that comes when a shape you have refused to see finishes assembling itself in the dark, the feeling of your glove finding a shoulder in black water where there should be nothing but silt, the give of it, wrong, too soft under fabric.

"I'm not promising you anything," Nadia said. "I want to be very clear. I look at Maas. I find out who he was and who wanted him gone. If your husband's in that water too, I'll see it, but I'm not going in looking for him, because that's how you find things that aren't there. You go in wanting a shape and the dark gives you the shape you brought." She closed the notebook on the line she had drawn. "But I'm going to look at Kanttekening. And I'm going to want that log."

"You'll have it."

Nadia stood, and pulled her coat closer, and paused with one hand on the back of the chair, and the pause was deliberate, the pause of someone deciding whether to ask a last thing. "Your supervisor," she said. "Verhoeven. Does he know you copied those numbers?"

Sanne thought of the flicker behind Bram's face in the cold room. The two coffees, one set down near her, not too near. Don't make this heavier than it is, Sanne. She thought about the eleven years of the hand on her line, the small pulls in the dark that meant I'm here; the towel warm from his own coat; the voice that shook at the crematorium, or seemed to shake. And she found she could not make the sentence come. Not yet. It was too large and too thin at once, a suspicion with no floor under it, and if she said it aloud to this careful tired woman she would be doing to Bram exactly what the room had done to her — writing a ruling before the man was dry.

"He knows I looked at the log," she said. "He thinks it's grief."

"Is it?"

"Ask me in a week."

Nadia held her eyes a moment, then nodded, once, the nod of a person filing something rather than accepting it, and went out into the rain, and the door swung shut and let in a breath of cold off the square before it settled.

Sanne stayed. She finished the bad coffee and watched the Waag go from grey to a darker grey as the afternoon failed, the old weigh house where they had once cut the dead open on a marble table with the whole city watching from the tiered dark, to learn how a body was made, to learn what it held and what it weighed. Forty metres away the black water lay flat under the rain where Victor Maas had waited for her, weighted with lead that the paper said was lost in the river that had taken her husband.

She had spent two years being grateful for a fast ruling. She would not be grateful again.

She turned her wrist over and looked at the two fading numbers on her skin, and she counted her breaths in threes, one two three, one two three, and this time the counting held, steady as a hand on a line, because for the first time since March two years ago she was going back down the line on purpose, into the cold, toward the shape she had been refusing to touch — and someone she had trusted with her life for eleven years was still, whether he knew it yet or not, holding the other end of her rope.

# Chapter Five — The Room

Victor Maas had lived on the third floor of a narrow house on the Nieuwe Prinsengracht, up a staircase built for people who owned less than he did. Sanne climbed behind Nadia, one hand on the rope banister, counting the treads out of habit. Twenty-one. The building smelled of other people's cooking and old varnish, and under it, faint, the smell every old Amsterdam house carried in January — damp brick and the cold coming up through the foundations from the water below.

Nadia had a key from the huisbaas and a card that got her past the questions. She'd told Sanne to come as a civilian, which meant hands in pockets and mouth shut, and Sanne had agreed because the alternative was sitting at home with the belts in her head. She had not slept well. She had not slept well in a week, since the recovery, since the weights had come up out of the Kloveniersburgwal with a body wrong-side against gravity, and she had learned not to fight the not-sleeping. You let the tiredness sit in you like ballast. You used it to go down.

The stairwell had a small window on each landing, painted shut, and through the last one she could see the canal, flat and brown under a sky the colour of a wet stone. A tram bell somewhere. A bicycle chained to a railing with three others, all of them beaded with the morning's damp. Ordinary Tuesday. A man had died and the city had not paused for it, which was the city's habit and, on most days, its mercy.

The door opened onto order.

That was the first thing. Not the poverty of it, not the smallness — the order. A single room with a kitchen along one wall, a bed made tight, a desk under the window that looked out over the canal. Books shelved by height. A row of shoes lined up by the door, toes to the wall, the way you leave them when you might need to go quickly and don't want to search. A single mug upturned on a folded cloth by the sink, dry. No dishes waiting. No coat thrown over a chair. Sanne noticed all of it and said nothing.

"He lived alone," Nadia said. It wasn't a question.

"He lived carefully."

Nadia pulled on gloves and handed Sanne a pair. "You're not touching anything. But if you see it before I do, tell me."

Sanne put the gloves on. The latex was cold and smelled of the box. She flexed her fingers into it the way she flexed them into wet gloves on the boat, a small ritual of getting ready to work with her hands in a place that did not want her.

The room told her things in the language she knew. A man who kept his shoes at the door and his bed hard and his books in rank was a man who managed fear by managing objects. She'd done it herself, the year after Daan. You could not control the water so you controlled the drawer. There was a smoke detector on the ceiling and a second one, newer, screwed into the wall by the bed at eye height, which no ordinary tenant does. There was a wooden wedge on the floor by the door, the kind you kick under to stop it opening. It had been used. The paint on the door edge was scarred where the wedge bit — not once, but a pale worried patch of scarring, weeks of it, months.

"He was frightened," Sanne said.

"People are frightened of a lot of things."

"He was frightened of the door."

Nadia looked at the wedge, then at the door, then wrote something in her notebook. She had a way of receiving a thing without agreeing to it that Sanne had decided she trusted. She was younger than Sanne by a few years and wore it lightly, a woman who had learned to make her face a closed drawer, and only her hands, quick and economical, gave away that she was working fast behind it.

"How long had he lived here?" Sanne asked.

"Eleven months, the huisbaas said. Paid a year up front, in the account, all correct." Nadia crouched by the door, level with the wedge, not touching it. "Kept himself to himself. Nobody knew him. The neighbour below thought he might be a teacher." She stood. "A man pays a year in advance and screws a second smoke detector into the wall at eye level and files down the doorframe with a wooden wedge, and nobody knows him. That's not a private man. That's a man in hiding who's decided hiding looks like privacy."

"It works until it doesn't."

"It always works until it doesn't." Nadia said it without weight, a thing she'd learned and put away and taken out only when it fit, and it fit here, in this careful room above the cold canal.

Sanne took the room in threes. In. Hold. Out. It was how she read a wreck — not all at once, which drowned you, but in measured passes, letting each breath carry back one true thing. First pass: the shoes, the wedge, the two smoke detectors. Second pass: the window locks, all engaged, on the third floor, above a canal. Third pass: the absence. No photographs on the walls. No cards on the shelf. No evidence anyone had ever been let in.

The desk held a laptop, closed. Nadia opened it with one finger. It woke to a login screen and, when she pressed a key twice more, to a message in white on black that meant the disk had been wiped — not deleted, wiped, the difference between throwing a letter away and burning the house it was written in.

"Clean," Nadia said. "Professionally. Someone came here."

"Or he did it himself."

"Or he did it himself." She closed the lid. "Either way there's nothing on it."

Sanne stood at the window. The canal below was flat and brown and held the houses upside down in it, gable by gable, a second Amsterdam that trembled when a bike went past and then stood still again. A coot worked its way along the far bank, unhurried. She thought about how a careful man protects the thing he is afraid of losing. Not on the machine they'll take. Somewhere else. Somewhere stupid and safe.

"He mailed things to himself," she said.

Nadia turned. "What?"

"Daan did it." She heard her own voice go level the way it did when she said his name. "Not — the same thing. But. He was a compliance man too. When he found something he didn't trust to a file he'd email it to himself. To an address nobody knew about. He said paper burns and servers get subpoenaed but a man's own outbox is just noise until you know to look." She stopped. "He said a lot of things like that."

Nadia was watching her now with a different weight. "You never told me your husband did this work."

"You never asked what he did."

"I'm asking."

"Compliance. Fraud, mostly. At a notary office." Sanne kept her eyes on the water. "He drowned in the Amstel two years ago. They called it a diving accident. A recreational dive. He was diving alone, which he never did, and his weight belt was wrong, which it never was." She turned from the window. "That's why I'm standing in your murder victim's flat with gloves on. You should know that before we go further, so you can decide if I'm useful or just broken."

Nadia didn't answer for a moment. Outside a gull complained about something and another answered it. A boat passed and the room's light shifted, the reflection off the canal moving across the ceiling and away.

"Both can be true," she said finally. "Show me the email thing."

It took an hour and it wasn't on the laptop. It was in Victor's own head, which was gone, so they worked backward from the room.

They worked the way Sanne worked a search grid, methodical, overlapping, nothing skipped because it looked empty. Nadia took the drawers and Sanne took the shelves, reading spines, tilting each book by the top edge to feel for anything laid flat behind it. Nothing. The books were only books — Dutch history, a run of them on the seventeenth century, on trade and the guilds, on the merchant families who'd built these canals; a shelf of dry professional texts, tax law, property law, the notarial code; and one small worn Bible, boat-sized, the kind you keep because a grandmother gave it to you and not because you read it. But a careful man, she thought, does not hide a thing in a book, because a book is the first place a careful man knows they'll look.

The room got colder as they worked, or she got colder standing still in it, the January damp coming up out of the walls the way it came up out of everything in this city built on water and driven piles. She kept her breathing even. She had learned, over two years, that a room where a frightened person had lived held the fear after they were gone the way a wetsuit holds the shape of the last diver — you could feel where a man had pressed himself against his own life. Victor had pressed himself into these forty square metres and screwed a second alarm to the wall and slept behind a wedged door, and it had not saved him, because the thing that came for him had not come through the door. It had come through the water, the same as it came for Daan, though she did not know that yet, standing there; she only felt the room's held fear and mistook it, for a moment, for her own.

Nadia found the phone bill in a drawer, folded in thirds, and a second bill under it for a webmail service Victor paid for annually, in cash, by voucher — a provider in Iceland that answered to no Dutch warrant and advertised exactly that. There was a username printed on the receipt. There was no password anywhere, and Nadia said passwords never were, and Sanne, looking at the row of books by height and the shoes by the door, said try the boat.

"What boat?"

"He grew up on a boat. Look." Sanne pointed without touching: a photograph on the shelf, curled at one corner, the only photograph in the room, a boy on the deck of a small cruiser, a woman's hand on his shoulder, a name painted on the transom behind them. *Aaltje.* "A careful man uses the one word he'll never forget and never write down anywhere it means anything."

"You'd be surprised what people write down."

"Not him." Sanne looked at the shoes, the wedge, the hard-made bed. "Not a man who lived like this."

Nadia typed it on her own phone, into the Iceland provider's login. It took the boat's name and a year Sanne guessed from the boy's age and a third try with the two joined, and then the screen filled with a mailbox, and the mailbox was almost empty, because Victor had been careful there too. He'd deleted as he went. But he'd been human, and humans miss the drafts folder, and in the drafts folder were nine messages he had written to himself and never sent, because sending leaves a line and saving doesn't.

Nadia read them standing up and didn't share for a long minute, and Sanne let her, watching the detective's face do the small work of a professional not letting her own face do anything. Somewhere below a door slammed and feet went down toward the street, a neighbour going out into the cold, a life continuing above the dead man's ceiling.

"Addresses," Nadia said at last. "It's a list of addresses."

"Of what?"

"Houses." She scrolled. "Canal houses. Grachtenpanden. Same six, over and over, in every draft, with numbers next to them. Two numbers each." She turned the phone so Sanne could see, and Sanne read the first line and felt something in her chest reset itself to a colder setting.

Herengracht. Then a figure. Then, in brackets, a second figure less than half the first.

"That's a price," Sanne said.

"Two prices."

"What a house sold for. And what it's worth." She didn't know how she knew that; she knew it the way you know the shape of a thing you've been circling in the dark, the way you know there's a wall ahead of you in black water before your hand finds it. "Daan used to say the same word for it. He'd tap the table. He said the whole trick is the gap. Everything's in the gap."

Nadia looked at her, and then at the six addresses, and then she took a photograph of the screen and one of the receipt and one of the boy on the boat, and she said, quiet, the way you'd close a door on a sleeping child:

"Then tomorrow we go and stand in the gap."

# Chapter Six — The Houses

The first house was on the Herengracht and it was beautiful and no one lived in it.

They knew that by ten in the morning. Nadia had parked the department car three bridges down and they walked, because a walking woman is furniture in this city and a parked car is a question. The cold had come down hard overnight, the first real cold of the year, and the canal had begun to skin at its edges where the water lay still against the stone, a thin grey rind of ice that a moorhen broke and re-broke as it fed. Their breath went ahead of them. Sanne had dressed for standing still outdoors, which was its own discipline, layered the way she layered under a drysuit, and still the cold found the seams.

The gable was a neck-gable, restored, the hoisting beam at the top freshly oiled and useless, the windows tall and clean and behind them nothing — no curtains, no plants, no coats on hooks, no single object that says a life has agreed to happen here. A brass plate by the door said nothing at all. The doormat was still in its plastic.

Sanne stood on the far side of the water and looked at the house the way she looked at a wreck before she went down to it: reading the surface for what the depth was hiding. Freshly pointed brick. New lead on the roof, still bright at the folds. A skip permit taped inside the ground-floor glass, three months expired, curling at its corners. Someone had spent a great deal of money making this house perfect and then had walked away and left it perfect and empty.

"Sold last year," Nadia said, reading from her phone, from the Kadaster, the public land registry that recorded every Dutch transfer in dry official Dutch. "For four point one million. Before that, sold eighteen months earlier for one point nine. Renovated in between, they'll say." She scrolled, her thumb leaving small ghosts on the cold glass. "Sold again four months ago. Four point four. Buyer's a company. The company's owner is another company."

"And Victor's number?"

"Victor's number says it's worth two." She looked up at the gable. "He wrote the real value next to it. Every time. Real value, recorded value. The recorded one climbs the ladder and the real one just stands here on the pavement being a house."

Sanne understood it then, fully, the way cold finds a seam: the house was not a house. It was a receipt. You bought it with money that had a smell, you sold it to yourself through a company you also owned, and each time it changed hands the paper said it was worth more, and the paper was signed and stamped and true in the only sense the law cared about, and at the end you had four million clean euros and a story about a renovation to explain them. The house never had to be lived in. Living in it would only wear it out.

"Six of these," she said.

"Six that Victor found. Probably more he didn't."

"Whose money?" Sanne asked. She was still looking at the house, at the skip permit, at the new lead bright at the folds of the roof.

"That's the whole question, isn't it." Nadia put her phone away, into a coat that was department-issue and warm and gave nothing away either. "Money that can't be what it is. Drugs, most of it, in this city, though it doesn't have to be. It comes off the street dirty and it can't go in a bank as what it is, so it has to become something else. A house is good. A house sits still. A house has a story people already believe about it — it goes up in value, everyone knows that, nobody's surprised when a canal house doubles. You just help it double on paper, and you own the paper, and now your dirty two million is a clean four." She looked up at the gable. "And the beautiful part is the house doesn't have to do anything. It just stands here and lets you lie about it."

"Daan called it ironing," Sanne said. "You run the dirty thing through something respectable enough to press it flat." She had not thought of that word in two years and it came up whole, in his voice, and she let it come and let it go.

They walked the rest. The city went about its morning around them — a delivery man with a hand-truck cursing the cobbles, two schoolchildren doubled on one bike, a woman washing a step that did not need washing because washing the step was what you did. Ordinary. And threaded through the ordinary, one after another, the empty perfect houses, each one a held breath.

The second was on the Keizersgracht and had scaffolding up and two Polish men on it who didn't look at them, sanding a window frame with the patience of men paid by the hour and not the result. The third, on the Reguliersgracht, was already perfect and empty and had a small tasteful sign in the window, a name in grey capitals over a phone number: *SLOOTWEG VASTGOED.* Property. Real estate. The fourth was the same sign again. So was the sixth.

"One agent," Nadia said. "Same name on three of them."

"Selling houses nobody buys to live in."

"Selling them very well."

Slootweg. Sanne turned the name over. It meant nothing to her, or nearly nothing — a common enough name, a canal-quarter name — and then a small thing surfaced and she held it up to the light and could not make it hold still. She had seen it somewhere before. Recently. She could not place it and did not force it; forcing a half-memory only drove it back down. She let it go and kept walking and trusted it to rise on its own, the way a thing rose when the weight came off it.

They kept their pace even and their voices low, two women out walking, and Sanne logged each house the way she logged a dive — depth, hazard, exit. Where the doors were. Where a car could wait. How many windows looked onto the water and how many of those had anyone behind them, which was none. The canals of the ring were the oldest planned water in the country, dug in rings three and a half centuries ago by men who understood money the way divers understand air, and the houses that lined them had been about money from the first brick — wide fronts for rich men, narrow for poor, the whole street a ledger you could read from a boat. Nothing had changed but the century. The houses still told you what they were worth. It was only that now some of them lied. The Reguliersgracht was the prettiest of the canals, the one on the postcards, seven bridges lining up down its length when the light was right, and this morning the light was not right, only the flat grey coming off a sky that had not decided to snow. The bridges receded into it, arch after arch, and under them the empty houses stood in their reflections.

They came back along the Reguliersgracht at the end of it, past the third house, and a man was standing on the step now who had not been there before. He was locking the door, or unlocking it, and he did the small human thing of turning to see who was passing, and then he did a slightly larger thing, which was to smile at them as though he had been expecting exactly these two women all his life.

"You're admiring the house," he said, in English first, then, reading them again, in Dutch. "Everyone does. It's the proportions. 1671. The width is the whole secret — the wider the front, the richer the man who built it. This one paid tax on his vanity for three hundred years." He came down one step. He was perhaps fifty, silver at the temples, a coat that had cost what Sanne earned in a month and hung as though it had been grown rather than made. He wore no gloves in the cold, which she noticed, a man who wanted his hands seen, well kept, unhurried. "Ruud Slootweg. Are you looking? I can always tell when someone's looking."

"Just walking," Nadia said.

"Nobody just walks past this water. They walk to it." He had a way of including you in a joke you hadn't made. His eyes went over Sanne, unhurried, professional, the way a man reads a room he's about to sell. "You have a directness. Rotterdam?"

Nadia said nothing, which was an answer.

"I like Rotterdam. They knock things down there when they're finished with them. Here we're too sentimental. We keep the corpse and call it heritage." He smiled to take the edge off, and there was an edge, and the smile did not take it off; it only announced that he knew it was there and had decided to allow it. "The house is available, if you'd like to see inside. Though I warn you, it's empty. People find that disappointing. They want a fire in the grate. I tell them an empty house is honest. It's not pretending to be anyone's home."

Behind him, through the tall clean glass, Sanne could see the emptiness he was selling: bare floorboards sanded pale, walls the flat white of a gallery between shows, a staircase turning up into grey light and nothing at the top of it. A beautiful box. A place money went to change its name.

"How much?" Sanne asked. It was the first thing she'd said.

He turned the full weight of himself onto her, and it was warm, and it was a searchlight. "Four and a half. For you, I'd find a reason it was less." A beat, held a moment too long to be only charm. "Do you dive?"

The water went very quiet around Sanne. She did not move. She had a diver's stillness, taught by years of hanging on a line doing nothing while your air ran down, and she used it now, and it cost her something to use it.

"Your hands," he said, gesturing, easy. "The little scars on the knuckles. My brother sails. Sailors and divers have the same hands — the sea keeps writing on them." He was already stepping back up, already elsewhere, the way a man leaves a conversation he has decided he has won. "It's a small city. Everyone's hands say something. I hope you find whatever you're walking to." He put his key in the lock. "The water's cold this time of year. Even for people who like it cold."

He went inside and shut the door of the empty house, and through the tall clean glass they watched him not turn on a light, because there was no reason to, because no one lived there and no one ever would. He crossed the bare floor once, unhurried, a dark shape moving through the pale room, and then a doorway took him and the house was empty again the way it was meant to be.

Nadia let out a breath that fogged and thinned. "He clocked you."

"He clocked both of us." Sanne looked at her own knuckles, the small white history there, the scars a wreck-diver collects on steel and shell, and thought that a man who reads hands for a living had read hers and decided to tell her he'd read them. That wasn't charm. Charm was the wrapping. "He wanted us to know he knew. That's a message."

"To who?"

"To whoever he calls when we're gone."

They walked back the way they'd come, unhurried too, because to hurry now was to answer him. The cold had deepened while they stood. The ice at the canal's edge had widened by a hand's breadth. Behind the glass the house stood perfect and lit only by the grey afternoon, holding the canal upside down in its windows the way all of them did, and Sanne thought about the gap — real value, recorded value — and about a compliance clerk who'd written six addresses to himself nine times and then been fitted with team-issue belts and dropped into the cold, and she understood that she and Nadia had just walked, in the middle of a working morning, straight up to the edge of the same gap Daan had fallen into, and smiled at the man standing on the far side of it.

At the corner she stopped and looked back once. The Reguliersgracht ran away behind them, bridge after bridge into the grey, and every house on it was somebody's home except the ones that were nobody's, and from here you could not tell which was which, which was the point, which had always been the point.

# Chapter Seven — Daan's Shadow

The box had been in the hall cupboard for two years, behind the vacuum cleaner and the folded drying rack, in the exact place you put a thing when you cannot throw it away and cannot look at it either.

Sanne took it down at eleven at night because she had not been able to sleep and because standing in the empty house on the Reguliersgracht had loosened something she'd spent two years tightening. She carried it to the kitchen table of the houseboat and set it under the one good light and sat in front of it with her hands flat on the lid, counting her breaths in threes, the way she did on the line before she went under. In. Hold. Out. The boat moved a little under her, the way it always did, patient, personal, the water shifting its weight beneath the hull as some late barge passed unseen out on the main channel and sent its wake in under all the moored boats one by one.

She'd packed the box herself. She remembered doing it and remembered almost nothing about it, which was its own kind of information. Grief had come down over that week like silt over a wreck and she'd worked inside it blind, putting Daan into cardboard by feel. People had come and gone on the boat, brought food, taken it away uneaten, said the things, and she had stood in the middle of it packing a man into a box with her hands while her head stayed somewhere out on the Amstel, on the surface, looking down at where they'd found him.

She took the lid off.

His reading glasses. She had forgotten the exact shape of them and now they undid her for a moment, the small oval lenses, one arm mended with a sleeve of heat-shrink because he refused to buy new ones while the old ones held. She had teased him for it. He had said a thing works until it stops working and not a day before, and had gone on wearing them, and here they were, still holding, and he was not. His watch, stopped, the date wheel halted on a number that meant nothing now. A paperback swollen from a beach, a crime novel he'd read on the Greek trip and laughed at for getting everything wrong. His work lanyard, KANTTEKENING NOTARIAAT in blue, his photograph on the card looking mildly annoyed to be photographed, which he had been. She laid each thing on the table in a row, spaced even, and only when she'd done it did she hear Victor's shoes by the door in her head, toes to the wall, and understand that she and a dead stranger arranged their fear the same way, the way a diver lays out kit before a dive so that his hands can find each piece in the dark.

She let herself sit with the glasses a moment longer than the rest. Then she set them down. Grief was a current; you did not swim against it and you did not let it take you; you ferried across it at an angle, using it, keeping your eyes on the far bank. She had learned that the hard way and she used it now.

Under the personal things were the papers.

She had known they'd be there. She had put them there. She had not read them then — could not, the words swimming, the coroner's fast clean sentences about *misadventure* and *recreational dive* and *equipment consistent with*, which she had signed for and shelved because to argue was to keep him dying. She'd been a diver eleven years and she had let a report tell her that her husband, who checked her gear and she checked his, who logged every dive in a hand like a monk's, had gone into the Amstel alone with a belt he'd loaded wrong.

She read them now.

Not the coroner's. Daan's own. A folder he'd kept at home, which the police had glanced at and returned because it was only work, only a compliance man's dull notes, and grief had swallowed it whole for two years. It had sat in the box under his glasses the whole time, patient as the boat, waiting for her to be able to look.

His handwriting.

That was the blow, and she took it standing, the way you take cold water — all at once, no gasping. Small, upright, level, the letters not touching, a hand that never hurried. She had read a thousand notes in this hand. Shopping lists. *Back by seven.* *Feed Joke's cat, she's in Antwerp.* And now this, the same hand, cool and exact, laying out the last thing he ever worked on. Lists. A column of addresses down the left of a page, and beside each a figure, and beside that a second figure in brackets, less than half.

Herengracht. Keizersgracht. Reguliersgracht.

The same houses.

Not all six — four of the six. Two years before Victor wrote them into an unsent email, Daan had written them here, in pencil, in his monk's hand, with the same two numbers beside each, the real and the recorded, the gap. He'd drawn a small box around the word *waardering.* Valuation. He'd underlined it once. Daan underlined things once; a second line meant he was certain, and there were pages further in with second lines, and Sanne made herself go slowly, breath in threes, because the thing she was walking toward she had been walking toward since the belts came up out of the Kloveniersburgwal and she wanted to arrive at it upright.

The boat knocked once against the mooring and settled. Out on the water a coot called and stopped. She turned a page.

She knew this office. Kanttekening Notariaat, on a quiet stretch near the Herengracht, a brass plate and a heavy door and a waiting room with good chairs, where Daan had gone every weekday for six years to read other people's contracts for the lie in them. He had liked the work and mistrusted the place, which was a thing she had understood only in pieces, over dinners, over the small complaints a person brings home and sets down like keys. *Something isn't right there and I can't yet say what.* She had heard it and half-heard it, the way you hear a partner's work, the way she supposed he had half-heard hers. She had not known it was this. She had not known it was worth dying over. He had not told her, and she saw now, in the level pencil, why: *not through work — through someone I trust.* He had been protecting her by keeping her outside it. He had stood between her and the water and she had not even known there was water, and he had gone into it alone.

A name recurred. Not Slootweg — Slootweg was there, once, in a margin, a question mark after it, the way you note a face you don't yet have a place for. The name that recurred, that Daan had written and boxed and come back to, was the office and the man who ran it.

*Aalving. C. Aalving. — the signature is always his. Everything passes his desk. Nothing this size moves without the notary.*

And below, later, the pencil pressed harder, the letters standing up off the page:

*The notary doesn't launder the money. The notary launders the paper. He makes the lie legal. That's what a notary is for — to make a thing true because he says it's true. He's not hiding it. He's certifying it.*

Sanne sat with that. She could hear him saying it. He'd had a way of getting quiet and precise when a thing angered him, the anger going down into the words instead of up into his voice, and she could hear exactly the flatness he'd have said this in, tapping the table once on *certifying.* Outside, the canal knocked once against the hull and was quiet. Joke's boat two moorings down showed a single lit window and then didn't, Joke going to bed, the old woman's night ending as Sanne's cracked open.

She turned the last pages the way you clear the last few metres to a body you already know is there — slowly, hands steady, eyes forward, because the worst thing you can do at the end of a search is rush the last of it and stir the silt and lose sight of what you came for. Daan's notes stopped being about houses and started being about who to tell. He'd written and crossed out a name at the police. He'd written *not through work — through someone I trust.* And then, near the end, in a hand very slightly less level than the rest, the only place in all those pages where his writing showed the smallest tremor:

*Told B. today. He knew Kanttekening from years back — said he'd help me take it up the right way, quietly, so it doesn't get buried. Relief to say it out loud to someone who understands the water this all sits in. B says give him the weekend.*

That was the last dated line. The date was four days before Daan drowned.

Sanne read it three times. She did not yet let the initial become a name. She had trained herself, over two years, not to let grief supply facts, because grief was a generous witness and lied to comfort you. It would hand you a face and swear to it. It had handed her a dozen small guilts over two years, each one certain, each one false — that she should have gone with him, that she should have checked his gear, that she had heard him leave that morning and had not got up. *B* could be anyone. Amsterdam was full of B's. She wrote it down on a clean sheet in her own hand, *B — knew Kanttekening — 4 days before*, and set the pen down and found her hands were steady, and that steadiness was the coldest thing in the room.

Because the floor had already gone. It had gone somewhere in the middle of the pages and she'd kept reading across the space where it had been, the way you keep finning forward over a drop-off before your eyes understand the bottom has fallen away beneath you into black. Daan had not loaded his belt wrong. Daan had found exactly what Victor found, two years earlier, four houses of the same six, the same notary, the same gap — and then he had told someone, someone he trusted, someone who understood the water, and four days later he had gone into the Amstel alone and not come out, and the department had ruled it in a week.

The same as Victor. The same speed. The same water. The same weights.

She had not drowned him. But she had spent two years believing the water had, and letting the men who ruled on it tell her so, and standing at his grave apologising to him in her head for not checking his gear that morning. All that apologising. All that guilt, carried down and worn smooth like a stone in a pocket. And it had been the wrong grief the whole time, grief for an accident that was not an accident, and under it, unknown to her, the real thing had been waiting, patient as everything else on this boat, for her to be able to look.

She put her palm flat on the pencil column of addresses, on his level, patient handwriting, and she stayed like that for a long time while the boat moved under her. She did not cry. That would come or it would not; she had stopped bargaining with it. She only kept her hand on the last work of his hands and let the boat rock her the way it had rocked them both, a thousand nights, in the years when the water had only ever been a thing they loved.

Then she got up, and put everything back in the box except the papers, and put the papers in a dry-bag, because she was a diver and that was what you did with the thing you could not afford to lose. She sealed it and rolled the top three times and clipped it, and set it by the door with her fins, and the ordinariness of the motion — a diver bagging her kit — steadied her more than any thought could have.

Tomorrow she would need the case photographs — Daan's, from two years ago, which she had never once looked at and which existed in a file with a number. She wanted to see how the belt had been arranged on him. She wanted to see the knots.

# Chapter Eight — The Signature

The dive-team locker room at seven in the morning smelled of neoprene and the anti-fungal spray Bram bought in bulk and made them all use, and it was empty, which was why Sanne had come at seven.

The heater ticked in the corner, warming up, losing to the cold that came off the concrete floor. Rows of drysuits hung from their shoulders like something between coats and bodies. Her own kit hung where it always did, third from the end, her fins above it, her mask in its box, everything where her hands could find it in the dark, because that was the whole of the discipline and the whole of the trust — that every diver on this team stowed the same way, checked the same way, so that any of them could dress any of them blind and know it was right. She had loved that once. She had thought it the cleanest thing she knew.

She'd signed the Maas evidence out of the property store the night before, on Nadia's requisition, one police diver checking the recovered equipment of another drowning, which raised no eyebrow because it was exactly her job. Two team-issue weight belts, serial-stamped, the ones that had gone down with Victor Maas. She laid them on the long bench under the strip light and stood over them with her coffee going cold beside her and did not touch them yet. She looked first, the way you always looked first, a full slow pass before your hands went in and changed anything.

You could tell a diver by his belt the way you could tell a sailor by his knots. It was not sentiment; it was habit worn into muscle. How many weights, and where. Whether the tail of the webbing was tucked left or right. Whether the buckle faced in or out. Which knot backed up the buckle, if any, and how the free end was dressed. Every diver on the team did it a fixed way and never thought about it, and if you swapped their belts blind they'd feel the wrongness before they saw it. Sanne could load her own in the dark, had done, in water the colour of nothing, her hands moving without her while her mind stayed free for the things that could kill her.

She photographed Victor's belts as they were, the flash whitening the neoprene backdrop, then took them apart slowly, logging each step in her own hand, the way you log a dive so that later, cold and dry and doubting yourself, you can trust the paper over the memory. Two nine-kilo blocks on the first belt, one on the second. That in itself was strange — a man Victor's size, in fresh water, in ordinary clothes, needed nothing like that to go down and stay down. She knew the numbers the way she knew her own weight; she had done the sums that keep divers alive ten thousand times. This was not weight to sink a diver. This was weight to keep a body on the bottom past the day it would otherwise rise. Whoever had done it knew how long gas builds in a corpse and had loaded against it, deliberately, with a margin. That was not a panicking hand. That was a technician.

She looked at how the blocks were seated. Not centred. Pushed to the wearer's right and snugged with a half-hitch of the tail through the second block before the buckle took the load — a way of stopping the weights from sliding that no manual taught and that Sanne had seen exactly one diver on this team do, because he'd shown her, years ago, his hands under hers on a cold boat on a grey morning, *like this, meisje, so they don't wander on you.* Her stomach did something small and cold and she did not look at it. She wrote down only what she saw. *Weights offset right. Half-hitch of tail through outboard weight, then buckle. Free end dressed short, tucked under standing part, right to left.* Just the facts, in her level hand. Let the paper hold it. Let her hands keep working.

She weighed the blocks on the kit scale to be sure, and the scale agreed with the stamps, and she wrote the numbers down. She checked the buckles for the serial stamps and found them, team stock, drawn from the same store she drew from, logged out to no one, which meant nothing, because the store was old and the log was old and half the team had a spare belt in a car boot from some job that had run long. She noted that too. She was building the thing the way you build a case for a coroner who will want to bury it — over-built, over-witnessed, every joint pinned, so that when they leaned on it, it would not move. She had signed a coroner's easy sentences once. She would never do it again, and she would never hand anyone the chance to make her.

Then she opened Daan's file.

Nadia had pulled it — a two-year-old accidental-drowning case, closed, the photographs sealed in a paper sleeve Sanne had never asked to see and would not have been given if she had, because you do not show a diver her husband on the bottom of the Amstel. She'd asked now. Nadia had brought it without a word and put it in her hands with both of hers, the way you hand someone something heavy, and had said only, *I'll be outside if you want me,* and gone. Sanne had waited until she was alone. Some things you did alone or you could not do them at all.

She spread the prints on the bench beside Victor's belts under the same hard light, and she made herself look at the equipment and not the man, the way she'd trained her whole life to look at the wreck and not the water. It was the hardest looking she had ever done. Her eyes wanted the edges of the frame, the pale shape of him, the hair moving in the small current the way weed moved. She did not let them. Breath in threes. In. Hold. Out. She had recovered bodies from this water and the water of every canal in the ring; she had brought up strangers and once, before she'd been pulled from it, had nearly been sent down for him and had refused and been right to refuse, because you do not send a diver for her own husband and a good supervisor knows it. Someone else had gone down for Daan. She had stood on the boat and held the line at the surface and felt, through the rope, the small tugs of another diver working over him, and had not looked. Now she looked. She kept her eyes on the webbing and the steel and did the work, and the work held her the way the line had held her, the one thing to grip in cold water.

His belt was in three of the photographs.

The coroner had written *equipment consistent with self-loading, weights unevenly distributed, consistent with an inexperienced or impaired diver.* She had signed under those words two years ago without reading them. Inexperienced. Daan, who had logged four hundred dives in a hand like a monk's. She looked now at the uneven distribution the report had used to bury him and saw it was not uneven. It was offset. Pushed to the wearer's right. And there, in the third photograph, in reasonable focus because some patient forensic technician two years ago had done his job even if no one had read it, was the tail of the webbing: run through the outboard weight in a half-hitch, then the buckle, the free end dressed short and tucked under the standing part, right to left.

The same hand.

Not the same method — the same *hand.* Method you could copy. You could read a manual, watch a video, learn to tie a thing the way another man tied it. This was not that. This was signature. This was a thing a man's fingers did without him, below thought, worn in over years until it was as much his as his walk — the same on Daan's belt in the Amstel two years ago as on Victor's belt in the Kloveniersburgwal last week, offset right, half-hitch, short tail tucked right to left, a small unteachable habit repeated across two years and two dead men who had both found the same six houses and the same notary and the same gap.

Sanne stood very still under the strip light with the two drownings laid out in front of her and let the cold finish arriving. It came up through the concrete and through her boots and into her the way the cold came at depth, not all at once but steadily, past every layer, until it was simply the temperature you were. She had been getting colder for a week. Now she was all the way cold.

A diver had done this. Not a fixer in a good coat, not a notary with a clean pen — those men signed paper. Slootweg sold the houses and Aalving certified the lie, and neither of them had ever knelt on a pitching deck with a belt in his hands. This was signed in webbing, by someone who loaded a belt the way you load one in the dark, from muscle, from years. Someone on the team, or close enough to the team to own the habit and the serial-stamped belts and the knowledge of how long a body waits before it wants to rise. Someone who had been trusted with a safety line. Both times. Someone Daan had known. Someone who understood the water this all sat in.

It changed the shape of everything. She had come to this room a week ago hunting a killer she pictured somewhere out there — in a good coat, behind a clean desk, on the far side of a canal. A man of the paper world, of valuations and companies owning companies. She had built him in her head as the opposite of herself, dry, soft-handed, a man who never got cold. And the belts on the bench told her she had been looking in the wrong direction the whole time. The killer was not on the far side of the water. He was on her side of it. He wore neoprene and smelled of the same anti-fungal spray. He knew, as she knew, exactly how much lead it takes to keep a man down, and he had done the sums the way she did them, without effort, from the same training, and he had knelt on a boat and loaded a belt against the day a corpse wants to rise, and he had done it twice, two years apart, to two men who had found the same six houses.

She did not let the initial from Daan's pencil become a name. She had promised herself that, on the boat, hand flat on his handwriting, and she kept the promise now because it was the only discipline left that was entirely hers. Grief would hand her the name gladly. It was already reaching for it. She would not take it from grief. She would take it from the water or not at all.

She wrote instead, in her own level hand under the two logs, the only thing she was certain of, and being certain of it was like standing on the bottom in four degrees of water with the whole city closed over her head like a lid:

*Same configuration. Same hand. A diver's hand. Daan did not drown. Victor did not drown. They were weighted by one of us.*

She photographed the two belts side by side, offset right, tail right to left, the signature and its copy, the flash going twice off the neoprene. She sealed Daan's prints back into their sleeve, one at a time, not looking at the parts of them she had trained herself not to look at, and she sealed Victor's belts into their bags and logged the seals. Everything in order. Everything where the paper could hold it.

Somewhere in the building a door opened and boots came down a corridor, unhurried, familiar, a man arriving early the way he always did, the way he always had, first in, last out, the one who checked the checkers. She knew the sound of him the way she knew the sound of her own gear. Sanne closed the file and picked up her cold coffee and arranged her face into the one she wore on the line, flat and ready and giving nothing to the water, because whoever the hand belonged to, he was still on her team, and he was still, every time she went down, the one holding the other end of her rope.

# Chapter Nine — The Mail

The sorting hall at Sloterdijk smelled of hot rubber and paper dust, and the conveyors never stopped. Sanne stood at the counter in her civilian coat and watched a woman named Agata run Victor Maas's name through three systems while the machines behind her fed envelopes into the dark at a rate that made counting pointless. It was the kind of place that ran the same at four in the morning as at noon, indifferent to weather, indifferent to the dead. Somewhere out past the loading doors the January cold sat over the rail yards, and inside it was all warm plastic and the drone of belts, and Sanne kept her hands in her pockets and made herself stand still.

"Nothing under registered post," Agata said. "Nothing held. You're sure it was PostNL?"

"I'm not sure of anything," Sanne said. "That's why I'm here."

Agata gave her a look that was not unkind, the look of a person who dealt all day with people who wanted a parcel to mean more than it did. Then she went back to the screen. Her nails clicked on the keys. Behind her a man in a hi-vis vest wheeled a cage of grey trays past and did not look at either of them, and the trays rocked and settled, thousands of letters going nowhere Sanne could follow.

Nadia leaned on the counter beside her, patient in the way she was patient, which was not patient at all but very still. She had driven up from the city in the grey Opel that smelled of other people's cigarettes, and she had said almost nothing on the way, which meant she was thinking hard about the thing they both already knew and neither of them had said out loud yet. That the belts were team issue. That the hand that had rigged Victor was a hand that knew the water and knew the department and had access to both. It was a thought with a shape you did not want to turn to face, and so they had driven in silence past the harbour cranes with the wipers going, and Nadia had smoked one cigarette with the window cracked and put it out half done.

"Try poste restante," Nadia said.

Agata typed. The screen threw its pale light up under her chin. "There's a hold-for-collection at the Singel office. In the name of—" she squinted "—V. Maas. Came in eleven days ago. Uncollected."

Eleven days. Victor had gone into the Kloveniersburgwal ten days ago. He had posted the thing, whatever it was, the day before he died, and then he had died, and it had sat in a steel drawer at the Singel waiting for a man who was never coming. Sanne did the arithmetic twice and it came out the same both times, the way arithmetic did. A man who knows he might die does not send a thing to himself by accident. He sends it to himself because he trusts no address that belongs to anyone else.

"We'll need to collect it," Sanne said.

"You'll need ID matching the name, or a warrant, or next of kin with proof."

Nadia already had her phone out. "The sister," she said. "Marije. Amstelveen."

Marije Maas lived in a ground-floor flat with a child's tricycle in the hall and a smell of cooking she apologised for as if it were a crime. She was younger than Victor had been, softer in the face, and she had cried already — that was plain — and put herself back together for the doorbell, and now she stood holding the door frame as though the two women on her step might take that from her too.

"Police," she said. "Again."

"Not the same police," Nadia said, which was true in a way that Sanne was only beginning to understand. "May we come in?"

They sat in a small front room with too many cushions. A boy of about four watched them from a doorway, thumb in mouth, until his mother sent him away with a look. There was a radiator ticking under the window and condensation on the glass, and beyond it a courtyard with a single bare tree and a trampoline furred with wet leaves. Sanne let Nadia do the talking. She was better at the shape of it, the softening, the questions that came in from the side. Sanne watched the sister's hands instead. You could learn more from a person's hands than their face. The face was managed. The hands forgot to be.

"Victor sent me a key," Marije said, when they got there. "Two weeks ago. In an envelope, no letter. I thought it was a mistake. He did things like that when we were kids — sent me things, made me guess." She looked at the window. "I thought it was a joke I didn't understand yet."

"Do you have it," Sanne said. First thing she had said.

Marije went out of the room. They heard a drawer, a kitchen drawer by the sound, the rattle of small objects that a house accumulates — batteries, twine, the keys to locks that no longer existed. She came back with a flat key on a paper tag, the kind of key that opens a coin locker. On the tag, in the small square hand Sanne had seen on Victor's compliance memos, a number. 114.

"He called me the night before," Marije said. She was looking at the key in Sanne's palm and not at Sanne. "He said if anything happened I should give the key to a policeman I trusted. Not just any policeman. One I trusted." She laughed, once, without any humour in it at all. "As if I know any policemen. As if trusting one is a thing a person can arrange."

"Where does it open," Nadia asked.

"Central Station," Marije said. "He said the left-luggage. The lockers on the east side, the old ones, not the machines." She finally looked up, first at Nadia and then, for longer, at Sanne. "Are you the one I trust? Because I don't know how to tell."

Sanne closed her hand around the key. It was cold and warmed slowly against her palm the way everything did. She thought of telling the woman a comfortable thing, and did not, because Marije Maas had already been told comfortable things by people in coats and one of those things had been that her brother drowned by accident.

"No," she said. "But I'm the one who's going to find who did this. That's not the same thing. It might be enough."

Marije looked at her a long moment. Then she nodded, slowly, the way people nod when they have decided to stop needing an answer they were never going to get. At the door she caught Sanne's sleeve, once, briefly, and let it go. "He was careful," she said. "My brother. He was the most careful man I ever knew. If he was afraid, you should be afraid." It was not a warning she needed to give. Sanne took it anyway, and carried it out into the cold with the key.

# Chapter Ten — Narrowing

The dive store at the team base smelled of neoprene and the pink antiseptic they used on the regulators, and it was the one room in the building with no window, which Sanne had always liked and now, standing in it alone at seven in the morning, found she did not. She knew what she was looking at. She had spent the night with Victor's spreadsheet and Victor's letter and the memory of two bodies, and she had made a list, and the list was short.

Who could manage a recovery from inside? Not a junior. A junior dived where he was told and signed nothing that mattered. It had to be someone who could take a call, put a team in the water, direct the search along the line he chose, and stand at the supervisor's shoulder when the ruling was written — or write it himself. Someone the coroner phoned by first name. Someone the young divers called by his first name too, and meant it warmly.

That was three people on the whole team. Then it was two, because Willemsen had been in Groningen the week Victor died, and the roster proved it, a training secondment stamped and dated and impossible to fake. Then it was one, because the second man, De Wit, had joined the team eight months ago and had never met Daan Kessler in his life, and whoever had done this had known Daan. Had known Daan well enough that Daan confided in him. Victor had died a stranger to the water. Daan had died a friend of it. That was the hinge. That was the thing that turned the case from a canal into a corridor with one door at the end.

That left the name at the top of the belt log, the name on the spine of the binder in his own careful capitals, the name that had signed out belts 07 and 09 — the two on Victor, she had checked the serials against the recovery inventory at three in the morning with a torch, twice, because the first time she had put the torch down and stood in her kitchen and been unable to move.

BRAM VERHOEVEN.

She did not want it. She wanted it less than she had ever wanted anything, and she was a woman who had wanted her husband to still be alive with a force that had nearly killed her, so she knew the size of the not-wanting.

Bram had carried her coffin-side at Daan's funeral. She stood in the windowless store with the binder open to his handwriting and she thought: no.

It was not a reasoned no. It was the body's no, the animal one, the same word she had said standing over Daan's covered face two years ago. It changed nothing. It never had.

And then she thought about the hand in the water. The economy of it. The way the belts had sat on Victor exactly as they had sat on Daan — not the way a manual specified, but the way a particular man rigged them, high on the hips, cinched with a half-twist he'd told her once was quicker, his own trick, his signature, he'd shown her that half-twist himself with his big fingers years ago on a training dock and said, nobody else does it this way, this is the Verhoeven way, and laughed. She could hear the laugh. That was the worst part, standing in the store — that she could hear the laugh and it was still, in her body, a good sound.

The half-twist had been on Daan. She had seen it two years ago and not understood it. She had thought, if she had thought anything, that Daan had rigged himself that way because he'd learned it off Bram, the way she had. The half-twist had been on Victor. She had photographed it herself, three days ago, in the cold, and filed the photograph, and not seen it until now.

Nobody else does it this way.

Sanne closed the binder very quietly, as though the sound might wake something, and put it back exactly where it lived, spine squared to the shelf edge, and wiped the bench where she had leaned, and left the store the way she had found it, which was a thing she knew how to do because she had learned it, she now understood, from the man who had made a career of leaving the water the way he found it.

"Yes," she said. "It's a someone."

He had sat there knowing, and been kind, and the kindness had been real, that was the thing she could not get her hands around, the kindness had been real and it had cost him nothing because he had already decided the water was a service and Daan had already been paid for. She did not cry. She had passed some border where crying was on the other side. She sat and let the cold find the seams, patient, personal, and she counted her breaths, and could not stop counting them in threes, and hated, for the first time in her life, the man whose voice made her do it.

# Chapter Eleven — Pressure

The team briefing room had strip lights that buzzed at a pitch Sanne had stopped hearing years ago and heard again now, standing at the back while Bram ran the morning board. The radiators were on full and the windows had steamed and someone had drawn a smiling face in the condensation and rubbed half of it out. Wet gear hung along the far wall, dripping onto the concrete. The room smelled of coffee and rubber and men who had been up since five.

He was good at it. He had always been good at it. He stood at the whiteboard with a marker and his reading glasses down his nose and moved men and jobs around the week with the ease of a man who had done it a thousand times, and the team liked him, and Sanne watched them like him and felt something cold and level settle in her chest that she recognised, distantly, as the feeling she got at the top of a bad dive, the last calm before the descent. # Chapter Twelve — The Offer

The café on the Zeedijk was too warm and smelled of frying and old beer, and Ruud Slootweg had taken the corner table with his back to the wall like a man in a film, which was, Sanne thought, exactly the kind of man he was — one who had learned everything he knew about menace from watching other people have it. # Chapter Thirteen — Into the Water

He would kill her and be sorry and it would not, in him, be a contradiction. He had wept at Daan's funeral. She had held his arm.

# Chapter Fourteen — The Line

The haul took her four metres off the bottom before she stopped fighting it.

One stroke of the hooked blade and the yellow leash parted with a small dead recoil and she dropped away from the surface into the dark, out from under his hands, off the log, out of the killing and back into the water — which was hers, which had always been hers, which was the one place in the entire world Bram Verhoeven had never truly lived. He stood on the bank and pulled strings and signed sheets and told the families everything possible was being done, and the last time he had actually killed with his own hands in the deep black cold he had had a bound and trusting man who did not fight and did not know the ground and had brought coffee for two. In threes. She watched him find it, watched his eyes come back from wherever they had gone, watched him count on her fingers and slow his lungs by main force, one, and two, and three, until the fog on his mask came and went in a rhythm instead of a flutter. Good hands, Daan had said of her, twenty-two years ago, to a man who would kill him for it. She put good hands on the boy now and brought him back from the edge his body had already stepped over.

The thin lead feeding his air ran up into the dark toward the surface, toward Bram. She could feel, through the water, the surface changing — a churning, a heaviness overhead, the pressure-signature of a man who had just felt his line go dead and slack in his hands and was now leaning out over the black wondering why the body had not come up, why nothing was coming up, why the water had gone quiet and wrong under him for the first time in his life.

She had a choice, and she made it in the cold with her hand on Tim's tank.

She could go up the shot line fast and hard in the dark and take Bram in the water from below, catch his boots and drag him off the bank into her element and hold him under the way he had held Daan, and no one would ever be able to prove it wasn't the river that did it. The wanting of it was enormous. It sat in her chest bigger than the cold, bigger than the fear had been. Two years of it. Daan's papers on a killer's kitchen table, next to the flask, next to the burn mark. I'll be quick, I'll be quick, meisje. She could give Bram Verhoeven the exact thing he had given her husband, measure for measure, and the water would keep the secret the way it kept every secret, and it would be, in the oldest arithmetic there was, the arithmetic older than the weigh house, fair.

And it would drown the truth with him. A dead Bram in the Amstel was another closed file, another ruling, another tidy sheet — and Bram had spent two years teaching her, without meaning to, exactly how closed files were made and who made them. If she killed him she became the last cleaner in the chain, the newest link, and Aalving and Slootweg would read about the tragic loss of a decorated supervisor over their coffee and go on selling grachtenpanden at numbers they wrote themselves, and Daan's death would stay an accident forever, because the only person left who could reopen it would be a murderer with a motive and a body she had put in the water with her own hands.

The ledger was in a safe-deposit box on the Herengracht under Nadia Okonkwo's name. The belts with the stamped serials were in a FIOD evidence locker with a signature and a date. The doctored equipment log, the matching weighting signature on two bodies, Joke's statement about a grey boat on the water the night Victor vanished — all of it, secured, waiting, needing only one thing to turn from a story into a case: a living witness who had heard Bram Verhoeven confess, at depth, with the comms recording every word onto the team server the way team comms always recorded on a hard job, the way Bram in his certainty and his grief had forgotten it would.

Sanne looked up the shot line, into the dark, toward the man who had killed her husband and wept at his funeral and held her arm.

Then she turned her back on it. She got her arm through the straps of Tim's harness, cinched him to her side, put her own spare regulator ready at her chest in case his air failed on the way, and started the boy toward the surface the slow safe way — along the bottom to the bank, away from the shot line, away from Bram, up into the orange light by the route no one was watching. Surface with the evidence. Surface with the boy. Let the honest machinery have the rest.

It cost her more than going up the line would have. She felt exactly how much it cost, the whole long swim, every metre of it counted off under her breath, the wanting still huge in her chest and getting no smaller for being refused. She counted breaths for both of them now, hers and the boy's, matching them, one and two and three, and she carried Victor Maas's killer's confession up out of the water in her skull like something recovered from the bottom, cradled, and she did not go back for him, and the not going back was the hardest hard dive of her life and she knew it even as she made it.

# Chapter Fifteen — Surfacing

She broke the surface twenty metres downstream of the staging point, in the black shadow of a moored barge, and got Tim's face clear of the water before her own.

The boy heaved and coughed and dragged air, real air, ragged and enormous, his hands scrabbling at her harness with no strength in them, and she trod water and held him and said, close to his ear, low, "You're up. You're up. It's over. Breathe. I've got you." She got the full-face mask off him and it sank away into the dark and she kept his head clear with a hand under his jaw and side-kicked them both to the barge's mooring ladder and hung there with him on the cold iron, both of them shaking, the orange sky enormous overhead after the seven metres of black. She checked him by feel, the way you check a casualty in the dark: airway, breathing, the grip of his hands coming back. Alive. Staying alive.

Up the bank, at the staging point, a torch was moving. Bram, on the cobble, playing his light out over the water where the shot line went down, hauling on the dead slack of the cut tending line and getting nothing, coiling it in and finding the cut end and staring at it. Sanne watched him from the dark of the barge's shadow and saw the moment the certainty left him — saw him drop the useless line and turn his torch out along the surface, sweeping it, a man who had felt a killing go wrong under his own hands for the first time in his life and did not understand how, whose whole method had just failed him in the one place he had always been in charge.

"Sanne." His voice came thin across the water, not through the comms now, the comms dead with the cut line, just his ordinary voice, an old man's voice, over black water. "Sanne!"

She said nothing. She got Tim's arms locked over a rung, one and then the other, and pressed his hands closed on the cold iron and made sure the boy would hold his own weight, and then she unclipped the sealed evidence pouch from her harness — the small waterproof case that held the backup: the second SD card off the comms rig, the one she'd wired in herself in the week before, spinning its own copy of every word Bram had said into her skull at depth, redundant, hers, the way the pony bottle had been hers, the way the second cutter had been hers. Belt and braces. Never take one of anything down where one failure can kill you. The only religion a diver had, and it had just kept two people alive and one case whole.

Bram found the ladder in his torch beam. He found the two heads in the water at the bottom of it. Sanne saw the light hit her mask and hold, steady, and she saw him understand the whole shape of it at once — the cut line, the living boy, the diver he had killed an hour ago breathing on the surface in the dark — and she saw what the understanding did to him.

⁂

He came down the bank fast for a heavy man of fifty-eight, half-running on the wet cobble, and he came into the water off the low apron at the staging point, still in his shell jacket, still in his boots, and — she saw it and her whole body understood it before her mind had the words — still wearing a weight belt. He'd rigged himself to go in after the body, that was the thing, he'd belted up in the minutes he thought she was dying to descend and finish it and recover what he needed to recover, the way he'd finished and recovered everything for two years. Lead on his hips. Fifteen kilos of it, maybe more, the amount a man puts on to sink and stay down and work. A topside man. A bank man. A man who had not made a hard dive in nine years, going into five-degree black moving water in boots and a canvas jacket with a belt of lead on him and rage where his training should have been.

"You don't get to," Bram said, wading to his waist, then to his chest, then swimming three strokes, then floundering as the bottom fell away under him and the cold hit his sternum and took his breath the way cold takes an unready body, all at once, in a gasp, in a clench of every muscle at once. "You don't get to take this from me. I built this. I kept you—" and the water came into the words and broke them and he coughed and went on trying to speak and could not.

He was ten metres from the ladder. Then, because the current here set toward the sluis and he did not know it in his body the way she knew it in hers, he was twelve. His arms went wide and slapped flat on the surface, the drowning arms, the ones that reach down for something to climb and find only more water. The weight belt on his hips pulled the way weight belts pull — honestly, without malice, straight down, doing the one thing lead does. And a diver ditches a belt in that second. A diver's right hand goes to the quick-release buckle without being told, drilled into the spine below thought, ten thousand times in the tank and in the dark, so that when the mind is gone the hand still knows: find the buckle, pull, drop the lead, live. But Bram was not a diver. Not any more, and maybe, in the way that counted at the bottom of a river at night, never. His hands clawed at the water instead of at the buckle. His head went under and came up. His mouth made a shape that was not a word.

Sanne hung on the ladder with Tim's shivering weight against her side and the evidence pouch clipped to her chest and she was, she measured it coldly, the way she measured everything now, four strokes from him. Maybe three. She was the best diver on the team and everyone knew it and she was three strokes from a drowning man, and she knew — her hands knew it, her shoulders knew it, her whole trained body strained toward it — exactly how to take him. Come in from behind, always from behind, out of the reach of the panic-grip that drowns rescuers; one hand to the quick-release and drop the lead in a single pull; roll him onto his back; get an arm across his chest and kick for the iron. She had done it for strangers. She had done it for a Danish tourist off the Oosterdok and a drunk boy off the Prinsengracht and a woman who had meant it and fought her the whole way up. It was the oldest thing she was for, the thing under everything else she was. Somewhere below the two of them a river was waiting that did not care who either of them was, or what he had done, or what she owed.

His head went under again and stayed longer. His hand broke the surface and reached, and it reached toward her, toward the ladder, toward the light, and it was the hand that had tucked her strap flat against her chest that evening, the hand that had carried Daan off the bottom of this same river, the hand that had cut her air an hour ago at the top of the line and been sorry.

She did not go.

She held the ladder. She held the boy. She held the pouch. She stayed where she was in the cold with the current pulling at her too, and she watched Bram Verhoeven fight the belt he had trusted, the weights that had been his instrument for two years, and lose to them the way Daan had lost and Victor had lost, dragged down by his own lead into the black water off the Amstel — and it was not quick. She had time to think that. It was not quick, the way he had promised her twice it would be quick, and she made herself watch all of it, every second of it, because if she was going to choose this then she was going to pay the full price of the choosing with her eyes open and not look away and not be able to tell herself afterward that she had not really seen. His reaching hand closed on nothing. It opened and closed on nothing. The orange light lay broken over the surface where he was and then the surface smoothed and re-formed and there was no hand. The river set toward the sluis and took the last ripples of him with it and went quiet, and the quiet came back over the water like a lid.

It cost her. It cost her the rest of her life, she thought, some cold clear part of her that was standing a little apart on the ladder and would go on standing there for years. She would carry the three strokes she did not take down into every dive she made after this, would feel them in the cold at seven metres for the rest of her working life. She had chosen the boy and the pouch over the man's life. It was defensible. It was, weighed honestly, even right. And it would never once let her entirely alone. She counted her breaths on the iron ladder, out of habit, out of need. One. Two. Three. Tim was crying against her shoulder in long shudders and did not know yet what he had just been beside, what the quiet on the water meant.

Up on the Blauwbrug, a car door slammed. A torch beam came swinging down the Weesperzijde, jolting, someone running with it, and a voice she knew — Rotterdam, sharp, carrying flat across the water — was shouting her name.

"Sanne! Sanne, where—"

"Down here," Sanne called up, and her voice came out level, and the levelness of it would frighten her later, in a warm room, when she had time to be frightened. "Nadia. Down here. I've got Tim. He's alive." A breath. One. "Get the harbourmaster. There's a man in the water off the staging point. Belted. He went down—" she found the number, kept it true "—four minutes ago." She looked at the flat black river moving toward the sluis. "You won't get him in time."

⁂

They got Tim onto a stretcher on the Weesperzijde under a foil blanket with a medic's hands on him and a line in his arm, and they got Sanne sitting on the cobble with her hood peeled back and her hair plastered flat to her skull and a second blanket round her shoulders that she kept letting slide, and the evidence pouch still clipped to her chest because she would not let anyone take it, not the medic, not the first uniform, until she had put it into Nadia Okonkwo's hands herself, with her own words on it, in the right order.

She put it into Nadia's hands. "Comms recording," she said. Her teeth were going now, the shivers coming up through the words. "Backup card. My rig, I wired it in myself, redundant to the team server copy so there's two, so there's always two. He confessed at depth. Daan. Victor. Aalving by name. Slootweg by name. It's all on there, start to end." She got the words out through the cold in the right order because the order was the case. "Chain of custody starts with you. Now. This second. Say it back to me."

"I've got it," Nadia said. She was crouched in front of Sanne in a wet coat with her jaw set hard, and she looked once at the black river where the harbourmaster's launch was now nosing its light back and forth over nothing, and then back at Sanne, and she did not ask the question that was standing plainly in her face — the four strokes, the three strokes, the best diver on the team sitting on a ladder while a man drowned an arm's length off. She was too good a detective, and too good a thing to Sanne by then, to ask it out loud on a bank at night with the medic in earshot. She would ask it later. Gently. Once. And Sanne would answer it honestly, and it would sit between them for years and not break them. "Sanne. Look at me. It's enough. You hear me? With this, it's enough."

And it was.

The honest machinery, once it was fed, moved the way honest machinery moves — slowly, and then all at once. The ledger came out of the box on the Herengracht: the deeds, the two sets of valuations, the laundered deltas totted up in a dead clerk's careful hand, C.A. and R.S. initialled in the margin beside the numbers that did not match the numbers. The belts came out of the FIOD locker, serials stamped along the buckle-plates, cross-matched now against the doctored equipment log that Bram had signed in his own competent hand across two years, the log that put team-issue gear on two drowned men and had been meant to prove they'd never touched it. The forensic pathologist, given a reason at last to look again and a name to look for, put Daan Kessler's weighting pattern beside Victor Maas's weighting pattern — the placement of the lead, the number of pieces, the particular economical way a particular pair of hands trimmed a body to sink and stay — and wrote the word signature in a report that made an Officier van Justitie sit up very straight at her desk and read it twice. Joke, from her houseboat below the sluis, gave a statement about a grey supervisor's boat on the water at two in the morning the night the clerk vanished, running without lights, and picked Bram's boat out of six photographs laid on her little galley table without a flicker or a pause.

They took Cornelis Aalving — De Notaris — out of his office on the Keizersgracht on a grey afternoon eleven days later, on laundering and accessory to murder, and the FIOD carried out his hard drives in banker's boxes past the tall front window where he had sold canal houses to buyers no one could ever quite find. They took Ruud Slootweg at Schiphol with a bag packed and a boarding pass in his coat, fraud and false instrument, the fixer with nothing left to fix. And in a fluorescent room in the Officier's building on a morning that smelled of institutional coffee, a functionary Sanne had never met and would never see again slid a single sheet across a laminate table and asked her to confirm that she understood the file on the death of Daan Kessler, previously ruled misadventure by drowning, was being reopened and reclassified.

Murder. The word she had been denied for two years, in ordinary civil-service type, on a form with a case number at the top.

Sanne read it twice, the way Nadia had read her report twice, the way honest things got read. She thought of Daan at Bram's kitchen table with his papers and his flask of coffee for two, asking his oldest friend what he should do. She thought of the three strokes on the ladder and the hand that had opened and closed on nothing. She signed where they told her to sign, and her hand did not shake, and she gave the pen back and stood up.

# Chapter Sixteen — The Weigh House

Six weeks on, De Waag stood in the middle of the Nieuwmarkt the way it had stood for five hundred years, dark brick and pointed towers, the old weigh house where the city had once brought everything that could be bought and set it on the scales to learn the truth of it. Sanne sat at a table by the window with a coffee going cold in front of her and watched the square do its ordinary afternoon business — the market packing down, a tram bell on the Kloveniersburgwal, two children arguing over a bicycle — and thought about weight.

They had weighed things here for centuries. Butter and cheese and lead, the anchors of ships, the great copper vats of the guilds, everything the city bought and sold hauled up onto the scale so that no one need take a seller's word for what a thing was. Later, upstairs in the octagonal room under the roof, the anatomists had weighed something harder to price. They had opened bodies on a marble table with the guild of surgeons watching in tiers by candlelight, to learn what a person was made of and how much of it there was, to put a number even on that. The building had always been about the same stubborn thing, under the brick and under the centuries: the civic faith that a thing had a true weight, and that you could find it if you were honest and had the right scale and the patience to wait for the beam to settle, and that the number, once you had it, did not care in the least who you were.

Aalving's people had bet against that faith. They had bet that a canal house did not have a true weight — that a valuation was only ever a story someone told, and that with the right notary and the right fixer and the right man inside the police dive team, you could write any number you liked beside a grachtenpand and no scale on earth would ever rise up and contradict you. For a while they had been right. For two years, with a friend of Daan's standing between the truth and the water, they had been exactly, profitably right, and men had died to keep them right, and the deaths had gone on the record as accidents with the forms filled in.

They had been wrong about the scale. The scale was slow, and the scale was old, and the scale had needed a diver to go down into the black and bring up what had been weighted to the bottom on purpose to hide it from the beam. But the scale had held. In the end, weighed against everything they had built to defeat it, the scale had held.

Nadia came in stamping the cold off her boots and sat down across from her without being asked and ordered a coffee she would not finish. She had a habit now of finding Sanne on the afternoons Sanne didn't dive, of turning up at De Waag or the boat or the bank with some small piece of the case in her pocket like an offering. Neither of them called it what it was. It did not need calling.

"Slootweg's turned," Nadia said. "Wants a deal. He's giving us the whole structure — the front purchases, the offshore chain, two more houses we didn't even have on the board." She turned her cup a quarter-circle on its saucer, a habit of hers when the news was good and she did not want to look too pleased with it. "Aalving's lawyers are trying to sever the murder counts from the fraud. Argue them as separate matters, different men, different water. They won't manage it. The weighting signature does it. Your pathologist won't move a centimetre, and they've had two experts of their own look at it and both of them have gone quiet."

"Good," Sanne said.

"The trial's next spring." Nadia looked at her then, the careful look, the one that had been asking the same quiet question for six weeks and had never quite let itself all the way out. "You'll have to give evidence. About the dive. The confession. The comms. About—" and she left the rest of it in the water where it had been sitting between them since the night on the bank.

"I know what I'll have to say." Sanne wrapped both hands round the cold cup. "I'll say all of it. The three strokes too. If they ask me whether I could have reached him — and Aalving's people will ask me, they'll have to, it's the only card they've got — I'll say yes. I could have reached him. I was three strokes off and I'm the best in the water on that team and I could have had the belt off him and had him on the ladder, and I stayed where I was." She met Nadia's eyes and did not look away, and it was the steadiest she had felt in six weeks, steadier than the form, steadier than the ladder. "I've decided I'd rather live the rest of it having told the truth about that than having told myself any of the kinder things I could tell myself instead. The truth has a weight too. I'd rather carry the real one."

Nadia held her look for a long moment. Then she nodded, once, and let it go, and the thing that had been standing upright between them since the bank at night finally sat down and became, instead of a question, the quiet thing that held the two of them together.

"You're still on the team," Nadia said. It wasn't quite a question either.

"I'm still on the team." They'd tried, gently, in a warm office with a counsellor's leaflet on the desk, to move her onto land — analysis, training, a supervisor's chair, God help her. She'd refused, and they had let her, because there was no one better in the water and everyone knew it, and because taking the water from Sanne Kessler would have been its own slow kind of drowning and someone in that office had understood it. She was changed in it, and she knew she was changed. She dived slower now. She signed her own fills in her own book and analysed her own gas and checked her own line twice and made the standby diver check it a third time. There was a new junior on the team — not Tim; Tim had gone home to Purmerend and his mother and a desk at a shipping office, and no one on the team blamed him, and Sanne least of all — a new junior, twenty-three, quick hands, too trusting, and she watched the boy in the water the way Bram had once watched her. The difference was that she taught him where to hide a second life, and a second cutter, and why. She taught him redundancy as if it were the only prayer there was, because she had learned that it was. "I'm still on the team, Nadia. It's the one thing in all of it they didn't manage to take. I'm not going to hand it to them late, out of grief. That would just be a slower way of letting them have it."

⁂

She dived the Amstel alone on a still grey morning in the week the clocks changed, off the books, which was against every rule she now kept and enforced on everyone around her, and she did it anyway, and no one stopped her, because Nadia had come to tend her line from the bank and Nadia, whatever else she was, was not a rule-follower when it counted, and this counted.

She went in off the Weesperzijde at the place the file gave, near the sluis, and she rigged herself the way she rigged for any hard dive because she no longer knew how to do it any other way — pony bottle slung and clipped, two cutters, gauge checked and checked again, line clean off the belay. She went down the shot line hand over hand, counting her breaths in threes, one and two and three, and the city closed over her head like a lid. Five degrees. The cold found the seams the way it always did, patient, personal, walking the zip line and the wrists of her seals, and she let it, and vented her suit, and sank. Somewhere down here, two years and some months ago, a man she had loved had been held under by a man he had trusted since before she knew either of them, and weighted with a careful economical pair of hands, and left, and then carried up again by the same two hands so that the lie would be tidy and she would have a pension and a closed file and a life.

She found the bottom at seven and a half metres and hung a half-metre off the silt, neutral, breathing slow, adding a trickle of air against the squeeze and dumping it, finding the balance the way she had found it ten thousand times, her torch making its small green world in the murk. She did not need a mark on the line or a bearing to swim. She had read the reopened file, all of it, the reconstruction and the diagrams and the pathologist's quiet devastating pages. She knew now, to the metre, where Daan had gone down. She swam to it, counting the kicks under her breath, and she hung over the grey silt at the place, and she took her right glove off in the cold water and felt the river close round her bare fingers like a grip.

She had brought his wedding ring. She had worn it on a chain under her suit for two years, against her sternum, against the breastbone, in the exact place where the tending line's pull had come through into her chest the night Bram reeled her toward the surface and the belt. She unclipped the chain in the cold with fingers already going stiff and clumsy, and worked the plain gold band off it, and closed it in her fist, and then opened her hand flat under the torch and looked at it. The plain band, worn thin at the underside from twelve years of his hand, the date engraved inside it that she no longer had to tilt to the light to read because she had read it in the dark a thousand nights.

She had thought, on the way down the line, that she would say something to him here, that there would be words for it. There were none that the water did not already hold. She had brought the truth up out of this river and given it to the honest scale and stood and watched the beam settle. That was the thing she had owed him, the only thing, and it was done. And it was imperfect — a killer drowned in the cold instead of tried in the light, three strokes she would answer for at trial next spring and to herself every working morning after, a friend of Daan's who had loved him truly and killed him anyway and been, at the very last, out on the black water in his boots and his lead, righter about the river than she had ever wanted him to be. Imperfect. Real. Weighed and true and enough.

She opened her hand the rest of the way.

The ring turned once in the torch beam, catching it, a small bright turn, and went down, and the silt took it the way the silt took everything the city gave it, softly, without comment, closing over it, and it was gone. She had let it go. And the letting go did not close the hole in her — she had not expected it to, and it did not — but it changed the hole's shape under her hands, made it a thing she could carry down here in the cold instead of a thing that carried her up in the night.

She hung there a moment longer over the place, bare hand empty, and she counted her breaths one last time, the old count, the one from the training tank at twenty-four. One. Two. Three. She had not stopped grieving; she understood, hanging in the cold with her hand open and empty over the silt, that she was not going to, not this year and not any year. Grief did not evaporate off a person like water off a warm stone. It only got a true weight, at last, one that a person could bend down to and pick up and stand under and walk. That was all the honest scale had ever promised anyone who came to it — not that the number would be small, not that it would be kind. Only that it would be true, and that you could build the rest of a life on a true number in a way you could never build it on a lie, however tidy.

Then she pulled her glove back on, and dumped a breath of air into her suit, and another, and let the water do for her the one thing it had never done for Daan, or for Victor, or, in the end, for Bram Verhoeven with the lead on his hips — the thing the water did only for the ones who knew it and were known by it in return. She went up. Off the bottom, up through the seven metres of nothing-coloured cold, up toward the coin of grey light that widened and paled as she rose, her own weight and the water's weight finding their old honest balance against each other, lift and load, buoyant, up and up until the lid of the city broke over her head and let her through into the air.

She surfaced into the ordinary morning. Nadia was on the bank with the line loose in her hands and her breath showing in the cold. The gulls were up and wheeling over the Magere Brug. A barge went by somewhere down toward the Amstelsluis and its wake reached her, slow, and lifted her, and set her down. The city went on spending and weighing itself, indifferent and alive and five hundred years deep in the business of finding out what things truly cost, and Sanne Kessler floated on the surface of the Amstel and breathed the cold clean air in threes, and rose.
