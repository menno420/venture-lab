# THE WEIGH HOUSE

*An Amsterdam crime novel*

> An Amsterdam police diver pulls a weighted body from a canal and recognises the killing — it is the same method that drowned her own husband two years ago, in a death the department ruled an accident.

---

**Content note:** Adult crime fiction. Contains murder, drowning, non-graphic violence, grief and the death of a spouse, criminal-finance themes, and brief strong language. No sexually explicit content; no gratuitous gore. For adult readers.

# Chapter One — The Recovery

The water at the Kloveniersburgwal was three degrees and the colour of nothing. Sanne went down the shot line hand over hand, counting her breaths in threes, and the city folded shut over her head. Cold came in at the seams of her drysuit the way it always did, patient, without malice, personal. Below her somewhere a man was waiting who would never be cold again.

They had put the tent up on the quay at half past five. Across the empty market square stood the Waag with its towers and its old stones, the weigh house, the place where they had once cut open the dead to see how they were made. This morning, for the first time in a thousand passings, she thought about it.

Visibility was nothing, less than nothing. The bottom was silt over centuries of silt, and the mud held onto everything with a soft and total grip.

The line loosened above her when she asked for slack. She felt Bram let it go — she knew the hand on the other end of that rope the way you know a voice in the dark. Bram Verhoeven had tended her line for eleven years. She trusted the rope because she trusted the hand. That was the whole of diving, in the end. You went into a place that wanted to keep you and you trusted a hand you could not see.

A dredging crew had raised the alarm at first light. At twenty-two breaths her glove found him.

Not a face. A shoulder, first — the give of it, wrong, too soft under fabric, and then the fabric itself, a coat, wool by the feel, buttoned. She held still and let the cloud of silt her own movement had raised drift off her, and she waited, and she counted. When she moved again it was slow.

He was on his back in the mud. That was the first wrongness, though she did not yet know to name it. Men who fell into canals did not lie neatly on their backs. They came to rest in the shapes that drowning left them, curled, turned, face into the silt. This man lay like a man laid down. His arms were at his sides. His coat was buttoned to the throat.

"I have him," she said.

Topside went quiet. Then Bram: "Take your time, Sanne. He's not going anywhere and neither are you."

She ran her hands down him in the dark, reading him by touch and patience. The coat. A jacket beneath it. A belt at the waist — a real belt, leather, ordinary. And then, lower, cinched hard over the coat itself, another belt, and this one was not ordinary. Webbing, stiff and flat. A quick-release buckle, the wide steel kind, the kind she wore at that moment around her own waist. And threaded onto the webbing, flat and heavy and unmistakable, the lead.

Dive weights.

Her breath fell out of its threes and she had to take it back deliberately, one two three, one two three.

Weight belts. Two of them, she found, working her hands around him in the cold and the black. One across the hips and one higher, under the arms, dragged tight so the body would not roll or rise. She ran her thumb over the blocks the way she counted her own lead before a dive — a full, deliberate load, far more than it would ever take to sink a man in a wool coat. Whoever had put this man in the water had wanted him to stay. They had weighted him the way you weighted a diver, deliberately, with knowledge, with the correct and specific tools for holding a thing beneath the surface.

"Sanne." Bram's voice, gentle. "Talk to me. Your breathing changed."

"He's weighted," she said. "Dive belts. Two."

A pause on the line.

"Rope him and bring him up," Bram said. "Don't work the scene down there. That's not our job. That's theirs."

He was right. Her job was recovery. She passed the recovery line under him, and the mud fought her for him and gave at last. She cinched the line and checked the clip twice. Then she stayed a moment with her glove flat on his chest, over the belt that should not be there, for three breaths, because it seemed to her that someone ought to.

"Coming up," she said. "Tell them to bring the board."

⁂

They laid him on the quay under lights and the sleet came down onto him and nobody wiped it away because you did not touch what you did not have to. Sanne came up the ladder and stood dripping on the stone while Tim unclipped her line.

He was younger than she'd expected. Forty, perhaps. A soft, unfinished face, the kind that looked apologetic even now, even done. The coat was good, the shoes were good — office shoes, thin-soled, no shoes for the water, no shoes for a January quay. His hands were pale and clean, the nails neat. A clerk's hands, maybe. She looked at him once, fully, because it seemed to her the least you could do for a person was to see them plainly at the end.

And around him, dark against the wet stone, the belts.

A scene-of-crime technician turned one of the belts to photograph the buckle, lifting it just clear of the coat. The strobe went off. And in the white flash, in the half-second the light stood everything still, Sanne saw it.

Stamped into the steel of the buckle plate, small, official, the way a thing is stamped that belongs to a fleet, to a store, to a service: a number. A serial. And beneath it, worn but legible, three letters she had seen ten thousand times because they were stamped on her own gear, on Tim's, on every set of lead in the shed at Zeeburg.

Team issue.

The belts around the dead man were theirs.

Sanne stood very still on the quay with the sleet running down the back of her neck, and she counted her breaths, and for the first time in eleven years the counting did not help. One two three. The number stayed, stamped now on the inside of her eyes the way the strobe leaves its ghost.

Bram came up beside her and put a towel around her shoulders, the way he always did, warm from inside his own coat, and he did not look at the belts. He looked at her.

"Cold one," he said. "Go get in the van."

She went. But she had seen the number, and she could not make herself unsee it.

# Chapter Two — Ruling

Sanne sat in the debrief room at Marineterrein with her hair still damp and a paper cup of coffee going cold in her hands and listened to a room decide what it had already decided.

The dead man had a name now. Victor Maas. Forty-one. A compliance clerk at a notary office in the Jordaan, a firm called Kanttekening Notariaat. Not reported missing. Lived alone. He had, someone said, a history — signed off work, stress, a huisarts note about low mood. The young prosecutor said it the way you'd read a weather report: "It fits. A man in a bad place who walks into a canal in January. It happens more than people think."

"He was weighted," Sanne said.

The room did the thing rooms did. It went smooth, the way water goes smooth over something dropped into it, closing the surface. The police doctor folded her hands. "People do weight themselves. Stones in pockets. Chains. A man who wants to be sure he won't lose his nerve and swim."

"With two dive belts. Cinched under the arms. From behind."

"We don't know from behind."

"I recovered him. I felt the buckles. They were dorsal — the release was at his spine, both of them. You can't reach your own spine and pull a two-belt release under water in the dark." She took her voice back down, flatter, colder, because heat lost you the room. "You physically cannot. Someone else put those on him. Someone who knew how to weight a body so it would stay down. That's not a man who wants to be sure. That's a man being made sure of."

"Sanne." Bram's voice, from the end of the table, easy and warm, the voice he used on the boat when the weather turned. "Nobody's saying you're wrong about what you felt. But we don't build a case in a debrief, and we don't build it on the recovery diver's read of a buckle in zero vis." He smiled at her, and then at the room. "She's the best pair of hands I've got in the water. In the water. This part isn't hers."

It was said with such affection that it took her a second to feel it land. He had, in one soft sentence, moved her out of the room while leaving her in her chair. You could not argue against being cared for without seeming to prove you needed it. The kindness closed the door and stood in front of it.

The talk moved to resourcing, to caseload, to winter. There was a detective coming in on it, someone said — a transfer from Rotterdam, Okonkwo — who would do the file, "and if it's what it looks like, it closes." Sanne knew what it looked like. It looked like a fast door closing on a slow question, and she had stood in front of that door before, and she had let it close, because the man closing it had put a towel around her shoulders warm from his own coat and told her to go get in the van.

In the corridor afterward, the woman from Rotterdam was reading the case tablet. "You're the diver," she said. Not a question. "Nadia Okonkwo. You said dorsal releases, in there. Somebody typed 'consistent with self-harm' before the man was dry. I've been here four days; I don't have friends to spend yet." She held Sanne's eyes a moment. "Your supervisor. Verhoeven. He's very keen to keep you out of it."

"He's protecting me. It's what he does."

"Mm," said Nadia, and did not agree or disagree, and that small refusal — the sound a person makes instead of the word yes — stayed with Sanne longer than it should have. She had said it as a defence and it had come out sounding like an accusation.

⁂

She did not sleep.

Home was a houseboat on the Kloveniersburgwal, a long low barge Daan had picked and loved past all sense, and at night the water threw the streetlight up onto the cabin ceiling in a slow moving grid that she had watched for two years and could not stop watching.

Daan Kessler had died in the Amstel two years ago, in March, in water not much warmer than this morning's. He had gone in off a friend's boat near the Magere Brug and not come up, and it had been Bram — Bram, who had been Daan's friend for twenty years before he was Sanne's supervisor for eleven — who had gone down and found him and brought him up. A diving accident. A regulator failure, they said, and a man alone in cold water, and the cold does what the cold does. The ruling had come fast. She remembered being grateful for how fast it came. Bram had handled everything, had spoken at the crematorium in a voice that shook, and afterward had sat with her in the dark and said, you don't have to do anything, Sanne, I've got the line.

She lay in the moving light and did the arithmetic she did not want to do.

Daan had died on a Tuesday. The ruling — accident, closed, no further inquiry — had been entered on the Friday. Three days. She had thought, at the time, that this was mercy. She thought now that it was speed, and that speed was not the same thing as mercy, and that she had confused the two because she had wanted to. A grieving woman will take whatever hand is offered her out of the water. She does not ask, in the moment, whose hand it is.

Victor Maas had come out of the water this morning, and the prosecutor was already saying it fits. Before the man was dry, before his mother knew.

The same speed.

She counted her breaths in threes and did not sleep, and below her the canal went on carrying the light up to the ceiling, the way it carried everything, the way it had carried her husband, the way it had carried a clerk named Victor Maas with two belts of police lead cinched at his spine by a hand that knew exactly how to make a thing stay down.

# Chapter Three — The Log

The dive shed at Zeeburg smelled of neoprene and salt, and at seven on a Sunday morning it was empty, which was why Sanne had come.

She let herself in with her own key. The lead was racked along the far wall, weight belts hanging from a steel rail like a row of dark waiting things, each one tagged, each one stamped. Everything in the work was accounting. Gear that could kill you was gear you kept count of; if a set of weights did not come back you wrote down why, because the water did not forgive an unkept account. Bram had raised her in that discipline. Count everything. Trust the count.

The log lived where it had always lived, a fat ring binder, because Bram did not trust the computer and kept the true record in his own square careful hand. She opened it on the bench under the light.

She had the serials, memorised on the quay in the white of the strobe and written on the inside of her wrist in the van, in ballpoint. Two belts. Two numbers.

She found the first serial on the fourth page she checked. It was there in Bram's hand, signed out eighteen months ago, and beside it in the return column, instead of a date and a countersignature, three words.

Lost — Amstel exercise.

The rain went on above her. The second serial took longer. It was signed out on a different date, four months later, and the return column said the same thing in the same square hand.

Lost — training. Not recovered.

Two team belts. Both signed out under Bram's name — as supervisor he signed out most things, that was procedure, half the entries on any page were in his hand. Both written off as lost. One of them lost, the log said, in the Amstel.

She made herself go slowly, because she knew what she wanted this to mean and wanting was how you drowned. Belts got lost; divers dumped weights and lived; you wrote it off. But lost belts stayed lost. Lead on the bottom of the Amstel did not climb out of the river and cross the city and cinch itself around the spine of a compliance clerk in the Kloveniersburgwal.

A civilian corpse was wearing two belts of police lead that the log said were lost. Which meant the log was wrong, or the log was a lie. Someone with access to this room had taken a belt off the rail and written lost beside its number and walked out with the lead under his coat, and no one would ever count it again, because a lost thing is a thing no one counts.

Someone inside.

"You're in early."

She did not startle. But something in her went very still and very cold, and she turned with the binder open in her hands and Bram was standing in the doorway in his old canvas jacket with two coffees, one in each hand, steam coming off them into the chilled air.

"I saw your bike," he said. "Thought you might want one of these." He set a coffee on the bench, near her, not too near. He did not look at the binder — he was very careful not to. "Couldn't sleep either?"

"No."

"No." He leaned against the tank rack and looked at her with those warm tired eyes. "You went and pulled the log."

There was no point in anything but the truth, or part of it. "The belts on Maas were ours. Team issue. I read the serials on the quay."

"I know they were ours." He said it easily, and it stopped her, that ease, the way a step that isn't there stops you. "I saw it too. I've already flagged it up the chain — Okonkwo can chase it. Half our old kit gets surplussed, Sanne. Lead's lead. It'll have an ordinary answer. They mostly do. Most of what looks like murder is just a sad man and cold water."

"The log says these two were lost. Not surplussed. Lost. In the water."

Something moved behind his face, very small and gone at once, but she had spent eleven years reading small movements in low light, and she saw it — the flicker of a man recalculating a sum he had thought was closed. Then he smiled, and the smile was sad, and the sadness was the worst of it because it was real, or looked so real she could not tell the false from the true.

"You've been up all night," he said gently, "chasing two numbers in my handwriting, and now you're standing in a cold room at seven on a Sunday building a story out of them. I know you. You get a wrongness in your head and you won't leave it, and mostly that's why you're the best I've got, because you don't leave things on the bottom." His voice dropped, and it was kind, it was so kind. "But you're doing the thing you did after Daan. You're taking a bad morning and you're making it carry more than it can hold. Maas is not Daan. This is not that. Give it to Okonkwo. Don't make this heavier than it is, Sanne."

She stood with the log open in her hands, and she thought: heavier. He had chosen the word. Of all the words in the language, that one. A man does not reach for weight, for lead, for the language of things that hold you down, unless the water is already in his mind, unless he has been thinking, somewhere below his own knowing, about how much it takes to make a thing stay.

"You're right," she said. "I'm tired."

"You are." Relief moved through him, small and warm. "Go home. Sleep. Let me close up."

She put the binder back exactly where it had been, square to the edge the way he liked things. She drank some of the coffee so he would see her drink it, and it was good coffee, and that was somehow the worst thing of all. Then she got her bike and rode home along the harbour in the fine grey rain.

But she had the two serials on the inside of her wrist, and she had seen the flicker behind his face, and she understood now — riding, counting her breaths in threes against the cold — that the thing she could not put down was not too heavy for her.

It was exactly as heavy as a man who did not want it lifted.

# Chapter Four — Nadia

The café was on the Nieuwmarkt, a brown bar with fogged windows, and from the table in the back corner Sanne could see the Waag across the square, and the water where they had brought Victor Maas up, forty metres away.

Nadia Okonkwo came in shaking rain off her coat and sat down and did not take the coat off, which told Sanne she meant this to be short.

"Off the record, like you asked," Nadia said. "I don't meet witnesses in cafés. So whatever this is, it's two people talking. Tell me why you're sure."

Sanne slid a card across the tile — the two serials, copied out in a steady hand. "The belts on Maas. Both team issue. This morning I checked them against our equipment log at the base. Both were signed out and then written off as lost. One of them recorded lost in the Amstel. The other in a training exercise. Neither was ever surplussed, sold, or recovered. According to our own paper, both of those belts are on the bottom of a river."

Nadia looked at the card. She did not pick it up. "Belts get written off."

"They do. But surplus lead has a paper trail. Sold, dated, signed. These were lost — and lost gear stays lost. It sits on the bottom. It doesn't come back up cinched around a man's spine with a dorsal release he couldn't reach." Sanne leaned in a fraction, no more. "For these two specific belts to be on Victor Maas, someone had to take them out of our racks, write 'lost' beside their numbers, and keep them. Someone with a key to that room. You don't write a thing off and then use it unless you needed it to be a thing nobody would ever count."

Nadia was quiet for a long time. Then she picked the card up. A defence could eat a diver's feeling about a buckle. A defence could not so easily eat a serial number written into a police log in a supervisor's own hand.

"This is very tidy," Nadia said, "for a suicide."

"Yes."

"Suicides aren't tidy. They're the messiest thing I see. People think a decision that size makes a clean shape. It doesn't. It leaves the stove on and half a letter and one shoe by the door." She pressed the card flat under one finger. "All right. Two serials and a story is not a case. But it's a thread, and it's a thread that goes into your own shed, which is exactly the kind of thread everybody in that debrief would like me not to pull." The tired flick again. "Which is a good reason to pull it."

"Then pull it from Maas's end," Sanne said. "Not the shed. Not yet." The belts led back into a building full of people who had watched a fast ruling written on her husband once already. "Find out who he was. A compliance clerk doesn't get weighted with police lead for stress leave. Somebody did that to him. So somebody had a reason, and the reason is in his life, not on the bottom of the Kloveniersburgwal."

Nadia took out a notebook. "Victor Maas. Kanttekening Notariaat, in the Jordaan. Compliance." She looked up. "You know the firm?"

"No." Sanne heard herself go on, because it was true and because Nadia would find it anyway. "But I know the world. My husband worked compliance. Anti-fraud, anti-money-laundering, the checks. He said the notary offices were where the money went to get clean. That a bent notaris could make anything look legal — a sale, a transfer, a company built to hold nothing but somebody else's money, all of it signed and stamped and true on paper. He said the whole city ran on it and nobody looked, because a notaris was a person you trusted by definition. That's the job. That's the whole job. To be trusted."

Nadia had stopped writing. "Your husband. He's—"

"Dead. Two years. A diving accident. In the Amstel. It was ruled fast, the way this is being ruled fast. He was a compliance man who died in the water and got a quick clean ruling. Now there's a compliance man who died in the water and he's getting a quick clean ruling. And the belts that held the second one down are logged as lost in the same river that took the first."

She had not meant to lay it out like that. But it was out now, and she watched Nadia not dismiss it. She did not soften her face into the careful patience people used on the bereaved. She sat with it. She let it be heavy, and she did not set it down.

"What was his name," Nadia said.

"Daan Kessler."

Nadia wrote it under Victor Maas, and drew a line between the two names, and Sanne felt the cold that comes when a shape you have refused to see finishes assembling itself in the dark.

"I'm not promising you anything," Nadia said. "I look at Maas. If your husband's in that water too, I'll see it, but I'm not going in looking for him, because that's how you find things that aren't there. You go in wanting a shape and the dark gives you the shape you brought." She closed the notebook on the line she had drawn. "But I'm going to look at Kanttekening. And I'm going to want that log."

"You'll have it."

At the door Nadia paused. "Your supervisor. Verhoeven. Does he know you copied those numbers?"

Sanne thought of the flicker behind Bram's face in the cold room. The eleven years of the hand on her line; the towel warm from his own coat. She found she could not make the sentence come. If she said it aloud she would be doing to Bram exactly what the room had done to her — writing a ruling before the man was dry.

"He knows I looked at the log," she said. "He thinks it's grief."

"Is it?"

"Ask me in a week."

# Chapter Five — The Room

Victor Maas had lived on the third floor of a narrow house on the Nieuwe Prinsengracht, up a staircase built for people who owned less than he did. Nadia had a key from the huisbaas. She'd told Sanne to come as a civilian, hands in pockets and mouth shut, and Sanne had agreed because the alternative was sitting at home with the belts in her head.

The door opened onto order.

That was the first thing. Not the poverty of it, not the smallness — the order. A single room with a kitchen along one wall, a bed made tight, a desk under the window that looked out over the canal. Books shelved by height. A row of shoes lined up by the door, toes to the wall. A single mug upturned on a folded cloth by the sink, dry.

"He lived alone," Nadia said. It wasn't a question.

"He lived carefully."

The room told her things in the language she knew. A man who kept his shoes at the door and his bed hard and his books in rank was a man who managed fear by managing objects. She'd done it herself, the year after Daan. There was a smoke detector on the ceiling and a second one, newer, screwed into the wall by the bed at eye height, which no ordinary tenant does. There was a wooden wedge on the floor by the door, the kind you kick under to stop it opening. It had been used — not once, but a pale worried patch of scarring on the door edge, weeks of it, months.

"He was frightened," Sanne said.

"People are frightened of a lot of things."

"He was frightened of the door."

"Eleven months here," Nadia said. "Paid a year up front. Kept himself to himself. Nobody knew him. A man pays a year in advance and screws a second smoke detector into the wall at eye level and wedges his own door, and nobody knows him. That's not a private man. That's a man in hiding who's decided hiding looks like privacy."

Sanne took the room in threes, the way she read a wreck — measured passes. The shoes, the wedge, the two smoke detectors. The window locks, all engaged, on the third floor. The absence: no photographs on the walls except one, no cards, no evidence anyone had ever been let in.

The desk held a laptop, closed. Nadia opened it with one finger. It woke to a message in white on black that meant the disk had been wiped — not deleted, wiped.

"Clean," Nadia said. "Professionally. Someone came here. Or he did it himself. Either way there's nothing on it."

Sanne stood at the window and thought about how a careful man protects the thing he is afraid of losing. Not on the machine they'll take. Somewhere else. Somewhere stupid and safe.

"He mailed things to himself," she said.

Nadia turned. "What?"

"Daan did it. He was a compliance man too. When he found something he didn't trust to a file he'd email it to himself. To an address nobody knew about. He said paper burns and servers get subpoenaed but a man's own outbox is just noise until you know to look."

They worked the room like a search grid, methodical, overlapping. The books were only books — Dutch history, tax law, the notarial code. Nadia found the phone bill in a drawer, folded in thirds, and a second bill under it for a webmail service Victor paid for annually, in cash, by voucher — a provider in Iceland that answered to no Dutch warrant and advertised exactly that. There was a username printed on the receipt. There was no password anywhere, and Nadia said passwords never were, and Sanne, looking at the row of books by height and the shoes by the door, said try the boat.

"What boat?"

"He grew up on a boat. Look." Sanne pointed without touching: the one photograph in the room, curled at one corner, a boy on the deck of a small cruiser, a woman's hand on his shoulder, a name painted on the transom behind them. *Aaltje.* "A careful man uses the one word he'll never forget and never write down anywhere it means anything."

Nadia typed it into the Iceland provider's login. It took the boat's name and a year Sanne guessed from the boy's age and a third try with the two joined, and then the screen filled with a mailbox, and the mailbox was almost empty, because Victor had been careful there too. He'd deleted as he went. But he'd been human, and humans miss the drafts folder, and in the drafts folder were nine messages he had written to himself and never sent, because sending leaves a line and saving doesn't.

Nadia read them standing up and didn't share for a long minute.

"Addresses," she said at last. "It's a list of addresses. Canal houses. Grachtenpanden. Same six, over and over, in every draft, with numbers next to them. Two numbers each." She turned the phone so Sanne could see, and Sanne read the first line and felt something in her chest reset itself to a colder setting.

Herengracht. Then a figure. Then, in brackets, a second figure less than half the first.

"That's a price," Sanne said. "Two prices. What a house sold for. And what it's worth." She knew it the way you know there's a wall ahead of you in black water before your hand finds it. "Daan used to say the same word for it. He'd tap the table. He said the whole trick is the gap. Everything's in the gap."

Nadia photographed the screen, the receipt, the boy on the boat, and she said, quiet, the way you'd close a door on a sleeping child:

"Then tomorrow we go and stand in the gap."

# Chapter Six — The Houses

The first house was on the Herengracht and it was beautiful and no one lived in it.

Nadia had parked the department car three bridges down and they walked, because a walking woman is furniture in this city and a parked car is a question. The cold had come down hard overnight; the canal had begun to skin at its edges.

The windows were tall and clean and behind them nothing — no curtains, no plants, no single object that says a life has agreed to happen here. The doormat was still in its plastic. Someone had spent a great deal of money making this house perfect and then had walked away and left it perfect and empty.

"Sold last year," Nadia said, reading from her phone, from the Kadaster, the public land registry. "For four point one million. Before that, sold eighteen months earlier for one point nine. Renovated in between, they'll say. Sold again four months ago. Four point four. Buyer's a company. The company's owner is another company."

"And Victor's number?"

"Victor's number says it's worth two." She looked up at the gable. "He wrote the real value next to it. Every time. The recorded one climbs the ladder and the real one just stands here on the pavement being a house."

Sanne understood it then, fully, the way cold finds a seam: the house was not a house. It was a receipt. You bought it with money that had a smell, you sold it to yourself through a company you also owned, and each time it changed hands the paper said it was worth more, and the paper was signed and stamped and true in the only sense the law cared about, and at the end you had four million clean euros and a story about a renovation to explain them. The house never had to be lived in. Living in it would only wear it out.

"Daan called it ironing," Sanne said. "You run the dirty thing through something respectable enough to press it flat."

They walked the rest. Threaded through the ordinary morning city, one after another, the empty perfect houses, each one a held breath. The third, on the Reguliersgracht, had a small tasteful sign in the window, a name in grey capitals over a phone number: *SLOOTWEG VASTGOED.* The fourth was the same sign again. So was the sixth.

"One agent," Nadia said. "Same name on three of them."

"Selling houses nobody buys to live in."

"Selling them very well."

They came back along the Reguliersgracht at the end of it, past the third house, and a man was standing on the step now who had not been there before, locking the door or unlocking it, and he did the small human thing of turning to see who was passing, and then he did a slightly larger thing, which was to smile at them as though he had been expecting exactly these two women all his life.

"You're admiring the house," he said. "Everyone does. It's the proportions. 1671. The width is the whole secret — the wider the front, the richer the man who built it." He came down one step. He was perhaps fifty, silver at the temples, a coat that had cost what Sanne earned in a month. He wore no gloves in the cold, which she noticed, a man who wanted his hands seen. "Ruud Slootweg. Are you looking? I can always tell when someone's looking."

"Just walking," Nadia said.

"Nobody just walks past this water. They walk to it." His eyes went over Sanne, unhurried, professional, the way a man reads a room he's about to sell. "The house is available, if you'd like to see inside. Though I warn you, it's empty. I tell them an empty house is honest. It's not pretending to be anyone's home."

Behind him, through the tall clean glass, Sanne could see the emptiness he was selling: bare floorboards sanded pale, walls the flat white of a gallery between shows. A beautiful box. A place money went to change its name.

"How much?" Sanne asked. It was the first thing she'd said.

He turned the full weight of himself onto her, and it was warm, and it was a searchlight. "Four and a half. For you, I'd find a reason it was less." A beat, held a moment too long to be only charm. "Do you dive?"

The water went very quiet around Sanne. She did not move. She had a diver's stillness, taught by years of hanging on a line doing nothing while your air ran down, and she used it now, and it cost her something to use it.

"Your hands," he said, gesturing, easy. "The little scars on the knuckles. My brother sails. Sailors and divers have the same hands — the sea keeps writing on them." He was already stepping back up, already elsewhere, the way a man leaves a conversation he has decided he has won. "It's a small city. Everyone's hands say something. I hope you find whatever you're walking to." He put his key in the lock. "The water's cold this time of year. Even for people who like it cold."

He went inside and shut the door of the empty house, and they watched him not turn on a light, because there was no reason to.

Nadia let out a breath that fogged and thinned. "He clocked you."

"He clocked both of us." A man who reads hands for a living had read hers and decided to tell her he'd read them. That wasn't charm. Charm was the wrapping. "He wanted us to know he knew. That's a message."

"To who?"

"To whoever he calls when we're gone."

They walked back the way they'd come, unhurried too, because to hurry now was to answer him. And Sanne thought about the gap — real value, recorded value — and about a compliance clerk who'd written six addresses to himself nine times and then been fitted with team-issue belts and dropped into the cold, and she understood that she and Nadia had just walked straight up to the edge of the same gap Daan had fallen into, and smiled at the man standing on the far side of it.

# Chapter Seven — Daan's Shadow

The box had been in the hold cupboard for two years, behind the vacuum cleaner and the folded drying rack, in the exact place you put a thing when you cannot throw it away and cannot look at it either.

Sanne took it down at eleven at night because standing in the empty house on the Reguliersgracht had loosened something she'd spent two years tightening. She set it on the kitchen table of the houseboat and sat in front of it with her hands flat on the lid, counting her breaths in threes. The boat moved a little under her, the water shifting its weight beneath the hull.

She took the lid off.

His reading glasses, one arm mended with heat-shrink. His watch, stopped. His work lanyard, KANTTEKENING NOTARIAAT in blue. She laid each thing on the table in a row, spaced even, and only when she'd done it did she hear Victor's shoes by the door in her head, toes to the wall, and understand that she and a dead stranger arranged their fear the same way.

Under the personal things were the papers.

She had not read them then — could not, the words swimming, the coroner's fast clean sentences about *misadventure* and *equipment consistent with*, which she had signed for and shelved because to argue was to keep him dying.

She read them now. Not the coroner's. Daan's own. A folder he'd kept at home, which the police had glanced at and returned because it was only work, only a compliance man's dull notes.

His handwriting. Small, upright, level, the letters not touching, a hand that never hurried. Lists. A column of addresses down the left of a page, and beside each a figure, and beside that a second figure in brackets, less than half.

Herengracht. Keizersgracht. Reguliersgracht.

The same houses.

Not all six — four of the six. Two years before Victor wrote them into an unsent email, Daan had written them here, in pencil, with the same two numbers beside each, the real and the recorded, the gap. He'd drawn a small box around the word *waardering.* Valuation. He'd underlined it once. Daan underlined things once; a second line meant he was certain, and there were pages further in with second lines.

He had worked at Kanttekening six years, reading other people's contracts for the lie in them. He had liked the work and mistrusted the place. *Something isn't right there and I can't yet say what.* She had heard it and half-heard it over dinners, the way you hear a partner's work. She had not known it was worth dying over.

A name recurred. Not Slootweg — Slootweg was there, once, in a margin, a question mark after it, the way you note a face you don't yet have a place for. The name that recurred, that Daan had written and boxed and come back to, was the office and the man who ran it.

*Aalving. C. Aalving. — the signature is always his. Everything passes his desk. Nothing this size moves without the notary.*

And below, later, the pencil pressed harder, the letters standing up off the page:

*The notary doesn't launder the money. The notary launders the paper. He makes the lie legal. That's what a notary is for — to make a thing true because he says it's true. He's not hiding it. He's certifying it.*

She could hear him saying it, quiet and precise, tapping the table once on *certifying.*

She turned the last pages slowly, hands steady. Daan's notes stopped being about houses and started being about who to tell. He'd written and crossed out a name at the police. He'd written *not through work — through someone I trust.* And then, near the end, in a hand very slightly less level than the rest, the only place in all those pages where his writing showed the smallest tremor:

*Told B. today. He knew Kanttekening from years back — said he'd help me take it up the right way, quietly, so it doesn't get buried. Relief to say it out loud to someone who understands the water this all sits in. B says give him the weekend.*

That was the last dated line. The date was four days before Daan drowned.

Sanne read it three times. She did not yet let the initial become a name. She had trained herself, over two years, not to let grief supply facts, because grief was a generous witness and lied to comfort you. *B* could be anyone. Amsterdam was full of B's. She wrote it down on a clean sheet in her own hand, *B — knew Kanttekening — 4 days before*, and set the pen down and found her hands were steady, and that steadiness was the coldest thing in the room.

Because the floor had already gone. Daan had not loaded his belt wrong. Daan had found exactly what Victor found, two years earlier, four houses of the same six, the same notary, the same gap — and then he had told someone, someone he trusted, someone who understood the water, and four days later he had gone into the Amstel alone and not come out, and the department had ruled it in a week.

The same as Victor. The same speed. The same water. The same weights.

She had spent two years apologising to him in her head for not checking his gear that morning. And it had been the wrong grief the whole time, grief for an accident that was not an accident.

She put her palm flat on the pencil column of addresses and stayed like that for a long time while the boat moved under her.

Then she got up, and put everything back in the box except the papers, and put the papers in a dry-bag, because she was a diver and that was what you did with the thing you could not afford to lose. The ordinariness of the motion — a diver bagging her kit — steadied her more than any thought could have.

Tomorrow she would need the case photographs — Daan's, from two years ago, which she had never once looked at. She wanted to see how the belt had been arranged on him. She wanted to see the knots.

# Chapter Eight — The Signature

The dive-team locker room at seven in the morning was empty, which was why Sanne had come at seven.

She'd signed the Maas evidence out of the property store the night before, on Nadia's requisition, one police diver checking the recovered equipment of another drowning. Two team-issue weight belts, serial-stamped. She laid them on the long bench under the strip light and did not touch them yet. She looked first, the way you always looked first, a full slow pass before your hands went in and changed anything.

You could tell a diver by his belt the way you could tell a sailor by his knots. It was not sentiment; it was habit worn into muscle. Every diver on the team did it a fixed way and never thought about it, and if you swapped their belts blind they'd feel the wrongness before they saw it.

She photographed Victor's belts as they were, then took them apart slowly, logging each step in her own hand. The load itself was strange — a man Victor's size, in fresh water, in ordinary clothes, needed a fraction of it to go down and stay down. She knew the numbers the way she knew her own weight. This was not weight to sink a man. This was weight to keep a body on the bottom past the day it would otherwise rise. Whoever had done it knew how long gas builds in a corpse and had loaded against it, deliberately, with a margin. That was not a panicking hand. That was a technician.

She looked at how the blocks were seated. Not centred. Pushed to the wearer's right and snugged with a half-hitch of the tail through the outboard block before the buckle took the load — a way of stopping the weights from sliding that no manual taught and that Sanne had seen exactly one diver on this team do, because he'd shown her, years ago, his hands under hers on a cold boat on a grey morning, *like this, meisje, so they don't wander on you.* Her stomach did something small and cold and she did not look at it. She wrote down only what she saw. *Weights offset right. Half-hitch of tail through outboard weight, then buckle. Free end dressed short, tucked under standing part, right to left.*

Then she opened Daan's file.

Nadia had pulled it — a two-year-old accidental-drowning case, closed, the photographs sealed in a paper sleeve Sanne had never asked to see, because you do not show a diver her husband on the bottom of the Amstel. She'd asked now. Nadia had brought it without a word and put it in her hands with both of hers, the way you hand someone something heavy, and had said only, *I'll be outside if you want me,* and gone.

She spread the prints on the bench beside Victor's belts under the same hard light, and she made herself look at the equipment and not the man. It was the hardest looking she had ever done. Breath in threes. The work held her the way the line had held her.

His belt was in three of the photographs.

The coroner had written *equipment consistent with self-loading, weights unevenly distributed, consistent with an inexperienced or impaired diver.* She had signed under those words two years ago without reading them. Inexperienced. Daan, who had logged four hundred dives in a hand like a monk's. She looked now at the uneven distribution the report had used to bury him and saw it was not uneven. It was offset. Pushed to the wearer's right. And there, in the third photograph, in reasonable focus because some patient forensic technician two years ago had done his job even if no one had read it, was the tail of the webbing: run through the outboard weight in a half-hitch, then the buckle, the free end dressed short and tucked under the standing part, right to left.

The same hand.

Not the same method — the same *hand.* Method you could copy. This was signature. This was a thing a man's fingers did without him, below thought, worn in over years until it was as much his as his walk — the same on Daan's belt in the Amstel two years ago as on Victor's belt in the Kloveniersburgwal last week, a small unteachable habit repeated across two years and two dead men who had both found the same six houses and the same notary and the same gap.

Sanne stood very still with the two drownings laid out in front of her and let the cold finish arriving, not all at once but steadily, past every layer, until it was simply the temperature you were.

A diver had done this. Not a fixer in a good coat, not a notary with a clean pen — those men signed paper. This was signed in webbing, by someone who loaded a belt the way you load one in the dark, from muscle, from years. Someone on the team, or close enough to the team to own the habit and the serial-stamped belts and the knowledge of how long a body waits before it wants to rise. Someone who had been trusted with a safety line. Both times. Someone Daan had known. Someone who understood the water this all sat in.

She did not let the initial from Daan's pencil become a name. She had promised herself that, and she kept the promise now because it was the only discipline left that was entirely hers. Grief would hand her the name gladly. It was already reaching for it. She would not take it from grief. She would take it from the water or not at all.

She wrote instead, in her own level hand under the two logs, the only thing she was certain of:

*Same configuration. Same hand. A diver's hand. Daan did not drown. Victor did not drown. They were weighted by one of us.*

She sealed Daan's prints back into their sleeve, and Victor's belts into their bags, and logged the seals. Everything in order. Everything where the paper could hold it.

Somewhere in the building a door opened and boots came down a corridor, unhurried, familiar, a man arriving early the way he always did, first in, last out, the one who checked the checkers. Sanne closed the file and arranged her face into the one she wore on the line, flat and ready and giving nothing to the water, because whoever the hand belonged to, he was still on her team, and he was still, every time she went down, the one holding the other end of her rope.

# Chapter Nine — The Mail

Victor Maas had a sister. Marije, in Amstelveen — the next of kin the fast file had phoned once and filed. Nadia drove them out through the rain and said almost nothing on the way, which meant she was thinking hard about the thing neither of them had said out loud yet: that the hand that had rigged Victor knew the water and knew the department and had access to both.

Marije Maas lived in a ground-floor flat with a child's tricycle in the hall. She was younger than Victor had been, softer in the face, and she had cried already — that was plain — and put herself back together for the doorbell.

"Police," she said. "Again."

"Not the same police," Nadia said, which was true in a way that Sanne was only beginning to understand.

They sat in a small front room with too many cushions.

"Victor sent me a key," Marije said, when they got there. "Two weeks ago. In an envelope, no letter. I thought it was a mistake. He did things like that when we were kids — sent me things, made me guess." She looked at the window. "I thought it was a joke I didn't understand yet."

"Do you have it," Sanne said. First thing she had said.

Marije went out of the room. They heard a kitchen drawer, the rattle of small objects. She came back with a flat key on a paper tag, the kind of key that opens a coin locker. On the tag, in the small square hand Sanne had seen on Victor's compliance memos, a number. 114.

"He called me the night before," Marije said. "He said if anything happened I should give the key to a policeman I trusted. Not just any policeman. One I trusted." She laughed, once, without any humour in it at all. "As if I know any policemen. As if trusting one is a thing a person can arrange."

"Where does it open," Nadia asked.

"Central Station. The left-luggage on the east side, the old ones, not the machines." She finally looked up, first at Nadia and then, for longer, at Sanne. "Are you the one I trust? Because I don't know how to tell."

Sanne closed her hand around the key. She thought of telling the woman a comfortable thing, and did not, because Marije Maas had already been told comfortable things by people in coats and one of those things had been that her brother drowned by accident.

"No," she said. "But I'm the one who's going to find who did this. That's not the same thing. It might be enough."

At the door Marije caught Sanne's sleeve, once, briefly, and let it go. "He was careful," she said. "My brother. He was the most careful man I ever knew. If he was afraid, you should be afraid."

⁂

Locker 114 was at knee height in the old bank of left-luggage boxes on the east concourse of Centraal, behind a pillar where the light didn't reach. The station roared around them. Nadia stood blocking the sightline from the concourse, a way of standing that read as waiting for a train and was in fact a perimeter. The key turned stiff and then gave. Inside, a padded envelope, A4, unlabelled, taped along the seam three times over. It was heavier than paper. Sanne put it inside her coat against her ribs and locked the empty door.

They opened it in the Opel in the P1 garage under the station, doors locked, the concrete pressing down over them. Inside was a laptop, an old business machine with a corporate asset tag scratched half off, and beneath it a printed spreadsheet folded twice, forty pages of it, and a single sheet of lined paper covered in Victor's square hand.

Nadia took the paper. Sanne took the spreadsheet and opened it across the wheel.

It was, at first, only columns. Addresses — grachtenpanden, the whole roll of them. Then dates. Then two columns of money side by side, and the difference between them worked out in a third, and the third column was the one that mattered. Recorded value. Real value. Delta. Some of the deltas were small. Most were not. One house on the Herengracht showed a delta of one point one million euro. Page after page, a decade of them, the quiet arithmetic of a city being bought with money that had to be made to have never existed.

At the head of the fourth column, initials. C.A. against the notarial line, again and again, page after page. And in the column beside it, the front man, the buyer of record who was never the buyer: R.S. R.S. R.S. Ruud Slootweg had put his charming name to eleven canal houses in four years and had, on paper, the wealth of a small country and, in the world, a leased BMW and a flat he rented.

"Aalving," Sanne said. "Cornelis Aalving. De Notaris. His office signs it, his valuation carries it, and Slootweg fronts the purchase." Her voice had gone flat and careful. "This is it. This is the whole machine on forty pages."

Nadia hadn't spoken. She was reading the lined sheet, Victor's own words, and her face had gone somewhere Sanne didn't like.

"Read it," Sanne said.

Nadia read it. "'If you are reading this I am probably dead and it will be called an accident. It will be called drowning. It was called drowning for the other one too. Kessler.'"

The garage was very quiet. Sanne did not move. The name sat in the car with them, her own name, her dead husband's name, in a stranger's hand.

"Go on," she said.

"'The valuations are the smallest part. Anyone can find the valuations if they look. The part no one finds is that the accidents are managed. The recoveries are managed. When they put someone in the water it is done by someone who controls what happens after — the search, the finding, the ruling. Someone inside. I don't have the name. I only know the water is not an accident. The water is a service. Whoever runs the recoveries runs the killing. Find that person and you have all of them.'"

Nadia lowered the sheet.

There was no name for the inside man. Victor had died without it. He had known there was a hand in the water and he had not lived long enough to learn whose.

But Sanne knew the shape of the hand. She had seen its work twice now, the same weighting, the same economy, the same knowledge of exactly how a body sits when a search team comes for it. Someone who controlled the search. Someone who signed the belts out. Someone who ruled the water an accident and made it stick. The knowledge was lower than a thought yet, a pressure behind the sternum, and she kept it there.

"We have all of them," she said. "Except the one who matters most."

# Chapter Ten — Narrowing

The dive store was the one room in the building with no window, which Sanne had always liked and now, standing in it alone at seven in the morning, found she did not. She had the belt log open on the bench — belt number, diver, date, job, the dullest document in the building. It was, Sanne now understood, a confession, if you read it the right way. She had spent the night with Victor's spreadsheet and Victor's letter, and she had made a list, and the list was short.

Who could manage a recovery from inside? Not a junior. It had to be someone who could take a call, put a team in the water, direct the search along the line he chose, and stand at the supervisor's shoulder when the ruling was written — or write it himself. Someone who signed the belts out and could lose one in the paper. Someone who knew the water so well he could put a man in it and know to the metre where he would be when they came looking, because he was the one who came looking.

That was three people on the whole team. Then it was two, because Willemsen had been in Groningen the week Victor died, and the roster proved it. Then it was one, because the second man, De Wit, had joined the team eight months ago and had never met Daan Kessler in his life, and whoever had done this had known Daan well enough that Daan confided in him. Victor had died a stranger to the water. Daan had died a friend of it. That was the thing that turned the case from a canal into a corridor with one door at the end.

That left the name at the top of the belt log, the name on the spine of the binder in his own careful capitals, the name that had signed out belts 07 and 09 — the two on Victor; she had checked the serials against the recovery inventory at three in the morning, twice, because the first time she had put the torch down and stood in her kitchen and been unable to move.

BRAM VERHOEVEN.

⁂

She did not want it. She wanted it less than she had ever wanted anything.

Bram had carried her coffin-side at Daan's funeral. Bram had sat in her kitchen for three nights running after, saying nothing, large and warm and present. Bram had been the one who told her, thirteen years ago, breathe in threes, Sanne, threes, when she had panicked at nine metres in the Amstel, and she had counted in threes every dive since, so that his voice was inside the most basic function of her body, teaching her to stay alive.

She stood with the binder open to his handwriting and thought: no. It was the body's no, the animal one. It changed nothing. It never had.

And then she thought about the hand in the water. The way the belts had sat on Victor exactly as they had sat on Daan — not the way a manual specified, but the way a particular man rigged them, snugged with the half-hitch he'd told her once was quicker, his own trick; he'd shown her that half-hitch himself with his big fingers years ago on a training dock and said, nobody else does it this way, this is the Verhoeven way, and laughed. She could hear the laugh. That was the worst part — that she could hear the laugh and it was still, in her body, a good sound.

The half-hitch had been on Daan. She had seen it two years ago and not understood it. The half-hitch had been on Victor. She had photographed it herself, three days ago, and not seen it until now.

Nobody else does it this way.

She closed the binder very quietly and put it back exactly where it lived, spine squared to the shelf edge, and left the store the way she had found it, which was a thing she knew how to do because she had learned it from the man who had made a career of leaving the water the way he found it.

⁂

She told Nadia in the Opel because there was no room in the building she trusted. She told it flat, the belts, the half-hitch, the roster, the three-then-two-then-one, and Nadia listened without interrupting and at the end put both hands on the wheel.

"You're sure," Nadia said.

"I taught my body to breathe with his voice. Threes. I've counted in threes on every dive for thirteen years. I counted in threes over Victor's body. Yes. I'm sure. I've never been sure of anything I wanted less."

Nadia nodded slowly. She did not say she was sorry. "We have the pattern. We don't have proof a court takes. The half-hitch is testimony, not evidence — divers copy each other's habits; you learned it off him yourself. The serials put the belts on the team, not on him personally. He'll say they were signed out and lost, and the log's his own paper; he can burn it tonight and who'd know." She was thinking out loud. "We need him near the water on a night he shouldn't have been. We need a man in a place he can't explain."

⁂

Joke was on her deck when Sanne came home in the last light, the old woman two moorings down, wrapped in a man's overcoat, watching the Kloveniersburgwal as though the canal owed her an explanation. "You look like your husband," she said, "the week before he stopped coming round." She said it without cruelty or softness, a woman who had outlived both.

Sanne sat on the edge of the neighbouring mooring and heard herself say, "Joke. The night the man was found up the canal here. Ten days ago. Did you see anything on the water that night."

Joke was quiet long enough that Sanne thought she hadn't heard. "Small boat," she said at last. "One of those flat aluminium ones, an outboard, no lights, which you're not to do. Late — two, three in the morning; I don't sleep. Big man in it. Slow past here and slow back an hour later, and lower in the water going back than coming, which I noticed, because I notice; that's all I've got left is noticing. Your dive people have a boat like that. Grey. I thought, at three in the morning, that's odd. The dive people don't work at three."

"The man," Sanne said. Her mouth had gone dry. "Could you—"

"Big. Grey hair, what there was of it, a beard. Careful the way big men are careful, that trained way. He knew the boat." She turned her old face fully to Sanne, and something in it had understood the question before Sanne had. "It's a someone, isn't it. Not a something. You've got the look. Your husband had the look."

Lower in the water going back than coming. A man who went out at three with nothing and came back an hour later carrying enough extra weight to sit the hull down. Sanne knew what weighed that much. She had lifted it, twice, off the bottom, into the light.

"Would you say it," Sanne said. "Later. To someone who wrote it down."

"I'm eighty-one," Joke said, "and I've buried everyone who could make me afraid. Yes." She turned back to the canal. "Get him."

Sanne sat on the mooring after Joke went in, with the man who had comforted her through the worst thing that had ever happened to her now standing in her mind exactly where she could no longer un-stand him — over her husband, in the dark, on the water, cinching the belt with the half-hitch he had taught her body to trust. He had sat in her kitchen with Daan's blood not a week cold and been kind, and the kindness had been real, that was the thing she could not get her hands around — real, and it had cost him nothing, because he had already decided the water was a service and Daan had already been paid for. She sat and let the cold find the seams, patient, personal, and counted her breaths, and could not stop counting them in threes, and hated, for the first time in her life, the man whose voice made her do it.

# Chapter Eleven — Pressure

Bram ran the morning board the way he always ran it, reading glasses down his nose, moving men and jobs around the week, and the team liked him. Tim was in the second row with his mug, laughing at something, young and open-faced and glad to be there, and Sanne looked at the back of his head and something in her went careful.

"Kessler," Bram said, not looking up from the board. "You're off the water this week."

She kept her face still. "Why."

"Occupational health." He capped the marker. Now he looked at her, over the glasses, and his face was exactly the face it had always been. "Two recoveries in a fortnight, both hard ones, and one of them—" a beat, delicate, paternal "—close to home, near enough. The Maas case has all the shape of the Amstel again. I know what that costs you, Sanne. I'm not going to let it cost more."

It was flawless because it was ninety per cent true — the truth carried the lie the way water carried a body, holding it up, hiding it in plain view. He was genuinely worried about her, and he was also taking her off the water, off the case, at the exact moment she had begun to move toward him, with a reason no one in the room could argue.

"Tim, you'll pair with De Wit on the Amstelkanaal survey. Kessler's on dry duties. Log audit, gear service." A small pause. "The belt log wants doing, actually. It's a mess. You could sort the belt log."

The room did not hear it. Sanne heard it. He was telling her he knew she had been in the log, and he was putting her back into it in daylight, watched, sanctioned, where anything she found became a thing she had been told to find and touched with her own hands, and where he could see her do it. He had turned the one hard document in the building into a thing she could not use.

"Fine," she said. "I'll sort the belt log."

"Good girl," Bram said, the way he had said it for thirteen years, and it landed on her now like a hand closing.

⁂

He came to find her in the store at the end of the day. The roller door was down. The base had emptied, and she was aware, in a way she had never once been in this room in thirteen years, that no one knew exactly where she was.

"You found the serials," he said.

He said it kindly. He had shut the door behind him. The store had no window. She had liked that once.

"Yes," she said. There was no profit in the other thing.

"Course you did. You were always the best of them at reading a thing." He smiled, and it reached his eyes, and that was the worst of it, that it reached his eyes. "Let me say a thing, and then I'll go and let you work. About the water." He looked at her, level, warm, terrible. "You have to trust the person on your line completely. Absolutely. Because they decide, Sanne. When you're at the bottom with a body and no light, the person holding your line is the only one in the world who decides whether you come up. That's the whole job. That's the whole thing. Trust on the line."

The strip light buzzed. She kept her hands flat on the binder so they would not do anything.

"I've held your line more times than either of us can count," Bram said gently. "Since you were a frightened girl at nine metres in the Amstel who forgot how to breathe. Threes, I told you. Remember? And you came up. You've always come up because I was on your line. I'd like you to remember that. Whatever the log says. Whatever you think you've read in it." He shrugged into his coat, big and slow and careful, the trained way, the way Joke had described a man in a boat with no lights. "Get some rest. You look like your husband looked, that last week. I didn't like seeing it on him. I like it less on you."

He left. Sanne stood in the windowless room and made her lungs do it slowly, and would not let the count go to threes, and it went to threes anyway. He had told her he was on her line. He had told her he had been on Daan's line too, that last week. He had said both things in the same voice, the kind one, and let her sort out which was the warning.

That afternoon, before he found her, they had moved the evidence out of every room he could reach. # Chapter Fourteen — The Line

The haul took her four metres off the bottom before she stopped fighting it.

Fighting it was the mistake, and her body wanted to make the mistake. Her lungs tried the panic thing, fast and shallow, and she took them by the collar the way she had been taking them by the collar since she was twenty-four. One. Two. Three. She stopped kicking, let herself be reeled, went limp in the harness so he'd feel less through the rope, and went to work with her hands in the dark where he could not see them.

Then Bram cut her air.

He did it from above — a valve he had rigged, the manifold, she never knew which — because the regulator in her mouth went from the easy pull of good air to the hard suck of nothing between one breath and the next, and then a mouthful of water, cold and mineral and tasting of the river. She was inside the killing now, in the room where it happened, and the room was her own body.

She cut the line.

In the dark she counted. Not breaths now — distance. Nine metres of horizontal from the shot line, north, on a bearing she could swim back on blind, a count she'd been keeping under the count of her breaths the whole way in without letting Bram hear her keep it. She had a choice, and she made it in the cold with her hand on Tim's tank.

She could go up the shot line in the dark and take Bram in the water from below, catch his boots and drag him into her element and hold him under the way he had held Daan, and no one would ever prove it wasn't the river. # Chapter Sixteen — The Weigh House

Six weeks on, De Waag stood in the middle of the Nieuwmarkt the way it had stood for five hundred years, dark brick and pointed towers, the old weigh house where the city had once brought everything that could be bought and set it on the scales to learn the truth of it. Sanne sat at a table by the window with a coffee going cold and thought about weight.

They had weighed things here for centuries — butter and cheese and lead, everything the city bought and sold hauled up onto the scale so that no one need take a seller's word for what a thing was. The building had always been about the same stubborn thing: the civic faith that a thing had a true weight, that you could find it if you were honest and had the right scale and the patience to wait for the beam to settle, and that the number, once you had it, did not care in the least who you were.

Aalving's people had bet against that faith. They had bet that a valuation was only ever a story someone told, and that with the right notary and the right fixer and the right man inside the police dive team, you could write any number you liked beside a grachtenpand and no scale on earth would rise up and contradict you. For two years they had been exactly, profitably right. They had been wrong about the scale. It was slow, and it was old, and it had needed a diver to go down into the black and bring up what had been weighted to the bottom on purpose to hide it from the beam. But it had held.

Nadia came in stamping the cold off her boots and sat down without being asked. She had a habit now of finding Sanne on the afternoons Sanne didn't dive. Neither of them called it what it was. It did not need calling.

"Slootweg's turned," Nadia said. "Wants a deal. He's giving us the whole structure — the front purchases, the offshore chain, two more houses we didn't have on the board. Aalving's lawyers are trying to sever the murder counts from the fraud. They won't manage it. The weighting signature does it; your pathologist won't move a centimetre. The trial's next spring. You'll have to give evidence. About the dive. The confession. About—" and she left the rest of it in the water where it had been sitting between them since the night on the bank.

"I know what I'll have to say." Sanne wrapped both hands round the cold cup. "I'll say all of it. The three strokes too. If they ask me whether I could have reached him — and they'll have to, it's the only card they've got — I'll say yes. I could have reached him. And I stayed where I was." She met Nadia's eyes and did not look away. "I'd rather live the rest of it having told the truth about that than having told myself any of the kinder things instead. The truth has a weight too. I'd rather carry the real one."

Nadia held her look a long moment, then nodded, once, and let it go.

"You're still on the team," she said. It wasn't quite a question.

"I'm still on the team." They'd tried, gently, to move her onto land. She'd refused, and they had let her, because there was no one better in the water and everyone knew it. She dived slower now, signed her own fills, checked her own line twice. There was a new junior — not Tim; Tim had gone home to Purmerend and a desk at a shipping office, and no one blamed him, Sanne least of all — a new junior, twenty-three, quick hands, too trusting, and she watched the boy in the water the way Bram had once watched her. The difference was that she taught him where to hide a second life, and a second cutter, and why. She taught him redundancy as if it were the only prayer there was, because she had learned that it was.

She dived the Amstel alone on a still grey morning in the week the clocks changed, off the books, and no one stopped her, because Nadia had come to tend her line from the bank.

She went in off the Weesperzijde near the sluis, rigged the way she rigged for any hard dive because she no longer knew another way, and went down the shot line counting her breaths in threes, and the city closed over her head like a lid. She knew now, to the metre, where Daan had gone down. She swam to it, hung over the grey silt at the place, and took her right glove off in the cold water and felt the river close round her bare fingers like a grip.

She had brought his wedding ring. She had worn it on a chain under her suit for two years, against her sternum. She worked the plain gold band off the chain with stiffening fingers and opened her hand flat under the torch and looked at it — worn thin from twelve years of his hand, the date inside it that she no longer had to tilt to the light to read.

She had thought there would be words for it. There were none that the water did not already hold. She had brought the truth up out of this river and given it to the honest scale and watched the beam settle. That was the thing she had owed him, and it was done. And it was imperfect — a killer drowned in the cold instead of tried in the light, three strokes she would answer for next spring and to herself every working morning after. Imperfect. Real. Weighed and true and enough.

She opened her hand the rest of the way.

The ring turned once in the torch beam, a small bright turn, and went down, and the silt took it the way the silt took everything the city gave it, softly, closing over it, and it was gone. The letting go did not close the hole in her — she had not expected it to — but it changed the hole's shape, made it a thing she could carry down here in the cold instead of a thing that carried her up in the night.

She hung there a moment longer, bare hand empty, and counted her breaths one last time, the old count. One. Two. Three. She had not stopped grieving; she was not going to. Grief only got a true weight, at last, one that a person could bend down to and pick up and stand under and walk. That was all the honest scale had ever promised: not that the number would be small, or kind. Only that it would be true.

Then she pulled her glove back on, and dumped a breath of air into her suit, and let the water do for her the one thing it had never done for Daan, or for Victor, or, in the end, for Bram Verhoeven with the lead on his hips — the thing the water did only for the ones who knew it and were known by it in return. She went up, through the seven metres of nothing-coloured cold, toward the coin of grey light that widened and paled as she rose, until the lid of the city broke over her head and let her through into the air.

She surfaced into the ordinary morning. Nadia was on the bank with the line loose in her hands and her breath showing in the cold. The gulls were up and wheeling over the Magere Brug. A barge went by down toward the Amstelsluis and its wake reached her, slow, and lifted her, and set her down. The city went on spending and weighing itself, five hundred years deep in the business of finding out what things truly cost, and Sanne Kessler floated on the surface of the Amstel and breathed the cold clean air in threes, and rose.
