# The Blue and the White — Part One: The Cold Trade


## 1. The Last of the Lapis

The last of the good blue lay in the grey gallipot no bigger than a thumb-joint of powder, and Clara Wijnants had known for a month that she was grinding the end of it and had ground it slow all the same, the way you eat slowly the last of a bread you cannot replace.

She had it out on the slab before the town was awake, in the one hour that was still hers the way it had always been hers, the black hour before the bells, when the cold came up through the floor-tiles of the poor shop and the canal outside lay unmoving and nobody wanted anything of her yet. The slab was the grey second-hand stone she had bought off a dead potter's widow in the spring, plain and cheap and dished now, a little, by her own working — no porphyry, no dark red speckle, not Gerrit's slab, which was somewhere under the Paardenmarkt with the rest of the old life, ground to gravel with the house. She had stopped grieving the slab. You could not grind on grief; she had learned that much, and learned it at a pan of dirty water, and did not need to learn it twice.

She wet the pinch of pigment with a little walnut oil, walnut for the blue as it should be, and set the muller to it. Gerrit's muller, that one she had kept, the smooth loaf of porphyry that had come up whole out of the ruin when almost nothing else had; it fitted her palms the way a thing fits that you have held ten thousand times, and the rhythm came up out of her wrists below thought, round and round, the paste gathering and spreading and gathering. This was the true ultramarine, the reclaimed blue, the blue she had washed grain by grain out of the frozen mud of her own dead house the winter of the great burst, and there was, this morning, almost none of it left. What she had was worth more than the slab it lay on and more than the lease over her head, and it would be gone before the year turned, and there was no coin anywhere in her keeping to buy the stone to make more.

The blue came up under the muller the way it always had, deep and even, a colour with the whole grave sky folded into it, and Clara looked at it the way you look at the face of someone you are about to lose and did not let her own face do anything at all.

Above her on the wall, in the good north light that had not yet come, the little bird hung in the dark. She did not need the light to see it. She had it by heart — the loose dragged paint that was hardly paint at all up close and was wholly a living goldfinch a step back, the thin chain running from the ring to the band about its leg, the pale warm-and-cool air around it that a dead man had got into a foot of panel and that no one living in Delft could get at all. It had come out of the ruin nearly whole, one small miracle in a quarter of the town that had none, and she had not sold it and would not, though there had been mornings this autumn when the selling of it would have paid the fee and the lease both and left her over. She ground her last blue under it and did not look up, and the bird waited on the wall to be seen, as it always did, patient as the dead are patient.

The trade was dying. She could say it now in the plain flat way she said everything to herself and never aloud. It was not dying of the burst, not directly, though the burst had started it; it was dying of the arithmetic that the burst had set running. There were too few painters left in Delft to keep a colour-woman, and the ones left were poor. Fabritius was in the ground. Half the studios in the Doelen quarter were rubble still, two winters on, black under the snow-melt, and the men who had worked in them had gone to Amsterdam or Rotterdam or into their graves, and the ones who stayed bought their umber by the paper and their blue not at all, or bought the cheap smalt that faded, or ground their own bad colour to save the stuiver. A painter's choice of blue was an economic decision as much as a seeing one; Gerrit had taught her that before she could have said it, and the economy had decided, and it had not decided in her favour.

There would have been a boy at the muller by now, in the old life. That was a thought she did not let herself have often, because there was no use in it, but the black hour let thoughts in that the day kept out, and she had it this morning, wetting the walnut oil: Barent would have been seventeen, and grown, and at the second slab with his sleeves shoved up, grinding the umber she ground alone now, and talking — he had never stopped talking, the boy, hungry and cheerful and clumsy with the wrong word, worshipping the painters he ground for, dreaming of the guild he could no more have afforded than the moon. He had gone from wanting everything to nothing between one cold breath and the next, on the twelfth of October, in the shop that no longer existed, and Clara had dug him out of it dead with her own hands and had never once, in the two years since, walked past a boy of his age at any trade without the grief coming up under her ribs like the blue coming up under the muller, deeper each time she folded it over. She did not grind two slabs any more. She had never taken another boy. There were reasons of money for that, and they were true, and they were not the reason.

She scraped the slab clean with the horn knife and worked the muller a last few turns over the smear, and stopped, because there was no more to add and the blue was as fine as it would go. She set it aside on the scrap of glass. So little. She had staked a marriage's worth of patience on that pot of blue, and it had bought her one hard year of standing at a slab in her own name, which was more than most of what she had staked in her life had bought her, and it was ending, and she would have to find another thing to stake or go under. That was the whole of the ledger, kept in her own hand now, and it leaned the wrong way, and no amount of grinding would tip it. Grinding made colour. It did not make more stone.

A board creaked overhead — the third step, that shrieked here too, in this house as it had in the last, a thing Clara found she was obscurely glad of, that even the poor house had a stair that told on the child. Then Grietje was in the doorway in her shift with the blanket dragged round her, hair on end, one fist closed.

"You're grinding the last of it," the child said. Not a question. She was nine now, and nine had opinions.

"I am."

"You said you'd let me watch when it was the last."

"You're watching," Clara said. "Come away from the door, you're letting the cold in."

Grietje came, and this time to be near the blue and not to be warmed. She set her fist on the edge of the slab and opened it and sorted the four things by the grey window-light that was just beginning: the chip of blue-and-white off some broken dish, the horn button, the river pebble, and the little lump of pyrite Clara had given her out of the old lapis, years ago, in the old shop — the gold that was not gold. She counted them every morning still, the way another child would say a prayer, laying them out and gathering them in, making sure the night had not taken any. The voice that counted them was her own hoarse-then-whole voice, come back to her over a pan of mud that first hard spring and never quite the same as before, a half-tone lower, a child's voice with a crack of the older thing in it that Clara heard every day and had stopped being unable to bear.

"Is that all there is," Grietje said, looking at the smear of blue on the glass, so small.

"That's all there is."

"Then what will you grind after."

"I don't know yet," Clara said, which was the truth, and the child, who could smell a soft answer the way she could smell bread, looked at her a moment with Neeltje's flat considering look and did not press it, which was its own kind of knowing. She put the pyrite to the last, as she always did, and closed her fist on the four things, and went to stand at the little grate where the turf was banked, holding her hands to a warmth that was mostly memory of warmth.

Clara ground the guild's umber next. She ground it every morning, as the bargain bound her — the town-work, the guild's own panels and the guild's own painters, at a rate the board had fixed below what the trade would bear, in exchange for the leave to sell the rest as she pleased, so that she bought her freedom by grinding a part of every year for the guild at a loss. A leash worn as a livery, she had called it to herself, walking out of the guildhouse that raw noon a year gone, and it had proved exactly that. She ground the umber clean all the same, because a grudged colour ground gritty gave Doncker a stick, and she would give Doncker no sticks; she ground it fine and true and at a loss, and cursed it under her breath, and it warmed her arms if it warmed nothing else.

She was scraping the umber when the knock came at the shop door, and it was still too early for any painter, and too early for the water-seller, and the knock itself was wrong — not the light rap of a customer but a solid workmanlike knock, the knock of a man used to knocking on doors that were slow to open, and low down, as if the man were broad and stood close.

"We're not open," Clara said, not moving from the slab.

"I know it," said a voice through the door, a big flat easy voice, a Delft voice with the harbour in it. "I've been standing across the way an hour watching your candle. I didn't want to knock before you'd done your quiet work. A colour-hand wants her quiet hour; I know that much." A pause. "I've come about blue, weduwe Wijnants. Not the painter's blue. The other kind."

Clara set down the horn knife. Grietje had turned from the grate, watchful. On the wall the north light had begun at last to come up, grey and even and without any flattery in it, and the little bird stepped out of the dark into it, and held still, and waited to be seen.

"There is no other kind," Clara said, to the door.

"There is now," the voice said. "That's rather the trouble."

She went and drew the bolt.


## 2. The Fire Blue

The man on the step was broad, as she had guessed from the knock, broad and not tall, in a good plain coat gone at the cuffs and a workman's hands that no coat could dress up — burn-marks on them, old and new, the shine of healed scald across two knuckles, and under the nails not paint but a grey grime she did not know. He had a sack over one shoulder. He took off his hat when the door opened, which not every man did for a widow at a shop door, and looked at her with a frank open face weathered red, and past her, without meaning to, at the wall — and stopped, the way they stopped, the seeing ones, and looked at the goldfinch a long moment before he said anything at all.

"That's a Fabritius," he said.

"It is."

"I heard the widow Wijnants had one. I didn't believe it." He looked at it a moment longer, and then, plainly making himself, brought his eyes back to her, and Clara marked him for it — that he had looked before he touched, and that he had made himself stop. "Coenraad Spronck," he said. "Plateelbakker. Or I will be, when the kiln draws right, which it does not yet." He hefted the sack a little. "May I come in out of the cold and waste a quarter-hour of your morning? I'll pay for the quarter-hour if you like. I've a proposal you'll think an insult, and then, I hope, you'll think again."

She let him in. It was that or stand with the door open bleeding heat, and there was, besides, the small unwilling fact that she had wondered every morning for a month what she would grind after, and here was a man come at first light to talk about blue.

He set the sack on the counter, where the light was, and Grietje drifted in from the back to see, because the child could not keep away from a sack that might have shining things in it, and Spronck glanced at her and had the sense not to speak to her, which put him up a further notch in Clara's ledger of men. He loosed the neck of the sack and tipped a little of what was in it out onto a square of paper on the counter, and Clara looked, and for a moment did not understand what she was being shown.

It was dirt. A drab, dark, greyish-black grit, dull as ash, dull as the sweepings of a hearth, with nothing in it, no colour, no promise of colour, nothing a painter would give a duit for. It looked, she thought, like the grit the lapis gave up in the very first breaking, before the washing, the ashes-with-a-rumour-of-sky — but this had not even the rumour. It was just dark.

"This," said Spronck, "is the finest blue in the world, or it will be. It's zaffer. Cobalt, roasted, ground with a little glass. Comes down from the Saxon mines by way of Amsterdam, and costs me dear, though not a tenth of what your lapis costs, which is why I can use it and a painter can't." He wet a fingertip and touched the grit and held it up so she could see it close, a dirty grey smudge on his weathered skin. "It goes on the raw ware looking like that. Like mud. Like nothing. You paint a whole dish in it and it's grey as a rainy sky, and any woman with sense would tell you you've wasted your morning." He set his hand down. "And then the fire has it. And it comes out of the kiln blue. Blue on the white, blue you'd swear no one had ground, because no one had — the fire ground it, the fire made it, out of that." He nodded at the grey grit on the paper. "I've been at this two years and it still stops my breath every firing. A blue you can't see till the fire's had it. That's the whole trade, weduwe Wijnants, and it's going to be a great one, and I am, at the moment, very bad at it."

Clara looked at the grey grit and did not touch it. Everything in her craft, everything the years and Gerrit and the porphyry slab had put into her hands, was a craft of the visible — of grinding a colour until you could see it come up under the stone, of washing it until you could see the dross fall away, of judging with the eye and the eye alone how far a paste was mulled and whether a blue was true. You saw the colour and then you sold the colour. She had spent her life on the proposition that the blue was in the stone and the work brought it out where the eye could find it. And here was a man asking her to grind a colour she would not be able to see until it was in another man's kiln and out the far side, past all her judging, past her eye, past her hand — to grind a blue on faith.

"I'm a verfmaalster," she said. "I grind colour for painters. I sell what a painter can see on the slab and take home wet."

"I know what you are. That's why I'm here and not at Doncker's." He said the name plainly, watching her, and she gave him nothing, and he went on. "The potters' boys grind our blue now, and they grind it like boys — coarse, streaky, full of grit that blows the glaze or burns black at the edge. Half my dishes come out of the kiln freckled and muddy because the blue was ground by a lad who'd rather be at the harbour. I've watched what you do. I stood in the market last spring and watched you grind a blue for that young fellow from the Oude Langendijk, the quiet one, and I watched him watch you, and I thought: that woman grinds finer than any three potters in this town, and she is grinding for a trade that is dying, in a shop she'll lose at the turn of the year." He was not unkind about it. He said it the way you'd say the tide was going out. "The painters are done in Delft, weduwe. I'm sorry to be the one at your door with it. But the kiln isn't. The kiln is just starting. And the kiln wants blue more than any painter ever did, because a painter uses a pinch and a pottery uses a pound, and there's going to be a pound wanted every day of the week for twenty years."

"You want me to grind zaffer for you."

"I want you to be my colour-hand. Grind the blue, judge the blue, mix the smalt and the zaffer to a paint the girls can lay clean and the fire won't spoil. Learn what the fire does to it — because you'll have to learn to see it blind, to know from the grey what the blue will be, and there's not a soul in this town can teach you that but the fire itself, over and over, at the cost of a firing each time you're wrong." He spread his burnt hands. "It's honest work and it pays honest coin, which is more than I can say for the guild's town-work you're grinding this minute at a loss — yes, I know about that too; everyone knows about that; it's the joke of the Voldersgracht that Doncker bound the widow to grind his umber for nothing. I can't match a great commission. There aren't any. But I can pay you week on week for as long as the kiln draws, which will be my whole life and after it, and I'd rather starve slower with you than eat freckled dishes without you."

Clara was quiet. Outside a cart went over the frozen ruts toward the Markt, iron rims ringing, and the turf-smoke came in low and brown under the door, the smell of the town breathing, and on the wall the goldfinch held still in the grey light, chained to its ring, waiting.

"It's a fool's blue," she said at last. "A blue you can't see. You could grind mud a year and call it blue and never know your grinding from your luck."

"You could," Spronck agreed. "Until the fire tells you. The fire doesn't flatter and it doesn't lie. It's the hardest judge I've ever worked for and the only honest one, and I think you'd like it, weduwe Wijnants, more than you'd like to." He tied the neck of the sack. "There's the insult delivered. Now think again on it, at your leisure. I'm at the old brewery De Drie Ackers, off the Oosteinde, that's the pottery now, the Blue Hand — anyone'll point you. Come and see a firing before you say no twice. That's all I ask. Come and see the fire make the blue, and then tell me you'd rather grind Doncker's umber for nothing till the lease runs out."

He put his hat on. At the door he stopped, and looked once more at the bird, and this time did not stop his eyes coming back.

"He's the one, isn't he," Spronck said quietly. "Fabritius. Died in the burst. That's the last of him, near enough, that little bird." He looked at Clara. "I lost a brother in it. And a brother's whole house. Everyone in this town lost somebody in it, and half of us lost the somebody and never found so much as a hand to bury." He was not asking her for anything; he was just saying it, the way you say a thing to another survivor, one flat plain sentence laid down between you. "The fire took the town," he said. "I've made it my whole living to make the fire give something back. That's the trade. I thought you of all people might see the sense in it." And he went out into the grey street with his sack of blue that was not blue yet, and left the door to Clara to close.

Grietje came and stood at the counter and looked at the little dark smudge of zaffer he had left on the square of paper, the dirt that was going to be the finest blue in the world, and put out one finger, and Clara said "Don't," and the child stopped, and they both looked at it.

"It doesn't look like anything," Grietje said.

"No."

"Neither did your blue," the child said, "in the mud. You told me. It looked like dirt in the mud and you knew it wasn't." She looked up. "You washed it out grain by grain and it was blue the whole time, only you couldn't see it."

Clara looked at her niece, the mended arm, the four things closed in her fist, the cracked whole voice, and did not say anything for a moment, because the child had read the thing truer than she had, the way the child had always read the pyrite truer, and there was nothing to add to it that would not spoil it.

"Get your porridge," she said. "The umber won't grind itself, and the guild won't thank me if it did."


## 3. The Widow's Leave

Willem Doncker came to the shop three days later, in the middle of the morning when the light was flat and grey and full, and he came, as he always came, correct as a deacon, with his hat in his hand and his condolence ready worn on his face like good cloth, and Clara knew before he had said a word that he had heard about Spronck, because Doncker heard about everything, and because a righteous man cannot resist coming in person to be sorry for you about a thing he means to prevent.

"Weduwe Wijnants," he said. "You keep the place well." He looked round the two poor rooms in a way that managed to praise and pity at once, which was a thing he could do with his face that Clara had never seen another man do. "Warmer than I feared. I worried for you, the winter coming and this so near the water."

"It keeps the rent low," Clara said. "What do you want, meester Doncker?"

He inclined his head at her plainness, forgiving it. He was in his middle fifties now, gone a little heavier, a prosperous man in sober expensive dark, and he had won — that was the thing to keep in the front of the mind, dealing with Doncker; he had won the ground and the stall and the good market-right on the new Voldersgracht for his nephew, and he had bound her leave in three cold ropes, and he had lost nothing in the doing of it but the one tenth part he had grudged her, the trade itself, and he had come, she was fairly sure, to see about taking even that.

"I hear you've had a caller," he said. "The potter. Spronck, from the Blue Hand." He said the pottery's name the way a man says the name of a tavern he wouldn't be seen in. "I won't pretend I'm glad of it. I'll be honest with you, Clara, as I've always tried to be — you've never given me credit for it, but I have." He set his hat on the counter, near the little square of paper where the zaffer smudge still was, and Clara saw him see it, and saw him decide not to remark on it, which was worse than if he had. "There's a question of your leave. I'd sooner raise it with you kindly, here, than have the board raise it coldly, there. That's why I've come myself."

"Raise it."

"Your continuance is a widow's right to carry on Gerrit's *enrolled trade*." He said the words as if reading them, and perhaps he was, from the copy of the grant he would have in his coat; Doncker never came to a fight without the paper. "The enrolled trade is the grinding and the sale of *colour to the painters of this city and to the guild's own work*. That is the trade Gerrit held and paid his master's fee on and that you continue by our leave. It is a painter's-colour trade." He folded his hands. "It is not a potter's trade. The plateelbakkers are of the guild, yes — we are all under St Luke, the potters and the glassmen and the rest — but a potter's blue is a separate matter, bought and ground and supplied under separate custom, and a colour-hand who deals raw blue to a pottery is not carrying on Gerrit's enrolled trade. She is entering a new one. And a widow's leave does not stretch to a new one. It stretches only so far as the dead man's grant, and no further. That is the law of it, and I did not make the law, and I take no pleasure in reading it to you."

There it was, then, laid out neat and cold, and Clara looked at it the way she looked at a ledger that leaned wrong: without flinching, because flinching changed nothing.

"So if I grind for Spronck," she said, "I break my leave."

"You risk it. The board would have to rule. I'd have to bring it." He said the last as though it were a burden being laid on him. "And I'll tell you plainly what the board would do, because you'd rather know: they'd rule against you, because the reading is sound, and because half of them owe me a turn, and because a woman grinding for a pottery outside the terms of her leave is exactly the loose thread they've been waiting to pull to be rid of the whole irregularity of you. You'd lose the leave. You'd lose the trade. You'd be a woman with a lease and a slab and no right to sell what you grind on it." He let that sit. "Or."

"Or."

"Or you supply your blue *through* me." He said it gently, as a mercy, which was how he said all his worst things. "I'm the colour-merchant to the trade, painters and potters both — I've the Amsterdam factors, the lapis and the zaffer both come through my hands or nearly; Spronck buys his Saxon cobalt off my books this minute, he only doesn't advertise it. If you grind for the potteries under my supply and my name, on my terms, there's no question of your leave, because you'd not be entering a new trade — you'd be working within mine. I'd pay you a grinding-wage. A fair one. You'd have the coin and the surety and none of the risk, and the fee for your continuance might even be found to have been paid, in a manner of speaking, out of the arrangement." He spread his hands, the picture of reason. "It's what I offered you before, Clara, in the passage, the day of the ruling. I offered to see you settled warm. You threw it back at me. I'm offering again, because I'm a stubborner man than I look, and because Gerrit was my friend, and because I do not, whatever you think of me, want to see you standing at a slab at sixty with the lead in your chest and nothing over you."

"You want to own the blue," Clara said. "All of it. The lapis and the zaffer both, the easel and the kiln both, and me grinding it in a corner of your name with my own name filed off."

"I want the trade *ordered*," Doncker said, and for once there was heat in it, the old real heat under the deacon's cloth. "I want it under one hand that knows it, so it doesn't run wild and cheap and freckled and shame the guild in front of the whole Republic. Do you know what's coming, Clara? Do you understand what these potters are doing? They're making a blue-and-white to undersell the porcelain the Company brings from the far side of the world. They'll sell it by the shipload. It'll go out on the Company's keels to every port there is, Delft's name on the bottom of every dish, and it will be, one way or the other, the thing this town is *known for* long after your painters and my lapis are forgotten together. That is not a corner trade for a widow to freelance in. That is the future of Delft, and it wants a firm hand and a good name on it, and it is not going to get either from a scatter of ex-brewers firing mud in their cellars and a widow grinding for whoever knocks." He picked up his hat. "I'm trying to build something that lasts. You only ever see the stall I took. You never once saw the town I was trying to keep from flying apart."

"I saw it," Clara said. "I've always seen it. That's the whole trouble with you, meester. You'd order the whole town into its right place and never once ask a single soul in it whether they'd sooner be free and freckled than warm and yours." She came round the counter, not to threaten him, only to open the door, which was answer enough. "Bring it to the board if you like. I've a while yet on my leave and my lease both, and I've not ground a grain for any potter, and until I do there's nothing to rule on. When there is, we'll all find out together how sound your reading is."

Doncker stood in the doorway a moment. The flat grey light was on the bird behind her, and he glanced at it, and something crossed his face that was not calculation, and Clara thought, not for the first time, that the worst of Doncker was that he was not a liar; that he meant every kindness even as he took every stall; that he had never once, in all the years, felt the two of them touch.

"You'll lose the lease at the turn of the year regardless," he said, quietly, not unkindly. "You've no market-right and no proper shop found and the fee still owing. Whatever you do about the potter, that clock runs. I could stop it, Clara. That's all I keep trying to say to you, and you keep hearing a threat in it. One word from me to the board and the deadline eases and the fee's forgiven and you're settled. One word. And you'd rather freeze free than say it." He put his hat on. "I don't understand you. Gerrit did, I think. I never have." And he went out, correct to the last, and did not slam the door, because Doncker never slammed anything; he closed it soft behind him, which was somehow the loudest thing a man could do.

Clara stood in the poor grey room and heard the child come to the inner doorway behind her.

"Is he going to take the shop," Grietje said.

"He's going to try. He tries and tries. It's what he's for." Clara looked at the little square of paper on the counter, the drab grey smudge of a blue no one could see. "Eat something," she said. "We're going out this afternoon. There's a fire I want to look at."


## 4. The Young Painter's Blue

But it was the young painter who came first, that same afternoon, before she could go to the pottery — came quiet as he always came, on the turn of the light, and Clara was almost glad of the delay, because she owed him the last of the blue and had been half dreading and half longing to be rid of the owing.

He was not a boy any more, though she still thought of him as the young one; he was a married man in his twenties with a growing houseful over on the Oude Langendijk, his wife's people's house, and a name just beginning to be said in the trade in the low careful way the trade said the names of men it wasn't sure of yet. He had joined the guild the Christmas before the burst and paid his six gulden and hardly sold a thing since, so far as Clara could tell; he painted slow, terribly slow, a picture a season if that, and Doncker thought him a poor bet and said so, and Clara had watched him watch her grind blue and thought Doncker, for once, entirely wrong.

"Weduwe Wijnants," he said, and took off his hat, and looked — not at the bird today, though he loved the bird; today he looked at her, and then at the slab, where the little scrap of glass still held the last of the reclaimed ultramarine, the very last, that she had ground out three mornings before and not sold to anyone, because she had known, grinding it, whose it was.

"I've the money this time," he said, awkward, and put it on the counter, honest coin counted slow. "I've come for the blue. For the — you know the one. The painting."

"The woman by the window."

"The woman by the window." He said it and then looked embarrassed to have said it, a man who did his saying with his hands. "I've the room laid in. The wall, the light coming across. I've been a year at the wall alone — you'll laugh — a year at a plaster wall and the light on it, getting the light to come *across* and not flat, the way it falls and models the air itself between the wall and the eye. And now I want the blue in it. In the cloth. In the shadow of the cloth, mostly — a blue in the shadow that no one's put there so, a blue you'd — " and he stopped, having run out past what he could say, and Clara took up the scrap of glass with the last of the true ultramarine on it and did not, this time, make him finish.

"A blue you could breathe," she said.

He looked up. "Yes," he said. "How do you — yes. Exactly that."

She did not tell him. She had never told him, and she would not now, that a dead man had stood at another counter in another shop that no longer existed and wanted the same thing in the same words, a woman by a window with the light coming across and a blue in the shadow of the cloth that no one had put there before, a blue you could breathe, and had never got to make it, because the light he chased his whole life had come through his north wall one grey Monday and the wall had come down on him in it. There was nothing in the telling for this one and a good deal that might weigh him, and the dead did not need the living to speak their names over every colour; they needed only the colour ground true and put where the light could find it. So she said none of it, and ground him the last of the reclaimed blue finer than she had ever ground anything, walnut oil for the blue as it should be, and watched him watch her do it, and knew as she did it that she was grinding the end of the old life and that after this scrap of glass was scraped clean there would be no more true ultramarine in her keeping and none she could ever afford to make.

It took a long while. She did not hurry it. Outside the light went from grey to the first faint gold of the low autumn afternoon, and the child sat on the stair and watched, silent for once, feeling the weight of the thing without being told it, the way she felt weights, and the young painter stood with his hat in his hands and watched the blue come up under the stone the last time, and no one spoke, and it was, though none of them said so, a kind of funeral, and a kind of christening, both at once, which was, Clara thought, most true things.

When it was done she wrapped it in a twist of paper, all of it, every grain, and then, because she was a fool, and because it was owed, and because the last of a thing should not be measured out mean, she added to the twist the small dry scrapings from the edge of the glass that she would ordinarily have kept for herself against nothing, and folded them in too.

"That's more than I paid," he said, who had the colour-hand's eye for it.

"It's what you paid," Clara said. "Take it. It's the last true ultramarine in Delft, near enough, and there'll be no more of it from me — I've no stone to make it and no coin to buy stone, and the trade that wanted it is dying under us both, so it's yours, all of it, and don't you dare waste it on a wall nobody breathes near. Put it where the light comes across. Put it in the shadow of the cloth, the way you said. Make the woman by the window, and get the blue right, and let it be the thing that lasts, because I'm telling you plainly, meester, it will be. Everything else in this trade is going out with the tide. Get that one blue right and it'll outlast the lot of us."

He looked at her, and at the twist of paper in his hand that was worth more than a month of his slow work, and something passed over his quiet careful face that she thought she understood the size of at last, and he said, low, "There's a man could have told me how to see it. Isn't there. In this town. Before."

Clara was still. So he had guessed something, or felt it, the way the seeing ones felt things.

"There was," she said. "He's dead. You'll have to find it yourself, in the dark, which is where the seeing's done. But you've his blue now, or near enough — the true stone, the last of it, better than he ever got on credit from me. Don't grieve that he's not here to tell you. Just get it right. That's the whole of what he'd want, and it's the whole of what I want, and it's more than the fee, so we'll call the fee paid and the rest a gift and neither of us will mention it again."

He put the twist of paper over his heart, inside the coat, the way a man carries a thing he's afraid of dropping, and put his hat on, and at the door he stopped and looked, at last, at the little bird on the wall in the failing light, the goldfinch chained to its ring holding still to be seen, and he looked at it a long, long time, longer than he ever had, and then he said, not to Clara exactly, "I'll get it right," and went out into the gold going blue, her last true blue over his heart, toward a wall he'd spent a year teaching to hold the light.

Clara stood in the door and watched him go the way she had once watched another man go down another street, and did not, this time, wish she had not looked. Then she scraped the empty glass clean out of habit, though there was nothing on it, and set it on the sill, and turned to the child on the stair.

"Get your cloak," she said. "The good one, it's cold. We're going to see a man make blue out of dirt, and I want to know whether he's a liar or not before I stake the rest of our lives on it."


# The Blue and the White — Part Two: The Kiln


## 5. De Blauwe Hand

The pottery had been a brewery, and you could still read the brewery in it the way you can read the drowned face of a man in the face of his son. The great hall off the Oosteinde had held the mash-tuns once, when Delft brewed for half the Republic and the canal by the door ran brown with the spent grain; but the water had gone bad and the taxes had gone high and Rotterdam had undersold them, and one by one the two hundred breweries had gone dark, down to a score, and their high stone halls stood empty and cheap along the canals, waiting. Coenraad Spronck had taken one of them for the price of a good horse, he told her, walking her in out of the cold, and swept out two centuries of malt-dust and set his wheels where the tuns had stood, and built his kiln against the old brew-house chimney, because a brewery, whatever else was wrong with it, was a house already built around a fire.

Clara stood inside the door with Grietje's hand in hers and looked, and did not, for a moment, say anything, because it was alive.

That was the first thing, and the thing she had not expected, and the thing that went into her under the ribs before she could set her guard against it. Her own trade was a solitude — one woman at a slab in a cold room before dawn, the whole labour of it silent and private and turned inward. This was a workshop full of people and noise and warmth: two men at wheels with the clay rising wet under their hands; a boy wedging a great grey lump of it against a board, thump and fold, thump and fold; a woman at a trough washing thrown ware; and along the north wall, in the good light, at a long bench, five or six young ones with brushes, painting. The heat of the kiln, banked but not out, came off the far wall and met the cold from the door and made the whole hall smell of wet clay and wood-ash and hot brick and, under it, faint and forever, the old ghost of malt. It was the smell, Clara thought, of a place where things were made and not merely broken smaller so that others might make; and she had not known, until she stood in it, how long she had grown used to the cold and the quiet and the turning-inward, and how much of that was the trade and how much was only grief.

"Blue Hand," Spronck said, seeing her look at the sign that hung within as well as without — a hand, painted blue, on a white ground. "For the potter's mark. And because a man came out of the burst with his brother's blood on his hand and made it a working hand instead, which is a thing I don't say to customers, and don't know why I've said to you." He shrugged it off. "Come and see the girls. That's where you'll do most of your quarrelling, if you come to us. The blue's no use ground fine if the hand that lays it lays it thick."

He took her to the long bench. The young ones looked up — an apprentice or two, and four girls between perhaps eleven and sixteen, in aprons blue to the elbow, and Clara felt Grietje go very still at her side, the way she went still before a thing she wanted too much to ask for. The girls were painting the raw ware, the dishes and jugs and tiles that had been thrown and dried to the hardness of biscuit and dipped in the white tin-glaze so that they stood there chalky and matte and waiting; and the girls painted on them, in that same drab grey-black she had seen on Spronck's paper, birds and flowers and knots and borders, whole worlds laid down in mud on chalk, grey on white, dull as a rainy day.

"It's grey," Grietje said, before Clara could stop her. "You're painting it grey."

The nearest girl, the eldest, sixteen and sure of herself, looked at the child and grinned. "It's blue," she said. "It just doesn't know it yet. Watch." And she dipped her brush and laid a bird's wing on the white in three quick strokes, grey and flat and nothing, and it was, Clara saw, a good bird, a swift confident bird for all it was the colour of ash — the girl could draw, whatever else — and Grietje looked from the grey bird to the girl's face and back, plainly not believing a word of it and plainly unable to look away.

"That'll come out of the kiln blue?" the child said.

"Blue as a summer," the girl said. "Bluer than that dish there" — a fired one, on the shelf, a finished plate, and Clara looked at it properly for the first time, a plate glazed white and painted with a spray of blue flowers, and the blue was — she went closer, without meaning to — the blue was good. Not ultramarine; nothing was ultramarine; but a clean true carrying blue, deep in the heart of the strokes and softening where the brush had run dry, sunk into the white glaze so that it was not on the plate but in it, part of the white now, one fired skin. It would not fade. It would not blanch or green or fall to grey the way azurite did, the way smalt did in oil; it was in the glass of the glaze forever, or as near forever as made no difference to anyone who would ever hold the plate. She put out a finger and touched it and felt only smooth cool glaze, the blue sealed under it, untouchable, done.

"How long does it hold," she asked Spronck. "The colour. In the weather, in the light."

"Forever," he said simply. "That's the thing of it. A painting fades and cracks and darkens and wants a fool with varnish every fifty years to save it. This is fired into glass. Bury it a thousand years and dig it up and wash it and there's your blue, exactly as the fire made it. It's the one colour we make in this town that death can't get at." He said it plainly, not selling now, and Clara stood with her finger on the sealed unfading blue and thought about the little bird on her wall at home, the loose dragged paint that was already, she knew, darkening year on year, the varnish already going amber, the whole miracle of it slowly dying even as it hung there being loved; and she thought about a blue that death could not get at; and something turned over in her that she did not let onto her face.

"Show me the zaffer," she said. "Show me it all. From the sack to the fire. If I'm to say no to you I'll say no knowing what I'm saying no to."

He showed her. It took the rest of the short afternoon and she forgot the cold and forgot, for whole stretches, to be guarded, which she noticed and did not entirely regret. He showed her the raw zaffer and the smalt, the roasted cobalt and the ground blue glass, and how they were mixed, and how coarse the potter's boys ground them and how much grit the coarse grind left; he let her take a muller to a little of the zaffer on a slab he had — a rough slab, a bad slab, and she itched to tell him so — and grind it, and she ground it finer in a quarter-hour than his boys ground it in a morning, dead fine, and felt under her hands the familiar truth that this at least was the same, that a colour ground fine was a colour ground fine whether the eye could see the end of it or not, that her hands knew the work even if her eye was blind to the result. And she looked at the fine grey paste she had made, that was going to be blue, that she could not see was blue, that she would have to take on the fire's word was blue, and she found that she wanted, with a want that surprised her, to see the fire prove it.

Grietje spent the afternoon at the long bench, and Clara, working the slab across the hall, kept half an eye on her the way she always did and saw a thing happen that she had not seen happen in two years: the child fell in among people her own kind. Machteld took her up — the eldest girl had a younger-sister hunger in her, being all brothers at home, and something in the grave watchful set of Grietje answered it — and put a brush in her hand and a spoiled tile in front of her and showed her, not gently, the way a competent sixteen shows a wanting nine, how to load the brush and lay a stroke and turn the wrist. And the other girls made room, and one of them, painting, told a long silly story about a boy at the harbour that Clara could not hear over the wheels but could see land, because Grietje laughed — laughed out loud, the cracked whole laugh, at a bench full of blue-armed girls in a warm loud workshop that smelled of clay and ash and old malt — and Clara stood at the slab with the muller in her hands and had to look back down at the grey paste for a moment and grind, because the sound of the child laughing among other children was a thing she had stopped letting herself want to hear, the way you stop letting yourself want a colour you cannot afford.

The child had been, since the burst, an old child, watchful and literal and too much alone with a grieving aunt in a cold shop before dawn; she had grown up sideways into Clara's solitude because there had been nowhere else to grow. And here was a place full of noise and warmth and hands making things and girls with brushes, a place that wanted a bird painted and did not care that the painter was nine or that her voice had a crack in it or that she counted four bright things every morning to be sure the night had not taken them; a place, Clara thought, watching, where a child could be a child among children and a maker among makers both at once, which was the whole of what she had not been able to give her in the cold two rooms across the town. Whatever else came of the pottery — the blue, the trade, the ledger leaning right or wrong — there was that, and it weighed, on the pan where such things weighed, more than the coin did.

When Clara came for her at the failing of the light, the child had a brush in her hand and grey on her fingers and a scrap of spoiled tile in front of her with a bird on it — a small clumsy grey bird, nothing like the eldest girl's swift one, a child's first bird, but a bird, unmistakably, and Clara stood behind her and looked at it and did not say the first thing that came, which was clumsy, and did not say the second thing, which was that they had no business here; she looked at the small grey bird that did not know it was blue and said only, "Is that a goldfinch."

"It's meant to be." Grietje looked up, grey to the wrist, alight in a way Clara had not seen her since before the burst. "The big girl said if it fires blue I can take it home." She looked at the bird, doubtful of it now under Clara's eye. "It's not very good."

"No," Clara said. "First birds aren't. Ask him" — she nodded at Spronck, across the hall — "ask him whether it can go in the next firing." And she watched the child's face open, and thought, walking home in the blue dark along the freezing canal with the child chattering the whole way about the fire and the girls and the blue that was hiding in the grey, that she had come to the pottery to find out whether Spronck was a liar, and had found out instead that the child had been alive in there in a way she was not alive at the cold slab at home, and that this, more than any ledger, was the thing she was going to have to weigh.


## 6. The Blue That Was Not There

She did not say yes to Spronck. She said she would come and grind for a firing or two, to see, unpaid, on no terms, owing nothing and owed nothing, and if the fire made a liar of him she would go home and grind Doncker's umber till the lease ran out and think no more about it. Spronck said that was fair, and did not push, which she was coming to see was his way — he pushed once, hard and honest, and then he let a thing sit and let the truth of it do his pushing for him. It was, she thought, the opposite of Doncker, who never stopped pushing because he never quite trusted the truth to be on his side.

So for a fortnight Clara went to the Blue Hand in the afternoons, when her own morning's umber was ground and the few painters who still came had come, and she learned the blue that was not there.

It was the hardest thing she had learned since she first learned to grind, and it undid, day by day, the deepest habit of her craft, which was to trust her eye. All her life the eye had been the final judge: you ground until it looked right, washed until it looked clean, mixed until it looked true, and the looking was the knowing. Here the looking told her nothing, or worse than nothing — it lied. She would grind a zaffer dead fine and mix it to a paint and lay a stroke on the white biscuit and it would sit there grey and drab and dead, and every instinct she had would tell her she had made a poor grey mud, and she would have to override the whole trained apparatus of her eye and say, against it: no, this will be blue, the fire will make it blue, and I will not know if I judged it right until the kiln is opened and it is far too late to change. It was like grinding blindfold. It was like — she came to the thought slowly, over the fortnight, and did not much like it — it was like faith, the kind the dominee preached and she had never much had, the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen. You laid the blue down in the dark and trusted the fire.

And she was wrong, at first, more than she was right. That was the humbling of it. A colour-woman of twenty years' standing, the finest grinder in Delft, and she spoiled ware for a fortnight like a beginner, because the things that governed the fired blue were not the things that governed the eye. Too much cobalt and it burned black and hard at the edge of a stroke, a scab instead of a wing. Too little and it fired thin and grey and washed-out, a bird's ghost. Grind it too fine and paradoxically it could crawl and freckle in the glaze; leave it a hair coarse and it dragged and streaked. The rules were the fire's rules, and the fire did not care what her eye thought it saw, and she had to learn them the way the potters had learned them, which was the only way there was: by firing, and opening, and looking at what the fire had actually done, and reasoning back from the fired truth to the grey lie she had laid down a week before, and remembering it, and doing better.

The first kiln-opening she stayed for undid her, a little, though she did not show it.

They had fired for a day and a night and let it cool for two — you could not hurry the cooling, Spronck said, or the ware crazed and cracked spite for spite, a thing she filed away — and then in the grey morning he broke the clay seal off the kiln-mouth and drew the ware out, and Clara stood among the potters and the girls and watched the dishes come out into the light, and the grey was gone.

It was gone entirely. Where there had been drab mud on chalk there was blue on white, clean and deep and true, sunk into the fired glaze and part of it forever — the eldest girl's swift birds flown up blue out of their ash, the flower-sprays open blue, the borders running blue and unbroken round the rims, a whole shelf of ware that had gone into the fire grey and dead and come out of it blue and alive, and Clara stood and looked at it and understood, in her body and not only her head, what Spronck had meant that first morning at her door. A blue you can't see till the fire's had it. She had thought it a merchant's phrase. It was a plain description of a daily miracle. The colour had been there the whole time, folded into the drab ore the way the blue of the lapis had been folded into the grey grit before the washing, the way — the thought came and she let it come — the way the finest of a thing is always folded into a shape that hides it, and wants only the right ruin, the grinding or the washing or the fire, to bring it up where the eye can find it at last.

And among the ware, near the front where the girl had set it to catch the best heat, was a small tile with a small blue bird on it — a clumsy, blue, unmistakable goldfinch, come out of the grey alive — and Clara picked it up before Grietje could, and turned it in the light, the child's first bird fired blue and true forever, death not able to get at it, and she put it in her apron pocket to carry home, and did not trust herself to say anything at all.

She was not always so rewarded. The same firing that gave up the girl's birds cracked a dozen good dishes clean across, for no fault of the blue, only the cooling gone too fast in the night when the boy on the watch let the fire fall wrong; and the firing after that came out with half its blues burned black and scabbed at the edges, and Clara knew before Spronck said a word that it was her fault, her grind, that she had pushed the cobalt too far chasing depth, and she looked at the shelf of spoiled ware, a week's clay and glaze and labour and fuel gone to freckled ruin, and felt the old flat weight of a thing broken that could not be unbroken. But it was a different weight than the burst had taught her. This was a ruin you could reason back from, a ruin that taught, a ruin she could pay for in a better grind next time; the fire had not taken anyone; it had only taken a dozen dishes and a lesson's worth of pride, and left the lesson. "The fire doesn't flatter and it doesn't lie," Spronck said, at her shoulder, looking at the scabbed birds without heat. "You went for depth. It'll do that. Pull the cobalt a touch and grind it a hair coarser and it'll come back to you." And it did, the firing after, the blues clean and deep and none of them burned, and Clara stood at the opened kiln and looked at what she had made blind and got right, and felt a thing she had not felt at a slab in years, which was not grief and not endurance but the plain clean pleasure of a hard craft coming under the hand at last.

It changed her, that fortnight, in a way she did not talk about and would not have known how to. Her whole trade had been a trade of the certain — you ground until the eye said true, and the eye did not lie to a good grinder, and there was a plain safety in that, a safety she had not known she leaned on until it was taken away. The fire took it away. The fire made her lay the colour down not-knowing, over and over, and wait days on the fire's verdict, and be wrong, and be humbled, and reason back, and try again; and it came to her, somewhere in the second week, mixing a paint she could not see the truth of, that this was nearer to how a life was actually lived than her old certain grinding had ever been. You did not, in a life, get to see the colour before you laid it down. You raised a child not-knowing what the child would fire to. You dug your dead out not-knowing whether you'd ever again be anything but a woman on a heap of grey dust. You laid the days down grey and drab and trusting, and only long after, if you lived, did you get to see what the fire of the years had made of them, and by then it was far too late to change a stroke, and the only question worth asking was whether you had ground the colour true when you could not see the end of it. She had thought, coming to the pottery, that she was learning a merchant's new blue. She was learning, it turned out, a thing about faith that the dominee had never once made her feel from the pulpit and the kiln made her feel every firing: that the colour is in the drab grit the whole time, folded and hidden and real, and wants only the right fire and a hand that will lay it down trusting, and that the laying-down-trusting is the whole of the courage there is.

She did not say any of that to Spronck. She said the blues were coming right, and he said he'd told her they would, and that was the whole of the conversation, and it was, between two people who each knew what the other wasn't saying, entirely enough.


## 7. The Kiln and the Powder

She had not, in all the fortnight of learning, stayed for a firing in the dark. She had ground the blue and laid it and stayed for the openings, which were morning work; the firings themselves ran a day and a night and were tended in shifts by the potters and their boys, and she had gone home in the evenings to the child and the poor shop and the banked turf. But a great firing came on in the last week of the fortnight, the kiln packed full against the deep cold coming, and Spronck asked would she stay and watch it through, because the blue in this one was mostly hers and he wanted her to see what the long heat did to it, the difference between a fire held steady all night and a fire let fall; and Clara sent word to the neighbour who would sit with Grietje, and stayed.

It was well enough at the first. The kiln-mouth glowed and the men fed it and the heat built through the evening, and Clara sat on an upturned tub with a mug of the small beer they all drank and watched the fire take hold, and it was, she thought, a companionable thing, a fire in the dark and people round it with work to do and time to talk, the oldest thing there was. Spronck talked about his brother, a little, and about the burst, the way people in that town talked about it, sidelong, in pieces, never head-on. The eldest girl, whose name was Machteld, painted at the bench by lamplight because she could not stop, and told Grietje's clumsy bird's whole future as a great plateelschilder, and Clara let her, because the child was not there to be embarrassed by it and because it cost nothing to let a girl dream out loud by a fire.

And then, deep in the night, they opened the kiln-mouth wide to feed it hard for the last great heat, and the fire roared.

It was only the fire drawing. Clara knew that. It was the fire pulling air through the wide mouth and up the old brew-house chimney, a great sucking roar and a blast of white heat that staggered the men back a step as they fed it, the whole kiln become for a moment one throat of noise and light — and Clara was on her feet with the mug fallen and shattered somewhere and her back against the cold far wall and no memory of having crossed to it, and the sound had gone white the way it had gone white that other morning, the pressure and the white, and she was not in the pottery, she was two streets from the Secret of Holland in a shop that no longer existed with the world tearing open, and her body knew it before her mind could catch the body and haul it back.

It lasted, she thought afterward, no time at all. A breath, two. Then Spronck's voice, not near her, careful not to come near her, from across the fire — "It's only the draw. It's only the fire drawing, weduwe. It does that. It'll settle." — the flat easy harbour voice pitched low and steady, saying the true thing over and over without moving toward her, and she stood with her back to the cold wall and her hands flat against it and made herself hear him, and made herself see the pottery come back around the white, the wheels, the bench, the men frozen mid-motion looking at her and then, at a word from Spronck, deliberately not looking, turning back to the fire, giving her the mercy of their turned backs. And the roar settled as the mouth was closed to, down to the steady breathing rush of the fire held hard, and the white went out of Clara's hearing, and she was in the pottery in the night, a woman of five-and-thirty with her back to a wall and her heart going like a hammer, alive, and the town not ending, and the fire only a fire.

"I'm sorry," she said, when she could.

"Don't be," Spronck said, still not coming near, letting her have her distance until she chose to leave it. "There's not a soul in this town the fire doesn't reach for, one way or another. Machteld can't hear the Nieuwe Kerk bell rung hard without going grey — her whole family's under it. Old Pauwels won't come to a night firing at all, works only the days. You're in good company at this fire, weduwe. We're all of us here because we chose to work next to the thing that frightens us most, and I've never been sure if that's the bravest thing about potters or the maddest." He said it lightly, and it was, she understood, a gift, the lightness — he was giving her a way to be a person again and not a spectacle, and she took it, and came off the wall, and picked up the pieces of the broken mug because her hands wanted something to do.

Later, when the fire had settled and the men had gone back to their easy talk and left the two of them a little apart in the warmth, Spronck told her about his brother, unasked, in the sidelong way the town told such things, feeding the fire and looking at it and not at her, which was the only way most of them could say it at all.

"He was a cooper," Spronck said. "Down toward the Geerweg, near enough the Secret that there was nothing to find. Wife, three little ones, the shop, the whole house — nothing. Not a stave, not a shoe. I went and stood where the street had been, after, the way everyone went and stood where their own street had been, and there was no telling his ground from the next man's ground; it was all one ground now, all rubble and burnt beam and that grey dust over everything, and I stood there a long time trying to work out which heap had been my brother's door, and I couldn't, and in the end I understood I never would, and that was the burying, such as there was — a man standing on a heap he couldn't name, in the grey dust, not knowing which grain of it was his." He fed the fire. "Half this town had that burying and no other. That's the thing nobody who wasn't here can be made to see. Not the number — you can't feel a number, and nobody ever got the number right anyway — but the not-knowing. The whole households gone with no grain left to bury. My brother's under the Paardenmarkt now, or under the new brick, or blown to Texel for all anyone can say, and I make dishes a mile off with the same fire that took him, and if you asked me was that a strange thing to do I'd tell you it was the only thing to do, because the fire was going to go on being fire whether I made peace with it or not, and I'd sooner it made me a living than made me a coward my whole life long."

Clara said nothing for a while. The fire breathed. On the bench Machteld's lamp burned steady over the grey birds that did not know they were blue.

"My apprentice," she said at last, which she did not, as a rule, speak of. "Barent. Fifteen. Grinding-boy. He was in the shop when it went. I dug him out. So I had the other kind — I had the grain to bury, I had the whole of him to carry out and lay in the hasty ground, which they told me, after, I should count a mercy, and I have never once managed to." She looked at the fire. "I don't know which is worse. Yours or mine. I've thought about it. I don't think there's a worse. I think there's just the two kinds, and everyone in this town carries the one or the other, and we none of us say which, and here we all are, working next to the fire on purpose." She almost smiled, at the fire, not at him. "You said that first day I'd be in good company at this fire. You were righter than you knew."

"I generally am," Spronck said, "about fires, if nothing else," and let it be light again, which was his gift, and they sat on in the warmth as the long fire burned down toward its steady heat, two people who had each dug what there was to dig and each come, by different roads, to sit up all night beside the thing that had done it, on purpose, to make it give something back.

It was near dawn, the fire steadying to its long even burn, when Grietje came.

The neighbour had fallen asleep and the child had woken and found her gone and would not be soothed, and had come out in the dark along the freezing canal in her nightgown and cloak to the one place she knew her aunt had gone, and stood now in the kiln-mouth's red light shaking, and Clara's whole heart went out of her at the sight of it — the child out alone in the dark that had swallowed her once, come looking, and Clara went to her and got down and held her, there in the red firelight, and felt the child shaking not only with cold.

"It's the fire," Grietje said into her shoulder, hoarse, the crack deep in her voice. "It's the fire, it sounds like — it sounds like the — "

"I know," Clara said. "I know it does. I heard it too, tonight. I stood right where you're standing and it took me back too. You're not wrong and you're not soft. It sounds like the twelfth of October and it will always sound a little like it, to you and to me, as long as we live." She held the child off and made her look. "But look at it. Look what it makes." And she took the little fired tile out of her apron, the child's first bird, blue and whole and alive, that she had carried these days meaning to give it back at the right moment and had not found the moment till now. "You made this. You laid it down grey in the dark and you couldn't see the blue in it, and you trusted the fire, and the fire made it blue and it'll be blue a thousand years. The same fire. The very same thing that frightens us both to the marrow — that's the thing that made your bird. It broke the town. And it made your bird. Both. It's the same fire."

Grietje held the tile in the red light and looked at it and looked at the kiln and looked at it again, the small blue bird that the frightening thing had made, and Clara watched the child do the hard sum, the sum she had spent the fortnight doing herself and half a lifetime before it: that the thing that breaks and the thing that makes can be the very same thing, and that you do not get to keep the making without standing near the breaking, and that the whole trade, the whole of living past a thing that should have killed you, was learning to stand near the fire on purpose and let it make something instead of only running from what it broke.

"Not to grieve the breaking," Grietje said, small, the old phrase, Clara's phrase, that the child had heard at the slab all her life. And then, working it out as she said it, making a new one, a fire one: "And not to fear the fire. Only to watch it."

"Only to watch it," Clara said, and thought that the child had said the thing better than she could have, the way the child said most true things, and held her in the red light until the shaking was only cold, and then took her home along the canal in the grey coming morning to sleep, and knew, walking, that she had made up her mind, and that it had been the child and the fire and the blue bird that made it up and not the ledger at all.


## 8. The Reckoning

She told Spronck the next day that she would be his colour-hand, and he shook her blue hand with his burnt one and did not make a speech of it, which she was grateful for, and they agreed a wage, week on week, honest coin, and it was the first thing since the burst that had felt like a beginning instead of a shoring-up. And within the week Doncker brought it to the board, exactly as he had said he would, and the reckoning came due.

She had known it would. She had chosen it open-eyed, the way she chose everything now. But knowing a blow is coming does not soften the land of it, and it landed hard, and from more sides than she had guarded.

The board, first. Doncker's reading of her leave was as sound as he had promised — a widow's continuance stretched to the dead man's enrolled trade and no further, and grinding raw blue to a pottery was, the elders agreed, murmuring, a new trade and outside the grant — and they were of a mind to rule against her and lift the leave, which would leave her a woman with a lease and a slab and no right to sell what she ground on it, exactly as Doncker had drawn the picture in her poor front room. They did not rule at once; Vromans, old and tired and no lover of Doncker, made them adjourn it a month for the potters to be heard, since it touched the potters' custom too; but the way the wind set in that room was plain, and Clara walked out of the guildhouse into the cold with the old feeling, the feeling of a door being closed soft behind her.

The lapis credit, next, and this was the one she had not seen coming from that quarter. The last stone she had bought, two winters back, to grind against the great commission that never came, she had bought on credit through an Amsterdam factor — and the factor was one of Doncker's, on Doncker's books, as half the colour-trade of the province ran on Doncker's books, and the credit that had sat quiet and unpressed for two years was called, that week, in full, with a politeness that fooled no one. Doncker did not have to say he had called it. He did not call it himself; a factor called it; the factor merely happened to be his; and the timing merely happened to fall in the same week as the board. It was, Clara thought, reading the demand, a very Doncker way to knife a person — at one remove, by the letter, with clean hands and a clear conscience, so that he could be sincerely sorry about it to your face and mean the sorrow. The sum was more than she had. It was more than the goldfinch would fetch, near enough. It was, laid against the fee still owing on her continuance and the lease coming due at the turn of the year, the exact arithmetic of ruin, drawn tight from three directions at once so that no one string could be blamed for the strangling.

And the nephew, last, and this was only spite, but spite has a long arm. Doncker's nephew, who had the good market-right and the good stall on the new Voldersgracht, put it about the trade that the widow Wijnants was finished, her leave as good as lifted, her credit called, her shop lost at the turn of the year — put it about in the low sure way the trade passed a name — and dropped his price on ground colour to the few painters left, below even the loss-rate Clara ground the town-work at, to make sure that whatever happened at the board, there would be no painters' trade left for her to fall back on. He could afford to; his uncle's supply was behind him; he could sell at a loss for a season the way a rich man can hold his breath longer than a poor one. And so the old trade, the painters' trade, the trade she had held by a widow's stubbornness through a whole hard year, was being quietly drowned under her even as the guild moved to bar her from the new one, so that she should be left, if it all fell as Doncker had arranged it to fall, with nothing at all: no painters' trade, no potters' trade, no leave, no credit, no shop.

She saw the whole shape of it laid out and had to grant it, coldly, the admiration one craftsman grants another's work: it was beautifully made. It was Doncker at his purest — no blow struck by his own hand, no rule broken, no unkind word he could not have sworn to before the dominee, only three quiet legal strings drawn tight at once from three directions, the board and the factor and the nephew, each of them able to say with a clear face that they had done nothing but their right, and the widow strangled clean in the middle of them with no throat you could point to as the one that did it. He would be sincerely sorry. That was the worst of it and always had been. He would come to her, when it was done, in some cold passage, and be truly grieved that it had come to this, and offer, even then, to see her settled warm, and mean every word; and he would never once, to his dying day, feel the sorrow and the strangling touch, because he had arranged it so that even to himself his hands were clean.

She sat up late over the ledger in the poor room with the child asleep above and the goldfinch dark on the wall, and did the sums, and they did not come out. There was no arrangement of them that came out. She could sell the bird and clear the credit and be left leaveless and shopless and trade-less in the spring, a woman of five-and-thirty with a niece to raise and nothing to raise her on. She could go to Doncker and say the one word he kept telling her would ease it all, and be settled warm, and grind in the corner of his name with her own name filed off, which was a kind of living and the kind she would rather die than take, and knew she would rather, and had known it for a year. Or she could refuse it all and go under free, which was no plan at all, only a preference.

She sat with the bad ledger a long time. The turf fell in on itself in the grate. On the wall the goldfinch hung in the dark, the loose dead paint slowly darkening, the varnish slowly ambering, the small miracle slowly dying as it hung there being loved, worth more this winter than everything else she owned put together, one panel of a dead man's late light that death was already, patiently, getting at.

She thought about the plate on Spronck's shelf, the blue sunk in the white glaze, the one colour they made in that town that death could not get at. She thought about the child's fired bird, blue a thousand years. She thought about a great commission that never came, that she had ground a marriage's patience against, and how she had waited her whole trade for the one order that would set her free, and how it had never come and was never going to come to a painters' colour-woman in a dying painters' town. And she thought — turning it slowly, the way she turned a stone in the light to read the vein — that she had been waiting for the wrong order in the wrong trade, and that Doncker, damn him, had said the true thing in her front room without knowing he was arming her: *they'll sell it by the shipload, out on the Company's keels to every port there is, and it'll be the thing this town is known for long after your painters and my lapis are forgotten together.*

She did not have a plan yet. But she had, at last, the right question, which was not *how do I save the old trade* but *how do I get out from under all three strings at once by pulling on the one thing none of them has thought to guard*; and she went up the shrieking stair to lie down beside the sleeping child with the question turning, and did not sleep, and was not, for the first time in that whole grinding year, only afraid.


# The Blue and the White — Part Three: The Blue and the White


## 9. The Empty Slab

Deep winter came down on Delft the way it always came in those years, hard and grey and early, the canals going first to slush and then to a black skin of ice you could hear the barges breaking at dawn, and the turf dear at the Turfmarkt, and the poor shop off the Nieuwe Langendijk holding its little warmth like a coin in a closed fist. Clara ground the guild's umber in the mornings, still, because the leave was not yet lifted and the bargain still bound her while it stood, and she ground it clean and at a loss under the darkening goldfinch, and in the afternoons she went to the Blue Hand and ground the blue that was not there and was slowly, week on week, getting right; and between the two she waited for the board to sit again, and did not have a plan, only the right question, turning.

Agatha came to her twice that winter, as she came most winters, because the two of them were widows of the same morning and there was a thing between them that wanted no speaking and would not be put down.

She was much aged, Fabritius's Agatha, aged past her years the way grief ages a person from the inside so that the face goes on ahead of the body; she had never remarried and never would, and she lived thin and quiet with what little the relief and the sale of a few of the surviving pictures had left her, and she wore her widowhood the way some women wear a religion, not for show but because it was the last of the marriage and she would not put it off. She came in out of the cold and sat by the little grate with her hands round the cup of warmed small beer Clara gave her, and looked at the goldfinch on the wall a long while, as she always did, the little bird her husband had made the year he died, that she had given into Clara's keeping herself, that first terrible spring, saying she could not have it in the house and could not sell it and could not burn it and could not look at it, and that Clara was the only one in Delft who would neither pity it nor price it, and would just let it hang and be seen.

"It's darkening," Agatha said, of the bird, which she said every time, half a grief and half a comfort.

"It is. Slowly. The varnish more than the paint. It'll want a careful hand in fifty years."

"There'll be someone." Agatha turned the cup in her thin hands. "I hear the potters have taken you up. The blue." She said it without the edge that Clara had half braced for, the edge the painters' half of the trade put on the word *potters*; Agatha had no edge left in her for such things. "Carel would have liked that, I think. He was always for the new thing, the light no one had painted, the box of glasses, the perspective — he'd have gone to the kilns himself, if he'd lived, and painted their dishes and shocked the guild and laughed at them for being shocked. He was never precious about where the beauty came from. Only that it came." She looked at Clara. "You've that in you too. I saw it the day you came for him, in the ruin, and carried my — carried the little panel out for me when I couldn't touch it. You don't grieve the beauty for changing its address. Most of us do. I do." She drank. "I came to say I'm glad of it. The blue. Whatever the painters say, and they'll say plenty — Doncker chief among them, I don't doubt — I'm glad there's one of us the fire didn't finish making things. That's all I came to say. That, and to look at the bird."

"You could take it back," Clara said, gently, as she said every winter, meaning it and half hoping the no. "It's yours. It always was. If a winter came you needed the selling of it — "

"No." Agatha set the cup down. "It's where it should be. In a shop where the seeing goes on, not in my cold room where only I'd weep at it, nor in some Amsterdam cupboard where a rich man shows it once a year to a friend who doesn't look. You keep it, Clara. Keep it hanging where the light comes across, and let the young ones stop in front of it, and that's Carel going on, which is more than a grave gives him." She stood, drawing her cloak, a thin aged woman with the whole of a dead great painter folded away inside her sober dark, and at the door she said the thing she said every year, the thing that was the whole of what the two of them had: "We're the ones who dug our own out with our hands. You and I. Not many can say it. Nobody who hasn't done it knows what it costs, and there's nobody I need to tell." And she went out into the cold, and Clara stood with the two cooling cups and the darkening bird and understood, not for the first time, that the panel on her wall was not hers to sell and had never been, whatever the market said it was worth; that it was held in trust, one widow's grief lodged in another widow's keeping; and that this, more than any argument of coin, was going to matter very much within the week.

The collector came in the worst week of the cold.

He came from Amsterdam, in a good coat and good gloves, with a manner Clara knew at once for the manner of a man who bought other men's ruin cheap and called it patronage, and he had heard — everyone had heard; the trade passed a falling name faster than a rising one — that the widow Wijnants was finished and had a Fabritius on her wall. He looked at the bird a long time, and he did know how to look, she granted him that; he looked the way the seeing ones looked; and then he named a sum, and it was a large sum, larger than she had let herself think the panel would fetch, large enough to clear the Amsterdam credit and the continuance-fee both and leave something over toward a spring.

"You're pressed, weduwe," he said, kindly, the way the well-fed are kind. "I know it; forgive me for knowing it; it's my trade to know it. I'm not here to rob you. That's a fair price and a better one than you'd get from anyone in this stricken town, and the picture wants a wall that can keep it — it's darkening already, you can see it, the varnish going; in five years in this damp it'll be half gone, and in a collection it'd be cleaned and kept and live another two hundred years. You'd be doing the bird a kindness as much as yourself." He drew off a glove, to show he was in earnest, that he'd count coin here and now. "And whatever's owed, it's paid, and you walk into the spring clear. Think what that's worth, to a woman placed as you are."

And Clara stood in the cold room and thought about it honestly, because she owed the child honesty if nothing else, and because a woman who would not weigh a real offer was a fool and not a free woman. It was true, all of it. The bird was dying on her wall. It would live in his collection. The sum would clear her debts and buy a spring. And she found, weighing it honestly, coin on one pan and the bird on the other, that she would not do it, and that the reason she would not do it was not sentiment, though she loved the bird, and was not spite, though she'd have hated to see it go to a man who bought ruin cheap; the reason was that the bird was the one thing she had that death could not entirely take by ordinary means, the last of a man's late seeing, and that if she sold it to buy herself out of this winter she would have done the exact thing the whole trade did, the thing she had spent her life half-ashamed of — spent her strength breaking a beautiful thing smaller so that others might have it, sold the seeing to buy the bread — and she would rather find another way to the bread. And under all of it, plainer than any of it: the bird was not hers to sell. It was Agatha's, given into her keeping and not her ownership, a widow's grief lodged with a widow, and a thing held in trust is a thing a person of any honour does not turn into coin however cold the winter, and there was an end of it, and the man could keep his fair price.

"No," she said. "Thank you. It's a fair price and I know it. But no."

He was surprised, and then, briefly, before he mastered it, contemptuous, the contempt of a man who cannot conceive of a poor person refusing money for a principle he can't see, and then he was smooth again and sorry to have troubled her and gone, back to Amsterdam with his gloves and his fair price, and Clara stood in the room where the refusal had cost her a spring and felt, oddly, not poorer but steadier, the way you feel when you find out under pressure that a thing you claimed to believe you actually believe.

Grietje had watched the whole of it from the stair.

"You could have sold it," the child said, when the man was gone. Not a reproach; a testing; the child took the measure of the world by pushing at it.

"I could have."

"It's worth a lot. He said. It's worth more than everything."

"It's worth a great deal of money," Clara agreed. "And we're very poor, and it's very cold, and I said no. You want to know why."

"Yes."

Clara sat down on the stair beside her, which she did not often do, and they looked at the bird together in the grey light, the small blue-eyed goldfinch chained to its ring, holding still to be seen, the loose dead paint darkening year on year.

"Because a man made that out of looking," Clara said. "Out of looking harder at a small chained bird than anyone had ever looked, until he could put down on a foot of wood not the bird but the *seeing* of the bird — the air round it, the light on it, the whole of how it is to truly look at a living thing. And then he died, in the burst, with almost everything else he ever made, and this is nearly all that's left of all that looking. If I sell it, it goes into a rich man's cupboard in Amsterdam and it's safe and it's kept and once a year he shows it to a friend. If I keep it, it hangs here in a colour-shop where painters and potters come, and every now and again one of them looks at it — really looks, the way you look, the way the young one looks — and learns something about seeing that they carry off and put in their own work. The bird teaches. That's what it's for. That's the nearest a dead man gets to going on: the thing he made keeps teaching the seeing after the seer's in the ground. And I won't sell that to buy a spring, because springs come round again and that doesn't." She stood up. "That's why. It's a foolish reason and it'll likely see us both hungrier, and I'd make it again."

Grietje looked at the bird, and then, slowly, she got up and went to the sill and took out her four things and laid them in the grey light — the tile, the button, the pebble, the pyrite — and beside them she laid the fifth thing now, the thing she carried these days along with the four, the small fired tile with her own clumsy blue bird on it, and she looked from the dead man's bird on the wall to her own bird in the fired clay, and Clara watched her make the connection with her whole grave face.

"I could make more of them," Grietje said. "Birds. On the plates. The blue kind, the kind that lasts. If I got good like Machteld. I could put birds on all the plates and they'd go — Machteld said the plates go on the ships, they go all over the world — I could put the bird on the plates and it'd go all over the world and it'd never fade, it's in the glass, and it wouldn't be *the* bird, it wouldn't be his bird, but it'd be — " she frowned, working it, nine years old and reaching for it — "it'd be the bird going on. The looking going on. On a thing that doesn't fade and goes everywhere." She looked up. "That's better than the cupboard in Amsterdam. That's the bird going *out* instead of getting *kept*."

And Clara stood very still in the cold poor room and understood that the child had done it again, had read the thing truer and faster than she could, had taken the whole tangle of the winter — the bird, the blue, the dying trade and the rising one, the seeing that must not be locked in a cupboard, the colour that death can't get at, the plate that sails the world — and tied it in one plain knot a nine-year-old could hold; and understood, all at once and completely, what the right question's answer was, and what she was going to do, and that she had known it since the kiln-opening and had only needed the child to say it plainly enough for her to stop being afraid of it.

She was not going to save the old trade. The old trade was going out on the tide and no stubbornness would hold it. She was going to walk out from under all three of Doncker's strings by pulling the one thing none of them guarded — the potters, the rising trade, the future of the town — and she was going to do it in her own name, with her own bird teaching on the wall, and she was going to bring to the blue-and-white the one thing no potter's boy in Delft could bring it, which was twenty years of grinding the truest blue in the world; and the child was going to paint the bird on the plates, the looking going on, going out, all over the world, on a colour that would not fade.

"Get your cloak," she said. "We're going to the pottery, and then we're going to see Vromans, and then, God help us both, we're going to see Doncker. And this time I've something to say to him."


## 10. Blue and White

She went to the potters first, because the plan was nothing without them, and because she had learned enough in a winter of the Blue Hand to know that the plateelbakkers of Delft, for all they fired mud in old breweries and were looked down on by the painters' half of their own guild, were not a scatter of ex-brewers to be ordered about by a colour-merchant. They were a rising power and they knew it, and what they wanted above all things was not to be told, by the old painters'-colour men, that their blue was a painters' monopoly to be bought only through Doncker's hands on Doncker's terms.

Spronck called them together for her — five masters, at the Blue Hand, of a grey afternoon, over the small beer, with the kiln banked and ticking as it cooled between firings — and Clara stood up in front of them, which she had not done in front of men since the burst, and laid it out plain, the way Spronck laid things out, once and honest.

She told them what Doncker meant to do: bar her leave at the board, so that no free colour-hand could grind for the potteries, and then supply the potteries' blue himself, through his own factors, at his own price, painters'-guild control of the potters' colour, a hand on the throat of the one trade in Delft that was growing. She told them she could grind their blue finer and truer than any boy in any of their shops, and that they knew it, or would when they saw a firing of it; that she would be their colour-hand, all of theirs, a free supplier in her own name and not in any merchant's; and that the price of that was standing with her at the board when it sat, the whole of the potters' custom speaking with one voice, so that the guild should understand that to lift her leave was not to discipline one irregular widow but to insult the entire rising trade of the town and hand its colour to a man who despised it.

"He'll tell you he's ordering the trade," she said. "Bringing it under one hand that knows it, so it doesn't run cheap and freckled and shame the guild. He said it to me in my own front room and he half believes it. But ask yourselves whose hand, and whose price, and whose name goes on the bottom of every dish. He'd have you make the blue-and-white that's going to be the pride of this town and the wonder of the world, and he'd own the blue in every stroke of it, and you'd buy your own colour from him at his rate forever. I'm offering you the other thing. A colour-hand who's yours and no one's, who grinds the truest blue in the province, who'll take an honest wage and leave you your own trade in your own names. That's the whole offer. I need you at the board. You need a blue Doncker doesn't own. I think that's a fair trade and I think you think so too, or Coenraad wouldn't have got you here."

They argued it, the way men argue a thing they have already half decided, and old Pauwels grumbled about a woman, and Spronck said the thing about the freckled dishes, and Machteld's master said he'd seen the widow's grind and would take her over any three boys in Delft, and in the end they came to it, the five of them, because it was plainly in their interest and because Doncker had, over the years, trodden on every one of them in his sincere ordering way and none of them had forgotten it. They would stand with her at the board. And more than that — this was Spronck's doing, and he did it without fuss, the way he did everything — they would clear the Amsterdam credit among them, an advance against the blue she would grind them through the year, so that Doncker's called loan should lose its teeth; and the Saxon cobalt they fired with came down through a Hamburg factor of the potters' own, nothing to do with Doncker's lapis network at all, so that the man who thought he had his hand on all the blue in Delft had, it turned out, no hand at all on the blue that was actually going to matter. His chokehold was on a dying colour. The rising one had never run through him and never would.

And so, through the tail of that hard winter, Clara ground blue for the potteries of Delft, all of them, from the Blue Hand outward, and the trade turned toward her the way water turns toward the low place, because the thing she had said was true and the firings proved it: her blue came out of the kilns cleaner and deeper and more even than any potter's boy had ever ground, no grit, no freckle, no burned scab at the stroke's edge, a blue that let the girls at the benches paint fine and know it would fire fine, and word went round the potters' half of the trade the way it had once gone round the painters' — *the widow Wijnants has the true blue* — only now there were pounds of it wanted where before there had been pinches, and coin coming in week on week where before there had been the slow bleed of a dying custom. She took proper rooms at last — the deadline near, the guild watching — a colour-shop in a sound brick house on the Oosteinde, near enough to the Blue Hand and the other potteries to serve them all, with a good grinding-room at the back and a room above for the two of them and a north wall for the bird; and she moved the grey slab and Gerrit's muller into it, and hung the goldfinch on the north wall where the potters and the girls and the few painters left would see it and, some of them, stop and look; and it was a shop found and held before the turn, in her own name, and it broke the third of Doncker's three strings before he had quite finished pulling the first.

And Grietje painted.

She was at the Blue Hand's long bench most days now, the youngest there, taken on by Machteld's master as a favour to the widow at first and kept on his own account within the month, because the child could draw, it turned out, could draw the way she read the pyrite and the ledger, truer and faster than she had any business doing at nine; and she painted birds. She painted birds on the biscuit-ware in the drab grey that did not know it was blue, birds and then flowers and then the fine running borders, learning the fire's rules the way Clara had learned them, laying the colour blind and trusting the kiln; and she got better through the winter the way children get better, in sudden lurches that stopped Clara's breath. And one grey morning at a kiln-opening Machteld handed Clara a fired plate, white and clean, with a goldfinch on it — not a clumsy first bird now but a good bird, a swift confident bird on the wing, blue on the white and sunk in the glaze forever, a bird that anyone who knew the little panel on the north wall would have known at once for its child, its grandchild, the looking carried down a generation and out onto a thing that would not fade and would sail, God and the Company willing, to the far side of the earth.

Clara held the plate a long time. Grietje watched her, anxious, wanting the verdict.

"It's a good bird," Clara said, which was true, and not the half of what she felt, and the child knew it was not the half, and glowed. "It's a good bird, and it'll be a good bird in a thousand years, in a house on the other side of the world, and the man who taught the seeing that taught your seeing is under the Nieuwe Kerk yard and will never know he did it, and that's the whole of the trade, Grietje, and you've got it, and I'm — " and Clara stopped, because she did not, as a rule, say such things, and because the girls were watching, and instead she did the thing she did instead of saying, which was to take the child's blue hand in her own blue hand, in front of the whole bench, and hold it, and the child understood the size of that, the way she understood most things, and let it be said that way, which was the true way, the family's way, the way that did not need words it could not carry.


## 11. The Guild's Blue

The board sat again on a raw bright morning at the tail of the winter, in the guildhouse on the Voldersgracht, and Clara went to it not alone, as she had gone the year before to the ruling that bound her, but with the potters of Delft at her back — five masters in their good plain coats, Spronck at the front of them, and behind the masters a scatter of their people, the journeymen and the older apprentices and, at the very back, uninvited and unremovable, Machteld and Grietje, blue to the elbow, come to see it; so that when the beadle opened the door on the board the board saw not a widow to be tidied away but the whole rising trade of the town standing in its hall.

Doncker had his paper, of course. He read the leave again, the widow's continuance stretched to the dead man's enrolled trade and no further, the grinding of raw blue to a pottery a new trade outside the grant; he read it well, correct as a deacon, sincere in every word, a man defending good order against irregularity; and Clara let him finish, because there was nothing in the reading she could argue and the argument was not going to be won on the reading.

It was Spronck who stood, when Doncker sat, and Spronck did not read anything, because Spronck had no paper and did not believe in them.

"Meesters of the board," he said, in the flat easy harbour voice that filled the hall without seeming to rise, "meester Doncker reads you the law very well, and I'll not argue the law with him; he's better at it than I'll ever be, and that's rather my point. He'll tell you a potter's blue is a new trade outside the widow's grant. Maybe it is. But a plateelbakker is of this guild — we're under St Luke, all of us, the potters and the glassmen and the painters together, it says so on the charter on your own wall — and the colour a guildsman uses in his guild trade is guild business, and who says otherwise? A colour-hand grinds blue for a member of this guild in the practice of his enrolled craft. If that's a new trade, then half of what everyone in this room does is a new trade, and we'd best all stop and re-enroll." A murmur; old Vromans's mouth twitched. "But set the law aside, because you'll settle the law however you please and we both know it. Here's the thing the law won't tell you, and I will, because I'm a potter and I know it in my hands. This town is going to live or die, these next twenty years, on the blue-and-white. The breweries are gone. The painters — God keep them — are few and poor, and I say it with no joy, for I've a Fabritius envy the size of a house. What's growing in Delft is the ware. It's going out on the Company's ships already, a dish at a time, and it's going to go out by the shipload, and the day is coming when a man in Batavia or Nagasaki or Amsterdam turns a plate over to read the mark and it says *Delft* on the bottom, and that day the name of this town is the blue-and-white and nothing else, forever." He let it sit. "And the blue in it — the actual blue, in the actual glaze — is going to be ground by somebody. You can let it be ground by boys who'd rather be at the harbour, coarse and freckled, and shame the mark. Or you can let it be ground by the finest colour-hand in this province, who happens to be a widow, who happens to grind truer than any man of us, and who asks nothing of this board but leave to do it in her own name. That's the choice. Not law. Just: whose hands do you want the blue of Delft ground by, when the whole world's reading the bottom of the plate." He sat down. "I'd think on it longer than one morning, meesters, if I were you. It's a longer question than it looks."

Doncker rose again, and Clara watched him, and saw — she had known him a long time — that he had understood, in the middle of Spronck's speech, that he had lost; not the law, he still had the law; but the room, and the tide, and the twenty years. He was too able a man not to see it. And she watched him decide, in the space of standing up, how to lose, and she gave him, in her own mind, the grudging due she always ended by giving him: that he lost like a man who cared about the thing more than the winning of it, which was the best and the worst of him.

"I withdraw the complaint," Doncker said.

There was a stir; the board had been ready to wrangle a morning. He raised a hand to still it, patient, correct.

"I withdraw it," he said again. "Meester Spronck has the right of it, though he puts it as a potter would, all tide and no order — there'll be a reckoning yet for letting the colour of this town run loose in one freelancing widow's hands, and when it comes freckled and cheap and shames the mark, you'll remember I stood here and warned you, and I'll take no pleasure in it." He gathered his paper. "But he's right that the ware is the future and right that her grind is the best in the province; I've never once said her grind was poor, only that a trade wants ordering, and I see this board has no stomach this morning for the ordering, and I'll not spend my standing on a fight the tide's already settled. Let the widow grind for the potteries in her own name. Let the board note that Willem Doncker said, in this hall, on this day, that it would rue leaving so great a trade unruled — and let the board note that Willem Doncker withdrew rather than break a woman who buried her husband and dug her own life out of the mud of this town with her hands. I've been called her enemy in this trade. I'd have you note I was the one who withdrew."

And it was, Clara thought, so exactly Doncker, so precisely and completely the whole of the man, that she nearly laughed and nearly wept and did neither: that he could not lose without arranging, even in the losing, to be seen to have lost nobly; that he was, even now, ordering the room's memory of him the way he had spent his life ordering everything, so that the account should read the way he needed it to read. And she saw, too, under it, the thing that was true in it, which was always the trouble with Doncker — that he had withdrawn, when he might have wrangled and possibly won a morning's damage; that some part of him, the part that had been Gerrit's friend before he was her enemy, had decided not to break her when he still could have tried; and that he would go home and believe, sincerely, wholly, that he had been magnanimous, and would never once feel the magnanimity and the two years' strangling touch.

He came to her in the passage after, as he had come the year before, because he could not help himself.

"You've won it," he said. "The blue of Delft. You know that's what you've won this morning? Not a widow's shop. The blue of Delft, for as long as the ware lasts, which will be longer than either of us." He looked at her, and there was no triumph in it and no rancour either, only the weary certainty she knew of old. "I meant it, that it wants ordering. It'll go cheap and freckled without one hand over it, and the world will tire of it, and in a hundred years they'll be making it in England and Germany better than Delft makes it, because Delft let it run loose. You'll not live to see me proved right. I take what comfort there is in that."

"You may be right," Clara said. "I've never once said you were only wrong, meester. That's the whole trouble between us. You've been half right about everything and you've used the half-right to take the whole stall every time, and called it order, and meant it." She looked at him, the heavy correct aging man who had been her husband's friend. "Go home, Willem. You've lost the blue and you've kept your dignity and you'll tell it, tonight, as a kindness you did me, and there'll be enough truth in that to let you sleep. I don't hate you. I never could quite manage it, which you've never forgiven me for."

Something went across his face, the old thing, quickly mastered. "Gerrit understood me," he said.

"Gerrit understood everyone," Clara said. "It's why he died poor. Good day, meester." And she left him in the passage, correct and aging and alone with his dignity, and went out to where the potters were, and Grietje, blue to the elbow, jumping down off the mounting-block to run to her; and it was not a rout of Doncker, and it was not clean, and it had cost her a winter and a spring and a friendship that had never been one; and it was, past all arguing, a victory, and she knew it for one, walking out into the raw bright noon with the whole rising trade of Delft at her back and the child's blue hand reaching for her blue hand, because she walked out of that house the free colour-hand of the potteries of Delft, in her own right and her own name, answerable to no man's roof, holding the blue that the world was going to want for two hundred years. Bought dear. Held narrow. Enough.


## 12. The Colour That Remains

By the spring she had a trade again, a whole one, growing where the old one had died, and the young painter came for blue on a fair evening in May — but he did not come for ultramarine, because there was none, and both of them knew there would be none; he came only to tell her, awkward, hat in his hands, standing under the goldfinch in the good north light of the new shop, that he had finished it.

"The woman by the window," he said. "It's done. Or — it's as done as I get them; I never quite know. But it's done." He looked at the bird on the wall and then, making himself, at her. "I wanted you to know. Because of the blue. It's — " he stopped, the man who did his saying with his hands, and then found, for once, a few of the words. "It's in the shadow of the cloth, the way I said. The way you ground it. There's a blue in the shadow no one's put there so, and when the light comes across her it — it holds. It holds the whole picture. People stop in front of it and they don't know why, and it's the blue doing it, the blue you can't quite see, in the shadow, holding the light." He turned his hat. "It's the last of it. Your reclaimed blue. The last true ultramarine in Delft, you said, and I've put it all in the one picture, and I think — I hope — it's the best thing I'll do. I wanted the one who ground it to know where it went."

"It went where the light comes across," Clara said. "That's where I told you to put it."

"It did." He almost smiled. "It did exactly."

She did not tell him, then or ever, that a dead man had wanted the same picture in the same words and never got to make it, and that he had made it now without knowing he carried it, the way water carries the finest colour it doesn't know it holds; there was nothing in the telling for him and a good deal that might weigh him, and the dead did not need their names spoken over every colour, only the colour ground true and put where the light could find it, and he had put it there. So she said none of it. She wished him well of the picture, and meant it, and watched him go out into the long gold evening a man who had made, with the last of her blue, the one thing that would outlast them both — a woman by a window, morning light across her, a blue in the shadow of the cloth that no one had put there before, a blue you could breathe — and she stood in the door of the sound new shop and watched him the way she had once watched two other men go down other streets, and did not, this time, wish she had not looked, and did not grieve, much, that the old blue was gone entirely now, out of her hands and into the light where it belonged.

Because there was the other blue now, the new one, the one that death could not get at, and it was going out into the world.

The great order had come in April, and it had frightened her, which was how she knew it was the real one, the order she had waited her whole trade for and been waiting in the wrong trade to receive. A merchant of the Company, in Amsterdam, had placed with the potters of Delft — with all of them together, more than any one pottery could throw or fire or paint alone — a service of blue-and-white for the East: hundreds of pieces, a whole freight of it, plates and dishes and jars to go out on the autumn ships to Batavia and beyond, Delft ware for the tables of the Company's men on the far side of the earth, and, the merchant wrote, if this first freight sold as he thought it would, there would be another, and another, for as long as the ships sailed and the world wanted blue. It was more blue than Clara had ground in her life. It was more blue than she could grind alone, and she had already taken on two girls to grind under her, and would take on more, and the coin of it would clear the last of every debt and buy the lease outright and set the child's whole future on a footing Clara had never dreamed of for her; and it was, she understood, holding the merchant's letter in her blue hand in the spring light, the thing itself, the great commission, come at last, twenty years late and in the wrong colour and better than she had ever let herself want.

Grietje would paint on it. That was in the letter too, in a way — the merchant had asked for birds, among the flowers and the knots; the Company's men liked a bird on a plate, it seemed, out there on the far side of the world; and the Blue Hand's youngest bench-hand painted the truest bird in Delft, everyone said so now, the widow's girl, the one who did the goldfinches. So the child would paint her blue birds on the white clay, hundreds of them, the looking going on and going out, sailing to Batavia and Nagasaki and back to Amsterdam on a colour that would not fade, in a thousand houses on the far side of the earth, the seeing of a dead man carried down through a living girl onto a thing that death could not get at; and it would not be his bird, and it would not be the little panel that hung darkening on the north wall being loved; but it would be the bird going on, going out instead of getting kept, exactly as the child had said in the cold winter room, which was better, Clara had come to think, than any cupboard in Amsterdam.

She stood in the door of the shop in the failing of the fair May evening, and Grietje came and stood with her, drying her blue hands on the rag, blue to the creases now the way Clara was, the trade gone into her for good.

The evening had come down to the blue hour, the little while at the edge of dark that the painters cursed and could not do without, when the gold goes out of the sky by degrees and every colour on the earth deepens and stands true a moment before the night takes it. The mended stretch of canal lay still and held the last of the light, blue on blue, the new brick of the rebuilding and the old stone of the standing houses all gone one deepening colour together, and the tower of the Nieuwe Kerk stood black against a sky the exact grave blue that no man could grind and every man tried to, the blue you could breathe, going deeper as they watched. Somewhere the Blue Hand's kiln was banked for the night, ticking as it cooled, full of grey ware that did not know yet it was blue, that would come out of the fire in the morning blue and alive and bound, some of it, for the far side of the world. Somewhere a woman called a child in. The water lay unmoving.

The cost was not paid off and would not be. Barent was in the hasty ground still and would stay there, a boy who had wanted the guild so badly it came off him like heat, and there was no blue, fired or ground, that gave him back, and Clara would carry him unmended to her own grave, which was the honest price, and she did not pretend it away. Fabritius was under the Nieuwe Kerk yard, and the little bird on the wall was dying slowly of being paint, however she loved it, and no firing could save what was made of oil and time. The town was three years into its twenty years' rebuilding and the ruin still ran black under the spring mud across the quarter, and no one would ever count the dead, and the not-counting was a wound the town would wear under its new brick forever. The fire had taken the town. She had made it her whole living, now, to stand next to the fire on purpose and make it give something back, and the giving-back did not cancel the taking; the two of them lived side by side in her, the burst and the blue, the same fire, and she had stopped expecting the one to pay off the other, because that was not how the ledger of a life came out. It came out both at once, the breaking and the making, the grief and the trade, and you did not get to keep the one without standing near the other, and the whole of the wisdom she had was in not fearing the fire and not grieving the breaking, only watching, and grinding true, and putting the colour where the light could find it.

"Bluest hands in Delft," Grietje said, holding hers up in the last of it, the old game, the child's whole small greedy joy in the mark of the trade.

"Not any more," Clara said. "There's two of us now. And there'll be more by autumn, the way the order's going. Bluest hands in Delft won't do. We'll have to think of something else."

Grietje considered this with her whole grave face, the way she considered everything, the mended arm, the cracked whole voice, the blue to the creases. "The bluest hands in Delft," she said at last, amending it, closing the account with Neeltje's flat certainty, "and everywhere the plates go. Which is everywhere." And she took her aunt's blue hand in her own blue hand, and Clara looked down at her, the child who was not the child she had been given and was, past all arguing, hers, and did not correct her, because it was true, or was going to be, which came to the same thing in that trade; because the plates were going everywhere the ships went, and the blue would not fade, and the child's bird would be on some of them, holding still to be seen in houses neither of them would ever stand in, on the far side of a world neither of them would ever cross; and because the light was almost gone.

"The bluest hands in Delft," she said, "and everywhere the plates go." And they stood in the door of the sound new shop and watched the blue go out of the water and the sky come down, and behind them in the dark the little bird held still on the wall in a light of its own making, chained to its ring, waiting to be seen; and above the mended canal the last of the day drained slow and even and without any flattery in it, the colourless Delft light gone at the end all to colour, deepening and deepening, until it was too dark to work by and exactly bright enough to see.


*THE END*

## About the series

*The Blue and the White* is the second novel of **Ultramarine**, a series set in Delft at the turning point of the Dutch Golden Age — the years around the Thunderclap of 12 October 1654, when the town of painters became the town of the blue-and-white that would sail to the far side of the world. Each book follows the widowed colour-grinder Clara Wijnants and her niece Grietje through one turn of that great change: what it costs to make a beautiful thing, and what it means to hand a craft on.

Book One, *Ultramarine*, tells how Clara reclaimed the truest blue in the province grain by grain from the ruin of her own house, and fought the guild for the right to go on. *The Blue and the White* carries the trade from the dying easel to the rising kiln — a blue born of the same fire that broke the town.

The story is not closed. It ends where the outline promises it will: a great order of blue-and-white bound for the East, more than one pottery can grind alone, and the child who will paint it standing ready at the slab, blue to the creases — the next blue not yet made. Readers who want more of Delft can begin at the beginning, with Book One.
