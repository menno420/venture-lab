## Description (paste into KDP "Description" — plain text)

The painters of Delft are dying out. The kilns are just beginning. And the widow who grinds the truest blue in the province must decide which age she belongs to.

Clara Wijnants kept her trade through the worst year of her life — the year the Secret of Holland blew a quarter of Delft into the sky and took her apprentice, her shop, and the painter Carel Fabritius with it. She dug her life out of the mud grain by grain, and the guild grudged her the right to go on. But a widow's leave stretches only so far, her lease runs out at the turn of the year, and the painters left alive are too few and too poor to keep her.

Then a broad, burn-scarred potter comes to her door at first light with a sack of drab grey ore and an impossible claim: this is the finest blue in the world — or it will be, once the fire has it. The delftware potteries are rising in the shells of the town's failed breweries, hungry for a colour no painter's boy can grind clean, and they want Clara's hands. To take the work she must learn a blue she cannot judge with her eye, master the very fire that broke the town, and face down the guild elder who has spent two years trying, sincerely and without once raising his hand, to close her door for good.

The Blue and the White is the second Ultramarine novel: a story of the Dutch Golden Age at its turning point, when Delft stopped being a town of painters and became the town of the blue-and-white that would sail to the far side of the world. About the cost and worth of beauty, the mercy at the root of a craft handed on, and learning to lay the colour down in the dark and trust the fire. Contains bereavement and remembered disaster handled with restraint; no explicit content. Stands alone; deeper after Ultramarine (Book One).
