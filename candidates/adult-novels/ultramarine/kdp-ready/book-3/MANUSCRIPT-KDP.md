# The Common Blue — Part One: The Great Order


## 1. The Order from the East

The blue went into the barrel by the scoop now, and Clara Wijnants still could not quite believe her own hands doing it.

She had the shop to herself in the black hour before the bells, the way she had always kept that hour, though the shop was a sound one now, brick to the ridge, dry in every corner, with a good grey light coming when the light came and a second slab beside her own that a paid boy would stand at after the bells rang. She stood at the first slab and worked the drab grey mess of the *zaffer* under the muller — the roasted cobalt ore, ground with its little glass, that had no more blue in it to the eye than a handful of ash — and measured it off against the smalt in the horn cup, and tipped the mix down through the funnel into the barrel that stood where a sack of pigment would have stood in the old life, a whole barrel, a cooper's barrel, and it was half full and would be filled by the week's end and emptied by the next.

That was the thing she could not get her eye to sit still against. In the old life a year's worth of the true blue had gone into a gallipot the size of a thumb-joint, and she had ground it grain by grain and sold it grain by grain and grieved every grain out the door. Now she ground the grey by the barrel and it went out of the shop by the barrelful, into the kilns off the Oosteinde, and came back to nobody's hand at all — it went onto the white ware and into the fire and out into the world, and she never saw the half of what her own colour became. She had made her peace with that, mostly. Peace was a thing you made the way you made colour: by working the same motion over the same ground until the lumps went out of it.

Above her on the wall, in the dark, the little bird hung where it always hung — the goldfinch on its foot of panel, the chain from the ring to the band about its leg, the loose dragged paint that was hardly paint at all up close and was wholly a living bird a step back. Clara did not need the light to see it. She had it by heart. It was held in trust, not owned; Agatha van Pruyssen had put it into her keeping two springs gone, and Clara kept it, and it was worth by now more than the shop and the slab and everything in the shop together, and she would not sell it, and it was dying all the same. She knew that and did not say it. Oil dies. It darkens and it cracks and the varnish yellows over it like the film over an old eye, and there was no firing in the world that could stop it, and the truest bird a dead man ever painted would be gone to a brown ghost in a hundred years while a clumsy blue bird on a fired plate would still be blue when the plate was dug out of the ground in a thousand. She had learned that in the kiln, and it had rearranged something in her she was not sure she had wanted rearranged.

A board creaked overhead — the fourth step, that shrieked in this house as the third had shrieked in the last one, and Clara had come to be glad of the noise the way she was glad of most small dependable things now. Then Grietje was on the stair in her shift, hair on end, one fist closed, and she came down and set her fist on the edge of the slab and opened it in the grey beginning of the light and sorted her things.

Five, now. The chip of blue-and-white off a broken dish; the horn button; the river pebble; the little lump of *pyrite*, the gold that was not gold, that Clara had given her out of the old lapis years ago in the old shop; and the fifth thing, the small fired tile with a blue bird on it, clumsy and true, the first she had ever painted and carried off the bench and would not be parted from. She counted them every morning still, laying them out and gathering them in, the way another child said a prayer, making sure the night had not taken any. She was ten, and would be eleven at the turn of the year, and the arm that had been broken under the fallen wall two years gone had healed a little crooked, so that she painted with her wrist held at a small odd angle no one but Clara would ever notice, and her voice when she counted was her own cracked-whole voice, come back to her over a pan of mud and never quite the child's clear treble it had been before the blast, a half-tone low, with the older thing living in the crack of it.

"You're grinding the grey," Grietje said. Not a question. She had opinions about the grey.

"I am."

"It's the ugliest colour there is."

"It is," Clara said, "until it isn't. Come away from the door."

"I'm not at the door." She was not; she had learned that answer. She stood and watched the grey go round under the muller, the dead ash-colour that was the truest blue in the world and did not look it and would not look it until the fire had it, and she watched with the flat grave patience she watched everything, the patience of a child who had once seen the whole visible world lie to her in a single white flash and had decided, young, to trust the thing under the look of a thing and not the look. "Machteld says the Blue Hand's kiln's cold," she said. "Since Sunday. They're waiting on something."

"They're waiting on the grey," Clara said, and did not know yet that she was wrong, that they were waiting on a great deal more than the grey, that it had already come down the water from Amsterdam in a fat man's letter-case and was two streets away drinking Spronck's small beer and would be at her door before the bells.

He came at first light, the way he had come the first time, two autumns gone, with the raw grey ore in a sack: meester Coenraad Spronck, broad in the door, burn-scarred hands, the flat easy harbour-voice that never hurried a thing. But he was not alone this time, and he was not easy. There was a second man behind him, a soft heavy man in good dark cloth and a good hat, a man who had never carried anything heavier than a purse, and Spronck brought him in the way you bring in weather.

"Clara," Spronck said. "This is a factor of the Company. Out of Amsterdam. He's come about an order."

The Company man took off his hat in the small shop and looked at the goldfinch on the wall a moment too long, the way men did, and then looked at Clara's hands, the way men also did, the permanent blue in the creases of them, and did not offer his own hand, which she was glad of. He named himself; she let the name go past her. Company men were interchangeable to her as *stuivers*. He had a paper. He always had a paper.

"The potteries of this town," he said, "are to have the honour of a great commission." He said it the way such men said things, as though the honour were a coin he was counting out and expected her to bite. "You are aware that the porcelain of China has failed us."

"I'm aware the ware's stopped coming," Clara said. She was aware of it the way the whole trade was aware of it — as a hole in the market that the blue-and-white was rushing to fill. Somewhere on the far side of the earth the Chinese kilns had gone cold in a war nobody in Delft could have named the sides of, a dynasty falling, a dynasty rising, the porcelain roads shut; and the ships came back to Amsterdam with their holds half empty of the blue-and-white the whole of Europe had learned to want, and the want did not go away because the supply did. Wants never did. Gerrit had taught her that before she could have said it. It was the first law of the colour trade and it was the first law of every trade under it.

"The Company," the factor said, "will not send its ships to the Indies with empty holds and its cabins undressed. If China will not furnish the table, Delft will." He unrolled the paper. "A service. Blue-and-white, in the Chinese manner or near it — chargers, dishes, bowls, cups, the whole dressing of a great house's table, and more than one house's — for the autumn ships to Batavia, and beyond Batavia wherever the Company's writ runs. This is the first order. If the first freight sells — and it will sell; there is nothing else for it to compete with; the world is starved of blue — there will be a second, and a third, for as long as the ships sail and the world wants blue, which is to say" — he almost smiled — "forever, madam, as trades reckon forever."

He set the paper on the slab, beside the barrel of grey, and Clara looked at it. She could read a number. Gerrit had taught her that too, over a ledger, the first winter, back when she was a colour-merchant's wife and not a colour-merchant and had thought the difference was small. The number on the paper was not a number she had a place for. It was not a number one pottery had a place for. It was a number for a town.

She stood and looked at it and felt two things come up in her at once, braided, the way things came up in her now, since the burst, never single any more. The first was the plain animal lift of it — of the coin, of the safety, of the years of the grey going out of her shop by the barrel and the money coming back by the bagful, of Grietje fed and dry and free of the almshouse forever, of the door Doncker had spent two years trying to shut standing open now so wide the wind went through it. And the second, come up under the first the way the blue came up under the muller, deeper each fold: the cold understanding of what such a number would cost to fill, and who would pay it, and that she did not yet know the half of it, and that the not-knowing did not make it less.

"Meester Spronck can't make that at the Blue Hand," she said. "Not by the autumn ships. Not by three autumns of ships."

"No," said the factor, pleased, as though she had passed a small test. "Which is why I am glad to find you have a woman who can count. Meester Spronck tells me the blue in this town comes and goes as it pleases, pottery to pottery, a dozen hands and a dozen blues, and half of it comes out of the kiln the colour of a bruise or the colour of soot. The Company cannot sell a service that is one blue on the charger and another on the cup. The Company wants one blue. Delft's blue. True across every kiln in the town." He put his hat back on. "Meester Spronck tells me there is a woman on the Verwersdijk side who can grind one true blue and teach it. I have come to see her hands. I have seen them." He nodded at the goldfinch, once, a man ticking a thing off. "Good day, madam. You will hear from Amsterdam."

When he had gone Spronck stood in the door and let the truth do its own pushing, which was his way. Clara looked at the number on the paper and at the barrel of grey and at the child at the slab, who had come to the paper and was looking at the number with her whole grave face, unable to read it and able to read exactly what it had done to the room.

"Well," Spronck said at last, in the harbour-voice. "There it is, then. A blue for the whole world." He said it flat, without any music, the way he said everything, and it was not flat at all. "More than one pottery can grind, Clara. More than one town, maybe. And they want you to be the grinding of it."

Clara set the muller down on the slab, deliberately, the way she did when she needed her hands still to think. Outside the first bell went, and then the others, ragged across the mended town, the bells that had been blown out of both church towers and rehung crooked and never rang quite together any more. She listened to them not ring together. Then she picked the paper up off the barrel, so that the grey should not get on the number, and folded it once, and did not answer him, because there was no answer yet, only the beginning of the long arithmetic of it, and she had learned not to speak the sum until she had done it.


## 2. More Than One Pottery

She did the arithmetic that night, at the shop table, with the tallow guttering and Grietje asleep above her and the goldfinch a shape in the dark, and it came out the way she had known it would come out, which was: not possible, and having to be done anyway.

She did it in blue. That was the only way she could hold a thing that size — to think of it not as plates and coin but as the colour that would have to cross all of it, barrel after barrel of the grey going true blue in kiln after kiln, and how much grey that was, and where the grey came from, and how many hands it would take to lay it on the white before the fire, and how many of those hands could be trusted to lay it on *true*. A charger took more blue than a cup, and a cup took more skill for its size than a charger; a bad hand could ruin a good blue and a good hand could not save a bad one; and the whole of it, every piece, had to come out of a dozen different kilns the one same blue, or the Company would ship it back and the second order would never come and the first would break whoever had staked on it. She could see the shape of the ruin as clearly as she could see the shape of the profit. They were the same shape. That was the thing about a great order that the factor's soft counting hands would never know: the bigger the thing you were promised, the bigger the thing that could fall on you.

De Blauwe Hand could not do it. She had known that in the first breath, standing over the paper. Spronck's was one pottery — one master, one great kiln, a wheel-room and a glaze-room and a paint-bench with Machteld at the head of it and Grietje at the foot and four others between, and it could turn out good ware at a good pace, and it could not turn out a tenth of that number by the autumn ships if every soul in it worked till they dropped and never slept. There were, she counted, perhaps a dozen potteries in the town now, risen in the shells of the dead breweries the way De Blauwe Hand had risen in the shell of De Drie Ackers, and half of them were new and thin and badly run and would founder inside three years; but the other half were sound, and if the sound ones were bound together — if the order were broken up among them and each fed its share of white ware into its own kilns — then the number stopped being a number for a town and became a number for six potteries, which was still enormous, and was no longer flatly mad.

But six potteries meant six masters, and six masters meant six blues.

She put that to Spronck the next morning, at the Blue Hand, in the wheel-room with the damp clay-smell and the slap of the wheels and the great kiln standing cold and black between firings like a house between deaths. She put it to him plainly because he was a plain man and it did not do to dress a thing up for Spronck; he took the dressing off anything you handed him and looked at what was under it.

"Six masters," he agreed. "And every one of them thinks his blue's the best blue and his glaze the whitest and his neighbour a thief. Adriaen Block over on the Molslaan won't sit in a room with Govert van Nes on account of a wheel-boy Govert took off him in the spring. Old Verhaar keeps his glaze-recipe in his head and won't write it down for his own son. You want to put a blue across the lot of them." He said it without heat, laying it out. "They'll take the Company's coin. Every man of them. They'd take coin off the devil in Lent. But they won't take another man's blue. A potter's blue is his own. It's the one thing in the trade that's his."

"It won't be another man's blue," Clara said. "It'll be mine. And I'm not a potter."

Spronck looked at her a while. He had a way of looking at a thing until it admitted what it was.

"No," he said. "You're not. That might be the whole of it, at that." He wiped his burn-scarred hands down his apron, though there was nothing on them; it was a thing he did when he was thinking. "A woman that grinds for all of them and belongs to none of them. Not Block's blue and not Van Nes's blue and not mine. The town's blue, out of the Verwersdijk, off a slab that was never in any of their kilns. They could all of them hate it equal, which is as near to fair as that lot will ever come." The flat mouth moved, not quite a smile. "You'd have to teach it. Each bench. Your hand into a dozen hands that aren't yours and never will be. And you'd have to hold it true when you weren't standing over it, which is most of the time, because you can't be in six paint-rooms at once and the fire doesn't care how tired you are. You know what you're asking to carry."

"I know what I'm asking to carry," Clara said.

She did not, wholly. Nobody knew the weight of a thing until it was on them; she had learned that under a fallen wall, digging with her hands. But she knew enough. She knew that to be the one true blue of a whole town's trade was to be, in the one way that mattered, unwidowed at last of the smallness the guild had bound her to — no longer the woman grudged a widow's leave, grinding umber at a loss for the guild's town-work, holding her trade by a rope they could cut. A blue the Company itself had sent for could not be cut by Willem Doncker. And she knew, in the same breath, standing in the cold wheel-room, that a thing that could not be cut by Doncker was exactly the thing Doncker would come for, because Doncker did not cut what he could own.

"There's the grey," Spronck said, as though he had followed her to the same place, which he had; it was not a long road. "Before any of it. Six potteries at that pace will drink more zaffer than this town has ever bought in a year. It comes down the one road, Clara. Hamburg, and behind Hamburg the Saxon hills, and behind that the one factor that'll deal with plateelbakkers instead of turning his nose up at us for tradesmen. We've kept that road our own two years now, ours and not the painters' colour-men's, and it's the whole reason we've a blue at all that Doncker's hand isn't on. Six times the grey down one narrow road." He let it sit. "You'd best hope nobody upstream of us works out what that road's worth. Because the day they do, they'll want the road, and it's a narrow road, and there's only the one."

"I've thought of the road," Clara said. She had. She had thought of it half the night, and it had frightened her more than the number, because the number was only large and the road was only narrow, and she had lived long enough to know that it is the narrow things that kill you, not the large ones. The burst had come out of a narrow thing — a lantern, a spark, a keeper stooping in the dark with a sample-scoop. Everything that had ever fallen on her had come down a narrow road she had known about and watched and been unable to widen.

She looked past Spronck to the paint-bench, where the girls were coming in for the day's work, unwinding their shawls, and Grietje among them at the low end, so small beside Machteld, taking up her brush and her little pot of the drab grey and bending to a plate with the odd small angle of her mended wrist, laying a bird onto the white that would not be a bird until the fire made it one — laying it down, that is, in faith, on the good evidence of the fire, a colour she could not see, the way she had learned to do a thing she could not see was there. She was good. She had been good for a year and she was better now, better than the two hands above her on the bench, and only Machteld and Clara knew it yet, and Grietje did not know it at all, which was as it should be for a while longer.

If Clara took the great order, it would be that bird — that clumsy true small bird, and ten thousand of its kind off ten thousand hands she would have to teach and stand behind and never wholly trust — that crossed the water to the far side of the earth. If she did not take it, the order would go to whoever could hold it, and the town's blue would come out of the kilns a dozen colours, and be shipped back, and the second order would go to Rotterdam or to Haarlem or nowhere, and the chance that had come once down the water in a fat man's letter-case would not come again in her working life. Wants did not wait. The market had opened a door because a war on the far side of the world had shut another door, and doors like that shut again.

"I'll do it," she said. "Tell Amsterdam. Tell the factor Delft has a blue."

Spronck nodded slowly, the way a man nods when a thing he expected has come and he is not sure whether to be glad of it. "You understand," he said, "that from the day you tell him, it's yours to carry, and it doesn't set down. Not for the tide, not for the fire, not for the child, not for you. A great order is a great weight, and a weight doesn't know it's a blessing."

"I've carried weight," Clara said.

"Aye," said Spronck, and looked at her mended town and her mended child and her blue-creased hands, and did not say the rest of it, which was that she had, and that carrying it had cost her a boy in the ground and a husband before him and a hoard of the truest blue in the world ground away grain by grain, and that a woman who has carried weight is not a woman who does not feel it — only a woman who has learned that setting it down does not make it lighter, it only makes it fall on someone else. He knew that. He had his brother's whole household under the Paardenmarkt with no stone over them, and he got up every morning and lit the kiln.


## 3. The Guild's New Interest

Doncker came on the third day, which was one day sooner than she had reckoned, and she took it as the measure of the thing: that Willem Doncker, who moved at the pace of a man who believed time was on the side of good order and against everyone in a hurry, had heard about the Company's paper and come in three days. He had never wanted a thing in a hurry in his life. He wanted this.

He came correct, as he always came. He was in his sixties now, and the flesh had gone off him this last year so that his good dark cloth hung a size large, and his colour was not good, a grey under the skin that Clara's eye read the way it read any grey — as a thing waiting for the fire that would or would not turn it — and he stood in the door of her sound shop and looked around it with the particular pain of a man seeing a thing prosper that he had judged, on excellent grounds, ought to have failed.

"You've done well for yourself," he said. It cost him. "Gerrit would not have known the place." He looked at the goldfinch, and then away from it, quickly; the bird cost him too, though Clara had never learned why, and had stopped wondering. "I'll not keep you. I'm told the Company has been to see you."

"You're told right."

"A great order." He turned his hat in his hands. "For the potteries. Blue-and-white, for the Indies." He said *the potteries* the way he had once said *the dyers* and *the fullers*, the way a man of the guild's older sort said the name of a trade he did not consider quite a trade — and then he heard himself say it, and Clara watched him hear it, watched the old contempt come up out of habit and the new arithmetic land on top of it, because the potteries were no longer a trade a guild-man could look down on; the potteries were where the money had gone. It was there in his face for a moment, the whole change of the world, the painters' colour dying under him while the kiln-blue rose, and his own long life spent mastering the one exactly as the town turned to the other. She almost pitied him. She had learned, these two years, that you could pity a man and still have to fight him, that the two did not cancel, and that pity that made you drop your guard was not pity but only softness wearing its coat.

"You'll need cobalt," Doncker said. "A great deal of it. More than this town has ever brought in." He said it pleasantly, laying it down where she could see it, the way the factor had laid down his paper. "The stuff comes a long way, and it comes dear, and it comes — I'm given to understand — down a single road, through a single factor, who deals with the potteries because no one better would deal with them." The old contempt, and under it the offer, and under the offer the threat, all three folded together the way he folded everything, so that you could not take one without the others. "I have thought, Clara, that a trade of that size ought not to hang on a single Hamburg thread. It is not prudent. It is not good order. I have correspondents in Hamburg. I have credit there your potters could not raise in ten years. If the town's blue is to be the town's living, it wants a merchant behind it who can guarantee the supply — who can hold the price steady, secure the road, stand surety when the Saxon mines are slow. A merchant of standing. A guild man." He let it sit, gently. "I am offering, in short, to make certain your great order does not die of thirst."

And there it was. She had known it would be this; she had not known it would be this naked. He had spent two years and all his weight trying to shut her door because a woman's independent trade offended his sense of the order of the world, and he had failed, because the world had reordered itself around him while he pushed; and now, too old to fail again and too shrewd not to see it, he had changed his aim entirely. He did not want to shut the door any more. He wanted to stand in it. He wanted to be the road. He had watched the fired blue rise and despised it as a kitchen colour, a common colour, a potter's daub beneath the dignity of the guild's true painters — and he had woken one morning, some morning this hard year, to the understanding that the common colour was going to be the richest trade in Holland, and that the man who held its supply would hold it whether he ground a grain himself or not. He had not learned to love the blue. He had learned to love owning it. That was worse. Love of a thing set a limit on what a man would do to it; love of owning it set none.

"That's a generous thought," Clara said.

"It is a prudent thought."

"It is." She kept her hands still on the slab. "And if I say the potters would rather keep their narrow road, and their poor factor that stoops to deal with them, and their supply in their own hands — thin as it is?"

Doncker looked at her with something that was almost kindness, the kindness of a man who has seen a great many people refuse the sensible thing to their own ruin and has stopped being surprised by it.

"Then I would say," he said, "that a narrow road is a narrow road, and the Company will not wait on it, and that when it fails — not if, Clara; a single thread always fails; I have watched threads fail for forty years — when it fails, mid-order, with the ships waiting and the penalty running and the whole town's living staked on it, you will come to Hamburg then, and to me, and the terms will not be these terms, which are generous, but the terms of a man who is the only water in a drought. I would rather sell you the water now, at a fair price, as a friend. Gerrit was my friend," he said, and the old grievance came up in his voice, worn smooth now, almost gentle, "before he was your husband. I have never wished you harm. I have wished you *order*. It is not the same thing as harm, whatever you have decided to believe of me."

He put his hat on. At the door he stopped, and looked back, not at her but at the barrel of grey standing where a sack of good pigment should stand, and his face did a thing she had not seen it do before, a kind of tired disgust that had gone all the way through disgust and out the far side into something like grief.

"A common blue," he said, quietly, to the barrel more than to her. "Do you know, I have spent my whole life on the true colours. The lapis that crossed the world. The vermilion, the lake, the lead-tin yellow a master would beggar himself for a pound of. Colours a man could put his hand to and know he was handling something the earth made rare on purpose. And it is all coming down, Clara, in my own lifetime, to *this*. Grey ash out of a Saxon hole, that any potter's brat can slap on a dish for a stiver, that comes out of the fire the same cheap blue every time, ten thousand plates of it, a hundred thousand, all alike, going out to be eaten off by Company clerks in Batavia and washed and stacked and broken and thought nothing of. A blue for anyone. A blue for no one." He shook his head slowly. "You were a colour-woman once. A real one. You ground the true blue. You knew what it was worth. And you have made yourself the grinder of the cheapest blue that was ever made, and called it a rise in the world." He opened the door on the mended street. "It is not a rise. It is a coming-down. You have only arranged to be at the bottom of it first."

He went out. Clara stood over the barrel a long time after the door had shut. She was not shaken — she had heard worse from him, and truer — but he had put his thumb on the one place that was not yet healed, and pressed, and he had known he was doing it, and done it not cruelly but almost as a warning, the way you show a child the burn on your own hand. *A common blue.* She looked at the grey ash in the barrel, that any potter's brat could slap on a dish, that came out of the fire the same cheap blue every time, going out to be eaten off and washed and broken and thought nothing of, ten thousand plates alike; and she waited to feel the shame he had meant her to feel, the coming-down of it.

And it did not come. Or it came, and something rose to meet it, and the two of them stood in her the way the lift and the dread had stood in her over the factor's paper, braided, neither cancelling. Because it was true, every word of it — the blue was cheap, and common, and alike, and going out to strangers who would think nothing of it — and there was a thing in the truth of it that Doncker, who had spent his whole long life on the colours the earth made rare on purpose, could not see, standing so close to the rare that he had gone blind to the common the way you go blind to your own breath. The true blue, the astronomical blue, the blue that rivalled gold — she had ground the last of it into one picture in one man's house, one blue for one patron, a colour so dear that only the rare could have it and only the very few could ever stand in front of it. And this cheap grey ash was going to go out on ten thousand plates to ten thousand tables, to Company clerks and their wives and, in the end, down and down, to anyone, everyone, a blue that anyone could have, a blue held by no one because it was held by all — and that was not nothing. That was very nearly the opposite of nothing. She did not have the words for it yet. But she stood over the barrel of the cheapest blue that was ever made and did not feel, as Doncker had meant her to feel, that she had arranged to be at the bottom of a coming-down. She felt, obscurely and for the first time, that she had been handed the making of something Doncker's whole rich rare beautiful dying world had never once managed to make, in all its centuries of grinding gold onto panel for the few: a blue for everyone.

*A common blue.* She turned the words over the way she turned a stone to find the grain. He had meant them for a wound. She thought — not yet in words, only in the place under words where the hands know a thing before the mouth does — that she might, given time, make them mean the other thing. Common as in *cheap*. Or common as in *held in common* — the common ground, the common good, the thing that belonged to everyone because it belonged to no one. It was the same word. It was only a matter of which way you turned it to the light.


## 4. The Last Ultramarine

The young painter's picture was finished that summer, and Clara heard of it before she saw it, the way you hear of a thing that is going to matter — sidelong, from a customer, a small remark that lodges and will not come out.

It was the woman-by-a-window picture. The one he had come for the true blue for, two winters gone, the last of her reclaimed ultramarine, the blue she had washed grain by grain out of the frozen mud of her own dead house and had ground away at last into one man's one panel: a woman by a window in the morning light, he had said, a blue in the shadow of the cloth, *a blue you could breathe.* He had it done now, they said. He worked slow — slower than any painter in the town, a picture a year, two pictures a year, where a man had to make six to eat — and he had a patron who kept him, a Delft man who bought the pictures as fast as the slow hands made them and hung them and let almost no one see them, so that the young painter's whole life's work was going, panel by panel, into a handful of houses in one town, one blue for one patron, seen by the few. She never did see the picture. She was not the sort who got shown into those houses, and the young painter, who would have shown her gladly, for she had given him the blue that was in it, was too shy and too poor and too taken up with the next slow picture to think of it, and the summer went, and the chance did not come, and Clara found she did not mind as much as she had thought she would.

Because she knew what was in it. She had ground what was in it. Somewhere in that town, in a room she would never stand in, hung a foot of panel with the last true ultramarine in Delft folded into the shadow of a woman's cloth — the supreme blue, the astronomical blue, the blue that had crossed half the world and rivalled gold by weight, ground to nothing at last in one picture for one man, a blue so dear that only the rare could have it and only the few could ever stand in front of it, going up in worth every year it hung unseen, the way the goldfinch was going up, the way all the rare beautiful dying things went up, by being kept from the light. It was the most beautiful thing her hands had ever helped to make and it was going to be seen, in her whole life and long after, by fewer people than would eat off one autumn's plates in one week in Batavia. She stood in her shop that summer with a barrel of grey ash in the corner, the cheapest blue that was ever made, and thought about that: the last true blue in one room for the few, and the first of the common blue going out by the barrel to everyone, and how the same pair of blue-creased hands had made them both, and how she could not, for her life, decide which was the sadder and which the gladder, and had begun to think it was the wrong question — that they were not a better and a worse but a closing and an opening, the old rare blue going into the dark to be kept and the new common blue going into the world to be used, and that she had been, without ever choosing it, the hinge the whole trade turned on, the last grinder of the one and the first grinder of the other, standing in the door between them with a foot in each.

Agatha van Pruyssen came for the goldfinch in the last week of summer, and Clara had been waiting two years for her to come, and still it went through her like cold water.

She came in the afternoon, in her widow's dark, thinner than Clara remembered and steadier, a woman who had done the long work of grief and come out the far side of it not healed — you did not heal — but load-bearing again, able to stand under the weight without her knees going. Clara knew the look. She wore it herself. Two widows in a small blue-smelling shop, and the dead man's bird on the wall between them, and neither of them looked at it for a while, the way you do not look straight at the sun you are both standing in.

They spoke of small things first, because that was how it was done, and because the large thing was too large to walk straight up to. Agatha was leaving Delft. She had a sister in Gouda, and a small annuity, and a life she could just afford there among people who had not watched her husband's studio come down; she was selling what there was to sell and going before the winter. Clara heard the shape of it under the words — a woman dismantling the last of a life that had ended two years ago at an easel on the twelfth of October, packing it into a barge, taking it somewhere it would weigh less because no one there would know what it had been.

"You'll want the painting," Clara said, when the small things ran out. She said it plainly, to spare them both the walking-up-to. "It's yours. It was always yours. I've only kept it."

"I know what it's worth now," Agatha said. Not greedily; only clearly. A widow on a small annuity did her arithmetic the way Clara did hers, in the dark, at a table, because no one else was going to do it for her. "A man came to me in the spring. A collector, out of Amsterdam. He named a sum for it that would keep me the rest of my life and my sister's too. He said it was the finest thing Carel ever did. He said there was almost nothing left of him, the fire took the rest, and so the little that came out whole is worth the more for being all there is." Her voice did not break. She had done her breaking. "He didn't know it was in your keeping. I didn't tell him. I said I would think."

There it was, then, laid on the slab between them the way the factor had laid his paper and Doncker his offer: the sum that would keep two widows the rest of their lives, hanging on the wall in a foot of darkening oil, held in trust and not in ownership, and the owner come at last to claim it, as she had every right in the world to do. Clara had known this hour would come. She had refused the collector's shadow a dozen times over two hard years, in her own mind, when the fee was owed and the lease ran down and the selling of the bird would have paid it all and left her over. She had refused it because it was not hers to sell. But it had never been hers to keep, either. It had only ever been hers to *hold*, and holding was a thing you did until the owner came, and the owner had come.

"It's yours," Clara said again. "Take it to Gouda. Or take the collector's sum. Either. It was never a question, Agatha. I kept it because you gave it to me to keep, and you can take it back with one hand any afternoon you like, and I'll help you crate it so the damp doesn't get in."

Agatha looked at the bird a long time. Then she did a thing Clara did not expect. She crossed the small shop and stood close under it, close enough to see the loose dragged paint that was hardly paint, the chain from the ring to the leg, the pale warm-and-cold air a dead man had got into a foot of panel that no one living could get; and she looked at it the way you look at a face, and then she turned and looked at the paint-bench through the doorway to the back, where the day's ware stood drying, the white plates with their drab grey birds waiting for the fire, Grietje's among them, laid down that morning in the small odd angle of her wrist.

"He'd have liked those," Agatha said. "Carel. The birds on the plates. Did you know he could hardly keep a coin? He'd have laughed at me, keeping his one bird in the dark against a sum, when there's his bird going out on a hundred plates to the ends of the earth for a stiver each. He never once painted a thing to keep it. He painted it to be *seen*. He was always short because he'd sooner a thing sold and hung where the light was than kept in a chest going up in value. It used to frighten me half to death, the way he wouldn't hoard." A small sound, not quite a laugh, the ghost of one. "And here I am, his widow, sitting on his last bird like a hen, because a man from Amsterdam told me it was worth more for being all there is."

"It is worth more for being all there is," Clara said, gently. "That's not a wrong thing to know. You've a life to keep. Keeping it isn't hoarding. It's living."

"No," Agatha agreed. "It isn't wrong." She stepped back from the bird. "But I've decided. I'll take the collector's sum — I've a sister and a roof to think of and I'm too old and too poor to be noble about it, and Carel would be the first to say sell the thing and eat. But the bird —" She stopped, and Clara saw her come to the edge of something and make herself go over it. "The bird itself, before it goes to the collector's dark chest to be worth more every year unseen, which is a kind of death Carel would have hated worse than the fire — I want the girl to have it a while. To copy. Your Grietje. Let her sit under it with her grey and take the bird off it, as many times as she likes, before it goes into the crate. Let her put it on the plates. Let it go out on the ships. Carel's bird on a hundred common plates to a hundred common tables at the far side of the earth, where it'll be *seen*, washed and stacked and eaten off and seen, every day, by people who never heard his name — that's the only monument he'd have wanted, and it's the one the collector's sum can't buy me. Let her make it common. He'd have liked that better than a burgomaster's best wall."

Clara did not say anything for a moment. The afternoon light was going long and gold across the shop, the way it did at that hour before the blue came into it, and it lay across the two of them and the bird and the barrel of grey ash in the corner, the cheapest blue that was ever made, that any potter's brat could slap on a dish, going out to be thought nothing of by everyone, everywhere, forever, as trades reckon forever.

"I'll tell her," Clara said. "She'll be careful with it. She's careful with everything that's true."

And later, when Agatha had gone, and Clara had brought Grietje in from the bench and stood her under the goldfinch and told her — that meester Fabritius's own bird was to be hers to copy, all autumn, before it went away; that she was to take it off the panel and put it onto the plates; that it was going to go out on the ships, out of her own small crooked wrist, to the far side of the world — the child had stood and looked up at the bird with her whole grave face, the mended arm, the cracked-whole voice, and had said, after a long time, in the flat certain way she said the truest things: "It's dying, though. You said. The oil dies." And Clara had said yes, it was dying, all paint dies. And Grietje had looked at it a while more, working it out, and then said: "Then I'll make it not." Not as a boast. As a plan. As a thing she had decided to do, the way she had decided, young, to trust the thing under the look of a thing; and she went back to the bench and took up her grey, and Clara stood under the dying bird in the last of the gold and understood that she had said yes to the great order for a hundred reasons of coin and safety and the shut door standing open, and that none of them was the reason, and that the reason was small and stubborn and ten years old and painting a colour it could not see onto a plate bound for a sea it would never cross, in the faith of the fire, making a dying thing not die.

# The Common Blue — Part Two: The Common Blue


## 5. A Blue by the Barrel

By the leaf-fall Clara had become a woman who walked, and she had not been that before. In the old life the whole of her trade had fitted between the four walls of a shop and a slab in the middle of it; she had ground where she stood and sold where she stood and gone nowhere. Now she went everywhere. She was up before the black hour still, to grind the town's grey by the barrel and set the day's standard true, and then she was out in it, out in the mended streets with a covered basket of the day's blue and a bundle of pattern-tiles under her arm, going pottery to pottery across the town like a physician on his rounds, because the one true blue could not be sent, it had to be carried, and it had to be carried by the one hand that knew what true was.

She had thought, that first night at the table, that the hard thing would be the six masters. It was not. The masters were bought, and a bought man stays bought as long as the coin comes, and the Company's coin came. The hard thing was the hands. She had, when she counted them one grey morning standing in Adriaen Block's cold paint-room, something near forty hands now painting to her blue across the town — girls mostly, and some boys, and a few grown women glad of indoor work, and two old men whose eyes were going and whose fingers still knew a line — and no two of those forty hands laid a colour down the same, and the fire did not forgive the difference. A blue too thin fired weak and grey and washed-out; a blue too thick fired black, burned, the cobalt drowning its own colour, a bird gone to a scorch. A hand that hurried freckled the ground; a hand that dawdled let the grey dry and dragged. And every one of the forty hands believed, in the way hands believe, that its own way was the right way, because its own way was the way that felt right to it, and feeling right was the whole of what a hand had to go on and was worth exactly nothing against the fire.

So she taught. She had not known she could teach; she had learned everything she knew by grinding it into her own wrists over years, alone, and a thing you learn that way you do not know how to hand across. But she found it, slowly, walking her rounds. She found that you could not tell a hand a thing; you had to give the hand a thing to do until the thing did the telling. She made pattern-tiles — a bird, a spray, a border of her own laying-down, fired true — and set them at each bench and made the hands paint against them, not copying the look, which fired away to nothing, but matching the *load*, the weight of grey the brush carried, the pull of it across the biscuit, the exact drab wrongness that would come out of the fire the exact right blue. *Trust the tile*, she said, over and over, in six paint-rooms, to forty hands. *Don't trust your eye. Your eye is a liar in this trade. The grey lies and the fire tells the truth. Lay it the weight of the tile and let the fire have it.* It was, she thought, walking home in the early dark, the whole of what the burst had taught her and Grietje both, made into a trade: do not believe the look of the world; believe the thing under it; lay the colour down in the dark on the good evidence of the fire.

Not every hand took it, and the ones that did not were rarely the young ones. It was the good hands, the proud ones, the master's own best painters, who fought her — because a good hand has a way, and its way is its worth, and here came a woman off the Verwersdijk who was not even a potter to tell it its way was wrong. At Govert van Nes's there was a man called Huybert, van Nes's own head painter, thirty years at a bench and a line on him like a wire, and he would not paint to Clara's tile. He laid his blue his own way, thin and quick and elegant, the way that had served him thirty years, and it was elegant, and it fired weak — a grey ghost of a bird where Clara's tile fired a bird that stopped your eye — and he would not see it, because a man who has been right for thirty years does not believe the fire over his own hand; he believes the fire has cheated him.

She did not argue with him. She had learned that arguing with a hand was arguing with the fire's own lie, the belief that the look was the thing. She waited for the drawing instead. She had them fire his birds and hers in the one kiln, side by side, his elegant thin ones and her loaded drab ones, and she stood with Huybert at the mouth of it in the cold when they drew it, and let the fire do the telling — his birds come out pale and washed and gone, thirty years of elegance fired away to a smear, and hers come out deep and whole and looking back at you off the white; and Huybert stood and looked at the two a long time, an old proud hand at the mouth of a kiln, and Clara watched something go out of his face and something harder come into it, the particular grief of a craftsman shown that the thing he does beautifully is the wrong thing done beautifully. "It's not a painter's blue," he said at last, bitterly, and he was right, it was not; a painter laid colour to be looked at wet, and this was a colour laid to be looked at after the fire, laid blind, laid on faith, and a lifetime of the one taught your hand exactly the wrong instinct for the other. "No," Clara agreed. "It isn't. It's a harder blue than a painter's. A painter sees what he's done. You have to lay this one down not knowing, and be right anyway, ten thousand times. I've never met a painter could do it. It takes a potter's nerve and a painter's eye both, and there's maybe four hands in this town have the two, and you're one of them, which is the only reason I've stood here an hour losing daylight over you." And Huybert, who had wanted to be told his elegance was wanted and had been told instead that a harder thing than elegance was wanted and that he alone might have the nerve for it, went and reground his blue to the weight of her tile, sullenly, and was, inside a month, the best hand at van Nes's and half in love with the fire he had cursed, the way the converted are; and Clara walked home in the dark and thought that this was the whole labour of the thing, sixty times over — not to teach a colour, which was an afternoon's work, but to break in sixty proud hands the lifelong instinct to trust the look, and set in its place the harder faith that the fire, and not the eye, told the truth.

Grietje rose that autumn the way bread rises, all at once and out of long slow work you could not see happening. Machteld came to Clara one evening at De Blauwe Hand — Machteld who was seventeen now and the head of the best bench in the town, sure-handed, grey already at one temple where the fright had put it the day of the hard-rung bell — and stood a while turning a plate in her hands before she said it.

"You should take her off my bench," Machteld said. "Grietje. Put her at the head of a bench of her own. She's better than me, meester Clara. She's been better than me since the spring and I've been too proud to say it and it's not right to keep a hand down for a bench-head's pride." She set the plate down. It was Grietje's — Clara knew the bird without being told, knew the small odd angle in the tail-line that was the crooked wrist making its one mark on the world. "The little ones copy her already. They watch her hand and not mine. She lays it the weight of your tile without the tile, now. She doesn't need the tile. She's got the fire in her eye — she paints what it'll be, not what it is. I've never seen it in anyone but you."

Clara looked at the plate a long time. She was proud, and afraid, and the two came up braided as they always came, because pride in a child was a hostage you gave the world, and she had given the world a hostage once and the world had struck the boy dead in a white flash before he was a man, and she had sworn something to herself over Barent's hasty grave that she had never put in words and that came down, when she turned it over, to this: never again to love a thing so that its loss could unmake her. And she had broken the oath the day she took the child in, and gone on breaking it every morning the child counted her five bright things at the slab, and there was no unbreaking it now.

"Not the head of a bench," Clara said. "Not yet. She's ten." But she heard the *yet* in it, and so did Machteld, and Machteld nodded and let it be.

That was the good of the autumn, and it was real. But under the good ran the other thing, and Clara felt it come up in the trade the way she felt the damp come up in a wall — slow, and certain, and everywhere at once. The order was too big and the tide did not move. Forty hands were not enough and there was no time to teach a hundred; the ones she had painted through their fingers to the bone, the little ones at the low ends of the benches nodding over their grey in the short cold afternoons, glad of the coin their families needed and too young for the pace of it. She came into Van Nes's paint-room one dusk and found a bench of children who had been laying birds since before the light, their hands stiff, one small girl of Grietje's age asleep sitting up with her brush still in her fist and a plate half-painted under her chin, and Clara stood in the doorway and felt the whole shape of the thing turn over in her, the coin and the cost, the rise and the coming-down, and could not for a moment tell Doncker's grey coming-down from her own bright rise, because they were, she was beginning to understand, the same motion seen from two ends: the making of a beautiful thing common, cheap, everywhere — and the hands it was ground out of, cheap and common and everywhere too, and worked to the bone in the short cold afternoons for the coin, so that a Company clerk in Batavia could eat off a blue he thought nothing of. She woke the child gently and took the brush out of her fist and did not know, going home in the dark, whether she was the answer to Doncker or the proof of him.


## 6. The Number and the Fire

The first great firing for the Indies order went into the kiln at De Blauwe Hand on a black night in November, and Clara stood the whole of it, because a firing that size was where the order would be made or lost and she would not have it made or lost out of her sight.

Spronck fired at night still, as the best of them did — the kiln wanted a day and a night and a day to come up and hold and come down, and the holding was done in the dark, when a man could read the fire by its colour through the spy-hole and by nothing else. The great kiln stood at the heart of the old brewery, a brick beehive taller than a man, and when it was up it roared — there was no other word Clara had ever found for it, a low continuous roar out of the stoke-holes, the peat and the faggots going in, the heat coming off it in a wall you leaned into from across the room — and the roar was the thing. The roar was where it lived, for her and for Grietje and for half the town that had been under the burst; the roar was the twelfth of October speaking again out of a brick throat, the same fire, and there was no getting used to it, there was only standing in it and letting it be what it was and not what the body kept insisting it was.

Pauwels would not come to a night firing. He never had. He worked days, and glazed, and went home before dark, an old potter with a good hand and a broken nerve, and no one at the Blue Hand ever said a word to shame him for it, because half of them carried the same crack and only Pauwels was honest enough to arrange his life around it. Machteld came, and stood the firing, and Clara watched her stand it — watched her go still and grey-faced when the roar came up to its full voice, watched her hold to the edge of the glaze-bench with both hands and breathe, deliberately, the way you breathe when the body wants to run and there is nowhere to run to — and did not go to her, because Machteld had told her once, flatly, that being gone to made it worse, that the only mercy was to be let stand it. So Clara let her stand it, and stood it herself beside her, and they were two women standing in the roar of a fire on purpose, which was the whole of the trade now, and neither of them said so.

The trouble came at the holding, in the dead middle of the night, and it came the way trouble always came in that trade, out of the tide. There were three hundred pieces stacked in the kiln, and the tide would not wait, and a man watching three hundred pieces of the Company's order come up to heat, with the ships' date running at him like water, wants the fire hotter and wants it done, and a hotter, faster fire is a worse fire. Spronck knew it. Spronck was the steadiest man Clara had ever stood beside and even Spronck, in the third hour of the holding with the sweat cutting tracks through the kiln-grime on his burned hands, said, once, low, watching the colour through the spy-hole: "I could push her. Bring her up another notch, hold her shorter, have her cooling by dawn and drawn by nightfall and the next three hundred stacked. We've the tide to think of, Clara. We're behind it already."

"You could push her," Clara agreed. She was watching the same colour through the same hole, the pale straw going toward the white that told the heat. "And you'd craze the half of it. Cool her too fast off too hard a fire and the glaze crawls and cracks. You know that better than I do. Push her for the tide and you'll draw three hundred cracked plates the Company ships back, and the tide won't care, and the second order won't come, and we'll have made bad blue fast instead of good blue slow, which is the one thing in this trade that's never once been worth it." She did not look away from the spy-hole. "Hold her. Lose the tide. Not the blue."

Spronck stood a long moment with his hand on the stoke-hole damper. Then he took the hand away. He had reason to hold her, and the reason had a name and a broken kiln-load behind it. Adriaen Block had pushed a firing the week before — Block, who was behind the tide like all of them and prouder than most and would not be seen to fail — had brought his kiln up hard and hot and cooled her fast to have the ware drawn and the next stacked, and had drawn, at the end of it, two hundred plates crazed. Clara had gone to see it, because a ruin was a thing to learn from and Block had let her come, sourly, because he wanted someone to tell him it was the clay and not the firing. It was not the clay. The glaze had crawled and cracked across every piece, a fine mad web of cracks all through the white and the blue both, the way a pond crazes over in the first hard frost — the glaze cooling faster than the body under it and tearing itself apart trying to keep up — so that two hundred true-blue birds sat on two hundred plates under a net of cracks like something seen through a shattered window, ruined, unsellable, a week of sixty hands' work and a barrel of the town's grey gone to shards a man could only grind up for the road. Block had stood over it grey-faced, and Clara had said the only merciful thing there was, which was true: "It's not the clay. It's the tide. The tide crazed it, not the fire. You pushed her for the ships and she cracked. We all want to push her. The whole town wants to push her. That's what'll break us, in the end — not Doncker and not the road; the tide in our own hands, making us hurry the one thing that can't be hurried." And she had carried the lesson to De Blauwe Hand and set it down in front of Spronck the way you set a crazed plate on a bench, and it was in the room now, in the dead middle of the night, when Spronck's hand went to the damper: two hundred of Block's plates in a web of frost-cracks, and the tide that had made them.

"Hold her," he said, to the fireman, and the fireman held her, and they lost the tide by that firing — brought her up slow and held her long and let her down slower, a day and a night and a day, the honest way, and drew her at last in the grey afternoon with the whole town's order that much further behind the ships.

And she came out true. That was the thing Clara took away from that first great firing, the thing she carried home in the dark to set against everything that was coming: they drew the kiln, plate after plate lifted out still warm and set down in the winter light, and the grey birds had gone blue in the fire, three hundred of them, true and even and alike, her blue, the town's blue, deep and clean and the same on the charger and the same on the cup, a blue you could stack and ship and lay a hundred of on a table and not find the two that did not match — and Grietje's bird among them, off the low bench, gone blue and alive on the white, indistinguishable now in its trueness from Machteld's and from the two old failing men's, the standard holding across every hand because the hands had trusted the tile and the tile had trusted the fire. It was the cheapest blue that was ever made and it had come out of the fire the exact right blue three hundred times, and Clara stood in the drawn kiln's fading heat and looked at three hundred alike true blue birds bound for the far side of the earth and felt the thing Doncker could not feel, rise in her past the tiredness and the tide and the fear of the roar: not that she had made something rare, but that she had made something *dependable* — a blue that would be there, and be true, the ten-thousandth time as the first, for anyone, forever, as trades reckon forever. There was a kind of beauty in a thing being the same and true and cheap and everywhere that the rare had never once had to be. The rare only had to be itself, once, for the few. The common had to be true every time, for everyone, or it was nothing. She had not known, before the kilns, that dependability was a kind of beauty. She knew it now, drawing true blue out of the fire at the cost of the tide, and it did not warm her, quite, because the tide was real and the ships would not wait, and she carried the cold of that home under the warm of the other, braided, the way she carried everything now.


## 7. The Supply Cut

The grey ran short in the black week after the new year, and it ran short the way Spronck had said it would, all at once and down the one narrow road, and Clara knew before anyone told her that Doncker's hand was on it, because she had spent two years learning the particular weather of Doncker's hand.

It came as a letter first — the Hamburg factor's letter, come down the frozen river by the last barge before the ice shut it, in a cramped foreign hand, and Spronck read it standing in the wheel-room with his face going flat and still. The Saxon mines were slow, the factor wrote; the winter was hard and the war on the continent had put soldiers on the roads the zaffer came down and a merchant had to feed a soldier at every toll now; the price of the raw cobalt had gone up by half since the autumn and would go higher; and — the factor was sorry, the factor valued the potters of Delft, the factor had dealt honestly with them these two years when better men would not — but the factor had been approached by a house of great credit, a house that would take his whole supply at the new high price and stand surety for a year in advance, coin down, a thing the Delft potters, God keep them, had never been able to do; and a poor factor in a hard winter with soldiers at the tolls could not turn away a house of great credit for the sake of old friendship, though it grieved him. He would send what he could. He could no longer promise the road was theirs.

"A house of great credit," Spronck said, and set the letter down, and looked at Clara, and neither of them had to say the name. "Out of Hamburg. Standing surety a year in advance, coin down." The flat harbour-voice had gone very quiet. "He told you he would. To your face. In your shop. He said the day the thread failed you'd come to him, and it'd be his terms. He didn't wait for it to fail on its own. He went and cut it."

"He didn't cut it," Clara said. She was looking at the letter and thinking in the cold clear way she thought when a thing had fallen and there was no use in the falling of it, only in the reckoning after. "He bought it. That's worse. A cut thread you can splice. A bought road is his now, honest, coin down, no crime in it a guild would hear. He hasn't done a thing a merchant mayn't do. He's found the one narrow place the whole town's blue comes through, and he's put his credit in front of it, lawfully, and now the grey comes to Delft through Willem Doncker or it doesn't come, and he'll sell it to us — he'll be generous — at the new high price, on his terms, when we come to him. Which we will have to. There's no other road."

She said it flatly and it landed flatly, the whole town's rebirth hanging on a Saxon hole and a Hamburg thread and a dying man's late shrewdness, and she felt the thing she had felt in the door of Van Nes's paint-room, the whole shape turning over: that it is the narrow things that kill you, not the large ones; that everything that had ever fallen on her had come down a road she had watched and been unable to widen; that she had thought of the road, half a night, over the factor's paper, and known it was the danger, and named it to Spronck in the cold wheel-room, and it had come anyway, because seeing the narrow road does not widen it, it only lets you watch the thing come down it.

They tried, in the short frozen weeks, everything a poor consortium of potters could try, and none of it was enough. Spronck went to two other factors he knew of the trade; one would not deal in a supply already promised to a great house, and one would deal only at a price that made the order a loss to fill. Block, who had correspondents of his own, wrote to Antwerp and got a promise of smalt — but smalt was the poor cousin of the blue, coarse and fading, and a service laid in smalt fired grey and thin and would come back from the Company as surely as a service laid in nothing. Clara went herself, on the ice, to the Company's own factor when he came through in the thaw, and put it to him plainly — that his great order would die of thirst, that the town could make his blue and could no longer get the grey to make it with, that a Hamburg house had bought the road — and the soft heavy man had listened, and turned his hat, and said the thing such men always said, which was that the supply was the potters' affair and the order was the Company's, and that the Company had ordered a service and would expect a service, and that if Delft could not furnish it there were kilns in Haarlem, and good day.

So it came down, at the end of the frozen weeks, to the thing Doncker had promised it would come down to, laid out on the slab as clean as a sum: go to Hamburg through Willem Doncker, on Willem Doncker's terms, and fill the order and keep the town's living and hand him the road and the price and the standing over the whole trade forever — or keep the road their own, and empty, and watch the order die on the tide, and the second order go to Haarlem, and the chance that had come once down the water go back up it, and the mended town's one great rising fall back into the rubble it had climbed out of. The water in the drought, at the drought's price. He had not needed to raise his hand. He had only needed to be patient, and shrewd, and to know, as he had known for forty years, exactly where the narrow place was.


## 8. The Reckoning

The bird went into its crate on the worst day, which was the way of that winter; the worst days came in bunches, as though ruin, once it found the narrow road to you, sent everything down it at once.

The collector's man came for the goldfinch in the last week of the frozen January, because Agatha was for Gouda before the spring and the sale was made and the sum was paid and the bird was his now, or his master's, to go into a dark chest in Amsterdam and be worth more every year unseen. Clara had known it was coming; Grietje had had it all the autumn to copy, had sat under it a hundred afternoons with her grey and taken the bird off it until she could paint it with her eyes shut, until it was hers as much as anyone living's; and still, when the man crated it — packed it in tow and wool in a wooden case, the loose dragged paint and the chain and the warm-cold air a dead man had got into a foot of panel, and closed the lid on it, and the good north light fell for the first time in two years on the bare pale rectangle of wall where it had hung — Clara felt it go out of the shop like a death, a small clean second death of the man himself, the last of him leaving her keeping for a dark chest where no one would see him, which Agatha had said was the death Carel would have hated worse than the fire. She did not say any of that. She helped the man carry the case to his barge so the damp should not get in, and came back, and stood in the shop that was suddenly larger and colder and wrong, and did not look at the bare place on the wall, and could not stop looking at it.

And Grietje, who had come in from the bench and seen the empty wall, stood under it a while with her whole grave face, and then went up to their two rooms and came down with her fist closed, and opened it on the slab, and laid out her five things by the grey window-light — the tile-shard, the button, the pebble, the pyrite, the gold that was not gold — and Clara saw, before the child said anything, that there were only four; that the fifth thing, the little fired tile with her own first clumsy blue bird, was not among them; and she opened her mouth to ask and Grietje said, in the flat certain voice, not looking up: "I gave it to Machteld. To keep. So there'd be one somewhere that isn't going away." And she gathered the four back into her fist, and Clara understood that the child had done a thing at ten that Clara at five-and-thirty was still learning: that you cannot hold a bright thing against its loss by keeping it in the dark; that the only way to keep it is to let it go where it will be seen; and that the child had given her own first true bird away to be kept by someone who would still be there, on the day she understood that everything she loved was going into the fire or into a crate or into the ground, and that keeping was not the same as saving, and had never been.

That was the small death. The large one came the same week, and it came, as such things do, in the plainest possible clothes: an empty barrel. The grey had run out. Not short — out. The last of the autumn's zaffer went under Clara's muller on a black morning and there was no more behind it, the Saxon road shut in Doncker's fist, and the benches across the town came in to their day's work and there was, for the first time in the order's life, no blue to give them. Forty hands, and no blue. She stood in the cold shop before the empty barrel where a whole barrel of the town's living should have stood, and did the last of the arithmetic, and it came out where she had known it would come out, which was nowhere at all.

She went to Doncker that afternoon. There was nothing else to do and she had never in her life been able to sit with a thing undone because it was hard; she went to him the way she had gone to the fallen wall, because the boy was under it and someone had to dig. Doncker received her in his good house on the Oude Delft, in a room hung with the true colours of his whole long life — a small dark Flemish thing with a blue in the shadow of a cloak that could only be lapis, a red lake glowing in a martyr's robe, the rare and beautiful dying gorgeous things he had spent sixty years mastering — and he was greyer than the autumn, thinner, a man Clara's eye read now as very near the fire that would or would not turn him, and he did not gloat. She would give him that, to her grave. He did not gloat. He heard her out, that the grey was gone and the order dying and the town with it, and he nodded, and he was almost gentle, the gentleness of a man who has been proved right about a thing he took no joy in being right about.

"I told you it would come to this," he said. "I did not wish it. You may not believe that, but I did not. I offered you the water before the drought, at a fair price, as a friend, for Gerrit's sake." He turned a cup in his hands, a small silver cup, an old man's fidget. "I have the road now. Hamburg is mine, a year in advance, coin down — the thing your potters could never do. I can have the grey to your kilns inside the month, enough for the whole order and the next. I will do it. I am not a monster, whatever you have decided. I will keep the town's living alive." He set the cup down. "The terms are these: the supply comes through my house, at my price, and the price is what the road costs plus what the road is worth, which is a great deal, because it is the only road. And the trade — the town's blue, the thing the Company sends for — is understood, henceforth, to stand behind my supply. My name on it. A guild house behind the potters' blue, as there should always have been a guild house behind it; a merchant of standing where there was a widow grinding on a second-hand slab. You will still grind. You are the best grinder in the province; I have never said otherwise; you will grind for the whole of it and be paid well to grind. But you will grind behind me. The road is mine, and the road is the trade, and the trade will be mine, and the town will keep its living, and that" — the tired gentleness again, all the way through and out the far side — "is not the worst end, Clara, for a woman the guild would not give a market-stall to three years ago. It is a great deal better than the boy in the ground got, or my brother's household, or the twelve hundred, or however many it was; nobody ever got the number right. You will be rich, and behind me, and alive. Take the water. The drought does not lift on its own."

And Clara stood in the room hung with the dying rare beautiful colours of his whole long life, with the water held out to her at the drought's price, the town's living in one hand and the town's freedom in the other and no way she could see to keep the one without letting go the other; and she thought of the empty barrel, and the forty hands with no blue, and the bare place on the wall where the bird had been, and the child's four things where there had been five, and Barent in the hasty ground who had wanted the guild so badly it came off him like heat and would have taken Doncker's terms in a heartbeat and been glad; and she found she could not answer him. For the first time since the fallen wall she stood in front of a thing and could not, for the length of a long breath, see the next motion — could not see where to put her hands. She had come to the bottom of the ledger and the ledger leaned all the way over, and there was no grinding that tipped it, because grinding made colour and did not make more road, and the road was his.

"I'll bring you an answer," she said, when she could say anything. "In three days."

"There is no answer but the one," Doncker said, not unkindly. "But take your three days. I have all the time there is, now. It is the one thing a man my age is rich in." And he saw her to the door of the good house himself, slowly, an old grey man in a room full of dying beautiful colours, the water and the drought both, and Clara went out into the frozen street with no blue and no road and no bird on her wall and three days to find the thing she could not see, and the light was going out of the short day into the blue hour, deepening over the mended town, and for once it gave her nothing at all.

# The Common Blue — Part Three: Everywhere the Plates Go


## 9. What the Blue Is For

She found the answer where she always found things, which was in her hands, in the grinding, at the black hour, when the day had stopped shouting arguments at her and left her alone with the plain motion of the work.

She ground nothing that morning — there was nothing to grind — but she stood at the empty slab in the dark out of habit, because the black hour was where she thought, and she made herself do the thing she had learned to do over the fallen wall: not to grieve the fallen thing, not to argue with the road that was shut, but to look, coldly, at what was actually in front of her, grain by grain, and to stop looking at the thing she wanted and look at the thing that was. And what was in front of her, when she stopped wanting the road back, was not the road at all. It was the ware. It was three hundred true blue plates already fired and forty hands already taught and a whole town already turned to the making, and — she made herself see it, in the dark, the way you see a colour that isn't there yet — a Company that wanted the ware more than any merchant in Hamburg wanted to sell them the grey.

That was the grain she had been grinding past for a week. She had been thinking of Doncker as the water in the drought, the only road, the man who held the narrow place. And he did hold the narrow place. But she had been reckoning the narrow place as the thing that mattered, and it was not the thing that mattered; it was only the thing that was in the way. The thing that mattered was on the far side of it: an appetite the size of the ocean. The Company did not want cobalt. The Company wanted plates, blue plates, ten thousand of them and ten thousand more, to fill its holds and dress its factories and sell to a world starved of the blue China had stopped making — and Willem Doncker, standing in the narrow place with his hand out for a toll, was not the Company's supplier. He was a tax on the Company's own order. He had made himself, with his year's surety and his coin down and his forty years' shrewdness, a wall between the greatest trading house in the world and the thing it most wanted, and he meant to charge them both to pass it. And a man who stands between an ocean and the thing it is pouring toward does not hold the narrow place. He drowns in it. He only has to be shown to the ocean.

She went to the Company factor the third morning, the day her three days to Doncker ran out, and she did not go to him the way she had gone before, as a poor potter begging the order not die of thirst. She went to him as a woman with a sum to lay on a table, and she laid it.

"You have a merchant in your road," she said. "Willem Doncker, of this town, a guild man. He has bought the cobalt supply your service is made of — the whole Saxon road, a year in advance, coin down — and he means to sell it back to the potters who make your ware, at his price, which is the road's cost plus whatever the road is worth to a man who owns the only one. And the road is worth, to him, the whole trade. He means to stand behind the town's blue and take his toll on every plate that crosses the water to you, forever, as trades reckon forever. You will pay it. It will be in the price of every service you order from this town from this year to the end. You are not buying blue plates from Delft. You are buying them from Willem Doncker, who has arranged to sit between you and them, and you did not agree to that, and you are going to pay for it in every hold you fill." She let it sit, the way Spronck let a truth sit. "Unless you would rather not."

The soft heavy man turned his hat. But he had stopped, she saw; the counting hands had gone still; a man whose whole life was the shaving of a toll off a price had heard the word *toll* and come awake. "And the alternative, madam," he said, "since you clearly have one, or you would not have walked here in this cold."

"The Company brings the grey itself," Clara said. She had ground this all the black morning and it came out clean. "You have ships. You have credit no Hamburg house can touch. You have factors in every port the cobalt passes through, and a name that stands surety without a coin down because it is the Company. Doncker bought one road because it was the only road a poor potter could reach. It is not the only road *you* can reach. Bring the raw cobalt on your own keels, into your own warehouse, and sell it to the potters at what it costs you and a fair turn — you, not him — and Doncker's road is a road to nowhere. He will have bought a year of Saxon zaffer, coin down, at a high price, to sell to a town that no longer has to buy it from him, because the Company decided it would rather not pay a toll to a Delft merchant to reach a Delft trade. Let him sit in his narrow place. You will have gone around it, and the blue will come cheaper to you than his road would ever have carried it, and the town will make your service, and the second, and the tenth, for as long as the ships sail." She looked at him. "You wanted one true blue, true across every kiln in the town. I can give you that. I have given you that; three hundred pieces of it are drawn and stacked at De Blauwe Hand this morning. What I cannot do is give it to you through a wall, at the wall's price. Take the wall down. It costs you nothing but the decision, and it saves you the toll forever."

The factor was quiet a while. Then he did the thing such men did when you had shown them their own advantage, which was to make it his idea, and Clara let him, because she did not care whose idea it was, she cared that it was done. "The Company does not care to pay a toll to reach its own order," he said, as though instructing her. "It is not good practice. It sets a precedent. Every town with a trade would grow itself a Doncker." He turned the hat once more and put it on. "I will write to Amsterdam. The Company will carry the cobalt. You will grind it. There will be no wall." He almost smiled, the same small tick as at the first. "You should have been a factor, madam. It is a waste, those hands on a slab."

"They're not wasted," Clara said. "They're the only part of this that touches the blue."

There was more to it than one morning, of course. There always was; the world did not turn on a single clean speech, whatever the stories said. The Company's writing to Amsterdam and Amsterdam's answer and the first cobalt coming down on the Company's own account took the rest of that hard winter, and it took, worse, the thing Clara had thought could never be got: it took the six proud masters, who would not sit in a room together, sitting in a room together. Because the Company would carry the grey, but the Company would not portion it out plate by plate to six squabbling potteries and keep six sets of books; it would deal with the town's blue as one thing or not at all. So the potters had to become, for the length of a purchase, one thing. They had to pool — a shared factor, a common purse, one buying-hand for the cobalt that fed all their kilns — the exact thing every one of them had spent his working life refusing to do, because a potter's supply, like a potter's blue, was his own, the one thing in the trade that was his.

And they did it, in the end, in Spronck's cold wheel-room, over the empty barrels, because the drought had made the argument that Clara never could have: that a road held alone was a road a Doncker could buy, and a road held in common was a road no one man could reach around, and that the only defence against a wall across the narrow place was to be, themselves, too many and too joined to be walled off from. Clara sat in the wheel-room while they argued it — Block who would not speak to Van Nes, Verhaar who kept his glaze in his head, all of them clutching their separate ruin — and she said, once, the only thing she said all afternoon, when Block spat that he'd not put his coin in a common purse with a man who'd stolen his wheel-boy: "You'll put your coin in a common purse, or you'll put your kilns cold and your hands on the parish, and your blue will go to Haarlem, and in ten years no one will remember Delft made a blue at all. The Company doesn't need you. It needs a town. Be a town, or be nothing. It's the same choice the blue is. A colour that's only yours is worth what one man can carry. A colour that's everyone's is worth the world." And she went back to looking at her hands, and let the truth do the pushing, and after a long while, and much cursing, they did it, because there was nothing else to do, and a common purse held against a common ruin was, it turned out, a thing even six proud men could reach when the alternative was the rubble.

She walked home that dusk with the thing done, or begun, the road going round the wall and the town becoming, for the length of a trade, one thing; and she turned Doncker's word over in the blue hour the way she had turned it since the summer. *A common blue.* He had meant it for the bottom of a coming-down. And she had made it, this winter, in a cold wheel-room over empty barrels, into the plain floor she and the whole town were standing on: the common blue, the common purse, the common road, the common good — the thing held by everyone because it was held by no one, that no single hand could own or wall or carry off, and that was worth, for exactly that reason, more than the rarest blue that was ever ground for one man's shadowed cloak. She had answered Doncker at last, and she had not done it by beating him. She had done it by making his narrow place irrelevant — by being too common to corner.


## 10. The Service

The great service for the Indies was made in the spring, in the long light coming back, and Clara would remember it the rest of her life as the busiest and best of her working years, the one time the whole of the trade she had been driven into by ruin turned, for a season, into something that felt like joy.

The grey came down on the Company's own account, barrels of it into the Company's warehouse and out to the pooled potters at a fair turn, and Doncker's bought road sat idle in Hamburg, a year of Saxon zaffer he had paid coin down for and could sell to no one who was forced to buy it — she heard, later, that he took a loss on it that would have broken a smaller man, and did not gloat even then, though she had earned the right. And the kilns roared, all across the mended town, the beehive kilns in the shells of the dead breweries, roaring the twelfth of October out of their brick throats day and night; and Clara walked her rounds with the blue and the pattern-tiles, and the forty hands became sixty and the sixty true, and the white ware went into the kilns grey-birded and drab and came out blue and alive, plate after charger after cup after bowl, the whole dressing of a great house's table and more than one house's, all of it her blue, the town's blue, true across every kiln, a service you could stack a thousand of and not find the two that did not match.

And Grietje came into her own.

It happened over the great centre-charger, the one large flat dish that would sit at the heart of the service, the piece the factor's letter had asked be made "finer than the rest, for the head of the table, where a governor will eat." Spronck gave it to Clara to see done, and Clara gave it to Grietje, because Grietje was the best hand in the town now and everyone but Grietje knew it, and because Clara wanted to see what the child would do with a white ground the size of a cartwheel and no pattern-tile to match.

She watched her do it. Grietje sat under the bare place on the wall where the goldfinch had hung — she always sat there now, by choice, though neither of them said why — with the great white charger before her and her little pot of the drab grey, and she did not paint Fabritius's bird. Clara had thought she would; had thought the child would put the dead man's chained goldfinch at the heart of the service the way she had put it on a hundred plates all autumn, faithfully, off the panel, the way you carry a thing forward by copying it true. But the child sat a long while with the empty wall above her and the empty white below her, and then she wet her brush and laid down, in the drab grey that was not blue and would be, a bird Clara had never seen.

It was a goldfinch still — the same small quick shape, the tilt of the head, the thing the dead man had known about a bird that Grietje had learned off him in a hundred afternoons and would never now unlearn. But it was not chained. There was no ring, no band, no thread from the ring to the leg; the bird sat on a spray of its own, wings a little open, as though it had that moment lit and might that moment go, and around it Grietje laid not the pale warm-cold air of a Delft window but a border of small blue birds, a ring of them, going round the rim, each a little different, each about to fly — a whole turning wheel of unchained goldfinches around the one at the centre, so that the eye went round and round the rim and could not tell the leading bird from the following, could not find where the flight began or where it would end. It was, Clara saw, standing behind the child's small crooked wrist, the bird carried forward and set free in the same stroke: Fabritius's goldfinch, that a dead man had painted chained to its ring in a foot of darkening oil for one house's wall, become a ring of loose bright birds on a fired ground the size of a cartwheel, bound for a governor's table on the far side of the earth, and after that — for the charger would be seen, and copied, and the copy copied — bound for everywhere, every table, forever, unchained, unowned, un-ownable, going round and round the rim of the world and never landing anywhere long enough to be caught.

"It's not his bird," Grietje said, when it was laid down, looking at the grey that was not blue. Not defending it. Telling the truth of it, the way she told the truth of everything. "I couldn't keep painting his. It was going to die and I kept it and it went in the crate anyway. So I made mine. Mine can't die. It goes in the fire and the fire keeps it." She looked up at Clara with the mended arm and the cracked-whole voice and the whole grave face. "You said the oil dies and the fire doesn't. So I put it in the fire. That's how you make it not."

And Clara, who had said yes to the great order for a hundred reasons of coin and none of them the reason, stood behind the child in the long spring light and understood that this was the reason, this exact bird, this un-chained un-owned wheel of blue birds a struck-mute child had made out of a dead man's chained one and put into the fire so that it could not die — that the whole two years of ruin and grey and tide and Doncker and the narrow road had been, without any of them knowing it, the long machine for getting this one bird made and fired and onto a keel bound east; that the true ultramarine, the astronomical blue, was in a dark chest in Amsterdam going up in value unseen, one blue for one owner, dying slowly of being paint and being kept; and that the cheapest blue that was ever made, the common grey ash any potter's brat could slap on a dish, was about to carry a set-free goldfinch to the far side of the world and back forever, seen by everyone, kept by no one, alive in the fire that could not get at it. She did not say any of that to the child. She put her blue hand on the child's blue shoulder, once, and took it away, and said, "Fire it well, then," and turned so the child should not see her face do the thing it was doing; and behind her the great charger with its wheel of unchained birds waited on the bench to go into the fire and come out the one thing in that whole trade that death could not have, which was a beauty made common enough to be everywhere at once and so past all reach of the crate and the chest and the grave.


## 11. The Guild's Blue

Doncker died in the summer, before the service sailed, and Clara went to the burial, and could not afterward have said with any honesty why, except that she had spent two years being fought by him and a person you have been fought by that long is a kind of intimate, and that the town was smaller by a shape she had spent so long braced against that its going left her, for a while, oddly without balance, like a woman leaning on a door that is suddenly opened.

He had been failing since the winter; the loss on the Hamburg cobalt had not helped him, though Clara did not think it had killed him — he was old, and the grey had been under his skin a year, and the fire he had been waiting for had come, as it comes, and turned him or did not; that was not hers to know. His nephew had the good Voldersgracht stall and the painters'-colour trade, which was a dying trade, the true rare beautiful colours going down and down as the fired blue rose, so that the nephew had inherited, Clara thought, exactly the thing Doncker had spent his life defending and the world had spent Doncker's life leaving behind: a stall full of dying gorgeous things that fewer men every year could afford to want. There was a justice in it that was too sad to enjoy.

Before the end, in the late spring, he had sent for her. She had not wanted to go and had gone, the way she went to hard things, and found him in the room hung with the true colours, smaller than ever, the good cloth swallowing him, the little silver cup on the table by his hand. He had wanted, it turned out, only to say one thing, and it took him a while to come to it, an old man arranging the last of his account.

"You beat me," he said. Not bitterly. Clearly, the way Agatha had said *I know what it's worth now* — a person doing their arithmetic in the open at last. "I did not think you would. I did not think a woman on a widow's leave with a second-hand slab would beat me. I thought the world would come right — that the true colours would hold, that the guild would hold, that good order would hold, that the potteries were a passing coarseness and the town would remember it was a town of painters. I was wrong about the world. I have been wrong about the world for ten years and too old to see it, and you saw it at five-and-thirty, grinding grey ash on a dead potter's slab, and I could not forgive you for it, and now I am dying and I find I would rather not go on not forgiving you. It is tiring, at the end, the not-forgiving. It weighs more than the thing itself." He turned the little cup. "Gerrit was my friend. Before he was your husband. I said it to you as a grievance, more than once — as though you had taken him from me, which you did not; the fever took him, and I was angry at the fever and it was easier to be angry at you. He was my friend, and he would have been proud of you, and it would have made me furious to watch, and he would have laughed at me for it, and been right." A long breath. "That is all. I wanted it said while I could say it and not have it be a deathbed thing, which is cheap. It is not a deathbed thing. It is only late." He looked at her with the tired clear eyes. "You have made a common blue, Clara. I called it a coming-down. I was wrong about that too, I think, though I am too old to feel it the way you feel it. A blue for everyone. My whole life was colours for the few. Perhaps yours was the better trade. I would not have said so a year ago. Go home. Grind your grey. Send my regards to the child; she has the thing Carel had, I saw it on the plate, the thing I spent sixty years selling to men who could not do it — she can make a dead thing look at you. Keep her out of the almshouse and out of a marriage that wastes her, if you can; the guild will not help you, the guild helps no one it can call a girl. Go home."

She had gone home. And now he was in the ground of the Oude Kerk, under his true beautiful dying colours, and Clara stood at the plain Reformed burial in the summer light and thought that she had won the thing she had fought two years for — the leave secure, the shop held, the trade legitimate, the Company behind the makers, the common blue the town's living and no one's toll — and that it had been bought, like everything, dear; that Doncker was dead and Barent was in the hasty ground and Fabritius under the Nieuwe Kerk yard and the true blue in a dark chest and the town three years and more into its twenty years of climbing out of the rubble, and that a victory in that trade, in that town, in that life, was never a rout and never a triumph and never came clean. It came the way the blue came: hard, by weight, by grinding, by the patient ruin of one thing to make another. It came narrow, and held, and cost what it cost, and you did not get to keep the making without standing next to the taking, because that was not how the ledger of a life came out. It came out both at once. She had known that since the fallen wall. She knew it at the grave of the man who had fought her, and felt, standing there, not triumph but the plain tiredness of a woman who has carried a weight a long way and is not yet allowed to set it down, and under the tiredness, braided with it, the other thing, the thing that would not stop rising in her these two years no matter what was taken: that the blue was made, and the child could make a dead thing look at you, and the service was going to sail.


## 12. Everywhere the Plates Go

The freight went down to the sea in the autumn of 1657, and Clara and Grietje walked beside the barge to the edge of the town to watch it go, because you did not, Clara had decided, let a thing you had made two years of your life ache to have leave without walking it to the water.

It went the way everything went in that country, by barge, down the still canal in the flat autumn light — the crated service, hundreds of pieces of it, packed in tow and straw, the whole dressing of a governor's table and more than one governor's, and the great centre-charger among them with its wheel of unchained birds, bound for the trekvaart and the river and Amsterdam and the Company's warehouse and, when the autumn ships had their holds full, for the open sea, for Batavia, for the far side of the earth wherever the Company's writ ran, which was, the factor had said, near enough everywhere. Clara walked beside it with her blue-creased hands and watched her colour go — barrels of the town's grey ground under her own muller, gone into the fire, come out blue, gone onto the water — and did not grieve it going, the way she had once grieved every grain of the true blue out the shop door. She had learned the difference. The true blue you grieved because it was finite and gone; each grain that left was a grain less in the world forever. The common blue you did not grieve, because it was not finite and not gone; it multiplied. What went down the canal was not the last of a thing. It was the first of a thing that would keep coming, plate after plate, ship after ship, for as long as the ships sailed and the world wanted blue, which was to say forever, as trades reckon forever. You could let a thing go without grief if there was more of it coming. That was the whole gift of the common. Doncker had never once had it, in all his rich rare beautiful life; every colour he had ever loved had been a thing you grieved to sell.

The cost was not paid off and would not be. She knew that walking beside the water and did not pretend it away. Barent was in the hasty ground still and would stay there, a boy who had wanted the guild so badly it came off him like heat, and no blue, fired or ground, common or rare, gave him back; she would carry him unmended to her own grave, which was the honest price, and the great order and the sailing freight and the whole town's rising did not cancel him and were not meant to. Fabritius was under the Nieuwe Kerk yard and his true bird in a dark chest going up in value unseen, dying slowly of being paint, however anyone loved it. Doncker was in the Oude Kerk ground under his dying colours. Agatha was in Gouda among people who did not know what her husband's studio had been. The town was three years and more into its twenty years' climb and the ruin still ran under the new brick across the quarter, and no one would ever count the dead, and the not-counting was a wound the town would wear under its new brick forever. All of that was true, and stood, and the freight sailed anyway, and both things were true at once and neither cancelled the other, and Clara had stopped, a long time ago, expecting the one to pay off the other, because that was not how the ledger came out. It came out both at once — the breaking and the making, the grief and the trade, the taking and the giving-back — and the whole of the wisdom she had was in not grieving the breaking, and not fearing the fire, only watching it, and grinding true, and putting the colour where the light and the whole wide world could find it.

They stood at the last bridge before the town gave out to the trekvaart and watched the barge go under and out, and the crated blue went with it, out toward the sea and the ships and the far side of the world; and the child stood at the rail of the bridge with the mended arm and the cracked-whole voice and held her blue hands up in the low gold light, the old game, the child's whole small greedy joy in the mark of the trade.

"Bluest hands in Delft," Grietje said.

"There's sixty of us now," Clara said. "More by spring, the way the second order's going. Bluest hands in Delft won't do any more. It's the bluest hands in the town, and the next town, and the one after that, all the way down the water." She looked at the barge going small toward the sea. "We'll have to think of something else."

Grietje considered it with her whole grave face, the way she considered everything, turning it over the way Clara turned a word to the light. And then she amended it, closing the account, with her dead mother's flat certainty come up in her cracked-whole voice: "The bluest hands in Delft," she said, "and everywhere the plates go. Which is everywhere. Which is the whole world." And she took her aunt's blue hand in her own blue hand, the small crooked wrist and the worn one, and Clara looked down at her — the child who had been struck mute in a white flash and had come back to her voice over a pan of mud and come into her own over a white charger, the child who was not the child she had been given and was, past all arguing, hers — and did not correct her, because it was true, or was going to be, which came to the same thing in that trade: because the plates were going everywhere the ships went, and the blue would not fade, and the child's own set-free bird would be on some of them, going round and round the rim of the world, holding still to be seen in houses neither of them would ever stand in, on tables neither of them would ever eat at, on the far side of a sea neither of them would ever cross; and because a blue held by no one because it was held by everyone was the one thing she had made in her whole grief-worn grinding life that death, and the crate, and the dark chest, and the narrow road, and the fire itself, could none of them get at, because it was too common to catch.

"The bluest hands in Delft," Clara said, "and everywhere the plates go." And they stood on the bridge in the last of it and watched the blue go down the water toward the sea, and the evening came down to the blue hour, the little while at the edge of dark that the painters cursed and could not do without, when the gold goes out of the sky by degrees and every colour on the earth deepens and stands true a moment before the night takes it. The mended town lay still and held the last of the light, blue on blue, the new brick and the old stone all gone one deepening colour together, and the tower of the Nieuwe Kerk stood black against a sky the exact grave blue that no man could grind and every man had tried to, going deeper as they watched. Somewhere behind them a kiln was banked for the night, ticking as it cooled, full of grey ware that did not know yet it was blue, that would come out of the fire in the morning blue and alive and bound, some of it, for the far side of the world. Somewhere a woman called a child in. The water lay unmoving, and carried, on its unmoving face, the whole of the failing light, and gave it back; and it was not the last of anything, the blue going down the water, only the first of a thing that would keep coming as long as there was fire to make it and a world to want it and a hand to grind it true; and the two of them, the worn hand and the small crooked one, blue to the creases both, stood and watched it go until it was too dark to see the barge, and long after that, until it was too dark to work by and exactly bright enough to see.


*THE END*

## About the series

*The Common Blue* is the third and final novel of **Ultramarine**, a series set in Delft at the turning point of the Dutch Golden Age — the years around the Thunderclap of 12 October 1654, when the town of painters became the town of the blue-and-white that would sail to the far side of the world. Each book follows the widowed colour-grinder Clara Wijnants and her niece Grietje through one turn of that great change: what it costs to make a beautiful thing, and what it means to hand a craft on.

Book One, *Ultramarine*, tells how Clara reclaimed the truest blue in the province grain by grain from the ruin of her own house, and fought the guild for the right to go on. Book Two, *The Blue and the White*, carried the trade from the dying easel to the rising kiln — a blue born of the same fire that broke the town. *The Common Blue* completes the arc: the rarest colour on earth made common, true across a whole town of rival kilns and sent by the barrel to the ends of the world, and the child struck mute by the blast come at last into her own as the painter of a blue that death cannot get at.

The story is closed here. It ends where the second book's forward hook promised it would: the great order of blue-and-white bound for the East, filled and sailing; the narrow cobalt road held in common against the man who would have owned it; and a dead man's chained goldfinch set free and multiplied onto ten thousand plates by a child's own hand — the bluest hands in Delft, and everywhere the plates go. Readers who want the whole of Delft's turning can begin at the beginning, with Book One.
