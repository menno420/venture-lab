## Description (paste into KDP "Description" — plain text)

China's kilns have gone cold in a distant war, and a starved world will pay any price for blue. So the great trading Company comes down the water to Delft with an order too big for any one pottery to fill: a service of blue-and-white for the autumn ships to the Indies, and another after it, for as long as the ships sail and the world wants blue.

Two years after the gunpowder blast that tore the heart out of Delft, Clara Wijnants is no longer the last grinder of a dying easel-trade — she is the blue-hand of the rising kilns, the finest maker of fired cobalt in the province. To fill the great order she must do the one thing the trade has never done: set a single true blue across a whole town of rival potteries, and teach it to sixty proud hands that will fight her for the right to be wrong. She must hold that blue against the tide that wants it hurried and the fire that will not be. And she must keep the town's one narrow road of cobalt out of the hands of Willem Doncker, the aging guild elder who spent two years trying to shut her door and has now, shrewd and dying, set out instead to own it — to buy the road, stand in the narrow place, and take his toll on every plate that crosses the water forever. He calls her blue a common blue, a kitchen blue, a blue for anyone. He means it for a wound.

The Common Blue completes the Ultramarine trilogy: the story of the Dutch Golden Age at its turning, when Delft stopped being a town of painters and became the town whose blue-and-white sailed to the far side of the world. About what it costs — and who pays it — to make a beauty common; about a dead man's chained goldfinch set free and multiplied onto ten thousand plates by a child who was struck silent and came back singing; and about learning that a blue held by no one, because it is held by everyone, is worth more than the rarest blue that was ever ground for one man's shadowed cloak. Contains bereavement and remembered disaster handled with restraint; no explicit content. The final Ultramarine novel; stands alone, and completes Ultramarine (Book One) and The Blue and the White (Book Two).
