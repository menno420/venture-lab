# Ultramarine — Bible Additions for Book 2 (*The Blue and the White*)

> **Status:** `draft`

New canon introduced in Book 2, to keep the series consistent going forward. Book
1's `characters.md`, `world.md`, and `style.md` remain the primary authority;
nothing here overrides them. Voice, register, and rules are unchanged (close
third on Clara, past tense, literary/restrained; see `style.md`). Real historical
persons continue to be handled strictly within the documented record
(`DECISIONS.md` [D-0003]); everything invented is flagged below.

## Timeline

- **Book 2 spans autumn 1655 → summer/May 1656** — roughly the year after Book
  1's close (spring 1655), covering the year of grace on Clara's guild leave and
  lease. Delft is ~3 years into its ~20-year rebuilding at the book's end.

## Continuity carried from Book 1's ending (fixed)

- Clara holds a colour trade under a **widow's leave bound in three ropes**: no
  market-right / no good Voldersgracht stall (that went to Doncker's nephew); must
  **find and hold a proper shop within the year or the leave lapses**; a high
  **continuance fee/bond** paid; and she must **grind the guild's town-work at a
  fixed rate below cost**. These are the plot's ticking clock in Book 2.
- **Fabritius's goldfinch panel** hangs in Clara's shop. Book 2 establishes its
  provenance explicitly: **Agatha van Pruyssen gave it into Clara's keeping** (not
  ownership) that first spring — it is held in trust, which is why Clara refuses
  to sell it even when ruin presses. Keep this consistent in any Book 3.
- The **unnamed young painter** (Vermeer, never named on the page) receives the
  **last of Clara's reclaimed ultramarine** for his "woman by a window" painting.
  By the end of Book 2 **there is no true ultramarine left in Clara's hands** and
  none she can afford to make — the old stone-blue trade is closed. Do not
  reintroduce a hoard of ultramarine in Book 3 without accounting for this.

## New invented characters

- **Coenraad Spronck** — invented. A **plateelbakker** (delftware potter) in his
  40s–50s, broad, burn-scarred hands, a flat easy Delft/harbour voice; pragmatic,
  honest, pushes a thing once then lets the truth do his pushing. Lost a **brother
  (a cooper) and the brother's whole household** in the Thunderclap, with no
  remains to bury — one of the two kinds of the town's bereaved (the other, like
  Clara with Barent, having a body to bury). Runs the pottery **De Blauwe Hand**.
  A collegial ally, not a romance — any warmth stays restrained per
  `DECISIONS.md` [D-0006].
- **De Blauwe Hand** ("The Blue Hand") — invented pottery, raised in the shell of
  a **failed brewery, De Drie Ackers, off the Oosteinde**. Named for the potter's
  mark (a blue hand on white) and for Spronck coming out of the blast with his
  brother's blood on his hand and making it a working hand. Grounded in the real
  pattern (`world.md`): empty brewery halls repurposed for pottery.
- **Machteld** — invented. The eldest **plateelschilster** (pottery-painter) at
  De Blauwe Hand, ~16, sure-handed, all brothers at home; takes Grietje under her
  wing. Her family died under the Nieuwe Kerk in the blast (she goes grey at a
  hard-rung bell).
- **Pauwels** — invented. An older potter who works days only, won't attend a
  night firing (blast-fear); minor.
- The **Amsterdam collector** (unnamed) and the **Company merchant** (unnamed) —
  minor, invented, transactional.

## Grietje's arc forward

- By the end of Book 2, **Grietje (~9→10) is the youngest bench-hand at De Blauwe
  Hand**, a genuinely gifted **plateelschilster** who paints **blue birds
  (goldfinches) on white ware** — carrying Fabritius's *Goldfinch* forward onto
  fired clay. Her voice is fully returned; she still counts her bright things (now
  **five**: the original four — blue tile-shard, horn button, river pebble, pyrite
  — plus **her own first fired blue-bird tile**). This sets her up as the coming
  generation of the craft for any Book 3.

## The fire-blue craft (grounded, with flags)

- The potter's blue is **cobalt** — **zaffer** (roasted cobalt ore ground with a
  little glass) and **smalt** — applied to the tin-glazed biscuit ware as a
  **drab grey-black** that turns true blue only in the **kiln firing**; it fires
  **into the glaze** and does not fade (contrast oil-ground painter's colour,
  which darkens/cracks/fades). This is grounded in `world.md`'s pigment section
  and the general history of delftware; the specific in-scene chemistry (burn-
  black from too much cobalt, freckling, crazing from fast cooling) is credible
  period potter's lore rendered for fiction, not a documented recipe — present as
  craft texture, not instruction.
- The potters' cobalt comes down via a **Hamburg/Saxon factor of the potters'
  own**, deliberately **outside Doncker's lapis/painter-colour network** — this
  is the plot mechanism by which Clara escapes Doncker's supply chokehold. Keep
  consistent: Doncker controls the **dying** stone-blue trade, not the rising
  fired-blue one.

## Thematic note (for series consistency)

- Book 1's motif set (blue as the cost/ruin of beauty; seeing vs. blindness;
  grinding/breaking to make; the blue hour; things reclaimed grain by grain) is
  extended, not replaced. **Book 2's additions:** *a blue you cannot see until
  the fire has it* (faith in a hidden colour — the "new seeing"); and **fire
  transmuted** — the element that destroyed Delft becomes, in the kiln, the maker
  of a beauty death cannot get at, without the making ever cancelling the taking.
  Companion refrain to Book 1's "not to grieve the breaking": Grietje's **"not to
  fear the fire, only to watch it."** Use sparingly, as with all refrains.
