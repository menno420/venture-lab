# Ultramarine — Style Sheet

> **Status:** `draft`

The register every chapter must match. Read the voice-lock aloud before drafting; when in doubt, match its rhythm and restraint.

## Voice-lock

> Blue came hard, the way most valuable things came to Clara Wijnants — by weight, by grinding, by the patient ruin of one thing to make another. The lapis had crossed half the world to reach her table: a knuckle of stone the colour of a bruised evening, veined with a gold that was not gold, only fool's gold, pyrite, worth nothing and beautiful for it. To free the blue she first had to break the stone, and she had learned long ago not to grieve the breaking.

## Rules (binding)

- **Close third person, past tense, Clara's POV throughout.** Do not head-hop. Everything is seen, known, and felt only as Clara sees, knows, and feels it.
- **Register: literary, sensory, restrained.** Concrete craft and period detail earn the emotion; do not over-explain feeling — render it in gesture, object, light. Aphoristic touches sparingly, in the manner of the voice-lock's last line.
- **Ground every scene in the physical:** pigment, stone, oil, canal-water, peat-smoke, cold, bread, cloth, coin. Use Dutch period nouns lightly (*gulden*, *stuiver*, *gracht*, *verf*, *muller*, *loodwit*, *trekschuit*) — italicise on first use, gloss in-line only if needed, never lecture.
- **Dialogue: plain, mid-17th-century plausible without pastiche.** No "prithee/forsooth." Sparing; let silence and action carry weight.
- **No anachronism.** No camera obscura as fact (perspective boxes and glasses are fine as Fabritius's known experiments); no tea or coffee as common; the Stadtholderless period (no Prince) in 1654. When history is uncertain — the death toll above all — write the uncertainty, do not invent precision.
- **Motifs to thread, not hammer:** blue as the cost and ruin of beauty; seeing versus blindness; grinding and breaking to make; the "blue hour" light; things saved from ruin grain by grain.
- **Avoid:** purple excess, modern idiom, therapy-speak, info-dumps, em-dash chains. Earn every large emotion with restraint before it.
- **Chapter length ~2100–2500 words.** Each chapter is a shaped scene or two with a turn, opening in-scene and ending on an image or beat that pulls forward.

## Craft guidance (expanded)

**Openings.** Begin inside an action or a sensation, never with weather-as-throat-clearing or a historical preamble. Let the reader deduce time and place from what Clara is doing with her hands. A chapter's first sentence should already be work: a stone breaking, oil poured, a coin refused.

**Sentence rhythm.** Follow the voice-lock — mostly plain declaratives, occasionally lengthened by a dash or a series that turns a physical process into a quiet claim about the world. Vary length; let a short sentence land the emotion the long one set up. Read for cadence, not just sense.

**Detail as feeling.** The book's method is displacement: grief, fear, and love are shown through pigment, tools, food, and light. When tempted to name an emotion, cut to the object instead — the muller set down, the blue jar counted, the child's collection of bright things. Trust the reader to feel it.

**Period texture, lightly.** Draw freely on `world.md` for the smell of the canals, the peat smoke, the prices in *stuivers*, the sober dark cloth — but never in a block. One or two exact details per scene do more than a paragraph. Never explain a Dutch word to the reader as a teacher would; let context carry it or gloss in three words.

**The disaster (Part Two).** Render it in fragments and close physical fact — dust, pressure, ringing silence, the wrongness of familiar streets — not in panorama or body-count. Follow the content note: injury and unidentifiable dead are on the page, but restraint and specificity, not spectacle. Barent's death and Fabritius's are felt through absence and aftermath more than staged.

**Continuity anchors.** Keep Clara's blue-stained hands, the ultramarine hoard, and Grietje's collection of bright things consistent across chapters; these recur and pay off. Grietje's muteness after the blast lifts by degrees — track it. The pyrite ("gold that was not gold") in the voice-lock is a seed worth echoing.

**Ending register.** The close should be quiet, earned, and clear-eyed — a remaking, not a triumph. End on image and gesture in the blue-hour light, faithful to the restraint the whole book has practised.
