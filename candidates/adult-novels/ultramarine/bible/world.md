# Ultramarine — World Reference

*Reference for fiction; where sources conflict (death toll, magazine founding date, exact time) the manuscript keeps the uncertainty.*

This is a working worldbuilding sheet for the chapter-writers. Facts carry inline source URLs; legends and disputed points are flagged so the prose can hedge rather than invent false precision.

## The Explosion — *de Delftse Donderslag*

On **Monday, 12 October 1654**, in the **late morning**, a state gunpowder magazine in Delft detonated. Sources cluster around *shortly before or after 10:30 a.m.*; one repeated account says half past eleven. Keep it **mid-to-late morning (~10:30–11:30)**, exact time left vague. ([essentialvermeer](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/donderslag.html); [Wikipedia: Delft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delft))

The magazine was one of several States-General arsenals in the town, this one nicknamed **"Het Secreet van Holland"** — the Secret of Holland. It stood in a **former convent of the Order of Poor Clares** (the Clarissen-/Sint-Claraklooster), repurposed as a powder store after the Reformation, in the **north-east Doelenkwartier near the end of the Geerweg**. Sources disagree on when powder was first stored there — 1572 in one account, 1637 in another — so treat the founding date as uncertain. The cleared site later became the **Paardenmarkt** (horse market), which it remains. ([essentialvermeer](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/donderslag.html); [Wikipedia: Delft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delft))

The store held **roughly 80,000–90,000 pounds of black powder** (about 30–40 tonnes; blast energy *estimated* near 22.5 tons of TNT). The keeper, **Cornelis Soetens**, entered that morning to take a sample, reportedly with a colleague from The Hague, and **lit a lantern** in the dark. He died in the blast, so the cause was never confirmed; the leading supposition is a **spark from his lantern**. The vivid detail of a **red cloak** he removed before entering is period tradition, not documentation — use sparingly. ([Wikipedia: Delft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delft); [essentialvermeer](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/donderslag.html))

The **north-east / Doelen quarter was flattened**. Streets named among the devastated include the **Doelenstraat, Verwersdijk, and Geerweg**; the **Schuttersdoelen** (militia hall and shooting-ground) was destroyed. Roughly **200 houses were razed outright and ~300 more damaged**. **Fire followed the blast** and did much of the harm to the partly-timber housing stock. **Stained glass in both the Oude Kerk and Nieuwe Kerk was blown out.** The report was heard as far as **Texel**, about 150 km away. ([essentialvermeer](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/donderslag.html); [lensonleeuwenhoek](https://lensonleeuwenhoek.net/content/delft-thunderclap-fire-following-explosion-killed-1200-people-and-destroyed-much-delft); [Wikipedia: Delft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delft))

The **States of Holland voted 100,000 guilders** toward relief; roof tiles were distributed (some, notoriously, later resold at inflated prices). Individual compensation is documented — Vermeer's mother, Digna Baltens, received 60 guilders for damage to her inn "Mechelen." A new magazine by Pieter Post was rebuilt outside the walls, and Delft was largely rebuilt within twenty years in brick and stone. ([essentialvermeer](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/donderslag.html))

**The death toll is genuinely unknown.** Conservative accounts say "more than 100 killed, thousands injured"; mid-range sources say "several hundred"; popular high-end claims reach ~1,000–1,200 (least reliable). Whole households vanished and many bodies were never found or identified. The honest, usable position: **nobody knew the true number.** ([essentialvermeer](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/donderslag.html); [lensonleeuwenhoek](https://lensonleeuwenhoek.net/content/delft-thunderclap-fire-following-explosion-killed-1200-people-and-destroyed-much-delft); [Wikipedia: Delft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delft))

Chroniclers of the ruin for a scribe character: **Dirck van Bleyswijck**, Delft historian and eyewitness (his *Beschryvinge der Stadt Delft*), judged it worse than the great fire of 1536. Painters **Egbert van der Poel** and **Daniel Vosmaer** made repeated views of the wrecked city. The **apple-clutching baby** found alive a day later is beloved tradition, not fact — the sort of miracle-tale a stricken town tells itself. ([essentialvermeer](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/donderslag.html); [National Gallery](https://www.nationalgallery.org.uk/paintings/egbert-van-der-poel-a-view-of-delft-after-the-explosion-of-1654))

## Carel Fabritius

Born February 1622 (baptised 27 Feb), **died in the explosion aged 32**. A **pupil of Rembrandt** in Amsterdam in the early 1640s, first trained as a carpenter (hence "Fabritius," from *faber*); married twice, secondly to **Agatha van Pruyssen** (1650). He moved to Delft around 1650 and **joined the Guild of St Luke in 1652**. He is regarded as the one Rembrandt pupil to develop a genuinely independent style — **light, spatial and perspective experiment, cool tonality** — a bridge toward Vermeer and Pieter de Hooch. Reportedly (via Houbraken) he died **at his easel working on a portrait**, with his pupil **Mathias Spoors** and the sitter **Simon Decker**. Only about a dozen paintings survive, the explosion having destroyed his studio and most of his output; among survivors is **_The Goldfinch_ (*Het puttertje*), 1654**, painted the year he died. ([Wikipedia: Carel Fabritius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carel_Fabritius); [Mauritshuis](https://www.mauritshuis.nl/en/our-collection/our-masters/carel-fabritius))

## Delft c. 1650s

A walled town of roughly **22,000–24,000** people, dense and unequal. It was laid out around old canals — **Oude Delft** (the oldest street/canal), **Nieuwe Delft**, the **Burgwal, Verwersdijk, and Brabantse Turfmarkt** — with a grid of **grachten** throughout. The **Markt**, one of Europe's larger market squares, anchored civic life between the **Stadhuis** and the **Nieuwe Kerk** (burial church of the House of Orange); the older **Oude Kerk**, with its leaning tower, stood on the Oude Delft. Street names came from trades: **Verwersdijk** = dyers (*verwers*), **Voldersgracht** = cloth fullers (*volders*). The **Doelenkwartier** in the north-east held the militia halls and shooting-grounds — the quarter obliterated in 1654. ([essentialvermeer maps](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/maps/delft/delft_in_vermeer's_time.html); [Britannica: Delft](https://www.britannica.com/place/Delft); [essentialvermeer: Voldersgracht](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/voldersgracht.html))

The economy was turning. Delft's once-**~200 breweries** had collapsed toward ~20, hurt by polluted canal water, taxes, and Rotterdam's competition; the empty brewery halls were being **repurposed for pottery**. **Delftware** (tin-glazed earthenware) was rising fast — spurred by the VOC's porcelain trade and disruption of Chinese exports — from about 11 potteries in 1640 toward 28 by 1670. Delft was one of the six VOC chambers. ([Aronson](https://www.aronson.com/city-of-delft-seventeenth-eighteenth-centuries/); [delftsaardewerk](https://delftsaardewerk.nl/en/learn/6691-the-city-of-delft-in-the-seventeenth-and-eighteenth-centuries))

Religion was ordered by the **Reformed (Calvinist) public church**, which owned both churches; only Reformed worship was public. **Catholics were tolerated in private** and used **schuilkerken** (hidden churches, no street entrance, no bell, no exterior sign) — Delft had three by ~1650, two in the **Bagijnhof** and one Jesuit house on the Oude Langendijk. The Republic had **no king and, in 1654, no stadtholder** — the First Stadtholderless Period (1650–1672). ([essentialvermeer: churches](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/oude_and_nieuwe.html); [essentialvermeer: Catholic](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/catholic_church.html); [Schuilkerk, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schuilkerk))

## The Guild of St Luke

Chartered **29 May 1611**, housed on the **Voldersgracht**, the Sint-Lucasgilde was a **broad craft guild** — painters, sculptors, delftware potters and pottery-painters, glassmakers, tapestry workers, faience-makers, booksellers and art dealers — governed by a board of six (two potters, two glass-artists, two painters). To join you had to be a **poorter** (enrolled burgher) and pay an entrance fee: **12 guilders** if born outside Delft, 6 if native, 3 for a master's son. **Membership was required to run a shop, sell to the public, or take apprentices.** Apprenticeship ran about **six years**; a trainee could not sign or sell his own work, which belonged to the master. Crucially for this book, **a master's widow could often continue the workshop under guild rules** — the narrow, grudging door through which Clara holds her trade. ([essentialvermeer: guild](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/saint_luke's_guild_delft.html); [Aronson: potters](https://www.aronson.com/the-delft-potters-guild-st-luke/))

## Pigments & the colour trade

Paint was **made fresh**, not bought in tubes. Dry pigment was mixed with a drying oil and **ground into paste on a flat stone slab with a hand tool, the *muller*** — **porphyry** the preferred stone for both slab and muller. **Linseed oil** was the common binder; **walnut oil** for whites, blues, and pale colours (it yellows less). Grinding took real skill and stamina — knowing how each pigment takes the oil and how far to mull it — and was usually done by apprentices or by specialist colour-sellers/grinders (the ***verfmaalder* / *verfmenger***). A widow grinding and selling colour is a plausible reconstruction, not a documented Delft case — present it as credible, not proven. ([CyPaint](https://cypaint.com/article/how-paint-was-made-in-the-1600); [Britannica: muller](https://www.britannica.com/art/muller); [essentialvermeer: palette](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/palette/palette_vermeer'_palette.html))

The economics of colour drive the plot. **Ultramarine**, ground from imported **lapis lazuli** (historically from Badakhshan), was the supreme blue and the **most expensive pigment of all — rivalling or exceeding gold by weight**. Purifying it meant crushing and **repeatedly washing** the stone for a **tiny yield (~30 g of good pigment per kg)**; it was often **specified in contracts and supplied by the patron**, and handled as a **stringy, hard-to-brush** paint. Cheaper blues: **azurite (*bergblauw*, "mountain blue")**, the common working blue, greener and prone to degrade; and **smalt** (cobalt-blue ground glass), the cheapest and most available but coarse and fading. The rest of the palette: **lead white (*loodwit*)**, the toxic workhorse ground and white, made by the stack process; **vermilion**, the brilliant mercuric red; **lead-tin yellow**, the standard opaque warm yellow; **madder / red lake** for translucent crimson glazes; and cheap, plentiful **ochres, umbers, and siennas** for the everyday tonal backbone. The rule to keep straight: earths cheap, lead white and common reds/blacks moderate, azurite dear, smalt cheap-but-poor, **ultramarine astronomically expensive** — a painter's choice of blue was an economic decision as much as an aesthetic one. ([essentialvermeer: ultramarine](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/palette/palette_ultramarine.html); [Jackson's: ultramarine](https://www.jacksonsart.com/blog/2019/11/05/the-story-of-ultramarine-blue-and-french-ultramarine/); [Jackson's palette](https://www.jacksonsart.com/blog/2023/11/20/recreating-the-colour-palette-of-johannes-vermeer/); [Daily Art Mag: lapis](https://www.dailyartmagazine.com/lapis-lazuli-pigment/))

## Money & daily texture

Currency: **1 gulden (guilder) = 20 stuivers; 1 stuiver = 8 duiten**; larger coins the **daalder** (~1.5 gulden) and **rijksdaalder** (~2.5). Wages (per year unless noted): unskilled labourer ~300 gulden; master carpenter ~1 gulden/day; skilled craftsman 400–600; a professional painter ~1,150–1,400. Everyday prices (c. 1650–60): white bread (1 kg) 2–3 st.; beer a pint 1–1½ st.; butter a pound 6–8 st.; beef or mutton 4–6 st. a pound; a herring ~2 st.; a modest genre painting 6–80 gulden; a small Delft house 800–1,500 gulden; a craftsman's annual rent 60–120. Treat these as well-grounded ballparks, not penny-exact. ([lensonleeuwenhoek: guilder](https://lensonleeuwenhoek.net/content/what-was-guilder-worth); [essentialvermeer: economy](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/references/vermeer-economy.html))

The street was a working, reeking place: **canals were open drains** (dyers' runoff on the Verwersdijk, fullers' waste on the Voldersgracht), thick with brewery effluent, fish, tanneries, horse dung. Expect **peat/turf smoke** (the fuel of most homes, sold at the Turfmarkt), brackish canal water, tar and rope from boat-repair, malt, herring and cheese. Food was **rye and wheat bread, cheese, butter, herring and fish, peas and beans, hutspot**, washed down with weak **small beer** (water being suspect); **tea and coffee were only just arriving and still rare in 1654**. Dress was **sober, dark, high-collared** wool with white linen — expensive and often willed. The mid-century sat in a **cold phase of the Little Ice Age**: hard winters, canals freezing solid, stormier autumns. For 12 October, keep it **cool, grey or crisp, damp, blustery** — never summer-warm. Funerals were frequent and, for the Reformed, plain; the dead were laid out at home, burial in or around the churches. After the Thunderclap the town faced **mass, hasty burial of the unidentifiable**. Between towns people travelled by **trekschuit** (the slow, cheap, punctual horse-drawn canal barge); within Delft, on foot or by small boat, goods by barge. ([essentialvermeer: Voldersgracht](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/voldersgracht.html); [Little Ice Age, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Little_Ice_Age); [essentialvermeer: trekschuit](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/travelling-in-the-netherlands.html))

## Period vocabulary (a compact glossary)

Spelling was unstandardised in 1654; variants existed.

- **gracht** — town canal (pl. *grachten*); **Markt** — market square; **Stadhuis** — town hall
- **Doelen / Schuttersdoelen** — militia hall and shooting-range; **schutterij** — the civic militia; **schutter** — a militiaman
- **Bagijnhof** — beguinage; **schuilkerk** — hidden church; **Turfmarkt** — peat market; **Vleeshal** — meat hall
- **Kruithuis** — powder house (generic); **Het Secreet van Holland** — this magazine's nickname; **Paardenmarkt** — the cleared blast site
- **poorter** — enfranchised burgher; **vroedschap** — regents' council; **burgemeester** — burgomaster; **schepen** — alderman; **weduwe** — widow
- **meester** — guild master; **leerjongen / leerling** — apprentice
- **Sint-Lucasgilde** — Guild of St Luke; **schilder** — painter; **schildersezel** — easel; **penseel** — brush
- **verf** — paint/colour; **verwer** — dyer; **verfmaalder / verfmenger** — colour-grinder / -mixer (feminine used in this book: *verfmaalster*); **volder** — cloth fuller
- **loodwit** — lead white; **bergblauw** — azurite; **smalt** — smalt; **lijnolie** — linseed oil
- **brouwer / brouwerij** — brewer / brewery; **plateelbakker** — delftware potter; **plateel** — the tin-glazed ware
- **gulden / stuiver / duit** — currency units; **daalder, rijksdaalder** — larger coins
- **trekschuit** — canal tow-barge; **trekvaart** — the tow-canal; **turf** — peat fuel; **klein bier / dun bier** — everyday small beer
- **hutspot** — stew; **haring** — herring; **stokvis** — dried cod; **de Delftse Donderslag** — "the Delft Thunderclap"

*(Vocabulary meanings are standard Dutch/period usage; street-name etymologies via [essentialvermeer: Voldersgracht](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/delft/delft_today/voldersgracht.html).)*
