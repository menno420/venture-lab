## The Story So Far

In the autumn of 1654, Clara Wijnants — a widow of thirty-four, two winters into carrying her dead husband's colour-trade on the Verwersdijk in Delft — ground and washed the costliest pigment in the world: true ultramarine, freed grain by patient grain from lapis lazuli. She kept a household of two she had not asked for: Grietje, her dead sister's daughter, eight years old, keeper of a row of small treasures — dearest of them a lump of pyrite, the gold that was not gold, worth nothing and loved the more for it — and Barent, fifteen, an almshouse boy who wanted the painters' guild so badly it came off him like heat. She kept, too, a hidden hoard: a plain grey pot heavy with years of the finest washed blue, her one way out, saved against the day a door worth it should open. And she kept a running account with Carel Fabritius, Rembrandt's boldest pupil, who owed her eight gulden and dreamed aloud at her counter of a woman by a window and a blue you could breathe — and an enemy of sorts in Willem Doncker, guild elder, patiently waiting to fold her stall away for his nephew and call it mercy.

On the twelfth of October the Secret of Holland — eighty thousand pounds of gunpowder in an old convent two streets from her door — spoke, and a quarter of Delft went into the sky. Clara dug herself out of the ruin of her own shop. She dug Grietje out from under the fallen stair: alive, hurt, and gone silent, the child's voice shut somewhere behind her eyes, not one word since. Barent she found under the fallen street wall, dead on the threshold with the beer in his hands. Fabritius she found dead at his easel in the wrecked Doelen quarter, her blue still on his fingers; from beside his body she carried away the one thing the blast had spared — the little panel of a goldfinch chained to its feeder — at the plea of his widow, Agatha: take it away from the fire, keep it where somebody will look at it the right way. The hoard-pot was smashed, the blue of years flung across the ruin and trodden into the mud.

They buried Barent fast, in the hasty ground, with too many others; no one would ever count the dead. Doncker came to the borrowed cold room where Clara and the silent child sheltered — correct, grave, sincerely sorry — to observe that a widow with no shop, no stock, and no boy would find the guild's door, which had always been leaned-to, very easy now to close; and that his offer stood. Winter came down hard over the wrecked town. The blue was in the mud. And Clara did not know how to start.

## 9. Ashes and Tiles

The ice on the water-butt had grown a finger thick in the night, and Clara broke it before the house woke, standing in the black yard in her shawl with the old woman's chopper, because the black hour before the town stirred was still the only hour that asked nothing of her, and she had never learned to sleep through it. She had spent a life meeting that hour over the porphyry slab, freeing blue out of stone by candle-light while the cold came up through the floor. Now she met it over a butt of frozen water in a yard that was not hers, breaking ice for a household whose charity wore thinner by the week, and her hands, that knew to the ounce how a muller should ride on its own weight, closed on the chopper's worn handle and did the dull work and did not complain. Complaining, like grief, was an extravagance. She had less of both to spare now than she had ever had.

She filled the two pails and stood a moment in the dark before she carried them in. The deep of the winter had come down on Delft in earnest, the hard clenched cold of the bad years, the canals frozen kerb to kerb and the snow lying grey and untrodden over the ruined quarter where nobody had the heart or the coin to clear it. From the cousin's yard on the Oude Delft she could not see the wreck, and was glad of that, and thought of it all the same, the way the tongue keeps going to the broken tooth. Two streets of rubble under the snow. And somewhere in it, trodden into the frozen mud of what had been her own back room, the blue.

She did not let herself stand long with the thought. She carried the water in.

The cold back room where they had been given leave to sleep held a bed, a stool, a chest that was not theirs, and the last grey of the fire the old woman allowed them, banked to nothing overnight so that the room by morning was scarcely warmer than the yard. Grietje slept in the middle of the bed with her knees drawn up and her mended arm held careful even in sleep, and on the sill above her the four things stood in their row, catching the first grey off the snow: the chip of blue-and-white tile, the horn button, the river pebble, and last, dearest last, the pyrite, the gold that was not gold. The child had carried them out of the wreck in one fist through all of it and set them out that first night without a sound, and set them out every night since, and it was, Clara had come to understand, the nearest thing to speech the child still allowed herself — that the row was kept, that the order held, that here at least was a small country where nothing more would be taken. Clara had stopped straightening them. They were the child's to keep. She only counted them, mornings, the way she had once counted the grey pot in the dark: to be sure.

She got the fire up with the day's grudged handful of turf and set the pea-porridge to warm, and when the child woke she woke without a word, as she woke every morning now, and lay a while watching Clara's blue hands at the fire before she rose. Clara talked into that watching, low, the way you talk to keep a hurt beast company — *there's porridge, mind your arm, the ice was thick again* — and got nothing back but the grave face; and once, only once that week, when Clara set the bowl in her lap, Grietje put out one finger and touched the back of Clara's stained hand, tracing a blue crease from knuckle to wrist the way she had done by candle-light in another life, and Clara held very still and did not spoil it, and the child took her finger back and ate her porridge, and that was all, and Clara carried it in her chest the rest of the day like a coal.

The goldfinch she kept in the chest, wrapped in the same dust-stiff rag she had carried it out in, under the folded spare shift, where the old woman would not come on it and ask. She did not take it out. She knew it was there the way she had known the grey pot was there, by putting her hand to the wrapping in the dark, and some nights that was the only thing in the room she could put her hand to and be sure of. A dead man's small bird, chained to its ring, holding still to be seen. She did not look at it. She was not yet ready to be seen by it.

Doncker came on the second day of the new year, when the cold was at its worst and the town had begun, in the way of towns, to turn from its grief toward its business. He came correct and grave as he had come to that same doorway in October, his good cloth brushed, a leather folder under his arm, and this time he was let in, because the old woman knew who he was and what he was worth and dropped him a stiff little courtesy and took herself out to leave the gentry to their affairs — and Clara understood from that alone that the household would be glad to have her off its hands, and could not even blame it.

He sat on the offered stool and refused the porridge she was bound by decency to offer, kindly, and he looked once, without seeming to, at the cold room and the mended child on the bed and the row of worthless things on the sill, and Clara watched him price it all, gently, the way he priced everything, and add it to his side of the ledger he carried in his head.

"I'll not waste your morning," he said, "with what you already know. The quarter's to be rebuilt, brick and stone, no more powder inside the walls, God be thanked, though late. The market's to be set out fresh on the Voldersgracht when the weather turns, the stalls redrawn, the rights looked into. And you know," gently, gently, "that a right looked into is a right that must answer for itself, and that yours, Clara, was never on the firmest ground even before the twelfth."

"I know the shape of it," Clara said. "You needn't draw me the whole picture. You've come to buy."

He inclined his head, as at a pupil who had learned her lesson, and opened the folder on his knee. "I've come to be of use," he said, "which you will hear as the same thing, and I'll not argue it, because you've earned the right to hear it however you like." He drew out a paper, close-written in a clerk's fair hand, and set it on the chest between them, and did not push it at her, which was its own kind of pushing. "The ground your house stood on. It's rubble and a cellar full of ice and it owes the town for its own clearing, and it will sell to no one but a man rebuilding that stretch, and I am the only man rebuilding that stretch. I'll take it off you and pay the clearing myself and give you, on top, a sum in hand." He named the sum. It was not nothing. It was a quarter of what the plot would have been worth with a house on it in October, and October would not come again. "And I'll speak for you to the board on the matter of the right — not to keep it, you'll not thank me for a lie, a widow with no shop and no stock two years past her husband cannot hold an enrolled trade and the board will not let her, my voice or no. But I can see it wound up decently. Surrendered, in good order, for a consideration, so it goes on the books as a thing given up and not a thing taken away. It will matter to the child someday, that her aunt gave a thing up rather than had it stripped from her."

"You've thought of the child," Clara said.

"I have a wife who thinks of little else since October," Doncker said, and for once there was no performance in it, and it was worse for that. "There's a place could be found for you. A decent house wanting a woman to keep it, over toward Gouda, my wife's people, good plain folk; you'd have your own room and the child schooled a little and no cold yard to break ice in before dawn. Or you'd take the money and go to your own people, if you've people left. Either way you'd be *settled*, Clara. Out of the cold. Not standing at a slab till your lungs go the way the grinders' lungs go, which you know better than I do, having ground the lead yourself these years against every warning. You needn't do this any longer. That's the whole of what I've come to say. You needn't."

And the terrible thing, the thing that had always been the trouble with Doncker, was that a great part of her wanted to lay down the chopper and say yes.

She had not slept warm in three months. She had watched a boy she loved go into the hasty ground and a child she had not asked for go silent, and buried the one and could not reach the other, and broken ice for her bread and eaten another woman's charity to the last cold spoon of it; and here on the chest was a paper that would end all of it — the cold, the debt, the guild's slow grinding no, the lead-dust in her chest — end it in the drawing of a name. A warm room. The child schooled. She looked at the paper in the grey light and felt the whole winter lean on the small of her back the way the fallen house had leaned, and thought: *I could put it down. I have carried it far enough. No one alive would say I had not carried it far enough.* She reached, and there was ink in the little horn Doncker had set out, correct man, prepared man, and a pen, and her blue hand went toward the pen the way a tired hand goes toward a warm wall.

Grietje got off the bed.

She did it without a sound, as she did everything now, sliding down with her good arm and crossing the cold floor in her shift, and Clara stopped with her hand above the pen and watched her, because you watched the child now, you did not let her out of the eye's corner. Grietje went to the sill. She reached up and took the last thing from the row, the pyrite, the gold that was not gold, and she carried it back across the room in her closed fist, and she did not give it to Clara, quite — she was too old for that and too far behind her own eyes for gestures — she only stood by Clara's knee and opened her fist on the edge of the chest, next to the paper, so that the fool's-gold sat there in the grey light with its worthless glitter, the pinch of stone Clara had picked out of a knuckle of lapis and given her, the thing the child had wept over when she learned it was worth nothing and loved the more for it. Then she looked at Clara, straight, out of Neeltje's grey eyes, and said nothing, because she had no words to give, but the thing was said all the same, laid on the chest beside the clerk's fair hand: *worth nothing, and kept anyway. That is a reason. You taught it to me.*

Clara took her hand back from the pen.

She was not certain, afterward, that the child had meant any of it; children put their treasures down where they please; it may have been only that the pyrite was cold in her fist and the chest was near. It did not matter what the child had meant. What mattered was the blue lying in the frozen mud two streets off, worth more than the plot and the porridge and Doncker's whole considered sum, worth more than the warm room in Gouda, and lying there unreclaimed because she had told herself it was gone, the way she had told herself the child's voice was gone, the way she had told herself a widow with no shop could not hold a trade. She had believed the arithmetic. She had nearly signed the arithmetic. And here was a worthless glittering thing on the chest to tell her that the arithmetic had never once, in her whole life, been the truest column in the book.

"Leave the paper," she said. "I'll not sign it today."

Doncker looked at her, and at the child, and at the pyrite, and something moved behind his correct face that might, in a smaller man, have been the beginning of understanding, and was in him only patience settling back into its long habit. "The offer stands to the first market of the spring," he said, rising, gathering the folder, leaving the paper where it lay because a paper left behind does more work than a paper carried off, and he knew it. "After that the board takes up the rights and it's out of my hands and yours both. Don't mistake stubbornness for strength, Clara. I've watched better than you break themselves on it." He set his hat on. "For the child's sake, think on the warm room. Whatever you think of me."

"I think of you exactly what you are," Clara said, without heat, "which is a man who grieves the dead sincerely and would take the living's last card all the same and see no quarrel in it. Mind the ice on the step. They've not sanded it."

He went. She heard his careful tread go down the passage and the street door, and the old woman's murmur, and then the cold ordinary morning closing over the place where he had been. Grietje had climbed back onto the bed and drawn her knees up and was watching her. The pyrite lay on the chest by the unsigned paper, glittering at nothing.

Clara picked it up. She turned it in her stained fingers a moment, the gold that was not gold, and then she reached past the child and set it back on the sill, last in the row, dearest last, where it belonged, and the child watched her do it and something in the small grave face eased, a fraction, no more, but Clara had learned to read a colour by less than a fraction and she read this one true. Then she went to the window and scraped a clear place in the frost with her thumbnail and looked out over the frozen roofs toward the quarter she could not quite see, where the snow lay grey over two streets of rubble, and she thought, for the first time since the white had taken the day, not *how does a person begin again from nothing* but a plainer thing, a working thing, a thing with her hands already in it: *the blue is in the mud. And the mud will not stay frozen forever. And I know, better than anyone left alive in this town, how to wash a colour up out of the dross it is bound to.*

She did not sign the paper. She did not throw it out either. She left it on the chest, where the sight of it would remind her, every cold morning, exactly what she was choosing against.

## 10. Blue in the Mud

She went back on the first day the thaw loosened the ground, and took the child with her, because she could not leave her in the cold room with the old woman's growing silence, and because some hard sure instinct told her that whatever she was going to do out there in the ruin, the child ought to see it done.

They came at the wreck of the Verwersdijk from the town side, over the makeshift plank bridges the labourers had thrown across the choked canals, and Clara had to stand a while at the edge of what had been her street before she could go into it. The thaw had left the ruin blacker and truer than the kind grey snow had let it be: two streets of tumbled brick and charred beam running down toward the Doelen, a well-head standing whole in the waste, a green door she remembered from October still upright against nothing, and over all of it the raw smell of wet ash and turned earth and a sweetish under-smell she did not name, for the child's sake and her own. Men worked here and there, clearing, carting; a surveyor and a boy stood plotting the new brick line of a house that had been somebody's grandfather's. Nobody minded a widow and a child picking their way in. There were a great many such, that winter, come back to poke at the place a life had stood.

She found her own ground by the cracked slab.

She had feared she would not know the place at all, one heap being the twin of the next, but the slab told her. Gerrit's porphyry slab, dark red and dished by two men's working, lay where the blast had flung it against the one surviving scrap of the back wall, cracked clean across into two halves and half-sunk in the frozen muck, and Clara stood over it and knew her own house the way you know a face in a coffin, by the bones under the ruin. Here had been the shop. Here the counter, gone to splinters. Here the low cupboard, burst; and there, in the black churned mud between the broken slab and the wall, was where the grey pot had stood, and where the white had thrown its blue across the room in a long stain in the hour the world ended.

She knelt down in the mud and began.

There is no dignity in it and she had expected none. She had brought what she needed in a basket like a woman going to market: a horn spoon, a knife, two shallow pans, a jug, a stoppered bottle of clean water carried across the whole town, and rags. She scraped the top muck away where the blue had fallen — she remembered the fall of it exactly, the eye that had spent a life adding up colour giving her now the one map that mattered — and worked down into the frozen crumb of the mud there was, faint, unmistakable to her and to no one else on earth, the blue. Not a hoard. Not the deep still cloud of it in the pot. A staining, a scattering, grains of the finest ultramarine ground grain by grain across years and flung by the powder into a yard of filthy earth, trodden by the diggers' boots, frozen and thawed, mixed now with brick-dust and ash and the pale calcite of its own crushing. To any other eye it was dirt. To hers it was the first water off the stone, the richest and the least, everything and nearly nothing, waiting to be freed.

So she freed it. She did the only thing she knew how to do, the thing she had done ten thousand mornings and taught to a boy now in the ground and a child now at her side: she took up the fouled blue mud a spoonful at a time and stirred it into water in the shallow pan, and swirled it, and watched. And the old law held, the law that did not care that the world had ended: the heavy dross fell fast. The brick-grit and the ash and the coarse calcite went down at once, and above them, hanging longest in the clouding water, the exact way she had shown the child by one candle a lifetime ago, hung the finest and the truest and the slowest to fall — the blue. She poured the top water off, at the breath of the right moment, carrying the best of it into the second pan and leaving the ruin behind, and did it again, and again, the whole patient freeing of one thing from all it was bound to, and slowly, wash on wash, spoonful on frozen spoonful, in a pan balanced on a cracked slab in the wreck of her own house, the blue came up out of the mud.

She did not weep, though it was the place to weep if there was ever a place. Her hands had stopped being cold. That was the strange mercy of the work: it took her, as it had always taken her, down below thought to a place where there was only the swirl and the settling and the pouring-off, the muller-rhythm of the wrists though there was no muller in her hands, and for whole minutes together she was not a widow in a ruin but a *verfmaalster* at her trade, freeing blue — the one country in all the world the white had not been able to take from her, because it lived in her hands and not in the house.

She had been at it perhaps an hour, and the child had drifted nearer through the whole of it, silent, watchful, crouching at last at the pan's edge with her mended arm held careful against her, when Clara's cold-stiffened fingers betrayed her and she went to pour the second wash a heartbeat too soon.

"Not yet," Grietje said.

Clara stopped with the pan tilted. The words had come small and hoarse and flat, a voice unused for three months, a rusted hinge of a voice, and for one moment Clara did not trust her ears, that had lied to her before under the ringing. But the child was looking at the pan, only at the pan, frowning at it with her whole grave face the way she frowned at the treasures on the sill, and she said it again, plainer, cross almost, a craftsman correcting a fumble: "Not yet. The best is slowest. You said." And she reached out with her good hand and put two fingers on the rim of the pan and held it level, held it back from the pouring, until the deep of the blue had hung and settled the fraction more it needed, and only then took her fingers away and let Clara pour.

Clara poured. The blue went over into the pan clean and did not spill a grain. And then she set the pan down very carefully on the cracked slab, because her hands had begun to shake and she would not shake with a pan of the true blue in them, and she looked at the child crouched in the wreck of the house with the winter light on her, and she did not gather her up and she did not weep on her and she did not say any of the enormous things rising in her, because the child had come back through a door held open by exactly one narrow thing, the work, the pan, the blue, and Clara understood with the whole of her hard-schooled heart that if she flooded her now with feeling she would frighten her back behind her eyes. So she did the one right thing. She picked up the horn spoon and held it out.

"Then you pour the next," she said, steady as she could make it. "You've the better eye for the moment. You always did."

The child took the spoon. And they knelt together in the ruin of the shop, the widow and the child who was not hers and was, and washed the blue up out of the mud grain by grain, hand over hand across a pan of dirty water, the way it had to be done, the way craft goes down a family or dies, and the child said the small things of the trade as they came to her, the words swimming back one by one out of wherever they had gone — *slow now; that's the dross; pour* — and Clara answered her plainly, in the same register, trade to trade, and did not once say *you spoke, you spoke, thank God you spoke,* though it beat in her the whole afternoon like a second heart.

Agatha found them there in the low sun.

Clara did not know how long she had stood at the edge of the ground before she spoke; the work took you down and you lost the world. She was thinner than October, and greyer, and dressed in the plain deep black that meant her people had found her a widow's clothes at least, and she stood at the broken threshold looking at the two of them crouched over their pans, and Clara rose stiffly to meet her, blue to the wrist, and the two women stood a moment in the ruin with nothing to say that the ruin had not already said. They had knelt across a dead man together. There was no starting smaller than that.

"They told me a woman was digging in the Verwersdijk wreck for pigment," Agatha said at last. "Washing dirt for colour. I thought — I knew it was you. Nobody else in this town would think there was anything left in the ground worth the having." She looked at the pan on the slab, the deep blue settling in it, and something crossed her worn face that was not quite a smile and was the nearest thing to one Clara would see on it. "He'd have liked that. That you came and dug his blue out of the mud. He always said you did the harder half."

"It's my blue," Clara said. "He never paid for it." And it was not unkind and Agatha did not take it unkindly; it was only true, the ledger standing plain between them the way ledgers do, and there was a rough comfort in the plainness, two widows counting what was owed and what was lost and not pretending either away.

Agatha had come, it turned out, to give the bird up for good. She was leaving Delft — her people were in Rotterdam, and there was nothing to hold her in a town that had killed her husband and buried his life's work under a burning wall — and a hundred times she had thought she ought to take the little goldfinch panel with her, the one thing spared, and found that she could not. "It's the last thing he saw finished," she said. "I can't have it on a wall to look at over my beer for forty years. And I can't sell it into some regent's stack to hang between his ships and his dead pheasants, to a man who'll never once ask if the bird was seen true." She looked at Clara steadily. "You saved it out of the fire. You knew what it was when you'd have left every other thing to burn. Keep it. Not to sell. Keep it where somebody comes who'll look at it the right way, a long time, before they touch anything. There'll be such a one, now and again, even here. Let him find it at yours."

And Clara, who had carried the bird against her heart out of the fire and could not yet bear to be looked at by it, found that she could bear this: to keep it not as a relic but as a thing set out for the ones who came to see. She said she would. Agatha did not thank her, which was right; you did not thank a person for a weight, you only shifted it to hands that could carry it. She looked once more at the child, at the mended arm and the blue hands and the spoon, and said, "She's grinding already," half a question.

"Washing," said Grietje, from the mud, not looking up. "You wash before you grind. Everyone knows that." It was the longest thing she had said in three months, and it was pure Neeltje, the flat certainty, the *everyone knows,* and Clara had to turn and look hard at the pan for a moment, and Agatha's worn mouth did move then, it did, before she gathered her black about her and picked her way back out of the ruin toward the living town and the barge to Rotterdam, and Clara never saw her again, and thought of her often, and hoped, without much faith in the hoping, that Rotterdam had been kinder to her than Delft.

They worked till the light went. And near the end, when the low sun had turned the frozen canal to a sheet of dull rose, a man came picking his way along the ruined stretch and stopped at her ground — a young man, quiet, plainly dressed, whom she knew a little: the painter from over on the Oude Langendijk, who had married the Bolnes girl and taken his oath in the guild the Christmas before the blast, who bought less colour than he wanted and looked at it a long time before he touched it. He stood at the edge of her wreck with his hat in his hand, and did not offer help, which she was grateful for, and did not gape.

"I heard you'd — " he began, and stopped, and looked instead at the pans, at the blue settling in them in the rose light, and his slow careful eyes went over it the way they went over a colour on her boards, weighing, and Clara saw the want come up in him, the honest craftsman's want, quiet and deep. "Is that the true stone," he said. "Ultramarine. That you've — washed out of the ground."

"It's the true stone," Clara said. "Come back in the spring, meester. When I've a slab to grind on and a roof to stand under. I'll have blue then, the best in Delft, if you can pay for it." She had not planned to say it. It came out of her plain and certain, the way *not yet* had come out of the child, and she heard in it a promise she was making not to him but to herself: *There'll be a spring, and I'll be standing in it, trading colour on my own terms in this town that tried to grind me into the ground with the rest.* The young man looked at her, and looked at the blue, and something passed over his quiet face that she would only understand the size of much later, and he said he would come back in the spring, and put his hat on, and went, careful, the way he did everything.

Clara knelt in the last of the light with the reclaimed blue safe in its bottle and the child asleep against a fallen beam, worn out with the first real work of her life, and she looked at the wreck of everything she had built going blue and violet as the dusk came down. She had lost the shop and the slab and the boy and the man and the whole of her old life in the white. She had a bottle of blue washed out of the mud, and a child's voice come back over a pan of dirty water, and a promise made to a quiet young painter and to herself. It was not much to begin again from. It was more than she had had that morning. She woke the child, gently, and they went home over the plank bridges through the deepening dark, and Clara carried the reclaimed blue against her heart where the bird had been.

## 11. The Widow's Blue

The board of the Guild of St Luke sat in judgment on the widow's right on a raw morning at the tail of the winter, in the guild's own house on the Voldersgracht, which the blast had cracked but not felled, and Clara came before it with her reclaimed blue in a stoppered pot in her basket and the child's hand in hers and Gerrit's surviving muller wrapped in a cloth, and set none of them down, and waited to be told whether she might go on being what she was.

She had brought the muller for a reason she could not have named to Doncker and did not try to. It had come up out of the mud whole — the loaf of dark porphyry that had fitted her palms ten thousand mornings, the slab cracked in two under the fallen house but the muller, being small and hard and made to take a beating, thrown clear and unbroken. She had washed it and it fitted her hand as it always had, cool and heavy and hers, and she had found she could not come before the men who would judge her trade without the tool of it against her side, the way another woman might not go into a hard room without her mother's ring on her finger. It steadied her. Let them see she came with the thing itself in her hand and not with a paper about the thing.

There were six of them at the long table, two potters, two glass-men, two painters, as the charter had it, and Doncker was not of them — he was a merchant and an elder, not a board-man that year — but he was in the room, correct and grave against the wall with the younger guild-men, there to see his patience come to its harvest, and Clara marked him and did not look at him again. The senior of the painters, a heavy old man named Vromans who had known Gerrit and bought colour from Clara for two years and paid late and honest, read the matter out flatly: that the widow Wijnants had held her late husband's enrolled trade by the widow's leave these two years and more; that the said trade was now without shop, stock, or standing, the premises destroyed in the late calamity; that the question before the board was whether the leave might continue or must lapse, the market being redrawn and the rights looked into.

Then he asked her, not unkindly, what she had to say for it.

She had thought a long time about what she had to say for it, in the cold room, over the reclaimed blue, and in the end she said very little, because she had learned across a life that a woman pleading was a woman half-lost in such a room, and that the men would forgive her many things sooner than they would forgive her a scene. She set the pot on the table. She unstoppered it. She turned it so the deep still blue caught the grey light from the cracked window, the truest ultramarine in Delft, and she let it sit there a moment in front of six men who had spent their lives knowing to the stuiver what such a blue was worth, before she said a word.

"That's the true stone," she said. "Purified, washed, ready to grind. There's not another pot of it this side of Amsterdam, and there'll not be, because the men who could make it are dead in the Doelen or scattered, and the stock in every shop in this quarter went into the mud on the twelfth of October, and I am the one grinder left in Delft who has any, because I washed it up out of that mud myself, grain by grain, on my knees, this past month, off the ground my house stood on." She let that sit too. "You've painters in this town who'll want it. The deacons' portraits will want the loodwit and the black, but the men who matter, the ones who'll be remembered, they'll want the blue, and they'll want it ground fine and washed clean by somebody who knows the trade to the bone, and there is one such somebody left, and she is standing in front of you asking leave to go on being what she has been these eleven years without costing this guild a stuiver or a scandal." She stoppered the pot. "That's what I've to say for it. Not that you owe a widow a kindness. That you need a grinder, and I am the grinder, and it would be a poor sort of good order that closed the last colour-house in the quarter to keep a rule tidy."

There was a silence, and then the men did what men do, which was to argue the thing among themselves as though she were not in the room. It was Doncker's ground and Doncker had prepared it, and his side of it came out of the younger men's mouths dressed as principle: that a right was a right and could not be bent to a hard case however sad; that if a widow with no shop might hold a trade on the strength of a pot of blue, then the door the guild had always only leaned-to was thrown wide, and every craftsman's relict from here to the Schie would claim the same; that it was not order. And it was true, and she had known it would be said, and she had no answer to it that was not the pot on the table, and the pot on the table was, she saw, not going to be enough by itself, because the men who feared for the rule feared it more than they wanted the blue.

It was the young painter who tipped it, though he never spoke, and did not know he had.

He was not of the board and had no standing to speak and did not try to, but he had come — she saw him at the back, quiet, his hat in his hands — and when the wrangle had run a while and old Vromans, tired of it, asked the room plainly whether any master present would stand surety that the widow's blue was worth the keeping of her, the young man from the Oude Langendijk put up his hand. Not to make a speech. He said only, in his slow careful way, that he had seen the colour she had washed out of the ruin, with his own eyes, in the wreck of her house, and that he had ordered blue of her for a painting he meant to make in the spring, and that he would put his name to it that there was no finer in the province and no one else he would trust to grind it, and that a guild that let such a maker go for want of a rule bent once would be the poorer in twenty years and would not know why. Then he sat down, plainly wishing he had not had to stand, and Clara understood that he had staked something of his own new small standing to say it, a man not a year in the guild speaking up against an elder, and she did not forget it.

They put her out while they settled it, into the cold passage, and she stood there with the child and the muller and the pot and did not let her face give anything away, and at the far end of the passage Doncker came and stood with her, because he could not help himself, because a righteous man must be seen to be kind at the last.

"You'll not win it clean, whatever they do," he said, low. "You know that. Even if they let you stand, they'll not give you the market-right, not the good stall on the new Voldersgracht — that's promised, that's my nephew's, and the board will not turn a promise over for a widow's blue. And they'll bind you. Watch. They'll let you grind and let you sell, because you've frightened them with the pot, but they'll bind you to terms a man wouldn't take, and call it a mercy, and you'll thank them for it because the other side of it is nothing." He looked at her, and there was no triumph in it, which was the worst of him, only a kind of weary certainty. "I'd have seen you settled warm, Clara. Remember that I offered it, when you're standing at a slab at sixty with the lead in your chest."

"I'll remember you offered," Clara said. "I'll remember what it cost, too, that you called it settling. Go and collect your nephew's stall, Willem. You've won the ground and the market and the good stall on the new street. Let me have the leave to grind, and you can have all the rest, and we'll both call it what it is when we're alone, which is that you got nine parts of ten and grudged me the tenth."

Something went across his face, and for once it was not patience. He was quiet a moment. "Gerrit was my friend," he said, "before he was your husband. I've not enjoyed any of this. You've never once believed that."

"I've always believed it," Clara said. "That's the whole trouble with you. You've meant every kindness and taken every stall all the same, and never once felt the two of them touch. I don't hate you, meester. I'd have had an easier winter if I could." And that was true, and it landed truer than any anger, and Doncker looked at her a moment longer and then inclined his head, correct as a deacon even in that cold passage, and went back in, and did not speak to her again.

They gave her the leave. Doncker had read it exactly, as he read everything: they upheld the widow's right to continue Gerrit's enrolled trade — grinding and the sale of colour — but they gave her nothing else and bound the leave in three cold ropes. She would have no place on the new market; the Voldersgracht stalls were drawn and hers was not among them; she might sell only from a shop, and must find and hold one within the year or the leave lapsed. She would pay a fee for the continuance, a sum set high enough to sting, a bond against the trouble she was held to represent. And she would supply the guild's own painters and the guild's own panels, the town-work, at a rate the board fixed, below what the trade would bear, in exchange for the leave to sell the rest as she pleased — so that she bought her freedom, in the end, by grinding a part of every year for the guild at a loss, a leash worn as a livery. It was a hard bargain and a narrow one and it left her poorer standing than she had been the morning of the twelfth, with a shop still to find and a fee still to pay and a year to do it in.

And it was a victory, and she knew it for one, walking out into the raw noon with the child and the muller and the pot, because she walked out of that house still a *verfmaalster* of Delft in her own right and her own name, unmarried, unsettled, answerable to no man's roof, free to grind the finest blue in the province and sell it to whom she chose, which was the one thing Doncker had wanted to take and had not taken. She had lost the ground and the stall and the warm room and a great deal of the money there was to lose, and she had kept the tenth part, the tenth part that was the trade itself, the standing at the slab, the door held open by a widow's stubbornness and a pot of reclaimed blue and one quiet young man's raised hand. It was not a rout of Doncker. It was a thing bought dear and held narrow. It was enough.

"Did we win?" Grietje asked her, on the plank bridge over the frozen Voldersgracht, in her hoarse recovering voice that Clara was still not used to and drank in every time it came.

"We kept the trade," Clara said. "We didn't get the good stall. We've a fee to pay and a shop to find and a hard year to do it in, and I'll be grinding the guild's umber half for nothing till I'm grey."

The child considered this with her whole grave face. "But we kept the trade."

"We kept the trade."

"Then we won," Grietje said, with Neeltje's flat certainty, closing the account, and Clara looked down at her, the mended arm, the blue hands, the voice come back over a pan of mud, and thought that the child had read the ledger truer than she had, the way the child had read the pyrite truer, and she took the small blue hand in her own blue hand and they went home to the cold room to begin the hard year, and it was, though neither of them said so, the first day since the twelfth of October that either of them had walked anywhere toward a thing instead of away from one.

## 12. The Colour That Remains

By the spring she had a shop, of a kind, and a slab to grind on, and the young painter came for his blue on a fair evening in May with the light going long and gold over a canal that men were already mending stone by stone, and Clara ground the reclaimed ultramarine for him on Gerrit's muller and a new-bought slab and did not grieve, much, that the old slab was not under her hands.

The shop was two low rooms off the Nieuwe Langendijk that she held on a year's lease and a hard bargain, well back from the ruin, damp, dark, cheap, and hers; the ground floor for the trade and the slab, the room above for the two of them to sleep dry at last. She had paid the guild's fee with the last of what she had and a little she had borrowed against the blue, and she had bought the new slab second-hand from a dead potter's widow, a plain grey stone, not porphyry, not Gerrit's, dished by no working but her own to come; and she had set Gerrit's surviving muller on it the first morning and put her palms to the cool weight of it and begun to grind, and the rhythm had come up out of her wrists unchanged, below thought, the one inheritance the white had left her whole. She ground the guild's umber at a loss in the mornings, as bound, and cursed it, and did it clean all the same, because a grudged colour ground gritty was a colour that gave Doncker a stick. In the afternoons she ground what she pleased and sold it to whom she pleased, and word had gone about the trade, in the way word goes, that the widow Wijnants had the true blue when no one else in Delft had a grain of it, blue washed up out of the ruin itself, and they came for it, the painters, quietly, one at a time, and she made them pay, and the ledger, kept in her own hand now with Gerrit's dead crossings-out on the older pages still, began, month by hard month, to lean a little less the wrong way.

The child ground beside her. Grietje's arm had knit true, and by spring she could take the small muller in both hands and set it going on the second slab, light and slow, the child's version of the woman's stroke, and she ground the earths that forgave a beginner and washed the blue that did not, and she talked while she worked, all day, the words all come back now and more arriving, so that the shop was full of her voice the way the old shop had been, the tuneless singing, the flat certainties, the questions out of nowhere. She was not the child she had been before the white; Clara did not fool herself that any of them were what they had been. There were nights the child woke with the sound of it in her ears and would not say what she had dreamed, and mornings she went to the sill first thing to count the four things and be sure, the tile, the button, the pebble, the gold that was not gold, before she would come to her porridge. The white had gone down into her and would be down there always, a coarse grain that would never quite wash out. But the finest of her had settled back, the best particles, the slowest to fall — the frank greed for anything that shone, the tenderness, the voice for singing that was Neeltje's and not Clara's, thank God — and Clara had learned to be grateful for what settled and not to grieve too long what was lost in the pouring, which was, she thought, the whole of the trade, and the whole of raising a child, and perhaps the whole of living past a thing that should have killed you, all one lesson, learned over and over at a pan of dirty water.

The goldfinch hung in the shop, in the good north light by the window, where a person coming for colour would see it.

She had not sold it and would not, and she had stopped being unable to look at it. It hung there small and whole in its pale warm-and-cool light, the loose dragged paint that was hardly paint at all up close and was wholly a living bird a step back, the thin chain running from the ring to the band about its leg, and it did what Agatha had said it would do: now and again, a person came for a paper of ochre and saw it, and stopped, and looked a long time before they said anything, and those were the ones Clara marked, the ones who looked before they touched. Most did not. Most bought their umber and never saw the bird at all, and that was the world, and she did not hold it against them. But the panel drew the seeing ones the way the blue drew the good painters, and the shop became, without her quite meaning it, a place in the wounded town where a certain kind of looking still went on, and she thought that was as near as a dead man got to going on: that the thing he made kept teaching the seeing after the seer was in the ground.

The young painter came for his blue on the fair May evening, and this time he had money, and paid it, honest coin counted slow, and did not ask for credit, and Clara ground him the true ultramarine finer than she ground it for anyone, walnut oil for the blue as it should be, and watched him watch her do it. He had come, he said at last, awkward, looking at the bird on the wall as much as at her, for a painting he was going to make. A woman, he said. By a window. With the light coming across her, morning light, off to the side, the way it falls and models a face and a wall and the air between them; and a blue in it, in the cloth, in the shadow of the cloth, a blue no one had put there so before, a blue you could — he stopped, having run out past what he could say, being a man who did his saying with his hands and not his mouth, and Clara stood very still with the muller in her palms.

"A blue you could breathe," she said.

He looked up. "Yes," he said. "How — yes. Exactly that."

She did not tell him. She could have told him that a dead man had stood at another counter in another shop that no longer existed, a lifetime and half a mile away, and wanted the same thing in the same words, a woman by a window with the light coming across and a blue in the shadow of the cloth that no one had put there before, a blue you could breathe, and had never got to make it, because the light he chased his whole life had come through his north wall one grey Monday and the wall had come down on him in it. She could have told the young man that he was going to make the painting the dead man had carried unmade into the rubble, without knowing he carried it, the way water carries the finest colour it does not know it holds. But there was nothing in the telling for him, and a good deal that might weigh him, and the dead do not need the living to speak their names over every colour; they need only the colour ground true and put where the light can find it. So she said none of it. She wrapped the blue in a twist of paper, and, because she was a fool, and because the light was going long and gold in the window and the bird held still on the wall to be seen, she weighed out a little more than he had paid for and folded that in too, and did not mention it.

"That's more than I bought," he said, who had the colourman's eye for it, the way the boy had used to.

"It's what you bought," Clara said. "Make the woman by the window. Get the blue right." And then, because it was owed, because a debt is a debt and this one had come down to her across the whole ruin to pay: "There was a man in this town could have told you how to see it. He's dead. You'll have to find it yourself, in the dark, which is where the seeing's done. But you've the blue now that he'd have wanted, the true stone, the best I have. Don't waste it on a wall nobody breathes near. Put it where the light comes across." He looked at her, and at the bird, and something passed over his quiet careful face that she thought she understood at last the size of, and he said he would, and put his hat on, and went out into the long gold evening with her blue over his heart, two rooms from a canal that had no powder anywhere near it now, and Clara stood in the door of her poor shop and watched him go the way she had once watched another man go, and did not, this time, wish she had not looked.

Grietje came and stood in the door with her, drying her blue hands on the rag.

The evening had come down to the blue hour, the little while at the edge of dark that the painters cursed and could not do without, when the gold goes out of the sky by degrees and every colour on the earth deepens and stands true a moment before the night takes it. The mended stretch of canal lay still and held the last of the light, blue on blue, the new stone of the mended quay and the old stone of the standing houses all gone one deepening colour together, and the tower of the Nieuwe Kerk stood black against a sky the exact grave blue that no man could grind and every man tried to, the blue you could breathe, going deeper as they watched. Somewhere a woman called a child in. The water lay unmoving. It was, Clara thought, a poor light to sell colour by and the truest light there was to see by, and she had lived her whole life in it and been too busy breaking stone to be grateful for it, and she was grateful for it now, standing in the door of a shop that was not the shop she had lost, with a child that was not the child she had been given and was, past all arguing, hers.

The cost was not paid off and would not be. Barent was in the hasty ground and would stay there, a boy who had wanted the guild so badly it came off him like heat, who had gone from wanting everything to nothing between one cold breath and the next, and there was no blue hour that gave him back, and Clara would carry him unmended to her own grave, which was the honest price and she did not pretend it away. The town was two years into a twenty years' rebuilding and the ruin still ran black under the snow-melt across the quarter, and no one would ever count the dead, and the not-counting was a wound the town would wear under its new brick forever. The white had taken more than it could ever give back, and beauty, she knew better than anyone left alive, was made by breaking, and cost more than it ever quite returned, and was worth the making all the same, which was the last hard thing the trade had to teach and she had spent a life learning and was only now, in the blue hour, in the door of the poor new shop, ready to say she believed.

"Bluest hands in Delft," Grietje said, holding hers up in the last of it, the old game, the child's whole small greedy joy in the mark of the trade, and Clara took the small stained hand in her own stained hand, blue to the creases and blue in the coffin, and did not correct her, because it was true, the two of them, and because the light was almost gone.

"Bluest hands in Delft," she said. They stood in the door and watched the blue go out of the water and the sky come down, and behind them in the dark shop the little bird held still on the wall in a light of its own making, chained to its ring, waiting to be seen; and above the mended canal the last of the day drained slow and even and without any flattery in it, the colourless Delft light gone at the end all to colour, deepening and deepening, until it was too dark to work by and exactly bright enough to see.
