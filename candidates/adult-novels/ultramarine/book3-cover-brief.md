# Cover Brief — *The Common Blue* (Ultramarine, Book 3)

> Production direction only. Cover-only production gate (adult text title, no
> character art required). Any art spend is an **owner decision** — this brief
> commissions nothing. Consistent with the literary/historical look of Books 1
> and 2: a single luminous pigment-and-material image, no faces, the Dutch Golden
> Age palette used as the book's own metaphor. This is the **trilogy-closing**
> volume; the cover should read as the third and final panel of a matched set.

## Spec

- **Trim / format:** reflowable ebook; cover **1600×2560 px min** (KDP),
  series-consistent with Books 1 and 2. Optional 5×8 or 5.25×8 paperback later;
  not in first-release scope.
- **Series line on cover:** *Ultramarine · Book Three* (or, if a series name is
  adopted, *A Delft Novel · Book Three*). **Title:** *The Common Blue.*
- **Subtitle (metadata, optional on cover):** *A Novella of Delft.*

## Palette

- Book 1 sold **ultramarine against dark** (the ground stone-blue in shadow);
  Book 2 turned on the **fired blue-and-white** (pigment fired into light). Book 3
  keeps the deep Delft blue and the chalk-white of tin-glaze, but now sets the
  blue-and-white in **motion and multiplication** — many, going out — with a
  cool **sea-and-sky** register behind the object (the East-bound freight, the
  water, the ships' horizon). Where Book 2 warmed toward a single fired plate,
  Book 3 **opens outward**: one hero object in the foreground, and behind or
  beyond it the suggestion of *more* — the same blue repeated, receding, sailing.
- The blue/white contrast remains the whole subgenre signature (it is literally
  Delftware). Book 3's added note is **openness/distance**: a low cool horizon, a
  reach of still water, the blue going somewhere. Keep it restrained and literary;
  no literal galleon clutter.

## Motif

- **Primary (recommended):** the **great centre-charger** of the East-bound
  service — a large blue-and-white dish painted with a **ring of unchained
  goldfinches** turning round its rim about one central bird, lit cool against a
  blue-dark ground with a low band of pale sea-light at the foot. This is the
  book's central image: Fabritius's *chained* goldfinch (Books 1–2) carried
  forward and **set free**, multiplied onto fired clay, bound for the far side of
  the world — the "common blue" made visible in one object.
- **Alternative:** a **stack or receding row of identical blue-and-white plates**
  (the blue that is the same, true, and everywhere — dependability as beauty),
  fading toward a cool horizon of water; or a **barrel of drab grey zaffer** in
  the foreground turning, along the frame, into a field of true fired blue plates
  behind it ("the cheapest blue that was ever made," made true ten thousand
  times).
- **Series continuity:** Book 1 = the **ground stone and muller** (the cold
  easel-blue, in shadow). Book 2 = the **single fired plate** (the kiln-blue, in
  light). Book 3 = the **many plates / the freight** (the common blue, going out).
  One luminous blue object per cover; across the three the frame moves from
  disaster-shadow → fired light → the wide reach of the sea. On one product page
  the three must read as an obvious, deliberate set that resolves.

## Typography

- Match Books 1 and 2: a restrained literary serif display for the title,
  generous spacing, ink in a bone/chalk white against the blue ground; author name
  smaller beneath; series line small. Must read as the closing member of a matched
  trilogy on one product page. No ornament that reads as cozy or romance — this is
  literary historical fiction.

## Comparables (look-and-feel, not claims)

- The literary Dutch-Golden-Age shelf, same as Books 1 and 2: Tracy Chevalier
  (*Girl with a Pearl Earring*), Deborah Moggach (*Tulip Fever*), Jessie Burton
  (*The Miniaturist*). Covers that sell a single luminous object, period restraint,
  and colour-as-subject. *The Common Blue* should sit beside its two predecessors
  as the volume that completes them.
