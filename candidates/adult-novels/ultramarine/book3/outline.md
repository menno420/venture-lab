# Ultramarine, Book 3 — *The Common Blue* — Outline

> **Status:** `draft`

**Premise.** Two years after the Delft Thunderclap, the widowed colour-grinder
Clara Wijnants has become the blue-hand of the rising kilns — and a great order
comes down from Amsterdam that is bigger than any one pottery can fill: a service
of blue-and-white bound for the East, hundreds of pieces for the autumn ships to
Batavia, and another after it "for as long as the ships sailed and the world
wanted blue." China's own porcelain has been cut off by its wars, and the Company
means Delft to fill the gap. To make blue by the barrel where she once ground it
by the thumb-joint, Clara must set a single true colour across a whole town of
rival potteries, hold that colour against the deadline of the tide, keep the
independent cobalt supply out of Willem Doncker's hands now that he covets the
very trade he despised, and reckon with what it costs — and what it is worth — to
make a beauty *common*: to turn the rarest blue in the world into a blue for
everyone, going everywhere the ships go. And the child who will paint it, Grietje,
struck mute by the blast and healed by the fire, comes at last into her own.

This outline is the source of truth for the Book-3 manuscript. It derives
strictly from the series `bible/` (`style.md`, `world.md`, `characters.md`,
`book2-additions.md`) and from Book 2's ending (spring 1656). Respect every
continuity anchor below and every beat in the sheet. Voice, register, and rules
are governed by `bible/style.md`; new canon is logged in
`bible/book2-additions.md` (unchanged) and this book's `DECISIONS.md` entries.

## Where Book 2 left her (fixed, do not contradict)

- **Spring 1656.** Clara holds a **sound new shop** (found and held before the
  leave lapsed) and is the **colour-hand of the kiln at De Blauwe Hand**, no
  longer the dying easel-trade. Delft is ~3 years into its ~20-year rebuilding.
- **No true ultramarine is left in Clara's hands** and none she can afford to
  make; the old stone-blue trade is closed. The **last reclaimed lapis** passed
  into the unnamed young painter's *woman-by-a-window* picture (never name him —
  `DECISIONS.md` [D-0003]).
- **Fabritius's goldfinch panel** (*Het puttertje*) hangs in the shop, **held in
  trust** — Agatha van Pruyssen gave it into Clara's keeping, not ownership. Clara
  will not sell it. The panel is "dying slowly of being paint"; the image lives on
  by being **copied onto fired blue ware**, not kept.
- **Grietje** (~10, aging toward 11–12 across Book 3): voice fully returned
  (cracked-whole), a **mended arm** from the blast; the **youngest
  plateelschilster** at De Blauwe Hand, paints **blue birds (goldfinches) on white
  ware**. She counts **five** bright things now (blue tile-shard, horn button,
  river pebble, pyrite — the gold that was not gold — plus her own **first fired
  blue-bird tile**). Track the healing; never state the count as five before Book
  2's reveal is honoured as already past.
- **Barent** is in the hasty ground — the unmended grief Clara carries to her
  grave. Do not resolve or cancel it.
- **Coenraad Spronck** — plateelbakker, master of **De Blauwe Hand** (in the shell
  of the dead brewery *De Drie Ackers* off the Oosteinde); broad, burn-scarred
  hands, flat Delft/harbour voice; lost a brother and the brother's household in
  the blast. Collegial ally, **not a romance** (`DECISIONS.md` [D-0006]).
- **Machteld** (~17) — eldest plateelschilster, sure-handed, all brothers at home,
  her family dead under the Nieuwe Kerk; goes grey at a hard-rung bell. Mentors
  Grietje. **Pauwels** — older potter, days only, won't attend a night firing
  (blast-fear); minor.
- **Willem Doncker** — colour-merchant and guild elder, self-righteous, aggrieved,
  sincere; not a cartoon. In Book 2 he was **outlived by a changing world** — his
  painters'-colour empire dying as the kiln-blue rose. His Book-1 line is unpaid:
  *"Gerrit was my friend, before he was your husband."*

## Continuity anchors (every chapter must respect)

- **Close third, past tense, Clara's POV throughout.** No head-hopping. (style.md)
- **Motifs to thread, not hammer:** blue as the cost and ruin of beauty; seeing
  versus blindness; grinding/breaking/firing to make; the "blue hour"; things
  reclaimed grain by grain. **Book 3's turn:** the **common blue** — a colour made
  *cheap by reach* where the old blue was *dear by rarity*; the democratising of a
  beauty (Doncker sneers "a common blue, a kitchen blue"; Clara reclaims *common*
  as *held in common, for everyone, everywhere the ships go*). Refrains available,
  used sparingly: Book 1's **"not to grieve the breaking"**; Book 2's **"not to
  fear the fire, only to watch it."** Book 3 may earn a third companion line about
  the *many* / the going-out.
- **Period fidelity** per `world.md`: gulden/stuiver; Little Ice Age cold; the
  Guild of St Luke covers plateelbakkers; breweries collapsed, halls repurposed
  for pottery; delftware rising toward 28 potteries by 1670, **spurred by the VOC
  porcelain trade and the disruption of Chinese exports** (the Ming–Qing wars cut
  off China's kilns; Delft fills the gap). **Delft was one of the six VOC
  chambers.** Between towns by **trekschuit**; goods by barge to the sea. Cobalt is
  the potter's blue (**zaffer / smalt**), drab grey-black raw, true blue only in
  the firing; the potters' cobalt comes via a **Hamburg/Saxon factor outside
  Doncker's lapis network** — the plot mechanism of the potters' freedom. Where
  history is uncertain (the death toll above all), keep the uncertainty. No
  anachronism (no camera obscura as fact; no common tea/coffee; no Prince, no
  stadtholder in 1656–57).
- **Real persons within the record** (`DECISIONS.md` [D-0003]/[D-0010]): the young
  painter stays **unnamed**; **Agatha van Pruyssen** survives and is treated with
  respect, given no invented crime or romance; **Dirck van Bleyswijck** may appear
  only as witness/chronicler; the Company merchant and Amsterdam collector stay
  minor, invented, transactional. No invented crimes or romances for real persons.
- **Trilogy close:** Book 3 is the **final** Ultramarine novel. It must *resolve*.
  Any forward note it plants is a **closing gesture** (the trade goes on; the ships
  still sail; the next blue not yet made), **not** a promissory hook for a Book 4.

## Beat sheet

### Part One — The Great Order
*(summer 1656; the order too big for one pottery; the old blue vs. the common)*

**1. The Order from the East.** Summer 1656. Clara mixes the fired blue at
scale in the sound new shop before the bells; the goldfinch on the wall, Grietje
counting her five bright things. Establish the new normal — she is the town's
best blue-hand, the easel-trade dead, the kiln-trade rising. A **Company factor**
brings the order down from Amsterdam through Spronck: a great **service of
blue-and-white for the autumn ships to Batavia**, hundreds of pieces, because
China's kilns are shut by war and the Company will have Delft's blue instead.
Wonder and dread braided. **The number is bigger than one pottery.** Motif: blue
by the barrel, not the thumb-joint. End on the impossible order.

**2. More Than One Pottery.** The scale crisis. De Blauwe Hand alone cannot fill
the freight by the tide. Spronck must go to the other potteries in the dead
breweries — rivals who do not trust each other — and Clara must be **one true blue
for all of them** or the service comes out of the kilns a dozen different blues
and the Company rejects the lot. Introduce the consortium and its frictions (a
proud rival master; potters who guard their own recipes). Clara's painter's pride
against the volume; she agrees to be **the town's blue-hand, not one pottery's.**
Turn: she takes on more than one woman can hold.

**3. The Guild's New Interest.** (Mirror of the Book-1/2 Doncker chapter.)
Doncker returns — changed. Now that the money is in the fired blue, he no longer
despises it; he **means to own it**, to control the cobalt and the export toll as
he controlled the lapis, through his capital, his nephew's good stall, and his
weight in the guild. Clara sees the new trap: the **independent Hamburg/Saxon
cobalt line** is the one thing keeping the potters free, and Doncker means to buy
or block it. The bureaucratic vice, inverted. His unpaid Book-1 note surfaces:
*Gerrit was my friend.* The threat to the supply named.

**4. The Last Ultramarine.** The old-blue counterweight. The unnamed young
painter's finished *woman-by-a-window* is spoken of / glimpsed — **the last true
ultramarine living on in one picture, owned by one patron.** Set against the
common blue about to go to the whole world. **Agatha** appears re: the goldfinch
panel (planting the trust-resolution). Clara reckons with the loss of the supreme
old blue and the coming of a blue for everyone; **Doncker's sneer — "a common
blue" — lands, and Clara begins to reclaim the word.** She commits fully to the
great order and to making the finest *common* blue in the world. End on decision.

### Part Two — The Common Blue
*(autumn 1656 → winter; the making at scale; the cost; the crisis)*

**5. A Blue by the Barrel.** The making at scale. Clara sets the **colour-standard**
for the consortium — benches of girls and boys across several potteries, all
painting to her one blue; zaffer and smalt ground in quantity, the recipe held
true across kilns. **Grietje rising:** good enough now that the younger hands copy
her bird; her pattern becomes the model. Warmth and pride — and the first strain
of **quantity over care**: hands worked raw, the bench-children, the pace of the
tide. The human cost opens.

**6. The Number and the Fire.** The cost sharpens. A great firing under deadline;
the temptation to push the kilns too hard and fast for the ships — **crazing,
burn-black, a ruined batch** when speed forces the fire. A bad firing loses a
chunk of the order. **Machteld's bell-fear and Pauwels's blast-fear** surface
under the night-firing pressure; the kiln's roar carries the twelfth of October
still. Clara **refuses to ship bad blue to make the tide** — the discipline of the
true stone carried to the common blue. Turn: the standard holds, but the ship's
date does not wait.

**7. The Supply Cut.** The trade crisis peaks. The **Hamburg/Saxon cobalt line
fails** mid-order — Doncker has moved on the factor (bought or blocked him), and
the continent's war has narrowed the road; the potters face **running out of blue**
with the freight half-made. Without cobalt there is no blue, fired or ground.
Clara must break the block or find another route — a supply reckoning that turns
on her reputation and the Company's own hunger for the ware (the Company wants
plates, not Doncker's toll). End at the edge: the blue running out, the tide
coming.

**8. The Reckoning.** (Mirror of ch. 8 — the low point.) Everything converges:
the cobalt short, the tide fixed, Doncker's move, the consortium fracturing under
strain, the ruined firing, the mounting human cost (a bench-child hurt; Grietje
pushed too hard; Clara's own body failing at the pace). The autumn ships will
sail without Delft's blue, or with too little, and the great order that was to be
the town's rebirth is about to break her and the potters both. **The goldfinch /
Agatha's need forces the sell-or-keep choice again at the worst moment.** End at
the low point, at the edge of defeat.

### Part Three — Everywhere the Plates Go
*(winter → autumn 1657; the remaking; the freight sails; the close)*

**9. What the Blue Is For.** The turn. Clara finds the way through: she **binds the
potters into a real shared factor** for the cobalt, beyond Doncker's reach — the
Company factor siding with the makers over the merchant-elder because the Company
wants the ware. The consortium re-coheres. The theme crystallises: the common blue
is worth making **because** it is common — a blue **held in common**, going to
everyone, the opposite of the one priceless blue owned by one man. **Clara
reclaims "common"** fully from Doncker's sneer. The order will be filled; Grietje
will paint the centrepiece.

**10. The Service.** The great blue-and-white service for the East is made —
Clara's fired blue finer than any potter's, the whole town's benches to her one
standard. **Grietje comes into her own:** she paints not a copy of Fabritius's
bird but **a bird of her own**, hers and no one's before her, onto the ware bound
for Batavia — the goldfinch carried forward and *multiplied*, going everywhere the
ships go, not sold. Meeting again with the young painter and/or Agatha; the
**goldfinch trust resolved** (returned to Agatha, or given outright, or released).
The "new seeing" resolves in fired blue at scale.

**11. The Guild's Blue.** (Mirror of ch. 11.) Clara outlasts Doncker. The Company
and the guild legitimate the potters' independent trade; **Doncker's monopoly
breaks** because the Company wants the makers, not the merchant's toll. He is not
routed — he is **outlived**, aging/failing, his nephew inheriting a dying
painters'-colour trade. His **last human, obsolete note**, and the Book-1 line
paid off: *Gerrit was my friend, before he was your husband* — a final grace
between two people widowed by the same change. Bought dear, held narrow — the
series signature, not a rout.

**12. Everywhere the Plates Go.** (Mirror of ch. 12 — the close.) Autumn 1657.
**The freight sails** — the blue-and-white going down the canal to the sea, onto
the VOC keels for Batavia and beyond; the first freight sells, and there will be
another and another for as long as the ships sail and the world wants blue.
**Grietje established** as the coming plateelschilster — the child struck mute by
the blast now the painter of the blue that goes everywhere; **her bird on some of
the plates, headed for houses neither of them will ever stand in.** The goldfinch
resolved; the last true ultramarine living on in one far picture; the **common
blue living everywhere.** Barent still in the hasty ground — the cost never
cancelled, carried whole. The blue hour closes over the mended town.
**Closing / forward note (organic, trilogy-final — a gesture, not a Book-4 hook):**
the ships still sailing, the next blue not yet made, the trade handed on to the
child; the pay-off of Book 2's line — *"the bluest hands in Delft, and everywhere
the plates go."* End on light and gesture in the blue-hour, the whole trilogy's
restraint kept to the last: the breaking and the making side by side, neither
cancelling the other. **THE END.**
