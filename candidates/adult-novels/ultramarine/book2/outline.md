# Ultramarine, Book 2 — *The Blue and the White* — Outline

> **Status:** `draft`

**Premise.** A year after the Delft Thunderclap, the widowed colour-grinder Clara
Wijnants is running out of the two things that keep her free — the reclaimed
ultramarine she washed grain by grain from the frozen mud, and the painters left
alive to buy it. As the last of the old blue passes into the one painting that
will outlast them all, a rising trade comes knocking: the blue-and-white
delftware potteries springing up in the town's dead breweries need a colour-hand
who understands blue — a *fired* blue, born of the same element that killed the
town, invisible in the raw ore until the kiln makes it. To keep her trade, her
niece, and her hard-won independence, Clara must learn to trust a colour she
cannot see, and answer Willem Doncker's last, quiet attempt to shut the door the
catastrophe left her.

This outline is the source of truth for the Book-2 manuscript. It derives
strictly from the series `bible/` (`style.md`, `world.md`, `characters.md`) and
from Book 1's ending (spring 1655). Respect every continuity anchor below and
every beat in the sheet. Voice, register, and rules are governed by

`bible/book2-additions.md`.

## Where Book 1 left her (fixed, do not contradict)

- Spring 1655. Clara holds a poor two-room shop off the **Nieuwe Langendijk**,
  well back from the ruin, on a **year's lease** and a hard bargain. Ground floor
  for the trade and a **plain grey second-hand slab** (not porphyry, not
  Gerrit's); the room above to sleep dry.
- The guild upheld her **widow's right** to continue Gerrit's enrolled trade —
  grinding and the sale of colour — but bound it in three ropes: **no
  market-right and no good Voldersgracht stall** (that went to Doncker's nephew);
  **she must find and hold a proper shop within the year or the leave lapses**;
  she paid a **high continuance fee/bond**; and she must **supply the guild's own
  town-work at a fixed rate below cost** (grinding umber at a loss) in exchange
  for leave to sell the rest as she pleases.
- **Grietje** (was 8, now ~9→10): voice returned, hoarse then whole; grinds the
  forgiving earths beside Clara; still counts her four bright things each morning
  (blue tile-shard, horn button, river pebble, the **pyrite — the gold that was
  not gold**).
- **Barent** is in the hasty ground — the unmended grief Clara will carry to her
  own grave.
- **Fabritius's goldfinch panel** (*Het puttertje*) hangs in the shop in the good
  north light, saved from the ruin; Clara will not sell it and has stopped being
  unable to look at it.
- **The unnamed young painter** (Vermeer — never named on the page, per
  `DECISIONS.md` [D-0003]) ordered her true reclaimed ultramarine for a painting
  he means to make: *a woman by a window, morning light across her, a blue in the
  shadow of the cloth — "a blue you could breathe."* This is the living thread
  from Book 1's last pages.
- **Willem Doncker**, colour-merchant and guild elder, won the ground, the stall,
  and nine parts of ten; not a cartoon — self-righteous, aggrieved, sincere.
  *"Gerrit was my friend, before he was your husband."*
- The reclaimed hoard is **finite and nearly spent** — she has been selling it
  grain by grain and cannot afford to import fresh lapis.

## Continuity anchors (every chapter must respect)

- **Close third, past tense, Clara's POV throughout.** No head-hopping. (style.md)
- **Motifs to thread, not hammer:** blue as the cost and ruin of beauty; seeing
  versus blindness; grinding/breaking/firing to make; the "blue hour"; things
  reclaimed grain by grain. **Book 2's new turn on the seeing motif:** a blue you
  cannot see until the fire has it — faith in a hidden colour. **Fire transmuted:**
  the element that destroyed Delft (the powder, "the white") becomes, in the
  potter's kiln, the maker of beauty — but never cheaply; the kiln's roar still
  carries the blast for Clara and for Grietje.
- **Period fidelity** per `world.md`: gulden/stuiver; Little Ice Age cold; the
  Guild of St Luke covers **plateelbakkers** (delftware potters) as well as
  painters; the breweries collapsing (~200 → ~20), empty brewery halls
  **repurposed for pottery**; delftware rising (~11 potteries in 1640 toward 28
  by 1670), spurred by the VOC porcelain trade. **Cobalt** is the potter's blue:
  applied under the tin-glaze as a drab grey-black **zaffer/smalt** and turning
  true blue only in the firing. Where history is uncertain (the death toll above
  all), keep the uncertainty. No anachronism.
- **New canon (see `bible/book2-additions.md`):** the pottery **De Blauwe Hand**
  (invented), rising in a dead brewery near the Oosteinde; its master **meester
  Coenraad Spronck** (invented plateelbakker); its young **plateelschilders**
  (pottery-painters, girls among them). Book-2 span: **autumn 1655 → summer
  1656.** Grietje's turn toward the plate.
- Real persons handled within the record (`DECISIONS.md` [D-0003]): the young
  painter stays unnamed; **Dirck van Bleyswijck** may appear only as witness/
  chronicler; **Agatha van Pruyssen** (Fabritius's widow) survives and is treated
  with respect. No invented crimes or romances for real persons.
- Refrain available from Book 1: **"not to grieve the breaking."** Book 2 earns a
  companion line about the fire.

## Beat sheet

### Part One — The Cold Trade
*(the dying oil-blue; the year's-grace clock; the fire-blue knocks)*

**1. The Last of the Lapis.** Autumn 1655. Before dawn in the poor shop off the
Nieuwe Langendijk; Clara grinds the last good grain of the reclaimed ultramarine
on the grey slab, the goldfinch on the wall in the dark. Establish the squeeze:
the lease running down, the guild's loss-making town-work ground each morning,
the fee still owed, the painters left alive too few and too poor to keep her.
Grietje wakes and counts her four bright things. End on a stranger's knock — a
potter come at first light for *blue*, but not the painter's blue. Motif opened:
the hoard ending; a beautiful thing worked down to its last.

**2. The Fire Blue.** The caller is **meester Coenraad Spronck**, rebuilding a
pottery, **De Blauwe Hand**, in a failed brewery; the plateelbakkers are rising
and starving for reliable blue. He shows Clara **zaffer** — a drab grey-black ore
with no blue in it at all — and asks her to be his colour-hand: a blue you cannot
see until the fire has it. Her painter's-craft pride recoils; the coin tempts.
Introduce the pottery world at a distance. Theme: seeing a colour before the fire
makes it. Turn: she says no, and cannot stop thinking about it.

**3. The Widow's Leave.** (Mirror of Book 1 ch. 3.) Guild business brings
**Doncker** back. He presses the letter of her continuance-leave: it covers the
enrolled sale of **colour to painters**; deal raw blue to the potteries and she
risks the leave, or must come under his supply and his terms — for he means to
control the town's cobalt as he controls its lapis. The bureaucratic vice; her
economic precarity sharpened; the year's deadline named aloud. She refuses to be
cornered, and sees the trap closing on both sides at once.

**4. The Young Painter's Blue.** The **unnamed young painter** comes for
ultramarine for the woman-by-window painting. Clara gives him the near-last of the
true reclaimed blue — the bridge from Book 1, "the blue you could breathe" — and
understands that the old blue is finishing in her hands and passing into the one
work that will last. A quiet, tender chapter that sets the real stakes: die with
the beautiful old trade, or learn the new one. End on her decision to go and look
at the fire blue.

### Part Two — The Kiln
*(learning the blue born of fire; the turn; the cost)*

**5. De Blauwe Hand.** Clara goes to the pottery in the shell of the dead
brewery: the white tin-glaze, the wheels, the young **plateelschilders** painting
birds and flowers on raw ware, the great kiln cold between firings. She begins to
learn the fire blue — the zaffer, the smalt, the strange chemistry that is not
oil-grinding at all. **Grietje** comes and is caught by the blue-on-white and the
girls with brushes. Warmth returns: the pottery is alive where the painters'
world is dying.

**6. The Blue That Was Not There.** The crux of the new craft: Clara lays a blue
that goes on grey-black and drab, and must trust the fire she cannot argue with.
The **kiln-opening** — the fired blue blooming out of the dull ore (a reveal that
rhymes with reclaiming the ultramarine from the mud in Book 1: colour born of
fire as the old blue was coaxed by grinding). Wonder and unease braided. A bad
patch too — a crazed glaze, a ruined batch — the fire's double face. Turn: the
new blue is hers to learn; she is good at it, better than any potter's boy.

**7. The Kiln and the Powder.** The emotional turn. A night firing — the roar,
the heat, the red mouth of the kiln — brings the twelfth of October up in Clara's
body, and flares Grietje's old terror (the muteness echo). Fire as destroyer and
maker in one. Barent's ghost (he worshipped makers; he would have loved this).
Clara reckons with her fear of fire and comes through it; Grietje steadies. The
book's cost/darkness beat — the Part-Two counterpart to Book 1's disaster, now
transmuted into fire mastered, not fire suffered. Earn the companion refrain.

**8. The Reckoning.** (Mirror of Book 1 ch. 8.) The pressure peaks. Doncker moves
formally — a guild challenge to her pottery dealing, or he calls in the raw-lapis
credit she cannot pay, and dangles supply of the potters' cobalt only on his
terms; his nephew undercuts her from the good stall; the lease is weeks from
lapsing and no proper shop is in reach. She is caught between a dead trade and a
new one not yet secured, with the deadline on her. End at the edge of defeat.

### Part Three — The Blue and the White
*(the remaking; the narrow victory; the forward hook)*

**9. The Empty Slab.** Deep winter; the low point. The last true lapis is gone
(given to the young painter), the painters' trade effectively dead, the lease
near lapsing, Doncker pressing, Fabritius's goldfinch panel now worth more than
everything she owns and a collector's offer on the table. The choice: sell the
bird and limp on, or refuse it and stake everything on the fire blue and the
potteries. Grietje's yearning — she wants to *paint the plate*. The decision
crystallises: throw in fully with the blue-and-white; become the colour-hand of
the kiln, not the easel; keep the bird.

**10. Blue and White.** Clara commits and brings a painter's colour-sense to the
pottery's blue — a fired blue finer than any potter had, the true stone's
discipline carried to the wheel. The trade turns toward her. Grietje paints her
**first blue bird on white** — Fabritius's goldfinch carried forward onto
delftware, the image *multiplying* instead of being sold. A meeting again with
the young painter and/or Agatha; the "new seeing" resolving in fired blue. The
goldfinch outlasts by being copied, not kept — the Book-1 coda paid forward.

**11. The Guild's Blue.** (Mirror of Book 1 ch. 11.) Clara confronts Doncker and
the guild and wins a hard, narrow victory: the leave secured and the pottery-
supply legitimated (the plateelbakkers, a rising power, will not be told their
blue is a painter's monopoly); a proper shop found and held before the deadline.
Doncker is not routed — he is **outlived by a changing world**: his painters'-
colour empire is dying while hers is reborn in the kiln he despised as beneath
art. His last humane, obsolete note. Bought dear, held narrow — the series
signature, not a rout.

**12. The Colour That Remains.** (Mirror of Book 1 ch. 12.) Spring 1656. The
young painter's finished picture spoken of — the woman by the window, the blue you
could breathe — the last true ultramarine living on. And the future: the blue-
and-white going out into the world on VOC keels, the plate that will outlast them
all; Grietje's blue bird headed for the far side of the earth on white clay. The
seeing-motif resolves; the blue hour closes over the mended canal.
**Forward hook:** a great order arrives — a service of blue-and-white bound for
the East — more than Clara and one pottery can grind alone; and the child who
will paint it is standing at the slab with blue to the creases, ready. End on
light, and on the next blue not yet made.
