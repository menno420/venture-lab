# Decisions — Ultramarine

Owner-delegated creative decisions for this novella (owner delegation 2026-07-12). All are reversible and owner-vetoable.

## [D-0001] Premise: the Delft Thunderclap of 1654

Chosen premise is the historical Delft gunpowder explosion of 12 October 1654, told through a fictional widowed colour-grinder, Clara Wijnants. Rationale: a specific, dramatic, historically rich Dutch event that is less over-written than the Golden Age studio novel or the 1953 flood; it yokes a natural three-act shape (before / catastrophe / after) to strong literary themes (the cost of beauty, a woman's economic precarity, seeing after loss). Original premise, no derivative source material.

## [D-0002] Scope and length: ~27k-word complete novella, 12 chapters

Target the lower-middle of the briefed 25–40k band: a complete, well-resolved novella of ~27k words in 12 chapters across three parts. Literary quality and a real ending are prioritised over length. Driven partly by a session token budget; a longer cut is an expansion follow-up ([see README expansion note]).

## [D-0003] Handling of real historical persons

Carel Fabritius, Cornelis Soetens, Johannes Vermeer, and Dirck van Bleyswijck are real. They appear only within the documented record: Fabritius dies at his easel in the explosion (documented, via Houbraken), his pupil Mathias Spoors with him; Soetens opens the powder store; Vermeer is a peripheral, newly-enrolled young painter; Bleyswijck records the ruin. No real person is given invented crimes, romances that contradict record, or defamatory acts. Clara's household and the rival Doncker are wholly invented.

## [D-0004] Fidelity to historical uncertainty

Where the record is genuinely uncertain — the death toll (contemporary estimates range from ~100 to ~1,200 and the true figure was never known), the exact ignition, the precise time — the novella declines false precision and dramatises the uncertainty itself. Legends (the baby found clutching an apple; Soetens's red cloak) are used, if at all, as things the characters *say*, not as fact.

## [D-0005] Title

Working and shipping title: *Ultramarine* — the costliest pigment, ground from imported lapis lazuli, which serves as the book's central material and metaphor. Reversible; alternatives considered: *The Secret of Holland*, *The Blue Hour*.

## [D-0006] Content register

"Adult" is interpreted as adult literary fiction — mature themes, disaster, grief, mortality — not erotica. No explicit sexual content; restrained throughout. Content note shipped in README.

Series line: *Ultramarine · Book Two* (a Delft novel). Reversible; alternatives considered: *Smalt*, *The Fire Blue*, *Delftware*.

## [D-0009] Book 2 spine: the turn from the easel to the kiln

The sequel is grounded strictly in the series `bible/` and Book 1's ending, and is a genuine continuation (not a reboot): same protagonist (Clara Wijnants), same POV/register, and the returning cast (Grietje, Willem Doncker, the unnamed young painter, Agatha van Pruyssen, the surviving Fabritius goldfinch). The spine dramatises Delft's **documented economic turn** — the collapse of the breweries and the rise of the **blue-and-white delftware potteries** (see `world.md`) — as Clara's dying painter's-colour trade forces her to learn the **fired cobalt blue** ("a blue you can't see till the fire has it"), transmuting the fire that destroyed the town in Book 1 into the maker of a colour death cannot get at. It ends on a forward hook: a great VOC order of blue-and-white bound for the East, and Grietje as the coming plateelschilster carrying Fabritius's bird onto the plate. New canon logged in `bible/book2-additions.md`.

## [D-0011] Book 3 title: *The Common Blue*

Book 3's shipping title is ***The Common Blue*** — the culminating turn of the series' blue-arc: from Book 1's *Ultramarine* (the astronomical blue, dear by rarity, for one patron), through Book 2's *The Blue and the White* (the fired blue-and-white of Delftware), to Book 3's **common blue** — the rarest colour on earth made cheap by reach and sent by the barrel to the whole world. "Common" is worked as the book's central double meaning: Doncker's sneer (*a common blue, a kitchen blue*) reclaimed by Clara as *held in common* — the common ground, the common good, a blue owned by no one because it is held by everyone. Series line: *Ultramarine · Book Three* (a Delft novel). Subtitle (metadata): *A Novella of Delft.* Reversible; alternatives considered: *Everywhere the Plates Go*, *The Bluest Hands*, *A Blue for the World*.

## [D-0013] Book 3 spine: making the rare colour common, and closing the trilogy

Grounded strictly in the series `bible/` (`style.md`, `world.md`, `characters.md`, `book2-additions.md`) and Book 2's ending, and a genuine continuation (not a reboot): same protagonist (Clara Wijnants), same POV/register, and the returning cast (Grietje, Coenraad Spronck, Machteld, Pauwels, Willem Doncker, Agatha van Pruyssen, the young painter, the surviving Fabritius goldfinch). The spine dramatises Delft's **documented economic take-off** — the VOC filling the porcelain gap left by the disruption of Chinese exports (the Ming–Qing wars), Delft as one of six VOC chambers, the blue-and-white sailing east — as a **great order too large for one pottery** forces Clara to set one true fired blue across a whole town of rival kilns, hold it against the tide, and keep the potters' narrow cobalt road out of Doncker's hands. Themes: **industrialising a colour** (its human cost and reward), **the common vs. the rare** (the fired cobalt everywhere vs. the lost true ultramarine in one picture), and **Grietje's coming into her own** (Fabritius's chained goldfinch set free and multiplied by her own hand). The trilogy **resolves**: the freight sails, Doncker is outlived (his Book-1 line — *Gerrit was my friend, before he was your husband* — paid off) and dies, the goldfinch trust closes, and the close is earned and clear-eyed, planting only an organic *closing gesture* (the trade goes on, the next blue not yet made), **not** a Book-4 hook. New Book-3 invented canon (rival potters Adriaen Block, Govert van Nes, Verhaar, and Huybert; the six-pottery consortium and shared cobalt factor) logged in the SELF-EDIT-PASS and consistent with `bible/book2-additions.md`.

## [D-0014] Handling of real persons and history in Book 3

Continues [D-0003]/[D-0004]/[D-0010]. The young painter (Vermeer) stays **unnamed** on the page (and his patron generic); **Agatha van Pruyssen** appears within record — a surviving widow leaving Delft, treated with respect, given no invented crime or romance — and her sale of the goldfinch panel to an (invented, unnamed) Amsterdam collector is a plausible private transaction, not a claim about the real panel's history. **Carel Fabritius** remains dead in the blast per record; the goldfinch's fate in the fiction (kept in trust, copied, then sold to a collector) is the licence the fiction takes on the invented provenance established in Book 2, not a statement about the real *Goldfinch*. The VOC/delftware economics (Chinese-porcelain disruption, VOC chambers and export, breweries→potteries, cobalt/zaffer/smalt via a Hamburg/Saxon factor) are drawn from `world.md`/`book2-additions.md`; in-scene potter's craft detail (crazing under fast cooling, burn-black from too much cobalt) is credible period texture, not a documented recipe. Where the record is uncertain — the death toll above all — Book 3 keeps the uncertainty (Doncker: "the twelve hundred, or however many it was; nobody ever got the number right"). One period anachronism ("the Mauritshuis") was caught and corrected in the self-edit pass. Clara's household, Willem Doncker, Coenraad Spronck, De Blauwe Hand, Machteld, and the Book-3 rival potters remain wholly invented.
