# Ultramarine — Part One: The Muller


## 1. Lapis

Blue came hard, the way most valuable things came to Clara Wijnants — by weight, by grinding, by the patient ruin of one thing to make another. The lapis had crossed half the world to reach her table: a knuckle of stone the colour of a bruised evening, veined with a gold that was not gold, only fool's gold, pyrite, worth nothing and beautiful for it. To free the blue she first had to break the stone, and she had learned long ago not to grieve the breaking.

She set the knuckle in the iron mortar and brought the pestle down. The sound was flat and small in the dark shop, no louder than a knuckle on a door, and under it the stone gave way to a coarse grit that held no colour yet at all — a dirty grey-violet, ashes with a rumour of sky in them. That was the trick of it, the thing she had spent a marriage learning. The blue did not lie on the surface where a fool would look for it. It lived deeper, bound up in the rock with the dross and the pyrite and the pale calcite that would cloud any wash, and it had to be worked free with oil and wax and resin and water and time, more time than any of it seemed worth, until you held in your hand a pinch of powder dearer than the same weight in coin.

The one candle threw her shadow tall against the plastered wall. It was the black hour before the town woke, when the cold came up through the floor-tiles into the soles of her feet and the canal outside lay unmoving, and she liked it best. Nobody wanted anything of her yet. She tipped the grit from the mortar onto the porphyry slab — the slab was Gerrit's, dark red and speckled and worn to a faint hollow in the middle where two men's working had dished it — and took up the muller in both hands. The muller was a smooth loaf of the same stone, cool and heavy, and it fitted her palms the way a thing fits that you have held ten thousand times. She wet the powder with a little linseed oil, walnut for the blue would come later, and began to grind.

There was a rhythm to it that lived below thought. Round and round, the muller riding on its own weight, the paste gathering and spreading and gathering, the grit going from a whisper to a hiss to a silence as the particles broke finer and finer under the stone. Her arms knew the labour and her mind went elsewhere. This was where the years had put her: alone in a cold room before dawn, making by hand the only thing she had that no one could argue was not hers.

Outside, the town began. She heard it before she saw any light of it — the first cart somewhere over toward the Geerweg, iron rims on the frozen ruts; a dog; the wet slap of a punt-pole as some barge-man took the dark canal while it was his alone. The Verwersdijk ran past her door, the dyers' water, and even in October it carried its colours down to the harbour: madder-red and woad-blue and a sick yellow from the weld-vats, a whole spilled palette that no one would ever buy, running out to sea. On a warm day the stink of it could turn the stomach. This morning the cold held it down, and what came instead through the shutter-cracks was peat smoke, the low brown smell of the turf fires being blown back to life all down the street, the smell of the town breathing.

Two streets off, past the Doelen, the old convent of the Poor Clares stood full of gunpowder. Everyone knew it. The Secret of Holland, they called it, half in joke, the way you make small a thing too large to keep looking at — eighty thousand pounds of black powder behind walls that had once kept nuns, and a keeper with a lantern to walk among it. Clara had lived beside it for eleven years and thought of it about as often as she thought of the sea being salt. It was simply there, the way the churches were there, the way the guild was there. You did not spend your mornings frightened of the ground you stood on.

She lifted the muller and looked at the paste, and there it was beginning: the blue coming up out of the grey like a bruise coming up under skin, deeper each time she folded it back over itself. Not the finished colour, not yet, only the promise of it. She scraped the slab clean with the horn knife and set the smear aside on a scrap of glass to be washed and washed again, and thought, as she always thought at this point and never said aloud, that it was a strange trade she kept, spending her strength to break beautiful things smaller so that other people might make beautiful things larger.

A board creaked overhead. Small feet, careful of the third stair that shrieked. Then Grietje was in the doorway in her shift with the blanket dragged round her, hair on end, blinking at the candle.

"You're grinding," the child said, as though Clara might not have noticed.

"I'm grinding. Come away from the door, you're letting the cold in."

Grietje came, but not to be warmed. She crossed to the slab and looked at the smear of blue on the glass with the frank greed she brought to anything that shone. In one fist she carried her treasures, as she always did, and now she opened the fist on the edge of the slab to sort them by the candle: a chip of blue-and-white tile off some broken dish, a horn button, a river pebble, and the little lump of pyrite Clara had given her out of the lapis — the gold that was not gold. The child had wept when Clara told her it was worthless. She loved it no less for that; if anything more.

"Is that the dear blue," Grietje said, meaning the smear.

"That's the start of it. Don't touch, your hands are clean."

"Yours never are." She said it without unkindness, a plain report of the world. Clara's hands were blue to the creases and would be blue in her coffin; eleven years of the trade had gone into the skin and would not come out for any lye. Grietje took hold of one of them and turned it over, tracing the stains as though they were a map of somewhere. "Neeltje said you had the bluest hands in Delft."

Clara let her hold it. The name went through her the way it always did, a cold draught from a door she thought she had shut. Neeltje, her sister, the child's mother, three years in the ground of the Oude Kerk. "She was flattering me," Clara said. "Doncker has blue hands too. Go and put wood on the fire before we both die of it, and don't wake for nothing tomorrow, you'll be no use to me by noon."

The child went, dragging her blanket, unbothered. She would be no use by noon regardless. She was eight and useless in the way of the young, all appetite and questions, and Clara had not asked to have her and could no longer imagine the rooms without her — which was its own kind of grief, if you looked at it directly, so she did not.

The street door rattled and swung, letting in a wedge of grey — day coming after all — and Barent shouldered through it backward with a sack of something heavy, blowing on his fingers, too big for the room the way he was too big for everything. Fifteen and built like a man of twenty, an almshouse orphan Gerrit had taken on the last summer he was alive, and the boy had stayed on with Clara after because where else would he go, and because he could grind all day without stopping and eat a week's bread in a sitting.

"Cold enough to freeze the canal solid," he announced, dropping the sack. "There's a skin of ice on the Verwersdijk already, mistress, out by the bridge. I saw the deacon's man break it with an oar."

"Wipe your feet. Whatever you tracked in, I'll be washing colour out of it for a month." She said it every morning. He wiped his feet, badly, every morning. He crossed to the slab and looked at the blue with the same hunger the child had, only his was aimed higher — not at the shine but at what shining was for.

"Is that for a painter?" he asked.

"It's for whoever can pay. Which is nobody yet, so it's for the shelf."

"Meester Fabritius, though. He'll want blue. He always wants blue." Barent said the name the way another boy might say the name of a general, or a saint. He knew the painters the way other apprentices knew fighting-dogs — who was rising, who was drinking his fee, who had a Rembrandt in his hand and who only a Rembrandt in his mouth. He wanted the guild so badly it came off him like heat. Six gulden to enter it, and a native Delft boy could do it for that, but Barent had no six stuivers to rub together, let alone gulden, and no master to article him but a widow who ground colour, which the guild did not count. He knew all this. He wanted it anyway. Clara found she could not tell him to want less. She had wanted things too.

"He may come," she allowed. "He owes me for the last blue and he'll want more, which is how it always goes with him. Grind that, it's the umber for the Guild's own panels, and mind you don't leave it gritty, they'll send it back and blame me and pay me late for the pleasure."

Barent took up the second muller and set to, and the two stones went round on their two slabs in a doubled rhythm, his heavy and quick, hers light and slow, and for a while there was only that and the fire catching upstairs and the child's voice through the boards singing something with no tune.

Clara wiped her hands on her apron, which did nothing, and knelt at the low cupboard behind her slab where the finished colours stood in their pots and papers. She was not looking for a colour. Behind the ranked gallipots, back in the dark against the cold wall, stood one that did not match the rest: a plain grey pot, unlovely, stoppered with wax and a scrap of bladder tied over, the sort of pot you would pass over a hundred times looking for something worth stealing. She did not take it out. She only put her hand to it in the dark, the way you touch the doorframe of a room where someone is sleeping, to be sure. It was there. It was heavy. Years of the finest wash, the first water off the best stone, the blue that was too good to sell to men who owed her six gulden — saved back grain by grain against the day a commission came that could pay what it was truly worth, and lift her clear of Gerrit's debts and the guild's grudging leave and Doncker's patient waiting, all at once. Her whole way out, standing in a pot too plain to rob. She closed the cupboard.

"Mistress?" Barent had stopped grinding. "Doncker's man was on the dijk yesterday. Asking after your stall at the market. What you take in a week."

"Was he." She stood, and her knees told her the cold and her age both. "Doncker's man is always asking. Grind."

The boy ground. Beyond the shutters the grey had thickened to a real dawn, flat and low over the frozen town, and the Verwersdijk was awake now, cart and clog and voice, the dyers cursing their vats, the bells getting ready to tell the hour. Somewhere out there the day's customers were putting on their coats — the painters and the potters and the guild's cheese-paring clerk, all of them wanting colour, none of them wanting to pay for it today. Clara set a fresh scrap of glass beside the first and reached again for the water and the washed-out blue, to begin the long freeing of it, grain by grain, from everything it was bound to. There was a great deal of the day left to break.

## 2. The Goldfinch

She knew his knock. Everyone else on the Verwersdijk knocked as if apologising for it; Fabritius knocked as though the door were an old friend he was glad to find at home. Two raps and the latch already lifting, and then he was in out of the cold with the grey light behind him and a board wrapped in a cloth under his arm, held the way you carry a thing you do not trust to a bag.

"Vrouw Wijnants." He said it warmly and looked, first thing, at her hands, which most people would not do, and then at the pots on the shelf, which was where his heart was. "You've been at the good blue. I can smell the walnut oil. Tell me you've been at the good blue."

"I've been at the umber, meester, which is what pays." She wiped her hands and did not offer them; you did not, in the trade, with the colour wet. "You've come to owe me more money."

"I've come to admire your merchandise and be tempted beyond my means, which is the same thing, yes." He set the wrapped board on the counter with a care that gave it away — whatever it was, it mattered to him more than he would say plainly — and pulled off his gloves finger by finger. He was a big-handed man, quick in the face, thirty-two and going a little grey at the temple already, with the restless eyes of someone forever measuring the room against some picture of the room he carried behind them. Barent had stopped grinding again. Barent always stopped grinding when Fabritius came, and stood by his slab holding the muller like a boy holding his hat in church.

"Barent," Fabritius said, not turning, "you've grown again. Stop it. You'll cost your mistress a fortune in bread."

"He does already," Clara said. "What do you want, and what can you pay for it, and let's have the second answer honestly this time."

He laughed, unoffended. That was the thing about him that undid her guard however she braced it: he took her dryness as wit meant kindly, never as the fence she meant it for, and so it never fenced him out. "The honest answer," he said, "is little enough. I want ultramarine, the true blue, a good half-ounce of it. And loodwit, a paper of the best you've ground, the dense white. And I can pay you—" he opened his purse and looked into it with theatrical dismay— "the sincere respect of a fellow craftsman and perhaps two gulden of the six I already owe, which would leave the account, by my reckoning—"

"At six," said Clara. "You'd pay me two of six and owe me the blue on top, so still six, and thank yourself for settling."

"—at six, yes." He grinned. "You have a merchant's soul, vrouw, buried very deep under the paint."

She should have said no. She knew the shape of the whole thing before it played out; she had lived it a dozen times with him and always ended holding paper instead of coin. Six gulden was six gulden — a fortnight's bread and beer for the three of them, a month's rent on a stall, real money in a household where the ledger already leaned the wrong way. And ultramarine at that. Not azurite, not the mountain-blue she'd have let a solid customer carry off on tick without a second thought, but the true stone, dearer than the silver in his empty purse. To hand it over for a promise was not trade, it was faith, and she was not a woman with faith to spare.

"Show me first," she said, "what you're wasting my good blue on. If it's worth it I'll think about the blue."

His face changed. That was the coin he could always pay in. He unwrapped the cloth from the little board with both hands, and turned it to the grey window-light.

It was small, no bigger than a folio page, and it was a bird. A goldfinch, a *puttertje*, sat on the topmost ring of its feeder against a wall of nothing — a pale wall, warm and cool at once, with the bird's own faint shadow thrown on it — and one thin chain ran from the ring to a band about its leg. The paint was laid so loose up close it was hardly paint at all, a smear here, a dragged streak of red on the wing there, a fleck of yellow; and yet the bird sat there in the light and lived, and the chain that held it was so plainly a chain, so light and so final, that Clara felt it in her own wrist. The wall behind was not blue. She noted that with the part of her that was always adding up colour. He had spent nothing costly on it at all. The whole small miracle was ochre and white and lead and lampblack and a breath of her red, and it was worth more than the pot behind her slab, and both of those things were true at once and did not cancel.

"It's chained," she said. Of everything she might have said.

"It's chained." He looked at it with her, tilting his head. "They chain them so, the little put-birds, and teach them to draw up their own water in a thimble on that chain, for a trick, for children to laugh at. I keep thinking about the seeing of it. You look and you think, poor bird, and then you look again and you think, but how well I can see it — the light on it, the exact air round it — because it must hold still, because of the chain." He said it lightly and it did not land lightly. "The thing that holds it there is the thing that lets me see it true. I don't know what to do with that. So I paint it."

He laid the board face-up on the counter and reached into his coat, and brought out, instead of coin, a small thing of turned wood and ground glass, a tube no longer than his hand with a lens set in each end. "Look through it," he said, holding it out across the counter, "the long way, at the pots on your shelf, and tell me what you see."

She should not have. But there was the child in her still, under the widow and the ledger, and she took it and put the cold rim to her eye. The shelf leapt at her — the ranked gallipots huge and near and set apart from one another, each floating in its own pool of air with a clean edge of shadow, the distances between them suddenly a real and measurable thing, as though someone had pulled the world an arm's length forward and cut it out from its own background with a knife. She lowered the glass. The shelf was only a shelf again, flat and near, the pots crowded together the way pots are.

"You see," he said, watching her face and pleased by whatever he found there. "It's not a truer world through the glass. It's a chosen one. It throws away the half of what the eye takes in and keeps the other half sharp, and suddenly a thing stands where it truly stands, so much air before it, so much behind. We look and we think we see, vrouw, but the eye is a lazy servant, it hands you the room all mashed together and calls it seeing. I want to paint the way that glass looks. The exact standing-apart of things in the light. The air with a shape to it." He took the tube back and turned it in his blue-flecked fingers. "Half the men in the guild think I waste my time with lenses and boxes and think it's a conjuror's trade. Let them. They paint rooms you can't walk into."

"You've a deal to say about seeing," Clara said, "for a man who paints a small sad bird nobody will buy. Who wants it on his wall? Who wants to be reminded?"

"No one. You're quite right." He wrapped it again, tenderly, as if it might take cold. "I'll be paid for the deacon instead. Simon Decker, the church man, wants his face on a panel looking pious and prosperous, and he'll pay for the loodwit in his own collar and a good deal besides, and he will never once ask me whether I can see him true. That's the trade. The bird is for me." He looked up. "The blue's not for the deacon, before you ask. The deacon gets earth and black like every other burgher who thinks he's modest. The blue's for a thing I haven't started. A woman by a window with the light coming across. I want a blue in the shadow of the cloth that no one has put there before, a blue you can breathe. I can't do it with bergblauw, it goes green and grey on me and dies in a year. I need the true stone."

And there it was, the place where her sense and her weakness parted company. She had a customer who could not pay, wanting the one colour she could least afford to give on trust, for a picture that did not yet exist, of a light he could describe but not yet make. Any decent merchant would send him down the street to Doncker, who would sell him azurite and call it good enough and sleep well on it.

She turned to the shelf.

She weighed him a half-ounce of the true ultramarine — not the hoard, never the hoard, but the good sellable blue, the middle wash, better than anyone else on the dijk kept and not the best she had — and folded it into a twist of paper, and set beside it the paper of dense white. Then, because she was a fool, and because the bird sat chained in its cloth on her counter still holding still to be seen, she weighed out a little more of the blue than she had said, and did not mention it, and folded that in too.

"That's more than a half-ounce," Barent said from his slab, who missed nothing where the blue was concerned.

"It's a half-ounce," Clara said. "Grind."

Fabritius looked at the twist of paper and then at her, and for a moment the quickness went out of his face and something quieter stood in it, and she was aware of the cold shop and the grey light and the boy pretending to grind, and of the fact that she was thirty-four and a widow two winters and had not been looked at plainly by a man in longer than that, and that she did not want to be, and that some disobedient part of her did. She put the counter between them a little more firmly with her hands flat on it.

"Eight gulden now," she said. "The six and the blue. Written in the book. And I'll want two of it by Martinmas or I'll come to your studio and take it out in brushes, meester, I mean it."

"Eight by Martinmas." He tucked the papers into his coat against his heart, next to the bird, which she noticed and wished she had not. "You'll have it, or the brushes, whichever's dearer. Vrouw Wijnants—" at the door, gloves half on, the cold coming in around him— "the woman by the window. When she's done, you'll come and see whether I got the blue right. You of anyone should say whether I got it right. You made it."

"I make the colour," Clara said. "The seeing's your trade, not mine."

"They're the same trade," he said. "You just do the harder half in the dark." And he was gone, two streets from a magazine neither of them thought of, out into the flat grey morning with eight gulden of her livelihood over his heart.

Barent had not gone back to his muller. He stood looking at the door as if the warmth had gone out with the man. "He talked to me," he said. "He said I'd grown."

"He says that to the doorpost. Grind."

But the boy was somewhere else, up in the guild-house of his own head. "When I've my six gulden," he said, "and I'm articled proper, and I've done my six years — I'll grind for a man like that. Or paint. I could paint. Meester Fabritius came up out of a carpenter's shop, everyone knows it, faber, the maker, that's what the name means, he told me once. He wasn't born to it either. A person can start from nothing and end at his easel." He said it to the room, to the blue on the shelf, not really to her. "You think I'm foolish."

She thought he had no six gulden and no master who could give him what he wanted and a hunger too big for the almshouse boy the world had made him, and that she could no more grind that hunger smaller than she could grind it away, and that it was the best thing in him and would likely break his heart. "I think," she said, "that a man at his easel still needs somebody to have washed his blue. There's no shame in the harder half. Now grind, before I dock you for the dreaming."

He grinned, wholly himself again, and set the stone going, quick and heavy and glad, and Clara stood a moment longer than she needed to, watching him work, before she turned back to the umber that paid.

## 3. The Widow's Right

The colour market kept the near end of the Voldersgracht on the mornings the guild allowed it, a double row of stalls under the fullers' bridge where the trade in dry pigment and oil and brushes went on within a stone's throw of the Sint-Lucasgilde's own house, so that the elders could keep an eye on who sold what to whom. Clara took a stall there two days in the week. It was hers by the same thread everything was hers by: a widow's right to carry on her dead husband's enrolled trade, which the guild permitted the way you permit a draught you cannot find the source of — with a sigh, and an eye out for the chance to stop it.

She set out her wares on the boards while the frost was still on them: papers of ground earth, umber and the ochres and a good burnt sienna, ranked cheap to dear; the lead white in its stoppered pots; a little azurite for those who couldn't reach higher; brushes of hog and squirrel that a widow in the Bagijnhof made and Clara sold on a half-share. She kept the true ultramarine at home. You did not lay the dear blue out on a public board for every apprentice with dirty fingers to finger and every rival to price against you. The blue you sold behind a closed door, to a face you knew.

Trade was thin and cold-handed. A potter's boy for umber; a glazier for a paper of the yellow; the young painter from over on the Oude Langendijk, the quiet one who'd married the Bolnes girl and taken his oath in the guild only last Christmas, come to turn over her earths with a slow careful thumb and buy less than he wanted and go away thinking. She liked him for the way he looked at a colour a long time before he touched it, and did not press him. He would be a real customer in five years or none at all; you could not always tell the difference early, though she thought with that one you could.

The trouble was that painters paid last of anyone. A potter needed his colour today to fire a kiln that paid this week, and paid you; a painter's fee sat six months off in a burgher's promise, and until it came he lived on the colourman's patience, which meant on hers. She kept the whole leaning account in her head the way Gerrit had kept it in the book: this one owing for a year of lead white and good for it; that one owing since spring and drinking his commissions before he'd earned them; Fabritius owing eight now and worth the risk because the work was worth it, which was not a thing a ledger could weigh, and yet it was the truest column in the book. Half of what she was owed she would never see. The other half she'd see late, in bad coin, with thanks. She had learned to price that in, to sell the earths dear enough that the painters' debts did not sink her — to make the men who paid carry, unknowing, the ones who didn't. It was not a trade Doncker would have recognised as sound. It kept three people fed. She thought that was soundness enough, whatever he thought.

Doncker came at the flat middle of the morning, when the market was too public for a scene and too quiet for one to go unnoticed — he timed things, Doncker, he was a man who timed things — and he came the way he always came, with two of the younger guild men trailing him and a look on his face of a man performing patience as a public service.

"Vrouw Wijnants." He surveyed her boards the way a landlord surveys a tenant's roof. He was a broad, well-kept man in his fifties, sober cloth of good weight, a clean white collar, a colour-merchant grown prosperous and a guild elder grown certain, and his hands too were blue at the knuckle, which she never forgot when she looked at him, for it meant he had ground colour himself once and knew exactly what her days were, and pitied her for them, and thought the pity a kindness. "You've a poor showing of the blue today."

"I've a fair showing of what sells."

"Azurite." He lifted the little pot, turned it, set it down a shade too hard. "Greening already at the edge, look. You'd not have caught that in your husband's day." He said it gently, which was worse than if he'd sneered. "Gerrit knew his stock. God rest him. There's not a man at this market doesn't feel the want of him."

"There's one woman does," Clara said, "more than the market. Was there something you wanted to buy, meester Doncker, or only something to say?"

The younger men shifted. Doncker did not. He laid both blue-knuckled hands on the edge of her boards, leaning in, lowering his voice to the register of a man who means to be reasonable in front of witnesses. "I want to say a true thing to you, vrouw, that no one else will trouble to say because they haven't the standing or the years. It does you no good and it does the trade no good, this. A woman keeping a colour-stall, grinding at a slab like a labourer, carrying a dead man's book of debts on her own back, hiring an almshouse boy she can't article and can't answer for. It isn't order. The guild suffers a widow to close out her husband's affairs — to sell his stock, collect his debts, and remarry or retire in decent time. That's the widow's right, and it's a mercy, and it was never meant to be a life. Two winters, Clara. You've long since sold his stock. Everything on this board you made yourself. That's not closing out a trade. That's carrying one on, in your own name, and the guild never gave you that, and one day soon it will notice it never gave you that."

It was, she thought, the trouble with Doncker: he was not lying. That was the vice of the whole thing. If he had been a cheat or a lecher she could have hated him cleanly and been the stronger for it. Instead he stood there sincerely defending good order against her, sure to the marrow that a world with her in it as she was, upright and unmarried and grinding her own blue and answering to no man, was a world tending toward disorder, and that stopping her was a duty rather than an appetite. She had watched appetite dress itself as duty her whole life. It fooled everyone, including the man wearing it.

"You want my stall," she said.

"I want the trade kept honest and answerable, which your stall, held by you, is not." He straightened. "But yes. Plainly, since you like things plain. My nephew comes into the guild at the new year. He'll want a stall and custom and stock, and there's a fair price to be had — a good price, Clara, a generous one for the stock and the goodwill and the market-right such as it is, more than you'd get selling it piecemeal to the men who'll wait for you to fail. Enough to clear Gerrit's debts. Enough to take you and the child somewhere you needn't stand in the cold breaking stone. You could keep house. You could remarry, you're not old. You needn't do this."

There it was, laid on the frost between them: the offer that was a threat with its collar turned up. Sell now, to me, at my price, while I call it a kindness — or hold on, alone, until the guild finds the will to close the door it has only leaned on, and then sell to no one, at nothing, in the cold. He believed it was mercy. That was what made it terrible. He would go home and tell his wife he had done what he could for Gerrit's widow, and believe it, and sleep.

Clara looked past him for a moment at her own boards: the ranked earths cheap to dear, the white she had ground with her lungs full of the lead-dust that she knew, everyone knew, went into the grinders and did not come out; the brushes on their half-share; the whole small careful edifice of a life held up by nothing but her own two blue hands and the guild's reluctance to be seen breaking a widow. And behind it all, unspoken, two streets from where they stood, the grey pot in the dark cupboard: the blue too good to sell, the one thing she owned that Doncker did not know the size of, the way out she was saving for a door that had not yet opened. She thought of it and said nothing of it. It was the only card she held that he could not price, and you did not show such a card.

She thought of the fever-winter instead, because Doncker had made her, invoking Gerrit like a saint she'd failed. It had come in with the hard cold of the fever-winter, going house to house down the Verwersdijk the way the cold went, and it had taken Gerrit in eleven days from a strong man complaining of a headache to a grey thing in the bed sipping at the small beer she held to his mouth. He had done his dying with his ledger open on the coverlet, fretting the accounts, telling her who was good for what and who to press and who to let alone, teaching her the trade in the one voice he had left because he knew, before she would let herself know it, that she was going to need it. *The blue,* he'd said near the end, meaning the hoard, the pot they'd built together grain by grain across the good years, *don't touch the blue, whatever they say. That's not stock. That's the door.* She had held his hand, which was as blue as hers, and promised. Then the guild had come, correct and gentle, to say how sorry it was and to ask what her intentions were. Doncker had been among them. He had been sorry then too.

"My husband," she said, "died in the fever-winter owing money he'd have paid in a year of health. I nursed him to the end of it in the room over that shop, and I buried him, and I paid down what I could of what he owed, a stuiver on the gulden, out of colour I ground myself with the same hands you're pitying. I've not missed a payment to the guild's own chest in two years. I keep an orphan I was not given and a boy nobody wanted, and I feed them off this stall you'd like to fold up and give your nephew for a wedding gift." She kept her voice level, which cost her. "You call that disorder. I call it the only order there is, meester, which is a person doing the work in front of them and paying what they owe. When the guild wants to tell me to my face that a widow may bury a man but not outlive him, let it send someone who isn't standing to profit by the telling. Until then I'll thank you to buy azurite or move along. You're keeping the light off my boards."

For a moment something crossed his face that might, in a smaller man, have been shame, and was in him only the discomfort of a righteous man met with more righteousness than he'd budgeted for. He gathered it up. "You mistake me," he said, and the gentleness was back, the terrible patient gentleness. "I'll pray you come to see it clearer. The offer stands till the new year. After that it's not mine to make." He inclined his head, correct as a deacon. "Vrouw Wijnants. Mind the azurite. It'll be green as pond-scum by Sunday and someone will remember they bought it from you." And he went, his two young men after him, up toward the guild-house where his kind of order kept its books.

Clara stood at her boards in the thin light with her heart going hard and her face giving away nothing, and after a while the quiet painter from the Oude Langendijk came back, as if he'd waited for the air to clear, and bought his one paper of ochre and paid for it in coin, honest small coin counted out slow, and said nothing about any of it. She was grateful to him for that past all proportion. She wrapped his ochre, and took his stuivers, and did not let her hand shake until he had gone.

## 4. Small Beer

By the time the market boards were stacked and carried home and the shop shut against the dark, the frost had come down hard over the whole town and the Verwersdijk lay silent under it, the dyers' colours frozen mid-run in the shallows at the canal's edge. Clara barred the street door, banked what was left of the peat, and climbed the shrieking stair to the two low rooms over the shop where the living part of her life was kept.

Grietje had the fire going and a pot of grey pea-porridge keeping warm at its edge, which was the child's whole idea of housekeeping and more than Clara had asked of her. They ate it with dark rye bread and a little cheese and the small beer that stood in for water — thin, sour-sweet, the everyday drink, drawn from the barrel in the corner that Clara refilled from the brewer on the Turfmarkt each week and watered when the week ran long. Grietje drank hers with the exaggerated care of a child who has been told once too often what things cost. She had grown up, this one, in a house where the cost of things was always in the air, and it had made her careful in a way that sometimes wrung Clara's heart and sometimes only made her tired.

"Was it Doncker today," the child asked, "at the market. Barent said his man was watching."

"It was Doncker. Eat."

"Barent says he wants to steal your stall for his nephew."

"Barent says a great deal for a boy paid to grind and not to talk." But she let it go, because it was true, and because she had long since decided the child was better armed knowing the shape of the wolves than kept soft in ignorance of them. Neeltje would not have agreed. Neeltje had wanted Grietje kept soft, kept a child, and had died of a chest-fever at thirty leaving the child to be raised hard by a sister who did not know any other way to raise anything. Clara thought of her sister more in this room than anywhere — Neeltje at this same table, laughing at Clara's blue hands, calling her the drudge and herself the pretty one, both of them younger and neither of them knowing what was coming for either. She had been the pretty one. It had done her no more good in the end than Clara's plainness had done Clara harm. The fever took the pretty and the plain the same.

"Was my mother pretty," Grietje asked, out of the nowhere children ask such things, mopping her bowl with the crust. She asked it now and then, always as if for the first time, always at night. She had been five when Neeltje died and what she owned of her mother was going the way the fine blue went in the wash — the coarse first, the memory of a face, then the finer things, the voice, until only the smallest, best particles hung on, and Clara did not know which of them would settle out of the child and be lost.

"She was the pretty one," Clara said. "Fair, where I'm dark. She'd have laughed to be asked. She liked to be looked at, your mother, and I never did, and we suited each other for it — she took the looking and left me the working, and neither of us minded." It was near enough true, and the parts that weren't the child did not need. "She sang. You've her voice for singing, not mine, thank God. And she couldn't grind colour to save her life. Wasted more blue than she ever washed. Our father despaired of her."

"You don't despair of me."

"Not yet." Clara looked at the small serious face across the candle. "Give me time."

The child smiled, which was what the dryness was for, and Clara gathered the bowls to have something to do with her hands, and did not say the rest of it — that she saw Neeltje in the child at the worst moments and the best, a tilt of the head, a stubbornness, and that raising her sister's daughter was a slow argument she went on having with a dead woman about how a life should be met, softly or hard, and that she suspected they had both been wrong, and that the child would decide it between them without knowing there was an argument at all.

When the bowls were cleared Clara did not send the child up to bed. Instead she set the candle in the middle of the board, and went down to the cold shop and came back up with the small dish of washed blue she'd left settling and a shallow pan and a jug of clean water. It was time. She had been putting it off, telling herself the child's hands were too young, too rough, too likely to waste. But her own hands had begun this trade at not much more than Grietje's age, over a slab in a poorer town than Delft, and there was no one else. There would never be anyone else. That was a thing she had stopped grieving and started planning around.

"I'm going to show you the washing," she said. "Sit. Watch first, hands in your lap."

She showed her. The coarse-ground blue, still full of its dross, stirred into water in the pan; the swirl and the settling, the way the heavy grit and the pale calcite fell fast and the finest blue hung longest in the cloud; the pouring-off, at the exact right moment, of the topmost water into a second pan, carrying the best and lightest colour with it and leaving the coarse behind. Then again. Then again. Each wash a purer, dearer, smaller blue. It was the whole secret of the trade in a nutshell, and it was maddeningly simple and nearly impossible to do well, and Clara had spent a life learning the precise breath of a moment when to pour and when to wait.

"The first water off is the least," she said, pouring. "The dross. The last water holds nothing at all. It's the middle you live by — and the very best, the true blue—" she tilted the second pan and let the child see the deep cloud hanging in it before it settled— "the very best is the slowest to fall and the smallest in the end. You wash a whole knuckle of stone for a pinch of it. You save that pinch. You don't sell it to a man who owes you six gulden." She heard herself and almost smiled. "You save it against a day worth it."

Grietje watched the blue settle with her whole face. "Show me," she said, and put her hands out, and Clara let her — guided her, hand over the small hands, through one wash and then another, the child's tongue between her teeth, the water clouding and clearing, and something passed between them across the pan that Clara had no word for and did not try to find one for, because to name it would be to admit how much of her hope had quietly moved into this child while she was not watching. Craft went down a family this way or it died. Love went down the same way, apparently, whether you meant it to or not, hand over hand across a pan of dirty water by one candle.

"Enough," Clara said at last, when the good blue was settling in the second pan and the child's hands were blue to the wrist and would stay so for days. "You've a start. It's slow work and it doesn't love you back and you'll do it a thousand times before you do it well. Off with you, it's late."

But Grietje lingered, drying her stained hands on the rag, looking at them front and back in the candlelight with an expression Clara recognised — the child had found she liked having blue hands, liked the mark of the trade on her, the way Clara at her age had liked it, before she learned what it cost. "Now I've got them too," the child said, pleased, holding them up. "Bluest hands in Delft. Both of us."

"Bed," said Clara, to keep from saying anything else.

When the child was up and the boards creaking overhead and going still, Clara sat on alone at the table with the candle and the ledger, as she did most nights, to put the day into figures. It was a plain reckoning and it did not lie the way people did. Fabritius, eight gulden, owing. Two potters, paid. The umber to the guild, delivered, not yet paid, and would be paid late. The rent on the shop, due at the quarter. The payment to the guild-chest, kept up. The small pile of what came in, the larger pile of what went out, and the gap between them that she closed each month with a little more of her own strength ground into colour, and could not close forever. Doncker was right about the arithmetic. She knew it better than he did. The stall did not quite pay. The trade did not quite hold. She was carrying it on her back down a slope, and one bad season, one lost customer, one turn of the guild's mind, and it would come down on her.

She ran her finger down the owings the way she did each night, not to change them — figures did not change for being stared at — but to hold each name a moment and weigh it. The painters were the worst of it and the dearest. There was the drinker on the Molslaan who owed since spring and would pay when his sea-piece sold, if it sold, if he lived to finish it. There was the portrait-man who paid promptly and painted badly and whom she could not afford to lose. There was Fabritius, eight now, whom any prudent woman would have cut off a year ago, and against whose name she felt the thing she had felt across the counter that morning and would not write down. A ledger held everything but the truth of why you kept the accounts you kept. She had learned to read the silences in it, the debts she would forgive and the ones she would chase, and it came to her, turning the page, that Gerrit's hand still ran through the older entries, his crossings-out, his little marks against a name that meant *press this one* or *let him be*, so that the book was a place they still kept the trade together, the two of them, his dead hand and her blue one down the same columns. She did not dwell on it. She had trained herself out of dwelling. But some nights the ledger was the nearest thing to his company she had left, and she sat with it longer than the accounts required.

Except for the pot. She sat in the candlelight and let herself think of it, once, the way she permitted herself to think of it only late and alone. The grey gallipot in the dark cupboard below, heavy with the true blue, the best of years of the best water, the pinch on pinch she had never sold. Worth more than the shop over her head. Enough, if the right commission came — a church wanting a great blue robe, a rich man wanting a wife immortal in a colour that would not green or die — enough to sell out from under the whole leaning weight of it at once. Clear Gerrit's debts to the last stuiver. Buy the stall outright, or a better one, past the reach of Doncker's patience. Set something by for the child. It was not a fortune. It was a door. She had ground it grain by grain out of stone and dark mornings, and she was keeping it against the day the door appeared, and the keeping of it was the one extravagance and the one hope she allowed herself, sitting up past the candle's worth of light while the frost tightened its grip on the town.

She closed the ledger. She banked the fire down to a red eye. Outside, past the frozen dyers' water and the sleeping street and the dark bulk of the Doelen, the old convent of the Poor Clares held its eighty thousand pounds of powder quietly through the cold, as it had every night of her life here, and she did not think of it, no more than she thought of the tower of the Oude Kerk leaning over her sister's grave or the salt in the far-off sea. She took up the candle and climbed the shrieking stair past the third step and looked in on the child, who slept with her blue hands open on the coverlet and her treasures in a row on the sill — the tile-shard, the button, the pebble, the gold that was not gold — catching the last of the light. Clara pinched the candle out.

It was the eleventh of October, and it was, though no one in the house had any way to know it, the last ordinary night any of them would have. She slept, and did not dream of blue, and the frost held the town till dawn.

## 5. The Twelfth of October

The blue that had settled overnight was ready to pour, and Clara stood over the second pan in the grey shop-light with the jug in her hand, waiting for the exact moment the water would tell her had come. It was slow work and it did not love you back; she had said as much to the child, and it was true, and she did it anyway, the way you keep faith with a hard thing because you have kept faith with it so long that stopping would be a kind of dying. The dross had gone down in the night. The true blue hung above it in a deep still cloud, the best of the last knuckle of stone, and she tipped the jug and poured the topmost water off clean into the waiting dish, carrying the finest of it over and leaving the coarse behind, and did not spill a grain. Her hands were steady. Whatever else went, the hands stayed steady. She had ground that steadiness into them across a marriage and a widowhood both.

It was an ordinary morning and it looked like every ordinary morning she had. The frost of the night had not lifted so much as loosened, gone from hard white to a grey damp that stood in beads on the inside of the shutters and would not dry. The light came flat and low and without any sun in it, the colourless Delft light that painters cursed and could not do without, a light that gave a thing its true weight and no flattery. Clara had learned to read the day by it the way a sailor reads water. Today it said: cold, still, no rain yet, trade thin. A Monday after a hard frost. Nobody's kiln firing, nobody's fee come in, nobody wanting colour they could put off wanting till the week turned.

"It's gone through the boards again," Grietje announced from the stair, meaning the cold, coming down with her blanket round her and her fists full of the morning's inventory. She crossed to the sill of the shop-window where she had shifted her treasures — the tile-shard, the horn button, the river pebble, the pyrite that was gold and not gold — and set them in their row along the wood, in the order she kept them, dearest last. She checked them each morning the way Clara checked the grey pot: to be sure. Then she gathered them back into her fist, all four, the way she always carried them; the sill was for the counting, not the keeping. "The button's cold. Everything's cold. Can I have bread before I wash?"

"You can have bread and then you'll wipe the slab down for Barent, and mind you do the corners, he leaves the corners." Clara set the poured dish to settle and went to cut the rye, and the child ate it standing at the window with her breath fogging the glass, looking out at the frozen dyers' water and the street coming awake, and Clara let her, because there was time, because it was only Monday, because the whole shapeless plenty of an ordinary day lay ahead of them and there was no reason on earth to hurry a child through her bread.

A customer came and went before nine, the potter's boy from the Molslaan for a paper of the umber and a half of the ochre, counting his master's coin out slow onto the counter in the poor light, and Clara wrapped his earths and took his stuivers and asked after the kiln, and he said it was firing Thursday if the frost broke and going cold if it didn't, and went. That was the morning's whole trade and she had expected no more. She stood the coin in the box and thought, without heat, that Doncker was right about the arithmetic, that the stall did not quite pay and the trade did not quite hold and one bad season would bring it down on her back; and then she stopped thinking it, because thinking it changed nothing, and there was blue to watch and a slab to keep and the long ordinary middle of a Monday to get through, and the getting-through was the only answer she had ever found to the arithmetic. You did the work in front of you. You paid what you owed. That was the only order there was.

Barent came in on the half-light with his shoulders up round his ears and a paper of something from the apothecary that Clara had sent him for on Saturday and he had forgotten until this morning, which was the way of him. "Litharge," he said, setting it down, pleased to have remembered at last. "And he says the price of the lead's gone up again, the whole lot of it, on account of the war-talk, and to tell you." He blew on his fingers and looked, first thing, at the blue in the dish the way he always looked at it, up past the shine to what the shine was for. "You poured the good one."

"I poured the good one. Wash your hands before you touch a thing, they're black to the wrist." She looked at him, big and cold-pinked and glad to be in out of the weather, fifteen and eating a week's bread in a sitting, and she thought, as she thought most mornings and never said, that she would have to do something for him before the year turned — put by a little toward his guild-fee somehow, or speak to a master who might article him, or at the least tell him plainly that she saw the hunger in him and did not think it foolish. She kept meaning to. There was always time. "Grind the umber first," she said instead. "The guild's panels. And don't leave it gritty."

"They'll blame you and pay you late," Barent said, in her own voice, grinning, and she did not deny it, and the two mullers went round on the two slabs, his heavy and quick, hers light and slow, and the child sang something with no tune at the window, and the peat-smoke came in low and brown through the shutter-cracks, the smell of the whole street breathing at once. It was, she would think later, the last of it. The last hour when the world was only itself, dense and cold and dull and entirely, unbearably ordinary, and she moved through it not knowing to hold it, because you never know to hold it, because if you knew you could hold nothing else.

She sent Barent out mid-morning for small beer, the barrel run low, and stood a moment in her own doorway while he went, drying her hands on the apron that never dried them, taking the air. The Verwersdijk lay grey and busy. A dyer two doors down cursed his frozen vat and beat at the crust of it with a paddle, and his wife, come out to shake a cloth, called across the frozen water to Clara that her man had walked to the fish-stall and found the herring dear and come home with none, and wasn't it always the way, and Clara agreed that it was always the way, and the woman went in, and it was nothing, a scrap of morning passed between two women over a frozen canal, and Clara would remember it after with a clarity that made no sense, the cloth, the cold, the price of herring, the last dull ordinary words traded on that street before the street stopped being.

The light was the thing she kept, though. She stood in it a moment longer than the errand asked, a widow with nothing much to sell and no sun to work by, and let herself simply see it, the way Fabritius was always at her to see. It was the colourless Delft light at its purest, the light with no warmth and no flattery in it, coming down even and grey out of a sky the same grey, so that the frozen dyers' water and the brick house-fronts and the barge of turf and the far tower of the Nieuwe Kerk all stood in it with their true weights and no shadows to speak of, each thing exactly and soberly itself. The bells had not long since told the half after ten from the Nieuwe Kerk, the notes coming flat across the frozen roofs, and the barge-man was working a punt of turf down the thin channel his own pole kept open in the ice, and the whole reeking working street went about its Monday under that weightless light, and it was, she thought, a poor light to sell colour by and a true one to see by, and she had lived in it eleven years and never once, until it was taken, thought to be grateful for the plain grey ordinariness of a morning in which nothing whatever was wrong.

Cornelis Soetens crossed the end of the dijk toward the Geerweg with his lantern in his hand.

She knew him to nod to, the way you know the men who keep the town's dull machinery — the lock-keeper, the weigh-house clerk, the keeper of the powder store, ordinary men in ordinary cloth doing the work that let the rest of you not think about it. He was unhurried. He had another man with him she did not know, a stranger in better cloth, from The Hague by the cut of him, some clerk come to see the powder counted or sampled or signed for, the sort of dull errand that brought dull men out on a cold Monday. Soetens had his keys at his belt and the lantern unlit in his hand, swinging a little as he walked, and he saw her in her doorway and lifted his free hand.

"Vrouw Wijnants. Cold enough for you."

"Cold enough to freeze the trade solid, meester Soetens. Mind the ice on the Geerweg, they've not sanded it."

"They never sand it." He said it comfortably, a man on a dull round he had walked ten thousand times, and touched two fingers to his hat, and went on toward the old convent of the Poor Clares with its eighty thousand pounds of powder behind the walls that had once kept nuns, to take his sample in the dark and light his lantern to see by. That was all. An ordinary greeting between two people who kept the town's small trades, on a grey morning, about the mind-your-footing weather. She watched him a moment out of no reason at all — a widow at her door with nothing to sell and the light too poor to work by, taking the air — and then she went back into the shop, because the blue wanted watching and the child wanted the corners done and there was the whole ordinary day yet to break.

She had her hand on the grey pot in the dark of the low cupboard. That was where she was. She had knelt to check it, the way she checked it, her fingers to the cool of it in the dark to be sure it was there and heavy and hers, the door and the way out and the one card Doncker could not price, and Grietje was at the slab wiping the corners as told, and Barent's foot was on the threshold coming back with the beer, and the flat grey light lay over all of it, the pots and the child and the boy and the blue-stained hands, when the world drew its breath.

There was a moment before the sound. That was the thing she would never make anyone understand who had not stood in it. A moment when the air itself changed, when a pressure came from nowhere and pushed against her ears and her chest and the backs of her eyes all at once, a great soft blow with no fist behind it, so that her body knew before her mind could that something enormous had already happened two streets off and was only now arriving. The child looked up. The pot was cool under her hand. And then the light went to white — not a flash across the dark but the whole grey day gone instantly, blindingly, silently white, the shutters and the pots and the small serious face all swallowed in it — and the floor was not under her, and the wall was not beside her, and there was no up, and the white became a roar that was not a sound because it was too large to be a sound, it was inside her teeth and her ribs and the marrow of her, and something struck her, and something else, and she was lifted and turned in a great tearing that took the room apart around her like a fist closing on a paper house, and she reached for the child in the white with a hand she could no longer feel, and did not find her, and the roar folded down over all of it and put out the light, and Clara Wijnants fell a long way into the dark and the dark took her whole.

## 6. In the Dust

She came back to a weight on her chest and grit in her mouth and no sound at all.

For a long time — she had no way to measure it, there was no time down there, only before and now — she did not know she was a person, or where a person's edges were, or that the black pressing on her had a name. She knew the weight. She knew she could not fill her chest against it. She knew the taste, chalk and lime and something under it like scorched iron, and she knew, distantly, the way you know a thing in a fever, that she was breathing very small breaths through a cloth of dust and that each one was a decision she had to make again. She made it again. Then again. That was all there was, for a while: the next breath, argued for in the dark.

Her hand moved. That was the first thing that was hers — her right hand, the blue one, closing on nothing, on grit, on a broken edge of something that cut it, and the small clean pain of the cut was a rope thrown to her and she took hold of it and climbed. She was Clara. She was under the shop. There had been a white. There had been — the pressure came back to her whole, the soft enormous blow — and with it the name of the child, and she tried to say it and got a mouthful of dust and could not, and the not-being-able turned the black around her from a place she was drowning in to a thing she meant to get out of.

She dug. There is no other word for what she did, and it was nothing like the word. She could not see. She could barely move the arm that was pinned and could move the other only a hand's breadth at a time, and she worked that hand into the dark above her face and pushed at what was there — plaster, first, soft and heavy and endless, the whole pulverised inside of her own walls come down to powder, and she pushed it aside a fistful at a time and it ran back over her wrist. Then a harder thing, angled across her, a beam, one of the black oak joists that had held the floor of the child's room over her head for a hundred years, and it had come down slantwise and it was the beam that had saved her, she understood that later, the beam had taken the weight of the falling house on its own back and left her a crooked pocket of air beneath it no bigger than a coffin. She got her shoulder under it. She braced her feet against something that gave and then something that held, and she pushed against the whole dead weight of the ruin with the strength she had ground into her back across eleven years of the muller, and the beam lifted, a finger, a hand, and rubble ran into the space where she had been, and she dragged herself up through the shifting dark toward a place where the black was going grey.

She came out of the ruin of her own shop the way the blue came out of the stone — by breaking, by ruin, by the patient working-free of one thing from all it was bound to — and she came out into a world she did not know.

She got to her knees on a slope of broken brick that had been her house, and she looked up, and there was too much sky. That was the first wrongness, before her eyes cleared enough for any other: the sky was where the street had been. The whole close weight of the Verwersdijk, the leaning house-fronts, the roofs that had shut out the sky her whole life here, were gone, and in their place was a low grey heaven full of dust, hanging, turning, coming down soft as snow over a plain of rubble that ran on and on to where the shapes of standing walls began again, far off, farther than any street should let you see. She knew the place and did not know it. There was the stump of the dyer's house two doors down, one wall of it standing with a cooking-pot still hung in the hearth and the rest gone to a heap. There was the canal, choked with brick and a drowned punt and a slick of something on the black water. There was a door, a whole green-painted door, standing up out of the rubble by itself with no house behind it. The dust came down over all of it and greyed it all the same grey, so that the ruin looked a hundred years old already, an old ruin, a thing that had always been broken.

And it was silent. That was the horror she was least ready for and would carry the longest. Her ears gave her nothing — the blast had taken her hearing down to a high thin ring and a great muffling, so that she moved through the wreck of the world as through deep water — and into that silence the town had not yet begun to scream. There was a moment, kneeling in the falling dust, when she thought she might be the only living thing left under the grey sky, that the whole world had been ground to this and she alone left to see it. Then her ears began to come back, or the town began, she never knew which came first, and the sound arrived: not one sound but the start of all of them, thin and far and then nearer, a woman somewhere calling one word over and over, a man screaming without words, a dog, a crackle that her mind refused for a moment and then named. Fire. Away to her right, where the ruin ran up toward the Doelen and the Geerweg, a red light was working in the grey, low and spreading, taking the broken timber the way the peat took a spark.

She turned back to the ruin. She did not decide to. The part of her that decided things had been left down in the dark with the beam. What turned her was older than deciding. Grietje. Barent. They had been an arm's length from her when the white came — the child at the slab, the boy on the threshold — and she went back into the broken house on her hands and knees, back down into the thing she had just clawed out of, throwing brick and plaster behind her like an animal digging, calling the child's name into the dust with a voice she could not hear herself make.

She found the slab first. The great porphyry slab, Gerrit's slab, dark red and dished in the middle by two men's working, was cracked clean across and half-buried, and near it, thrown against the one standing scrap of the back wall, the low cupboard was burst open and its pots smashed to a rubble of their own, umber and ochre and the white all gone to a trodden smear in the grey. And there, flung wide across the broken brick where the blast had thrown it, was the blue. The grey pot was gone — she found a curve of it later, a grey shard among the grey — and the true ultramarine, the best of years, the pinch on pinch washed grain by grain against the day the door would open, lay scattered across the ruin in a long blue stain, a smear of impossible colour thrown down into the dust and the trodden white, the one bright thing in a world gone grey, the door blown open at last onto nothing. She saw it and it did not touch her. That was how she knew what kind of morning it was: she knelt in the spilled ruin of her whole way out, the blue that was worth more than the house now buried under the house, and she felt nothing about it at all, because she was listening, under the ringing, for a child's breath.

She heard it. Or felt it — a small shifting, off to the left, under the wreck of the stair. The shrieking stair, the one with the third step that had cried out under small careful feet every dawn of the child's life here, had come down folded on itself against the surviving corner of the back wall, and it had come down the way the beam had, crookedly, meanly, leaving a wedge of dark beneath it, and in that dark, when Clara had torn the treads away with her bleeding hands, was Grietje.

She was alive. She lay on her side in the little cave the fallen stair had made, curled the way she slept, grey from head to foot with the dust so that only her eyes were the child's, open and enormous and moving, tracking Clara's face as she tore the last of the wreck away. There was blood, darkened almost black with dust, down the side of the small head and matting the hair where something had struck her, and one arm was held against her the wrong way, and a great livid weal was rising across the cheek. But she was alive, she was breathing, she was looking at Clara with those two washed eyes in the grey mask, and Clara got her arms under the small body and drew her out into the falling dust and held her, held her whole against her chest, rocking, saying her name, and could not hear whether she said it aloud.

"Grietje. Grietje. I have you. I have you now."

The child did not answer. Clara drew back to see the small face and the child looked at her, straight and knowing, her eyes going over Clara's features one by one as though checking them against a row on a windowsill, and her mouth moved, and no sound came. She was not senseless. She knew Clara. She held to Clara's sleeve with her good hand hard enough to hurt. But when Clara said her name again and again and begged her, once, to say something, only say something, the child only looked at her out of that terrible knowing stillness, and did not speak, and Clara understood with a cold drop of her heart that the child had gone somewhere behind her own eyes and pulled the door to, and that no calling was going to fetch her back today.

She did not let herself feel it. There was still Barent.

She sat the child against the one standing corner, out of the worst of the falling grit, and put the small good hand to grip a broken baulk of timber and folded her own hand over it a moment so the child would know to hold, and then she went back, one more time, into the ruin, toward the threshold where the boy had been.

She found him under the fallen front of the house, where the whole street wall had come in on the doorway. She knew him by his boot. She dug to him with her hands past all sense, past the fire's growing light off to the right and the dust still sifting down and the town's screaming coming up now on every side, she dug to the boy who could grind all day and eat a week's bread in a sitting and wanted the guild so badly it came off him like heat, and when she had cleared the weight from him she saw at once, in the way the body lay, in the stillness that was not the child's stillness, that there was no calling him back either, not today, not ever, that Barent had been on the threshold with the beer in his hands when the wall came down and had not known even the white, had gone from wanting everything to nothing between one cold breath and the next. She knelt over him in the grey. She put her blue hand on his cold cheek, gently, the way you settle a sleeper you do not mean to wake. The dust came down over both of them and greyed them the same grey.

She did not weep. That would come, later, when there was a later, when there was time and safety enough to afford it; grief was an extravagance and she could not spare it yet, no more than she could spare the blue lying scattered and worthless in the wreck behind her. She knelt one moment with her hand on the boy, and then she took off her own shawl, stiff with dust, and laid it over his face, because it was the only thing she had to give him and because she could not bear the dust coming down into his open eyes. Then she went back to the child, who had not moved, who was watching the fire with her grey face and her ruined arm and her shut voice, and Clara gathered her up against the ruin of her own body and stood, swaying, and turned her back on the boy and the blue and the broken slab and everything she had built and kept and hoped, and carried the living child out over the wreck of the world toward the one place her unhinged mind could still name, because she had to know, because he too had been two streets from the Secret, because there was nothing left to do with the horror but carry it toward the next thing.

## 7. At the Easel

She should not have gone. There was no sense in it, and the part of her that kept sense had not come back yet, and so she went. The child was hurt and needed a surgeon and there was no surgeon, needed water and there was no clean water, needed to be laid down somewhere out of the cold and there was no somewhere; the whole quarter was a plain of broken brick with the fire walking through it, and into that Clara walked the other way, north and east, toward the Doelen, toward the studio near the shooting-ground where a man painted light no one else in Delft could paint, carrying an eight-year-old who would not speak, because he owed her six gulden and she had promised to come and see whether he had got the blue right, and the promise had come loose in her head from everything that should have held it in place, and it was the only thing left in there she could still walk toward.

The way that should have been two turns of a familiar street was an hour of climbing over the unrecognisable. There were no streets. There was the memory of streets, a line here where the rubble ran lower between two heaps and you understood a lane had been, a bridge's stump, a well-head standing whole in a waste of brick. There were people now, moving as she moved, grey figures wading through the grey, some digging with their hands where a house had been and calling down into it, some walking with the particular slowness of the stunned, some carrying others. A man passed her with a door for a stretcher and a shape on it under a coat. A woman sat in what had been a doorway with a child in her lap and rocked and did not look up, and Clara could not tell from any distance whether the child in the lap was hurt or worse, and did not go to see, because she had her own in her arms and the arithmetic of that hour was terrible and simple: you carried your own. She held Grietje's grey face against her shoulder so the child would not see the things she was carrying her past. Some of them were people. Some of them had been people that morning and were now only shapes the blast had made, thrown and broken and greyed, past knowing, past name, a boot, a hand, a stillness in the rubble that the eye learned in one hour to slide across because it could not do anything else and stay a mind. She saw more of the dead in that crossing than she had seen in her whole life before, and not one of them whole, and not one of them she could have named, and she understood, wading through it, that no one would ever count them, that they had gone past counting in the white, that whole houses full of the living had become part of the grey plain she was climbing and would be dug for and not found.

She smelled the fire before she reached the Doelen, and under it another smell she would not name. The shooting-ground quarter had taken the worst of it, being nearest; here the ruin was not even heaped but flattened, pounded, the walls not fallen so much as gone, and the fire had come through and was still in it, working low and red in the broken timber, and the dust was mixed now with smoke that caught in the chest. She found the studio by the pear tree. There had been a pear tree in the little yard behind the house where Fabritius kept his painting-room, an old espaliered pear against a south wall, and the wall was down and the tree was down but the shape of it lay there, the trained black branches flung out flat over the rubble like a hand pressed to the ground, and by it she knew the place, because the house that had stood over it was not a house any more.

He was near the easel. That was the thing she would carry longest out of that whole grey day, longer than the blue in the dust, longer than the boy under her shawl: that they had been at work. The blast had found them at the easel in the north light doing the thing they did, and it had brought the room down on them where they stood, and when Clara came over the broken wall into what had been the painting-room she saw the easel first, thrown and splintered but a easel still, and the panel near it face-down in the grit, and the man beyond it in the rubble, and she knew before she reached him. He lay half under the fallen north wall, the good wall, the one that had given him his light, and it had given him his death; and there was a younger one nearer the door, a boy not much past Barent's age, the pupil, Mathias, who ground his master's colours and stretched his panels and had wanted, no doubt, everything Barent wanted, and he was dead too, quick and clean it looked, thrown against the doorframe that no longer led anywhere. There had been a third man there that morning, the deacon come to sit for his prosperous pious face; Clara did not look for him and did not find him and never learned. She set the child down against the fallen pear, gently, in the one clear space, and she went to the man near the easel.

He was not marked, much. That was the strange mercy and the strange horror of it both. The wall had come down and something in the fall had killed him, but his face was his face, greyed with the dust like all the faces, quiet, the restless measuring gone out of it at last, the eyes half-shut as though he had looked at something a long way off and was still looking. His big hands were open. There was blue on the fingers of the right one, her blue, the true ultramarine she had staked him more of than she should, ground into the creases where it would never come out, so that his hand and her hand were stained the same, and Clara knelt in the wreck of his studio and looked at that a long time, the shared blue on the two hands, hers living and his not, and something in her that had held all the way from the shop, all the way across the grey plain, all the way past the boy and the child's shut voice, something in her leaned and did not quite go over.

Around him lay the work. She saw it without meaning to, the colourman's eye adding up ruin as it had always added up colour: the panels stood and stacked against the walls that were no longer walls, cracked across, scorched at the edges where the fire had licked and gone on, faces and rooms and windows half-seen under the fallen laths, a whole life's looking come down to kindling in an afternoon. He had spent thirty-two years learning to see a thing where it truly stood, so much air before it and so much behind, the exact standing-apart of things in the light, and the light he had chased his whole life had come through the north wall this morning as it came every morning, and the wall had fallen on him in it. She thought of the perspective glass he had held across her counter, the world pulled an arm's length forward and cut clean from its background with a knife. The white had done that to the whole quarter. It had pulled everything forward and cut it from everything it had been, and left it standing sharp and separate and past all putting-back, and there was no seeing to be got out of that, only the looking, which she did, because someone had to, because he had taught her that the looking was the harder half and was done in the dark.

She heard the woman before she saw her. Agatha had been out of the house when it came — at the market, at a neighbour's, Clara never learned and it did not matter, the small ordinary errand that had put a wall between her and the white — and she came now over the rubble the way Clara had come toward her own ruin, at a stumbling run that was not a run, and she came into the broken room and saw her husband near his easel and made a sound that Clara heard even through the ringing in her ears, a sound that was not a scream and was worse than a scream, low, and torn out of the body, the sound of a woman's whole life going down in front of her. She went to him and fell by him and gathered his greyed head up out of the dust into her lap the way Clara had gathered the child, and rocked, and said his name, and Clara, who could not comfort her and could not leave her, did the only thing there was, which was to kneel across the body from her and be there, a second living woman in the wreck, so that Agatha did not do it entirely alone.

They did not speak for a long while. There was nothing to say that the ruin had not already said. At last Clara moved, because the fire was working nearer and the smoke was thickening and there was a hurt child against the pear tree, and she began, gently, to draw the fallen laths and the broken plaster off the rest of him, to free him from the wall that had held him, so that at least he need not lie half-buried in it while the fire came. And it was in doing that, in clearing the rubble away from near the easel with her raw blue hands, that she uncovered the panel.

It had fallen face-down. She turned it over because her hands were clearing everything and it was a thing to be cleared, and it came up out of the dust into the smoke-grey light and it was the bird. The goldfinch. The little *puttertje* on the topmost ring of its feeder, the loose dragged paint that was hardly paint at all up close and was wholly a living bird a step back, the thin chain running from the ring to the band about its leg, the pale warm-and-cool wall behind with the bird's own faint shadow on it — whole. Not a crack across it. Not a scorch. A skin of dust lay over it and under the dust the bird sat in its painted light exactly as it had sat on her counter, holding still to be seen, and Clara knelt in the wreck of the man who had made it, with the fire coming and the dead all round, and looked at the one thing in the quarter that the white had spared, and could not make it mean anything and could not look away from it. *The thing that holds it there is the thing that lets me see it true.* He had said that, across her counter, in the cold, a lifetime ago, that morning. She did not know what to do with it. She had not known then either.

She wiped the dust from it with the edge of her sleeve, without thinking, the way you clean a child's face.

Agatha saw it. Clara held it out to her across the body, not knowing what else to do with a thing so light and so final in a place where everything else was broken, and the woman looked at the bird a long moment through her ruined face, and did not take it. "Not to burn," she said. Clara felt the words more than heard them. "Everything else is gone. Not that too. Take it — take it away from the fire. You made the — " and she stopped, because there was no colour in it that Clara had made, it was ochre and lead and black and a breath of red, he had spent nothing costly on it at all, and they both knew that and it did not matter; what she meant was *you knew him, you knew what it was, take it where it will not burn.* "Take it," she said again, and bent back over her husband, and would not be moved from him, and Clara understood she was being asked to save the one thing and leave the woman with the other.

So she took it. She took nothing else out of that place — not the brushes, not the ground colours spilled in the rubble, not the other panels she could see the corners of, cracked and scorched, the vanishing work of a man the world had barely begun to see; she left it all to the fire because she could carry a child or she could carry a life's work and she could not carry both, and she had already chosen the child that morning without knowing she was choosing. But the little bird she wrapped in the dust-stiff rag from about her own shoulders, and put inside her bodice against her heart the way he had put her blue against his, and she gathered up Grietje from against the fallen pear, and she looked one last time at Agatha bowed over the body in the smoke, a woman she could neither comfort nor abandon and was now, terribly, abandoning, and she said, "I'll come back for you. When there's help. I'll come back," and did not know if it was heard or true, and turned, and carried the living child and the saved bird out of the fire, back the long grey way she had come, into a town that no longer had a shape.

## 8. The Reckoning

You cannot count what will not hold still to be counted, and the dead of the twelfth would not.

That was the thing Clara came to understand in the days after, the days that ran together into one grey unslept smear the way the washed colours ran when you poured too soon: that there would be no number. In the ordinary run of dying a town knew its dead. It laid them out at home, and washed them, and named them in the church-book, and put them in the ground it kept for them, one grave, one name, a plain Reformed burial and the bell if you could pay for the bell. That was order. That was how the town told itself who it was. The white had taken even that. Whole households were simply gone — the family, the house, the record of the house, the neighbours who might have said what family it was — pounded into the grey plain together, past name, past finding, and the men who dug through the rubble in those days brought up more that could not be known than could, so that the counting broke down almost before it began. Some said more than a hundred. Some said many hundreds. Some, later, said a thousand and more, the way a stricken town will reach for a number large enough to hold its horror, and the truth was that nobody knew, and nobody would ever know, and the not-knowing was itself a kind of wound the town would carry: that it could not even say how many of its own it had lost.

The carts went by all day. Clara heard them from the cold back room of her mother's cousin's house on the Oude Delft, the far side of the town from the ruin, where she had carried Grietje that first night and where the old woman had taken them in without a word because that was what you did — heard the carts on the stones, the slow iron-rimmed wheels, going down toward the ground the churches kept and, when that would hold no more, toward the pits dug hasty at the edge of the quarter for the ones who could not be named. There was no keeping the old order in it. You did not lay out and wash and name a thing the blast had made unnameable. You gave it what ground you had, quickly, before the sickness that comes after such things could start, and you said what words could be said over the many at once, and the town, which had always buried its dead one careful name at a time, buried them now the way the blue was ground, in the mass, past telling one grain from another.

She saw the chronicler once in those days. She had gone back toward the ruin — she had to, there were things there, though she could not yet have said what, and the pull of it was not reason but something under reason — and she saw a soberly-dressed man picking his way through the wreck of the Doelen quarter with a boy carrying his ink, stopping, looking, writing standing up with the board on his forearm, writing it down. Someone said his name, Bleyswijck, the one who kept the town's history, and Clara watched him a while from across a heap of what had been houses. He did not weep and he did not gape. He looked at the ruin the way she looked at a colour, a long time before he touched it, and set down what he saw in his small steady hand, and there was something in it she was grateful for past reason, the same gratitude she'd felt for the quiet painter counting out his honest coin while her hands shook: that someone would witness it plainly, would write *this was here, this many houses, this church's glass blown out, this quarter that was and is not,* would hold the shape of the thing in words when the thing itself had lost its shape. It would be judged worse than the great fire of the old century, she heard him say to the boy with the ink. He said it flatly, a man measuring, and wrote it down.

The child did not speak in any of it. She went where Clara put her and held what Clara gave her to hold and ate a little, when Clara sat with her and coaxed it, of the bread the cousin could spare; and she watched everything, the carts and the diggers and the queues at the one working pump, with the grey knowing stillness she had brought out of the wreck of the stair, and she did not say one word. Clara talked to her all the same, low, all day, the way you talk to keep a hurt animal company — this is the way, mind the ice, hold my sleeve, we'll have soup when we're warm — and got nothing back but the small hand tightening, and told herself it was the blow to the head and would lift, and did not let herself count the days it was not lifting, because there was a great deal that week she could not afford to count.

They found Barent for her. She had marked the place as well as she could that first day — the fallen front of the house, the boot — and told the men who dug, and they brought him out on the third day, and there was that much mercy in it at least: that he had a body to bury, whole enough to know, when so many had not. She buried him with what burial there was. It was not the plain decent Reformed funeral a boy should have had, the walk and the bell and the named grave; there was no coin for a bell in that week and no bell to be had and no separate ground to spare, and he went into the hasty part of the churchyard with too many others and a young preacher saying the words fast over the row of them, hoarse, having said them too many times already that day. But Clara stood over him while it was done, which was more than most of that grey week got, and she had washed the dust from his face herself the night before with clean water she had queued an hour for, so that the boy who had tracked dirt across her floor every morning of two years went into the ground clean, and she stood there with Grietje's silent hand in hers and did not weep even then, because the child was watching her face to know how the world was to be met, and Clara would not teach her, that day, that it was to be met by going down. She thought: *he wanted the guild so badly it came off him like heat, and he had no six gulden, and now he has no need of them.* She thought: *a man at his easel still needs somebody to have washed his blue.* She had said that to him, a lifetime ago, meaning it kindly, and it was the last true thing she had told him and she was glad, at least, that the last thing had been true.

The town began, even in that week, to be a town again, which is to say the living began to reach for what living things reach for, and not all of it was fine. Word came quickly that the States of Holland had voted money toward the relief — a great sum, a hundred thousand gulden, the figure passed hand to hand in the queues and the ruins like a warmth people held to — and roof-tiles were to be given out to those whose houses might yet be mended, and both those things were true and neither did Clara any good, whose house was not to be mended but was gone to the grey plain past mending. And already, before the dust had well settled, before the pits at the quarter's edge were filled, there were men making a trade of it. She heard of the tiles resold, the relief-tiles meant to be given, bought up cheap or taken and sold on dear to the desperate at a profit, and she was not even surprised, because she had spent her life watching appetite dress itself as something better, and a catastrophe was only a market like any other to the men who read it that way. There would be fortunes made out of the twelfth of October. There always were, out of such days. The town would be twenty years rebuilding and there was money in every year of it for the men positioned to take it.

Doncker came to the cousin's house on the Oude Delft before the week was out. She had known he would, the way you know the cold will come up through the floor. He came correct and sober and grave, his good cloth brushed, his blue-knuckled hands folded, and he stood in the low doorway of the cold back room and said the right things in the right order — his sorrow, his shock, the horror of it, the mercy that she and the child were spared — and Clara stood with her ruined hands at her sides and let him say them, and waited, because she knew there was a second thing, and there was. He did not press it hard. He did not need to. He only let her see, gently, in the register of a man performing patience as a public service, that the ground her shop had stood on was rubble now and her stock was gone into the mud and the market itself was in question, half its stalls emptied by the blast, the guild in disarray, and that when the time came to settle who would trade colour in the rebuilt town and where and by what right, a widow with no shop and no stock and no boy and a right the guild had only ever grudged her would find the door, which had always been leaned-to, very easy now to close. He was sorry for it. He would help her, if she would let him, to a decent settlement while there was anything left to settle. The offer stood. He inclined his head, correct as a deacon, and went, and Clara stood in the doorway and watched him pick his careful way back up the Oude Delft, and understood that the white had done in an hour what his patience had been years about, and that he knew it, and that he grieved the dead sincerely and would take her trade all the same, and saw no contradiction in it, and never would.

She had nothing left to answer him with. That was the plain reckoning, and it did not lie the way people did. The shop was gone. The slab was cracked in two under a fallen house. The stock was trodden into the grey. Barent was in the hasty ground. The boy who owed her eight gulden was dead at his easel and she would never be paid and did not want to be. And the blue — the hoard, the door, the way out she had ground grain by grain across a marriage and a widowhood and kept in a pot too plain to rob, the one card Doncker could not price — the blue was scattered across the ruin of her own back room under the weight of the whole fallen house, spilled into the trodden white and the broken brick and the mud, worth more than everything around it and worth exactly nothing where it lay, the finest colour in Delft gone down into the muck like all the rest.

She sat in the cold back room on the far side of the town with the child asleep against her, the child who had not said one word in eight days, whose arm the surgeon had set at last and whose head was healing and whose voice had gone somewhere behind her eyes and pulled the door to. On the sill beside the low bed the child had set out, that first evening, without a sound, the four things she had carried out of the ruin in her fist through all of it — the chip of blue-and-white tile, the horn button, the river pebble, and the pyrite, the gold that was not gold, worth nothing and kept anyway, dearest last — and Clara looked at them in the last grey light and could not think how a person began again from nothing, with a mute child and a dead boy and a lost trade and a rival at the door and the true blue lying in the frozen mud two streets from where the Secret of Holland had been. She had told the boy there was no shame in the harder half, the freeing of the blue in the dark from everything it was bound to. She had told the child you wash a whole knuckle of stone for a pinch of it, and you save the pinch, and you do not grieve the breaking. She had said all of it and believed it, and now she sat in a borrowed room with her hands as empty as they had ever been, and outside the early dark came down over the wrecked town, and the frost with it, hard, the first real frost of a winter that had not properly begun, and there was no fire worth the name and no shop to go back to and no colour to grind and no way she could see, that night, to the far side of any of it. Winter was coming on, and the blue was in the mud, and she did not know how to start.

# Ultramarine — Part Three: The Blue Hour


## 9. Ashes and Tiles

The ice on the water-butt had grown a finger thick in the night, and Clara broke it before the house woke, standing in the black yard in her shawl with the old woman's chopper, because the black hour before the town stirred was still the only hour that asked nothing of her, and she had never learned to sleep through it. She had spent a life meeting that hour over the porphyry slab, freeing blue out of stone by candle-light while the cold came up through the floor. Now she met it over a butt of frozen water in a yard that was not hers, breaking ice for a household whose charity wore thinner by the week, and her hands, that knew to the ounce how a muller should ride on its own weight, closed on the chopper's worn handle and did the dull work and did not complain. Complaining, like grief, was an extravagance. She had less of both to spare now than she had ever had.

She filled the two pails and stood a moment in the dark before she carried them in. The deep of the winter had come down on Delft in earnest, the hard clenched cold of the bad years, the canals frozen kerb to kerb and the snow lying grey and untrodden over the ruined quarter where nobody had the heart or the coin to clear it. From the cousin's yard on the Oude Delft she could not see the wreck, and was glad of that, and thought of it all the same, the way the tongue keeps going to the broken tooth. Two streets of rubble under the snow. And somewhere in it, trodden into the frozen mud of what had been her own back room, the blue.

She did not let herself stand long with the thought. She carried the water in.

The cold back room where they had been given leave to sleep held a bed, a stool, a chest that was not theirs, and the last grey of the fire the old woman allowed them, banked to nothing overnight so that the room by morning was scarcely warmer than the yard. Grietje slept in the middle of the bed with her knees drawn up and her mended arm held careful even in sleep, and on the sill above her the four things stood in their row, catching the first grey off the snow: the chip of blue-and-white tile, the horn button, the river pebble, and last, dearest last, the pyrite, the gold that was not gold. The child had carried them out of the wreck in one fist through all of it and set them out that first night without a sound, and set them out every night since, and it was, Clara had come to understand, the nearest thing to speech the child still allowed herself — that the row was kept, that the order held, that here at least was a small country where nothing more would be taken. Clara had stopped straightening them. They were the child's to keep. She only counted them, mornings, the way she had once counted the grey pot in the dark: to be sure.

She got the fire up with the day's grudged handful of turf and set the pea-porridge to warm, and when the child woke she woke without a word, as she woke every morning now, and lay a while watching Clara's blue hands at the fire before she rose. Clara talked into that watching, low, the way you talk to keep a hurt beast company — *there's porridge, mind your arm, the ice was thick again* — and got nothing back but the grave face; and once, only once that week, when Clara set the bowl in her lap, Grietje put out one finger and touched the back of Clara's stained hand, tracing a blue crease from knuckle to wrist the way she had done by candle-light in another life, and Clara held very still and did not spoil it, and the child took her finger back and ate her porridge, and that was all, and Clara carried it in her chest the rest of the day like a coal.

The goldfinch she kept in the chest, wrapped in the same dust-stiff rag she had carried it out in, under the folded spare shift, where the old woman would not come on it and ask. She did not take it out. She knew it was there the way she had known the grey pot was there, by putting her hand to the wrapping in the dark, and some nights that was the only thing in the room she could put her hand to and be sure of. A dead man's small bird, chained to its ring, holding still to be seen. She did not look at it. She was not yet ready to be seen by it.

Doncker came on the second day of the new year, when the cold was at its worst and the town had begun, in the way of towns, to turn from its grief toward its business. He came correct and grave as he had come to that same doorway in October, his good cloth brushed, a leather folder under his arm, and this time he was let in, because the old woman knew who he was and what he was worth and dropped him a stiff little courtesy and took herself out to leave the gentry to their affairs — and Clara understood from that alone that the household would be glad to have her off its hands, and could not even blame it.

He sat on the offered stool and refused the porridge she was bound by decency to offer, kindly, and he looked once, without seeming to, at the cold room and the mended child on the bed and the row of worthless things on the sill, and Clara watched him price it all, gently, the way he priced everything, and add it to his side of the ledger he carried in his head.

"I'll not waste your morning," he said, "with what you already know. The quarter's to be rebuilt, brick and stone, no more powder inside the walls, God be thanked, though late. The market's to be set out fresh on the Voldersgracht when the weather turns, the stalls redrawn, the rights looked into. And you know," gently, gently, "that a right looked into is a right that must answer for itself, and that yours, Clara, was never on the firmest ground even before the twelfth."

"I know the shape of it," Clara said. "You needn't draw me the whole picture. You've come to buy."

He inclined his head, as at a pupil who had learned her lesson, and opened the folder on his knee. "I've come to be of use," he said, "which you will hear as the same thing, and I'll not argue it, because you've earned the right to hear it however you like." He drew out a paper, close-written in a clerk's fair hand, and set it on the chest between them, and did not push it at her, which was its own kind of pushing. "The ground your house stood on. It's rubble and a cellar full of ice and it owes the town for its own clearing, and it will sell to no one but a man rebuilding that stretch, and I am the only man rebuilding that stretch. I'll take it off you and pay the clearing myself and give you, on top, a sum in hand." He named the sum. It was not nothing. It was a quarter of what the plot would have been worth with a house on it in October, and October would not come again. "And I'll speak for you to the board on the matter of the right — not to keep it, you'll not thank me for a lie, a widow with no shop and no stock two years past her husband cannot hold an enrolled trade and the board will not let her, my voice or no. But I can see it wound up decently. Surrendered, in good order, for a consideration, so it goes on the books as a thing given up and not a thing taken away. It will matter to the child someday, that her aunt gave a thing up rather than had it stripped from her."

"You've thought of the child," Clara said.

"I have a wife who thinks of little else since October," Doncker said, and for once there was no performance in it, and it was worse for that. "There's a place could be found for you. A decent house wanting a woman to keep it, over toward Gouda, my wife's people, good plain folk; you'd have your own room and the child schooled a little and no cold yard to break ice in before dawn. Or you'd take the money and go to your own people, if you've people left. Either way you'd be *settled*, Clara. Out of the cold. Not standing at a slab till your lungs go the way the grinders' lungs go, which you know better than I do, having ground the lead yourself these years against every warning. You needn't do this any longer. That's the whole of what I've come to say. You needn't."

And the terrible thing, the thing that had always been the trouble with Doncker, was that a great part of her wanted to lay down the chopper and say yes.

She had not slept warm in three months. She had watched a boy she loved go into the hasty ground and a child she had not asked for go silent, and buried the one and could not reach the other, and broken ice for her bread and eaten another woman's charity to the last cold spoon of it; and here on the chest was a paper that would end all of it — the cold, the debt, the guild's slow grinding no, the lead-dust in her chest — end it in the drawing of a name. A warm room. The child schooled. She looked at the paper in the grey light and felt the whole winter lean on the small of her back the way the fallen house had leaned, and thought: *I could put it down. I have carried it far enough. No one alive would say I had not carried it far enough.* She reached, and there was ink in the little horn Doncker had set out, correct man, prepared man, and a pen, and her blue hand went toward the pen the way a tired hand goes toward a warm wall.

Grietje got off the bed.

She did it without a sound, as she did everything now, sliding down with her good arm and crossing the cold floor in her shift, and Clara stopped with her hand above the pen and watched her, because you watched the child now, you did not let her out of the eye's corner. Grietje went to the sill. She reached up and took the last thing from the row, the pyrite, the gold that was not gold, and she carried it back across the room in her closed fist, and she did not give it to Clara, quite — she was too old for that and too far behind her own eyes for gestures — she only stood by Clara's knee and opened her fist on the edge of the chest, next to the paper, so that the fool's-gold sat there in the grey light with its worthless glitter, the pinch of stone Clara had picked out of a knuckle of lapis and given her, the thing the child had wept over when she learned it was worth nothing and loved the more for it. Then she looked at Clara, straight, out of Neeltje's grey eyes, and said nothing, because she had no words to give, but the thing was said all the same, laid on the chest beside the clerk's fair hand: *worth nothing, and kept anyway. That is a reason. You taught it to me.*

Clara took her hand back from the pen.

She was not certain, afterward, that the child had meant any of it; children put their treasures down where they please; it may have been only that the pyrite was cold in her fist and the chest was near. It did not matter what the child had meant. What mattered was the blue lying in the frozen mud two streets off, worth more than the plot and the porridge and Doncker's whole considered sum, worth more than the warm room in Gouda, and lying there unreclaimed because she had told herself it was gone, the way she had told herself the child's voice was gone, the way she had told herself a widow with no shop could not hold a trade. She had believed the arithmetic. She had nearly signed the arithmetic. And here was a worthless glittering thing on the chest to tell her that the arithmetic had never once, in her whole life, been the truest column in the book.

"Leave the paper," she said. "I'll not sign it today."

Doncker looked at her, and at the child, and at the pyrite, and something moved behind his correct face that might, in a smaller man, have been the beginning of understanding, and was in him only patience settling back into its long habit. "The offer stands to the first market of the spring," he said, rising, gathering the folder, leaving the paper where it lay because a paper left behind does more work than a paper carried off, and he knew it. "After that the board takes up the rights and it's out of my hands and yours both. Don't mistake stubbornness for strength, Clara. I've watched better than you break themselves on it." He set his hat on. "For the child's sake, think on the warm room. Whatever you think of me."

"I think of you exactly what you are," Clara said, without heat, "which is a man who grieves the dead sincerely and would take the living's last card all the same and see no quarrel in it. Mind the ice on the step. They've not sanded it."

He went. She heard his careful tread go down the passage and the street door, and the old woman's murmur, and then the cold ordinary morning closing over the place where he had been. Grietje had climbed back onto the bed and drawn her knees up and was watching her. The pyrite lay on the chest by the unsigned paper, glittering at nothing.

Clara picked it up. She turned it in her stained fingers a moment, the gold that was not gold, and then she reached past the child and set it back on the sill, last in the row, dearest last, where it belonged, and the child watched her do it and something in the small grave face eased, a fraction, no more, but Clara had learned to read a colour by less than a fraction and she read this one true. Then she went to the window and scraped a clear place in the frost with her thumbnail and looked out over the frozen roofs toward the quarter she could not quite see, where the snow lay grey over two streets of rubble, and she thought, for the first time since the white had taken the day, not *how does a person begin again from nothing* but a plainer thing, a working thing, a thing with her hands already in it: *the blue is in the mud. And the mud will not stay frozen forever. And I know, better than anyone left alive in this town, how to wash a colour up out of the dross it is bound to.*

She did not sign the paper. She did not throw it out either. She left it on the chest, where the sight of it would remind her, every cold morning, exactly what she was choosing against.

## 10. Blue in the Mud

She went back on the first day the thaw loosened the ground, and took the child with her, because she could not leave her in the cold room with the old woman's growing silence, and because some hard sure instinct told her that whatever she was going to do out there in the ruin, the child ought to see it done.

They came at the wreck of the Verwersdijk from the town side, over the makeshift plank bridges the labourers had thrown across the choked canals, and Clara had to stand a while at the edge of what had been her street before she could go into it. The thaw had left the ruin blacker and truer than the kind grey snow had let it be: two streets of tumbled brick and charred beam running down toward the Doelen, a well-head standing whole in the waste, a green door she remembered from October still upright against nothing, and over all of it the raw smell of wet ash and turned earth and a sweetish under-smell she did not name, for the child's sake and her own. Men worked here and there, clearing, carting; a surveyor and a boy stood plotting the new brick line of a house that had been somebody's grandfather's. Nobody minded a widow and a child picking their way in. There were a great many such, that winter, come back to poke at the place a life had stood.

She found her own ground by the cracked slab.

She had feared she would not know the place at all, one heap being the twin of the next, but the slab told her. Gerrit's porphyry slab, dark red and dished by two men's working, lay where the blast had flung it against the one surviving scrap of the back wall, cracked clean across into two halves and half-sunk in the frozen muck, and Clara stood over it and knew her own house the way you know a face in a coffin, by the bones under the ruin. Here had been the shop. Here the counter, gone to splinters. Here the low cupboard, burst; and there, in the black churned mud between the broken slab and the wall, was where the grey pot had stood, and where the white had thrown its blue across the room in a long stain in the hour the world ended.

She knelt down in the mud and began.

There is no dignity in it and she had expected none. She had brought what she needed in a basket like a woman going to market: a horn spoon, a knife, two shallow pans, a jug, a stoppered bottle of clean water carried across the whole town, and rags. She scraped the top muck away where the blue had fallen — she remembered the fall of it exactly, the eye that had spent a life adding up colour giving her now the one map that mattered — and worked down into the frozen crumb of the mud there was, faint, unmistakable to her and to no one else on earth, the blue. Not a hoard. Not the deep still cloud of it in the pot. A staining, a scattering, grains of the finest ultramarine ground grain by grain across years and flung by the powder into a yard of filthy earth, trodden by the diggers' boots, frozen and thawed, mixed now with brick-dust and ash and the pale calcite of its own crushing. To any other eye it was dirt. To hers it was the first water off the stone, the richest and the least, everything and nearly nothing, waiting to be freed.

So she freed it. She did the only thing she knew how to do, the thing she had done ten thousand mornings and taught to a boy now in the ground and a child now at her side: she took up the fouled blue mud a spoonful at a time and stirred it into water in the shallow pan, and swirled it, and watched. And the old law held, the law that did not care that the world had ended: the heavy dross fell fast. The brick-grit and the ash and the coarse calcite went down at once, and above them, hanging longest in the clouding water, the exact way she had shown the child by one candle a lifetime ago, hung the finest and the truest and the slowest to fall — the blue. She poured the top water off, at the breath of the right moment, carrying the best of it into the second pan and leaving the ruin behind, and did it again, and again, the whole patient freeing of one thing from all it was bound to, and slowly, wash on wash, spoonful on frozen spoonful, in a pan balanced on a cracked slab in the wreck of her own house, the blue came up out of the mud.

She did not weep, though it was the place to weep if there was ever a place. Her hands had stopped being cold. That was the strange mercy of the work: it took her, as it had always taken her, down below thought to a place where there was only the swirl and the settling and the pouring-off, the muller-rhythm of the wrists though there was no muller in her hands, and for whole minutes together she was not a widow in a ruin but a *verfmaalster* at her trade, freeing blue — the one country in all the world the white had not been able to take from her, because it lived in her hands and not in the house.

She had been at it perhaps an hour, and the child had drifted nearer through the whole of it, silent, watchful, crouching at last at the pan's edge with her mended arm held careful against her, when Clara's cold-stiffened fingers betrayed her and she went to pour the second wash a heartbeat too soon.

"Not yet," Grietje said.

Clara stopped with the pan tilted. The words had come small and hoarse and flat, a voice unused for three months, a rusted hinge of a voice, and for one moment Clara did not trust her ears, that had lied to her before under the ringing. But the child was looking at the pan, only at the pan, frowning at it with her whole grave face the way she frowned at the treasures on the sill, and she said it again, plainer, cross almost, a craftsman correcting a fumble: "Not yet. The best is slowest. You said." And she reached out with her good hand and put two fingers on the rim of the pan and held it level, held it back from the pouring, until the deep of the blue had hung and settled the fraction more it needed, and only then took her fingers away and let Clara pour.

Clara poured. The blue went over into the pan clean and did not spill a grain. And then she set the pan down very carefully on the cracked slab, because her hands had begun to shake and she would not shake with a pan of the true blue in them, and she looked at the child crouched in the wreck of the house with the winter light on her, and she did not gather her up and she did not weep on her and she did not say any of the enormous things rising in her, because the child had come back through a door held open by exactly one narrow thing, the work, the pan, the blue, and Clara understood with the whole of her hard-schooled heart that if she flooded her now with feeling she would frighten her back behind her eyes. So she did the one right thing. She picked up the horn spoon and held it out.

"Then you pour the next," she said, steady as she could make it. "You've the better eye for the moment. You always did."

The child took the spoon. And they knelt together in the ruin of the shop, the widow and the child who was not hers and was, and washed the blue up out of the mud grain by grain, hand over hand across a pan of dirty water, the way it had to be done, the way craft goes down a family or dies, and the child said the small things of the trade as they came to her, the words swimming back one by one out of wherever they had gone — *slow now; that's the dross; pour* — and Clara answered her plainly, in the same register, trade to trade, and did not once say *you spoke, you spoke, thank God you spoke,* though it beat in her the whole afternoon like a second heart.

Agatha found them there in the low sun.

Clara did not know how long she had stood at the edge of the ground before she spoke; the work took you down and you lost the world. She was thinner than October, and greyer, and dressed in the plain deep black that meant her people had found her a widow's clothes at least, and she stood at the broken threshold looking at the two of them crouched over their pans, and Clara rose stiffly to meet her, blue to the wrist, and the two women stood a moment in the ruin with nothing to say that the ruin had not already said. They had knelt across a dead man together. There was no starting smaller than that.

"They told me a woman was digging in the Verwersdijk wreck for pigment," Agatha said at last. "Washing dirt for colour. I thought — I knew it was you. Nobody else in this town would think there was anything left in the ground worth the having." She looked at the pan on the slab, the deep blue settling in it, and something crossed her worn face that was not quite a smile and was the nearest thing to one Clara would see on it. "He'd have liked that. That you came and dug his blue out of the mud. He always said you did the harder half."

"It's my blue," Clara said. "He never paid for it." And it was not unkind and Agatha did not take it unkindly; it was only true, the ledger standing plain between them the way ledgers do, and there was a rough comfort in the plainness, two widows counting what was owed and what was lost and not pretending either away.

Agatha had come, it turned out, to give the bird up for good. She was leaving Delft — her people were in Rotterdam, and there was nothing to hold her in a town that had killed her husband and buried his life's work under a burning wall — and a hundred times she had thought she ought to take the little goldfinch panel with her, the one thing spared, and found that she could not. "It's the last thing he saw finished," she said. "I can't have it on a wall to look at over my beer for forty years. And I can't sell it into some regent's stack to hang between his ships and his dead pheasants, to a man who'll never once ask if the bird was seen true." She looked at Clara steadily. "You saved it out of the fire. You knew what it was when you'd have left every other thing to burn. Keep it. Not to sell. Keep it where somebody comes who'll look at it the right way, a long time, before they touch anything. There'll be such a one, now and again, even here. Let him find it at yours."

And Clara, who had carried the bird against her heart out of the fire and could not yet bear to be looked at by it, found that she could bear this: to keep it not as a relic but as a thing set out for the ones who came to see. She said she would. Agatha did not thank her, which was right; you did not thank a person for a weight, you only shifted it to hands that could carry it. She looked once more at the child, at the mended arm and the blue hands and the spoon, and said, "She's grinding already," half a question.

"Washing," said Grietje, from the mud, not looking up. "You wash before you grind. Everyone knows that." It was the longest thing she had said in three months, and it was pure Neeltje, the flat certainty, the *everyone knows,* and Clara had to turn and look hard at the pan for a moment, and Agatha's worn mouth did move then, it did, before she gathered her black about her and picked her way back out of the ruin toward the living town and the barge to Rotterdam, and Clara never saw her again, and thought of her often, and hoped, without much faith in the hoping, that Rotterdam had been kinder to her than Delft.

They worked till the light went. And near the end, when the low sun had turned the frozen canal to a sheet of dull rose, a man came picking his way along the ruined stretch and stopped at her ground — a young man, quiet, plainly dressed, whom she knew a little: the painter from over on the Oude Langendijk, who had married the Bolnes girl and taken his oath in the guild the Christmas before the blast, who bought less colour than he wanted and looked at it a long time before he touched it. He stood at the edge of her wreck with his hat in his hand, and did not offer help, which she was grateful for, and did not gape.

"I heard you'd — " he began, and stopped, and looked instead at the pans, at the blue settling in them in the rose light, and his slow careful eyes went over it the way they went over a colour on her boards, weighing, and Clara saw the want come up in him, the honest craftsman's want, quiet and deep. "Is that the true stone," he said. "Ultramarine. That you've — washed out of the ground."

"It's the true stone," Clara said. "Come back in the spring, meester. When I've a slab to grind on and a roof to stand under. I'll have blue then, the best in Delft, if you can pay for it." She had not planned to say it. It came out of her plain and certain, the way *not yet* had come out of the child, and she heard in it a promise she was making not to him but to herself: *There'll be a spring, and I'll be standing in it, trading colour on my own terms in this town that tried to grind me into the ground with the rest.* The young man looked at her, and looked at the blue, and something passed over his quiet face that she would only understand the size of much later, and he said he would come back in the spring, and put his hat on, and went, careful, the way he did everything.

Clara knelt in the last of the light with the reclaimed blue safe in its bottle and the child asleep against a fallen beam, worn out with the first real work of her life, and she looked at the wreck of everything she had built going blue and violet as the dusk came down. She had lost the shop and the slab and the boy and the man and the whole of her old life in the white. She had a bottle of blue washed out of the mud, and a child's voice come back over a pan of dirty water, and a promise made to a quiet young painter and to herself. It was not much to begin again from. It was more than she had had that morning. She woke the child, gently, and they went home over the plank bridges through the deepening dark, and Clara carried the reclaimed blue against her heart where the bird had been.

## 11. The Widow's Blue

The board of the Guild of St Luke sat in judgment on the widow's right on a raw morning at the tail of the winter, in the guild's own house on the Voldersgracht, which the blast had cracked but not felled, and Clara came before it with her reclaimed blue in a stoppered pot in her basket and the child's hand in hers and Gerrit's surviving muller wrapped in a cloth, and set none of them down, and waited to be told whether she might go on being what she was.

She had brought the muller for a reason she could not have named to Doncker and did not try to. It had come up out of the mud whole — the loaf of dark porphyry that had fitted her palms ten thousand mornings, the slab cracked in two under the fallen house but the muller, being small and hard and made to take a beating, thrown clear and unbroken. She had washed it and it fitted her hand as it always had, cool and heavy and hers, and she had found she could not come before the men who would judge her trade without the tool of it against her side, the way another woman might not go into a hard room without her mother's ring on her finger. It steadied her. Let them see she came with the thing itself in her hand and not with a paper about the thing.

There were six of them at the long table, two potters, two glass-men, two painters, as the charter had it, and Doncker was not of them — he was a merchant and an elder, not a board-man that year — but he was in the room, correct and grave against the wall with the younger guild-men, there to see his patience come to its harvest, and Clara marked him and did not look at him again. The senior of the painters, a heavy old man named Vromans who had known Gerrit and bought colour from Clara for two years and paid late and honest, read the matter out flatly: that the widow Wijnants had held her late husband's enrolled trade by the widow's leave these two years and more; that the said trade was now without shop, stock, or standing, the premises destroyed in the late calamity; that the question before the board was whether the leave might continue or must lapse, the market being redrawn and the rights looked into.

Then he asked her, not unkindly, what she had to say for it.

She had thought a long time about what she had to say for it, in the cold room, over the reclaimed blue, and in the end she said very little, because she had learned across a life that a woman pleading was a woman half-lost in such a room, and that the men would forgive her many things sooner than they would forgive her a scene. She set the pot on the table. She unstoppered it. She turned it so the deep still blue caught the grey light from the cracked window, the truest ultramarine in Delft, and she let it sit there a moment in front of six men who had spent their lives knowing to the stuiver what such a blue was worth, before she said a word.

"That's the true stone," she said. "Purified, washed, ready to grind. There's not another pot of it this side of Amsterdam, and there'll not be, because the men who could make it are dead in the Doelen or scattered, and the stock in every shop in this quarter went into the mud on the twelfth of October, and I am the one grinder left in Delft who has any, because I washed it up out of that mud myself, grain by grain, on my knees, this past month, off the ground my house stood on." She let that sit too. "You've painters in this town who'll want it. The deacons' portraits will want the loodwit and the black, but the men who matter, the ones who'll be remembered, they'll want the blue, and they'll want it ground fine and washed clean by somebody who knows the trade to the bone, and there is one such somebody left, and she is standing in front of you asking leave to go on being what she has been these eleven years without costing this guild a stuiver or a scandal." She stoppered the pot. "That's what I've to say for it. Not that you owe a widow a kindness. That you need a grinder, and I am the grinder, and it would be a poor sort of good order that closed the last colour-house in the quarter to keep a rule tidy."

There was a silence, and then the men did what men do, which was to argue the thing among themselves as though she were not in the room. It was Doncker's ground and Doncker had prepared it, and his side of it came out of the younger men's mouths dressed as principle: that a right was a right and could not be bent to a hard case however sad; that if a widow with no shop might hold a trade on the strength of a pot of blue, then the door the guild had always only leaned-to was thrown wide, and every craftsman's relict from here to the Schie would claim the same; that it was not order. And it was true, and she had known it would be said, and she had no answer to it that was not the pot on the table, and the pot on the table was, she saw, not going to be enough by itself, because the men who feared for the rule feared it more than they wanted the blue.

It was the young painter who tipped it, though he never spoke, and did not know he had.

He was not of the board and had no standing to speak and did not try to, but he had come — she saw him at the back, quiet, his hat in his hands — and when the wrangle had run a while and old Vromans, tired of it, asked the room plainly whether any master present would stand surety that the widow's blue was worth the keeping of her, the young man from the Oude Langendijk put up his hand. Not to make a speech. He said only, in his slow careful way, that he had seen the colour she had washed out of the ruin, with his own eyes, in the wreck of her house, and that he had ordered blue of her for a painting he meant to make in the spring, and that he would put his name to it that there was no finer in the province and no one else he would trust to grind it, and that a guild that let such a maker go for want of a rule bent once would be the poorer in twenty years and would not know why. Then he sat down, plainly wishing he had not had to stand, and Clara understood that he had staked something of his own new small standing to say it, a man not a year in the guild speaking up against an elder, and she did not forget it.

They put her out while they settled it, into the cold passage, and she stood there with the child and the muller and the pot and did not let her face give anything away, and at the far end of the passage Doncker came and stood with her, because he could not help himself, because a righteous man must be seen to be kind at the last.

"You'll not win it clean, whatever they do," he said, low. "You know that. Even if they let you stand, they'll not give you the market-right, not the good stall on the new Voldersgracht — that's promised, that's my nephew's, and the board will not turn a promise over for a widow's blue. And they'll bind you. Watch. They'll let you grind and let you sell, because you've frightened them with the pot, but they'll bind you to terms a man wouldn't take, and call it a mercy, and you'll thank them for it because the other side of it is nothing." He looked at her, and there was no triumph in it, which was the worst of him, only a kind of weary certainty. "I'd have seen you settled warm, Clara. Remember that I offered it, when you're standing at a slab at sixty with the lead in your chest."

"I'll remember you offered," Clara said. "I'll remember what it cost, too, that you called it settling. Go and collect your nephew's stall, Willem. You've won the ground and the market and the good stall on the new street. Let me have the leave to grind, and you can have all the rest, and we'll both call it what it is when we're alone, which is that you got nine parts of ten and grudged me the tenth."

Something went across his face, and for once it was not patience. He was quiet a moment. "Gerrit was my friend," he said, "before he was your husband. I've not enjoyed any of this. You've never once believed that."

"I've always believed it," Clara said. "That's the whole trouble with you. You've meant every kindness and taken every stall all the same, and never once felt the two of them touch. I don't hate you, meester. I'd have had an easier winter if I could." And that was true, and it landed truer than any anger, and Doncker looked at her a moment longer and then inclined his head, correct as a deacon even in that cold passage, and went back in, and did not speak to her again.

They gave her the leave. Doncker had read it exactly, as he read everything: they upheld the widow's right to continue Gerrit's enrolled trade — grinding and the sale of colour — but they gave her nothing else and bound the leave in three cold ropes. She would have no place on the new market; the Voldersgracht stalls were drawn and hers was not among them; she might sell only from a shop, and must find and hold one within the year or the leave lapsed. She would pay a fee for the continuance, a sum set high enough to sting, a bond against the trouble she was held to represent. And she would supply the guild's own painters and the guild's own panels, the town-work, at a rate the board fixed, below what the trade would bear, in exchange for the leave to sell the rest as she pleased — so that she bought her freedom, in the end, by grinding a part of every year for the guild at a loss, a leash worn as a livery. It was a hard bargain and a narrow one and it left her poorer standing than she had been the morning of the twelfth, with a shop still to find and a fee still to pay and a year to do it in.

And it was a victory, and she knew it for one, walking out into the raw noon with the child and the muller and the pot, because she walked out of that house still a *verfmaalster* of Delft in her own right and her own name, unmarried, unsettled, answerable to no man's roof, free to grind the finest blue in the province and sell it to whom she chose, which was the one thing Doncker had wanted to take and had not taken. She had lost the ground and the stall and the warm room and a great deal of the money there was to lose, and she had kept the tenth part, the tenth part that was the trade itself, the standing at the slab, the door held open by a widow's stubbornness and a pot of reclaimed blue and one quiet young man's raised hand. It was not a rout of Doncker. It was a thing bought dear and held narrow. It was enough.

"Did we win?" Grietje asked her, on the plank bridge over the frozen Voldersgracht, in her hoarse recovering voice that Clara was still not used to and drank in every time it came.

"We kept the trade," Clara said. "We didn't get the good stall. We've a fee to pay and a shop to find and a hard year to do it in, and I'll be grinding the guild's umber half for nothing till I'm grey."

The child considered this with her whole grave face. "But we kept the trade."

"We kept the trade."

"Then we won," Grietje said, with Neeltje's flat certainty, closing the account, and Clara looked down at her, the mended arm, the blue hands, the voice come back over a pan of mud, and thought that the child had read the ledger truer than she had, the way the child had read the pyrite truer, and she took the small blue hand in her own blue hand and they went home to the cold room to begin the hard year, and it was, though neither of them said so, the first day since the twelfth of October that either of them had walked anywhere toward a thing instead of away from one.

## 12. The Colour That Remains

By the spring she had a shop, of a kind, and a slab to grind on, and the young painter came for his blue on a fair evening in May with the light going long and gold over a canal that men were already mending stone by stone, and Clara ground the reclaimed ultramarine for him on Gerrit's muller and a new-bought slab and did not grieve, much, that the old slab was not under her hands.

The shop was two low rooms off the Nieuwe Langendijk that she held on a year's lease and a hard bargain, well back from the ruin, damp, dark, cheap, and hers; the ground floor for the trade and the slab, the room above for the two of them to sleep dry at last. She had paid the guild's fee with the last of what she had and a little she had borrowed against the blue, and she had bought the new slab second-hand from a dead potter's widow, a plain grey stone, not porphyry, not Gerrit's, dished by no working but her own to come; and she had set Gerrit's surviving muller on it the first morning and put her palms to the cool weight of it and begun to grind, and the rhythm had come up out of her wrists unchanged, below thought, the one inheritance the white had left her whole. She ground the guild's umber at a loss in the mornings, as bound, and cursed it, and did it clean all the same, because a grudged colour ground gritty was a colour that gave Doncker a stick. In the afternoons she ground what she pleased and sold it to whom she pleased, and word had gone about the trade, in the way word goes, that the widow Wijnants had the true blue when no one else in Delft had a grain of it, blue washed up out of the ruin itself, and they came for it, the painters, quietly, one at a time, and she made them pay, and the ledger, kept in her own hand now with Gerrit's dead crossings-out on the older pages still, began, month by hard month, to lean a little less the wrong way.

The child ground beside her. Grietje's arm had knit true, and by spring she could take the small muller in both hands and set it going on the second slab, light and slow, the child's version of the woman's stroke, and she ground the earths that forgave a beginner and washed the blue that did not, and she talked while she worked, all day, the words all come back now and more arriving, so that the shop was full of her voice the way the old shop had been, the tuneless singing, the flat certainties, the questions out of nowhere. She was not the child she had been before the white; Clara did not fool herself that any of them were what they had been. There were nights the child woke with the sound of it in her ears and would not say what she had dreamed, and mornings she went to the sill first thing to count the four things and be sure, the tile, the button, the pebble, the gold that was not gold, before she would come to her porridge. The white had gone down into her and would be down there always, a coarse grain that would never quite wash out. But the finest of her had settled back, the best particles, the slowest to fall — the frank greed for anything that shone, the tenderness, the voice for singing that was Neeltje's and not Clara's, thank God — and Clara had learned to be grateful for what settled and not to grieve too long what was lost in the pouring, which was, she thought, the whole of the trade, and the whole of raising a child, and perhaps the whole of living past a thing that should have killed you, all one lesson, learned over and over at a pan of dirty water.

The goldfinch hung in the shop, in the good north light by the window, where a person coming for colour would see it.

She had not sold it and would not, and she had stopped being unable to look at it. It hung there small and whole in its pale warm-and-cool light, the loose dragged paint that was hardly paint at all up close and was wholly a living bird a step back, the thin chain running from the ring to the band about its leg, and it did what Agatha had said it would do: now and again, a person came for a paper of ochre and saw it, and stopped, and looked a long time before they said anything, and those were the ones Clara marked, the ones who looked before they touched. Most did not. Most bought their umber and never saw the bird at all, and that was the world, and she did not hold it against them. But the panel drew the seeing ones the way the blue drew the good painters, and the shop became, without her quite meaning it, a place in the wounded town where a certain kind of looking still went on, and she thought that was as near as a dead man got to going on: that the thing he made kept teaching the seeing after the seer was in the ground.

The young painter came for his blue on the fair May evening, and this time he had money, and paid it, honest coin counted slow, and did not ask for credit, and Clara ground him the true ultramarine finer than she ground it for anyone, walnut oil for the blue as it should be, and watched him watch her do it. He had come, he said at last, awkward, looking at the bird on the wall as much as at her, for a painting he was going to make. A woman, he said. By a window. With the light coming across her, morning light, off to the side, the way it falls and models a face and a wall and the air between them; and a blue in it, in the cloth, in the shadow of the cloth, a blue no one had put there so before, a blue you could — he stopped, having run out past what he could say, being a man who did his saying with his hands and not his mouth, and Clara stood very still with the muller in her palms.

"A blue you could breathe," she said.

He looked up. "Yes," he said. "How — yes. Exactly that."

She did not tell him. She could have told him that a dead man had stood at another counter in another shop that no longer existed, a lifetime and half a mile away, and wanted the same thing in the same words, a woman by a window with the light coming across and a blue in the shadow of the cloth that no one had put there before, a blue you could breathe, and had never got to make it, because the light he chased his whole life had come through his north wall one grey Monday and the wall had come down on him in it. She could have told the young man that he was going to make the painting the dead man had carried unmade into the rubble, without knowing he carried it, the way water carries the finest colour it does not know it holds. But there was nothing in the telling for him, and a good deal that might weigh him, and the dead do not need the living to speak their names over every colour; they need only the colour ground true and put where the light can find it. So she said none of it. She wrapped the blue in a twist of paper, and, because she was a fool, and because the light was going long and gold in the window and the bird held still on the wall to be seen, she weighed out a little more than he had paid for and folded that in too, and did not mention it.

"That's more than I bought," he said, who had the colourman's eye for it, the way the boy had used to.

"It's what you bought," Clara said. "Make the woman by the window. Get the blue right." And then, because it was owed, because a debt is a debt and this one had come down to her across the whole ruin to pay: "There was a man in this town could have told you how to see it. He's dead. You'll have to find it yourself, in the dark, which is where the seeing's done. But you've the blue now that he'd have wanted, the true stone, the best I have. Don't waste it on a wall nobody breathes near. Put it where the light comes across." He looked at her, and at the bird, and something passed over his quiet careful face that she thought she understood at last the size of, and he said he would, and put his hat on, and went out into the long gold evening with her blue over his heart, two rooms from a canal that had no powder anywhere near it now, and Clara stood in the door of her poor shop and watched him go the way she had once watched another man go, and did not, this time, wish she had not looked.

Grietje came and stood in the door with her, drying her blue hands on the rag.

The evening had come down to the blue hour, the little while at the edge of dark that the painters cursed and could not do without, when the gold goes out of the sky by degrees and every colour on the earth deepens and stands true a moment before the night takes it. The mended stretch of canal lay still and held the last of the light, blue on blue, the new stone of the mended quay and the old stone of the standing houses all gone one deepening colour together, and the tower of the Nieuwe Kerk stood black against a sky the exact grave blue that no man could grind and every man tried to, the blue you could breathe, going deeper as they watched. Somewhere a woman called a child in. The water lay unmoving. It was, Clara thought, a poor light to sell colour by and the truest light there was to see by, and she had lived her whole life in it and been too busy breaking stone to be grateful for it, and she was grateful for it now, standing in the door of a shop that was not the shop she had lost, with a child that was not the child she had been given and was, past all arguing, hers.

The cost was not paid off and would not be. Barent was in the hasty ground and would stay there, a boy who had wanted the guild so badly it came off him like heat, who had gone from wanting everything to nothing between one cold breath and the next, and there was no blue hour that gave him back, and Clara would carry him unmended to her own grave, which was the honest price and she did not pretend it away. The town was two years into a twenty years' rebuilding and the ruin still ran black under the snow-melt across the quarter, and no one would ever count the dead, and the not-counting was a wound the town would wear under its new brick forever. The white had taken more than it could ever give back, and beauty, she knew better than anyone left alive, was made by breaking, and cost more than it ever quite returned, and was worth the making all the same, which was the last hard thing the trade had to teach and she had spent a life learning and was only now, in the blue hour, in the door of the poor new shop, ready to say she believed.

"Bluest hands in Delft," Grietje said, holding hers up in the last of it, the old game, the child's whole small greedy joy in the mark of the trade, and Clara took the small stained hand in her own stained hand, blue to the creases and blue in the coffin, and did not correct her, because it was true, the two of them, and because the light was almost gone.

"Bluest hands in Delft," she said. They stood in the door and watched the blue go out of the water and the sky come down, and behind them in the dark shop the little bird held still on the wall in a light of its own making, chained to its ring, waiting to be seen; and above the mended canal the last of the day drained slow and even and without any flattery in it, the colourless Delft light gone at the end all to colour, deepening and deepening, until it was too dark to work by and exactly bright enough to see.
