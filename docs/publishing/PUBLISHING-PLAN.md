# Publishing Plan — the 14-Book Catalog

> **Status:** `plan`

*Money seat (venture-lab). Drafted 2026-07-12. Every market figure below is cited with a source URL and accessed date. This is a PLAN: every account-creation, upload, and pricing click is an OWNER action — the seat executes nothing here.*

## Provenance — what we took from the external review, and what we threw out

The owner commissioned a GPT deep-research review of the catalog. Its **craft critiques were discarded as ungrounded**: they show no evidence the model read the manuscripts (it described "Lull" as ~80k words ending on a cliffhanger when it is 12 chapters ending on a closed choice; it invented a "bureaucratic prologue" for the crime novella, which actually opens on a canal body recovery). We do not act on that half.

Its **market claims were treated as input to verify, not trust.** We checked each against live sources. Most held up; two were corrected and three carry caveats (below). That verified market direction is what this plan is built on.

## 1. Verified price points per category

| Category | Recommended price | Basis | Verified? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Adult / literary novella (ebook) | **$3.99–$5.99** | Sits inside the 70% royalty band; industry convention for standalone novellas | Convention (see caveat) |
| YA novella (ebook) | **$2.99–$3.99** | $2.99 is the floor to keep the 70% royalty tier; $0.99 is a valid impulse entry point but drops to 35% | Convention + threshold verified |
| Kids picture book (paperback, print) | **List $12.99–$14.99** | Accepted self-pub "sweet spot" | Verified |

**Royalty threshold (verified, hard fact):** KDP pays **70% only on ebooks priced $2.99–$9.99** (inclusive) in eligible territories incl. US/UK/DE/NL; below $2.99 or above $9.99 pays **35%**, and a per-MB delivery fee is deducted from the 70% tier. So a $0.99 YA price earns 35%, not 70% — flag this trade-off when pricing. [KDP royalty rates, accessed 2026-07-12](https://www.kdpeasy.com/guides/2026-kdp-royalty-rates); [35% vs 70%, accessed 2026-07-12](https://www.vappingo.com/word-blog/amazon-kdp-royalties-35-vs-70/)

**CORRECTION #1 — kids print cost.** The review claimed $4–8 print cost. Actual KDP **premium-color print cost for a 24–40pp paperback is a flat ~$3.60 on Amazon.com** (the per-page charge only kicks in above 42 pages). A 32-page picture book ≈ $3.60, not $4–8. [KDP printing cost, accessed 2026-07-12](https://kdp.amazon.com/en_US/help/topic/G201834340) At a $12.99 list, paperback royalty (60% of list − print cost) ≈ **$4.19/unit**; at $14.99 ≈ **$5.39/unit**.

**Novella-length caveat (verified, qualitative).** Short standalone titles are a discoverability handicap **outside a series or Kindle Unlimited (KU)** — KU rewards short, frequent, series-based publishing via pay-per-page-read. Novellas still sell as series entry points and to commuters. Net: a standalone novella outside KU/a series is disadvantaged, but this is market consensus, not a measured figure. [Just Publishing Advice, accessed 2026-07-12](https://justpublishingadvice.com/why-do-short-ebooks-sell-so-well/)

**Price-band caveat.** The exact YA-vs-adult bands are industry convention, not a codified KDP rule — treat as directional.

## 2. Priority order for publication

The gating variable is **production cost, not length**: a title needs either just a **cover** (cheap, adult/literary standalones) or a **cover + full interior illustration** (expensive, kids picture books). We did not read the manuscripts, so the owner must confirm which cover-only titles are true novellas vs. full novels; the ordering below is by gate + discoverability, which is what actually blocks a publish click.

**Tier 1 — publish first (cover-only, no illustration gate).** The adult/literary standalones. Start with the cleanest discoverability:
1. **The Slow Word** — no collision, distinctive.
2. **The Painted Stones** — no exact collision (recheck on KDP before lockdown).
3. **The Lantern Door** — no collision, distinctive two-noun phrase.
4. **Lull** — confirmed novella; near-unsearchable bare word, needs subtitle *"A Novella"*.
5. **The Last Good Frequency** — adjacent to two 2026 "Last Frequency" titles but the extra word differentiates.

**Tier 1b — cover-only but fix the title first (real collisions):**
- **The Undertow** — most crowded title in the catalog (Jo Baker; Jeff Sharlet NYT bestseller). Needs a strong subtitle or rename before publishing.
- **Ultramarine** — collides head-on with Mariette Navarro's 2025 literary novel of the same name. Subtitle or rename.
- **Hollowtide** — collides with an active fantasy series. Add a genre-signalling subtitle.
- **The Weigh House** — no collision but weigh/weight homophone risk; subtitle *"A Novel"*.

**Tier 2 — GATED ON ILLUSTRATION (owner money decision, see §3).** The kids picture books: **Comet Biscuit** (×3 series), **Bram the Yak Cannot Whisper**, **Dormouse** (publish as *"Pippa and the Tear in the Night"* — the bare "Dormouse" is buried under Julia Donaldson et al.), **Star Pirates** (kids/YA — prefer this over the alt "The Wrong Way Down", which collides with an established crime series). **Tummel** sits between tiers depending on whether it ships illustrated — owner to confirm.

## 3. The kids-book illustration decision (owner money-action)

Kids titles cannot publish without interior art. Three options — this is a real spend decision, so it is an OWNER-ACTION, not a seat call:

- **(A) Commission art.** Realistic 2025/26 rates: **~$100–$400 per spread**; a 32pp book has ~12–15 spreads → **~$1,200–$6,000/book**; Reedsy's market average is **$1,540–$4,950** for a 24pp book; a budget freelancer can land under $1,500 with quality/consistency risk. [Reedsy children's-book cost, accessed 2026-07-12](https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/how-to-self-publish-a-childrens-book/cost-to-self-publish-childrens-books/); [Reedsy illustration, accessed 2026-07-12](https://reedsy.com/design/book-illustration) For a 3-book Comet Biscuit series + 2 standalone kids titles, commissioned art is a **four-figure-per-book, low-five-figure-total** outlay.
- **(B) AI-generated art.** Tool cost is near-zero (~$0–$30/mo). **KDP permits AI-generated cover and interior art but requires you to DISCLOSE it at publish time** (AI-*generated*, not AI-*assisted*); the disclosure is internal to Amazon and, per Amazon, does not affect royalties or ranking. [KDP Content Guidelines, accessed 2026-07-12](https://kdp.amazon.com/en_US/help/topic/G200672390) Caveats: undisclosed or low-quality AI content risks title removal / account action (secondary-sourced, not verbatim KDP); there is a **3-new-titles-per-day** anti-spam cap; and the **copyright status of purely AI-generated images is legally unsettled**, which can limit exclusivity. [Authors Guild, accessed 2026-07-12](https://authorsguild.org/news/amazons-new-disclosure-policy-for-ai-generated-book-content-is-a-welcome-first-step/); [PW daily-limit, accessed 2026-07-12](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/digital/content-and-e-books/article/93207-kdp-will-limit-daily-number-of-new-titles.html)
- **(C) Park.** Defer all kids titles until Tier-1 revenue (or the Stripe Kit line) funds commissioned art.

**Seat recommendation:** **Park the kids titles (C) and publish Tier 1 first.** Kids books carry the full illustration cost up front against the same unknown-author zero base case — spending four figures on art before any title has sold is the wrong order. If the owner wants to test the kids line cheaply, pilot **AI art on ONE title (with disclosure)** rather than commissioning the whole series, and treat the legally-unsettled image IP as the reason not to build the flagship series on AI art long-term.

## 5. Translation strategy

- **Novellas → English-first.** The Netherlands ranks **#1 globally** and Germany **#4** on English proficiency (EF EPI 2025), and English-language children's-book sales in NL reportedly grew ~153% (2018–2022; single-source, unattributed — see caveats). English-first reaches those readers without a translation spend. [EF EPI NL, accessed 2026-07-12](https://www.ef.com/wwen/epi/regions/europe/netherlands/); [The Low Countries, accessed 2026-07-12](https://www.the-low-countries.com/article/english-books-are-popular-with-dutch-speakers-so-what/)
- **Kids trilingual editions already exist (sunk cost)** — but publication stays **gated on illustration** (§3), same as the English kids editions. Producing translations does not unlock a publish click; art does.
- **Dutch print gotcha (verified):** KDP supports Dutch for ebook and print, **but does not allow PDF manuscript upload for Dutch** (PDF is allowed for EN/DE/FR/IT/ES/PT/CA/GL/EU only). Picture books are normally laid out as fixed-layout PDF, so a **Dutch picture book has no clean PDF path** and needs a non-PDF workaround. German has no such limit. [KDP language support, accessed 2026-07-12](https://kdp.amazon.com/en_US/help/topic/G200673300)
- **German market context:** ~€9.9B / ~$10.7B (2024), print-dominant with only ~6% ebook share, and a fixed book-price law (Buchpreisbindung) that binds print retail pricing — though it does not meaningfully bind self-published print-on-demand or ebooks. English-first is defensible for reach but not a guaranteed edge against German-language competitors. [Germany publishing, accessed 2026-07-12](https://www.trade.gov/market-intelligence/germany-publishing)

## 6. Conservative revenue framing

**Base case for an unknown author with no marketing is ≈ $0.** This plan does not project sales. Unit economics only: a novella at $4.99 earns ~$3.49/sale (70%); a $12.99 kids paperback earns ~$4.19/unit after print cost. Those are *per-sale* figures, not forecasts — zero sales is the honest default until a title demonstrates otherwise. Kids titles additionally carry four-figure up-front art cost against that same zero base, which is why Tier 1 (cover-only) goes first. Any revenue is upside; nothing here is a promise.
